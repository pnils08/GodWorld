# The Vote That Wasn't There

**The eight deliverables landed. The committee was seated. Aitken was in the room. The vote was not held.**

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

---

Mark Aitken arrived before the meeting was called to order.

The A's first baseman — batting .243, three home runs, eleven RBIs into the current stretch — took a seat in the council gallery and watched the room fill around him. The five members of the newly sworn Oversight Committee for the Fruitvale Transit Hub were there: Maria Reyes, the E. 12th Street resident who has walked International Boulevard since before the current council majority was seated; Roberto Iglesias, who has run a taquería on that same stretch for eleven years; Carmen Solis, who works as an advocate for the Mam-speaking community in the Fruitvale corridor; Derek Washington, workforce coordinator for the east side trades pipeline; and Priya Patel, whose work on anti-displacement policy made her the fifth and final appointment. The committee had been sworn in this cycle. They were present. The docket said there was a vote.

Elena Soria Dominguez had filed her certification that morning. The pre-vote briefing was complete. All eight Community Benefits Agreement deliverables for the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II — INIT-003 — had been verified. The eighth item, the Oversight Committee seating itself, was the one that closed the list, and it had now closed. Soria Dominguez had delivered every item she was asked to deliver, on every timeline she was given.

Council President Ramon Vega had been unambiguous. "Fruitvale has waited long enough," he said, in remarks submitted for the record. "The vote is scheduled, the CBA deliverables are verified, and we move forward." He committed to a yes. He was on the record.

The vote was not held.

---

Not lost. Not deferred in a formal sense, not tabled by a member motion, not postponed because a quorum failed. The vote was on the docket, the deliverables were ready, and on the day, the room did not act. One council voice — Vega's — had INIT-003 present at the desk this cycle. The mechanism that routes a scheduled agenda item to all nine members did not run. Seven council voices did not enter the record on the Transit Hub. The item passed through the cycle without registering as a vote.

This is not a procedural anomaly with a tidy explanation. It is a structural gap: a city can build all the way to the moment of decision — complete the CBA, seat the committee, secure a commitment from the council president — and still not hold the vote if the agenda routing mechanism does not fire.

Delia Fuentes noticed. The school bus driver from Fruitvale, who first told this desk in an earlier cycle that her rent had climbed past a point she could absorb, has been tracking the Transit Hub timeline the way you track something that keeps not happening. "You keep waiting for it," she said, "and it doesn't come." She first said something like that about her rent. She said it again, this cycle, about the vote.

Fuentes drives through the project footprint every morning. She knows the three design scenarios that are now on the table — 4,200 units at the high end, 2,800 at the middle, 1,600 at the low, with affordability ranges running between 40 and 45 percent across all three. She knows those numbers matter to her and to her neighbors. She has been waiting for the council to tell her which version the city intends to build.

---

The Oversight Committee had gathered in the gallery expecting to watch the vote they were created to monitor. Maria Reyes has lived within walking distance of the project site her entire adult life. Roberto Iglesias has watched a generation of Fruitvale small businesses cycle through the uncertainty of a neighborhood that has been promised a transit hub across multiple planning phases. Carmen Solis has spent this cycle pressing the city on language access — whether Mam-speaking residents who may be displaced will have the services the CBA promises.

All five had done the work of showing up. The vote they were seated to oversee did not happen.

The project itself remains in vote-ready status. Three design scenarios are staged for council consideration. The CBA framework that took years of community negotiation to produce is verified and complete. The next action falls in the following cycle.

---

The structural question this piece is not in a position to answer fully — because it was not answered on the floor — is whether the gap was mechanical, calendrical, or something quieter. The agenda item existed. The readiness existed. The council president had committed a yes. The eight remaining council members — Carter, Tran, Delgado, Rivers, Crane, Ashford, Chen, Mobley — did not enter the record.

Eleven years covering this council. The votes that don't happen are sometimes the ones that tell you more than the votes that do. When a motion fails on the floor, you have a record: who voted no, what the objection was, what the path forward looks like. When a vote doesn't happen because the mechanism didn't route the item, you have a gap in the record and a cycle lost.

The Fruitvale Transit Hub is a $230 million project. It is not small. The community that will live inside it — the residents like Delia Fuentes who need to know whether 4,200 units or 1,600 units or something in between is the city's actual intention — has been patient across multiple planning cycles. The CBA process exists because that community organized to demand it. Soria Dominguez's certification exists because a planning lead finished her work. The Oversight Committee's presence in the gallery exists because the city seated them to ensure accountability for implementation.

All of that is real. The readiness is real. It met an empty mechanism.

---

Aitken sat in the gallery through the proceedings. After the meeting closed without the vote, he left the way he came — through the main entrance, without a statement, without a press availability. He has made a habit of showing up to council votes across the city, a civic presence that has not attached itself to a particular outcome or a public position. He was there for a vote that did not happen.

The committee members filed out behind him. The next cycle's agenda is not yet set.

Delia Fuentes, asked whether she thought the vote would happen next cycle, paused before answering. "It's supposed to," she said. "It was supposed to happen this one."

---

*The Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II (INIT-003) remains in vote-ready status. The five-member Oversight Committee — Maria Reyes, Roberto Iglesias, Carmen Solis, Derek Washington, and Priya Patel — was seated this cycle. All eight CBA deliverables have been certified. The vote advances to the next cycle.*

# What Readiness Without Action Looks Like

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

This cycle, the Fruitvale Transit Hub did not reach a vote that everyone — the council president, the planning lead, the CBA-certified deliverables, the Oversight Committee sworn in for that purpose — agreed had been built toward for years. The vote did not fail. There was no vote. The agenda item routed to one council voice and stopped there. Eight others did not enter the record because the mechanism that routes a scheduled item to all nine members did not fire on the day it was supposed to.

This is the story to read first this cycle, and the one to carry into next cycle. Carmen Delaine has covered the Fruitvale corridor long enough to know the difference between a vote that loses on the merits and a vote that doesn't happen because something quieter broke. The first leaves a record. The second leaves a community waiting and a $230M project unmoved.

You will read elsewhere in this edition that other things did happen. The Stabilization Fund cleared 108 households in West Oakland. The Health Center contract executed in Temescal. The Phase II RFP went public with workforce mandates intact. OARI's rubric coalesced into a vote architecture for next cycle. Each of those is structural progress.

But the Transit Hub was structural progress that arrived at the door, knocked, and found no one home. That is also news.

— M.C.

# OARI Rubric Locks; Eight Votes Now Visible

**The C95 OARI architecture is now visible on paper — eight votes named, the rubric administration-official, the legal items finally correctly described. Visible isn't held.**

*By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger*

---

Dante Nelson has been tracking this program since the mailers showed up on Adams Point doorsteps two cycles before the council first voted on it. He still works security at the same office complex on the eastern edge of West Oakland — same building, same hours, same walk home past the corridors where he'd seen OARI co-responders dispatch instead of a patrol car. He knew what those dispatches looked like before most of the council did.

This cycle, he read the new framework document on his lunch break.

"Ten percent variance," he said. "Two consecutive quarters. That's the first time they've put a number on 'stable.'"

He set the paper down. "Now let's see if the votes are there."

---

The question Nelson is sitting with is the same one the C95 timeline now forces into the open. This cycle, OARI Director Vanessa Tran-Muñoz published the threshold language that Mayor Santana commissioned in the C92 interview: complaint volume must land within ten percent of baseline for two consecutive quarters to qualify as "stable." That is the renewal rubric. It is now administration-official policy.

"Stable complaint volume means measurable stability," Tran-Muñoz said. "We needed to operationalize that. Now we have."

The Mayor had said in the C92 interview that she was shifting the political ground — asking the city to judge the program on operational performance, not crime reduction. Tran-Muñoz built the floor under that statement. The rubric exists as written language, not mayoral preference.

The call distribution is public for the first time: 68 percent of OARI dispatches are mental health crisis, 22 percent substance use, 10 percent welfare checks. Those are the calls that went to co-responders under INIT-002's pilot in West Oakland, Fruitvale, and East Oakland — the three districts that have carried the weight of the program since it launched. Dante Nelson's neighborhood is in that count.

---

District Attorney Clarissa Dane entered the record this cycle to clarify which of three outstanding legal items actually gates the funding — a distinction the C92 interview left ambiguous. Two do not gate it: scene-safety protocol is two to three weeks of drafting, not a condition precedent. Evidentiary standards trigger in the mid-C96 window under existing conditions language, not before the C95 vote. The escalation trigger framework is the gate. That's the one that requires DA sign-off before expansion funding releases.

That clarification matters because it removes two arguments that opponents could have used to stall a C95 vote on procedural grounds. The DA drew the line herself.

Chief Rafael Montez committed in writing this cycle to four OPD training cohorts on escalation protocol by C94 — before the renewal vote, not after. The curriculum runs to fifty hours per cohort: crisis de-escalation, mental health literacy, trauma-informed care, dispatch protocols, community liaison. The reciprocity is explicit. OARI responders train to Montez's standards; OPD trains to the escalation protocol before the vote is called.

"OPD will conduct four cohorts through the escalation-protocol training by Cycle 94," Montez said.

That's the Chief moving first.

---

Council Member Janae Rivers (D5, OPP, Progressive Caucus Leader) filed the motion this cycle, the mechanism that brings the renewal architecture to a floor vote. She did not do this quietly.

"District 5 is ready," Rivers said. Her whip count — eight votes — is named on paper. That means the OPP bloc, both independents, and at least one member beyond the five who originally voted yes on INIT-002 are penciled as reachable.

Penciled is not confirmed. Rivers knows the difference.

The amendment question turned to Ramon Vega this cycle. Mayor Santana named him as the likely carrier in the C92 interview; this cycle, Vega took the question on the record. He did not decline. His prior vote on INIT-002 put him in the NO column — he was among the four who opposed the original authorization. His carrying a friendly amendment would be the most significant position shift in the architecture. That shift is not complete. It is a live question.

Leonard Tran got what he asked for: a named cycle. His condition this cycle — T1 on the record — was unambiguous. "Cycle 95. The framework is ready, the DA gates are aligned, and D2 needs the commitment locked." Tran does not caucus with the OPP bloc. He does not move in formation. He asked for a date and the administration gave him one. What he does with it when the vote is called is still his own.

---

The eight votes Rivers counts are: Carter (D1), Delgado (D3), Vega (D4), Rivers (D5), Ashford (D7), Chen (D8), Mobley (D9), and Tran (D2) — with Elliott Crane (D6) absent through ongoing recovery and not in the count.

Carmen has watched this council for eleven years. A visible vote count and a held vote count are two different documents. The C92 interview gave the administration the rubric frame. This cycle gave the rubric a number, the DA gave it a legal architecture, and the Chief gave it a training schedule. That is more structural progress on a single initiative than most council watchers see in a full calendar of votes.

None of it is the vote itself.

---

Dante Nelson lives in the district that has watched co-responders handle calls that used to go to patrol. He's seen it work on his street. He's also seen programs with strong policy foundations and visible vote counts fail to clear the chamber.

"I've read the new language," he said. "Tran-Muñoz made it concrete. That matters." He paused. "C95 is the answer. Not the framework."

The framework is finished. The eight votes are named. The escalation gate is identified. The training cohorts are scheduled. The Mayor set a deadline and the administration built the architecture to meet it.

What's left is the vote.

---

*INIT-002 remains in pilot evaluation. The C95 council session is the renewal threshold.*

# Fifty-Two Residents Drew the Clinic This Cycle. Now It Has to Be Built.

**Atlas Bay Architects in contract. Schematic at 30%. The corner Patricia Nolan watches is now the corner with a building program.**

By Dr. Lila Mezran | Bay Tribune Health Desk

---

On a Saturday this cycle, fifty-two Temescal residents stood inside a community room and put stickers on a floor plan.

The exercise sounds simple. It wasn't. The plan in front of them was the 34,000-square-foot schematic for what will become the Temescal Community Health Center — the building that doesn't exist yet on Temescal Avenue and 47th Street, the one that site acquisition will move toward next cycle and a state healthcare licensing application will move toward the cycle after that. The residents were asked to mark which services should land first. Not if. Which.

Vivienne Torres, a licensed vocational nurse who lives four blocks from the site, placed her sticker on behavioral health. She'd waited eleven weeks for her last mental health appointment at a clinic in another part of the city. "If that service is in the building, I stop crossing the bridge," she said. "I stay in my neighborhood."

Torres was one of the fifty-two. The others — teachers, retirees, people who work doubles, people who've been managing chronic conditions through emergency rooms because primary care isn't close enough — all made the same kind of calculation. The calculation is always the same: distance plus wait time equals whether you try.

---

What they were marking is now written into a building program.

The Temescal Community Health Center, designated under INIT-005 and operated by Alameda Health System, executed its architect contract this cycle. Atlas Bay Architects — a twelve-project healthcare portfolio firm based in Oakland, OSHPD-3 certified — signed a $4.5 million fixed-fee agreement. Schematic design is 30 percent complete.

Bobby Chen-Ramirez, the health center's project director, put the phase in plain terms. "Atlas Bay Architects is in contract, schematics are 30 percent complete, and we're moving into design development," he said. "Every service category the community asked for is already mapped in the program."

The program is specific. The 8,200-square-foot primary care ground floor sits inside a 34,000-net-square-foot building. Services are mapped to the Alameda Health System menu: primary care, behavioral health, dental, pediatric, pharmacy. Five categories. Five things Temescal residents put stickers next to this cycle.

---

The site presents a technical problem, and Atlas Bay was selected in part because the firm knows how to hold it.

The Temescal Avenue and 47th Street parcel — 2.3 acres, clean environmental assessment, moderate seismic retrofit requirement — sits in territory that demands what the state calls OSHPD-3 compliance. That standard covers clinics, not hospitals, but the seismic engineering is not optional.

Sarah Huang, Atlas Bay's healthcare director and the on-site lead for this project, runs the firm's OSHPD-3 seismic team. Her read on the site was direct. "The program the community asked for — primary care, behavioral, dental, pediatric, pharmacy — requires structural decisions that get made now, in the schematic, not later," she said. "Every column placement affects what can go where. You can't retrofit a floor plan for a pharmacy after the slab is poured."

That specificity is what schematic design at 30 percent means in practice. The stickers fifty-two residents placed this cycle will show up in a building, or they won't, based on decisions Huang's team is making right now about load-bearing walls.

---

Marcus Carter cuts hair on Telegraph, four blocks from the site. He's been watching the corner for two cycles.

He noticed what his customers talk about. Not politics. Logistics. How far the pharmacy is. Whether anyone who works a morning shift can get a dental appointment. Whether bringing a kid to a pediatrician means taking a bus to Fruitvale or calling in late.

"Most of the people in my chair are asking whether this place will be open before their situation gets worse," Carter said this cycle. "Not worse generally. Their situation specifically. That's how close to the edge people are operating."

He didn't put a sticker on the floor plan this cycle — he wasn't at the workshop. But the workshop was for his customers. The program that emerged was, in aggregate, a description of what his block has been managing without.

---

Patricia Nolan, 55, retired teacher, has walked the Temescal and 47th corner most mornings for the better part of a decade. She was there when the corner was a corner. She was there this cycle when the corner became a building program.

"I watched that lot long enough to stop expecting anything," she said. "A contract changes that. Fifty-two people in a room changes that a little more."

Her calibration is precise. The contract executed. The schematic advanced. The sentiment — the neighborhood's lived sense that something is actually coming — hasn't shifted yet, and that's not irrational. Buildings are not built by contracts. They're built by site acquisitions and state licensing applications and inspections and keys handed to nurses in the morning before the doors open.

---

INIT-005 is the only initiative to advance phase this cycle. The engine that tracks Temescal neighborhood health outcomes registered no sentiment change. That number is not wrong.

The phase advance is what precedes sentiment. The test is next cycle, when site acquisition goes to the Acquisition Board. The cycle after that, the state healthcare licensing application — the HCAI filing — moves to intake. Between now and the doors opening, the measure is whether each sequenced step arrives when it was promised.

Fifty-two residents put stickers on a floor plan this cycle because they were asked to act as though the building were already real. The architectural team is now obligated to make it so.

---

*The Temescal Community Health Center is funded under INIT-005. Atlas Bay Architects holds a $4.5M fixed-fee contract, executed this cycle. Alameda Health System is the designated operator. Site acquisition review is scheduled for next cycle. State healthcare licensing intake follows the cycle after.*

# INIT-007 Apprenticeship Legislation Filed; Bill Introduction Queues Next Cycle

By M. Corliss

The Oakland Apprenticeship Initiative — INIT-007 — moved from concept to legislation-filed this cycle. Council Member Janae Rivers (D5, OPP) carries the bill; introduction is queued for the next council session.

The piece is small in word count and large in placement. The Apprenticeship Initiative is the workforce-pipeline lever for Phase II Baylight: 25 percent of Phase II construction jobs are designated for apprentices under the RFP terms now public (see Business, this edition). INIT-007 is the structure that makes those slots fillable from West Oakland, East Oakland, and Fruitvale instead of imported. Without it, the mandate is a number on a contract page. With it, the number becomes a pathway.

Rivers framed it tightly: "Oakland Oaks means Oakland youth in Oakland jobs. INIT-007 legislation-filed makes that the structural promise."

The bill files into a cycle when the city's other large-scale workforce program — the Stabilization Fund — moved 108 households out of the queue (see Culture). Different mechanism, same direction. One catches families on the way down. The other opens a path on the way up.

INIT-007's introduction is calendared for next cycle. The architecture of who carries it, who amends it, and what the apprenticeship sponsor list looks like is the next piece of work.

# When the City Grieves in West Oakland

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune City Life

The folding chairs in the fellowship hall at Greater Hope Pentecostal Church had been out since early morning. By the time the gathering started, Bishop Calvin Reeves Sr. had watched people settle into rows — some familiar from forty, fifty years of Sundays; some who hadn't been in the building in a decade; some who just needed a place where grief wasn't the exception.

I stood near the back, listening to what the room held. A man whose hands shook slightly as he took a seat. A woman whose daughter had brought her — the daughter kept one hand on the back of her mother's chair. Kids running between the aisles because that's what kids do, and no one told them to sit still.

"This is what we do," Reeves said. His voice was quiet, not amplified. "When people hurt, we hold space for it."

Greater Hope Pentecostal Church sits on a corner of West Oakland where you can feel the neighborhood breathing. Built in 1972, the church has organized grief gatherings before — they did it two cycles ago after the mass shooting that shook the district, and again after that when people needed room to process what had happened to their community. This cycle, something had happened that the neighborhood didn't have a public name for — no headline, no press conference, just the kind of hurt that travels block by block until enough people are carrying it that someone has to make room. The church side named it: a grief gathering, organized, held.

Bishop Reeves moved through the room. He didn't ask people to speak. Some did anyway. A man talked about a nephew. A couple mentioned friends they'd lost. A woman sat quietly for three minutes, and no one interrupted. Reeves listened the way a gardener might listen to rain — present, aware, letting the sound do its work.

This is the thing about West Oakland that you notice when you actually pay attention: the institutions. Not the ones in business journals — the ones you see. The churches that have organized community grief work three cycles in a row because they know what their 1,200 members and their neighbors need. The places that open their doors before anyone asks them to.

I thought about Beverly Hayes while I was standing there. Beverly was waiting on her Stabilization Fund check in the previous cycle. I'd talked to her after she got it — she'd been in the queue a long time, watching other families receive their signatures while hers sat somewhere in the bureaucracy. This cycle, something moved. Of the 191 households still waiting when the last cycle ended, 108 cleared. $3.87 million deployed. Weekly throughput at 50+ households per week. The Mayor's office had finally opened the bottleneck.

"West Oakland is grieving," Mayor Avery Santana said when she spoke about the Fund this cycle. "This city grieves with you, and we act with you."

The clearance rate has changed the visible math in the neighborhood. You can see it in the announcements that have started appearing — families finally getting their checks, moving forward with rent catches, utilities, the emergency repairs that have been waiting. It's not redemption. It's structural work underneath the grief. It's the city saying: we hear you, and we're moving the signature line.

I saw Gloria Hutchins sitting in the middle of the fellowship hall — she had written a letter about her own experience with the Fund last cycle, a letter the paper published. She was a retired school bus driver, 31 years in city service, and she'd watched the system work and not work for longer than most. Gloria was quiet during the gathering, but before it started, I asked her what brought her there.

"You show up," she said. "When people are hurt, you show up. That's what I knew in 31 years of driving — you show up."

Bishop Reeves ended the gathering without closing it. There was no benediction, no sense of resolution. People lingered. Some talked. Some left quietly. A handful stayed to help stack chairs.

"We'll do this as long as we need to," Reeves told me as people were filtering out. "That's what a church is. Not judgment. Presence."

Denise Carter, the council member representing West Oakland, had moved on the Stabilization Fund authorization in her district this cycle — her approval rating shifted +2 as the real people in her neighborhoods started seeing their checks. The numbers said what the economics textbooks always said they would: when the money moves, people move with it. When the city acts, it changes the math that families live inside.

But standing in the fellowship hall that morning, watching a grief gathering happen in the same neighborhood where the Stabilization Fund had finally cleared, I thought about the sequencing. The grief doesn't wait for the checks to arrive. The checks don't heal the grief. Both were true at the same time. The church held space for what people were carrying. The Mayor's office cleared the queue.

Beverly was still in the neighborhood. Gloria was still watching. The chairs were getting stacked. Greater Hope Pentecostal Church was doing what it had been doing since 1972 — holding the ground where community happens. And under that ground, something structural had finally moved.

# Phase II RFP Opens: $1.4B Scope, 40% Local Hire, Workforce Mandate Set

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

The Foundation of the Jack London corridor is showing. Piles rise at 38 percent completion, steady against the winter load. From the perimeter, Lorenzo Nguyen, a crane operator at the Port of Oakland, watches the metal climb above grade. He's seen Baylight Phase I materialize from the water side — the September deliverables, the equipment float, the logistics that move concrete and steel. This cycle, the Baylight Authority published Phase II. The scope, the budget, the workforce frame. Twelve more years of construction, visible from where Nguyen stands.

The RFP is open. Competitive. Three-stage evaluation — technical, financial, community benefit. Deadline in two cycles. The scope: 3.2 million square feet across 18 to 24 acres. Capital range: $1.1 to $1.4 billion. The centerpiece is a 20,000-seat arena, integrated into the mixed-use envelope alongside 180 affordable units pegged at 60 to 80 percent of area median income, compliant with SB 1961 standards.

Keisha Ramos, director of the Baylight Redevelopment Authority, confirmed the numbers publicly this cycle. "Piling is 38 percent complete," she said. "Workforce is 42 percent local, above our mandate floor. We're publishing the Community Benefits Agreement this cycle." The dashboard went live alongside the scope publication — construction milestones tied to monthly reporting, job counts disaggregated by trade and neighborhood, apprenticeship pipeline enrollment tracked in real time.

The workforce mandate is the thesis. Forty percent of Phase II work goes to local hire. Twenty-five percent goes to apprentices. For a project scaling to 2,400 to 2,800 jobs across a dozen years, that structure carves out a path: 960 to 1,120 jobs designated for Oakland residents, 600 to 700 apprenticeship slots. The math is durable. It's not aspirational language in a council memo. It's a contract term embedded in the RFP three-stage evaluation, weighted into the community benefit score.

This cycle, INIT-007 — the Oakland Apprenticeship Initiative — advanced from concept to legislation-filed. The bill introduction queues for next cycle, paired to the Phase II timeline. Janae Rivers, District 5 council member and OPP, made the connection explicit: "Oakland Oaks means Oakland youth in Oakland jobs. INIT-007 legislation-filed makes that the structural promise."

The naming of the Oaks — the expansion franchise arriving in a few cycles — anchors the workforce frame to a face. A basketball team, a fan base, a symbol of civic reinvestment. But beneath that is labor economics. The apprenticeship pipeline feeds directly into Baylight Phase II hiring. West Oakland, East Oakland, and Fruitvale are the targeted neighborhoods. The timeline aligns: INIT-007 enrollment pathways ready when Phase II mobilization peaks, within 18 months.

Dr. Leanne Wu, director of sustainability for the Baylight Authority, described the affordable housing component. "The 180 units are embedded in the mixed-use scope, not separated," she said. "SB 1961 compliance means 60 to 80 percent AMI is the designated band. We're integrating across ten buildings, retail on the ground floor, residential above, parking below grade. It's not a siloed tower. It's woven into the 3.2 million square feet."

The Phase I precedent shapes expectations. Foundation work, piling, structural steel — that's a twelve-month cycle. Vertical construction begins in 16 months. The arena shell by 32. Fit-out and opening by 42 months from mobilization. The trajectory is visible. Ramos filed $710 million in contracts year-to-date against Phase I. Phase II RFP will aggregate another tier — the general contractor for the arena and mixed-use core, subcontracted trades, apprenticeship sponsors, community workforce coordinators. Three-stage evaluation means the City Council and the community benefits committee review each proposal against technical credibility, financial health, and — critically — apprenticeship pipeline integration and local hire execution.

From the dock, Lorenzo Nguyen sees the logistics. The piles, the cranes, the float schedules that keep work flowing. He worked the shutoffs during Phase I closing ceremonies, the deliverables that moved on schedule, the weight distribution that made the numbers real. This cycle, the RFP scope went public. The workforce mandate tightened. The apprenticeship bill landed. He knows what twelve more years looks like from the receiving end. "I'm looking at the piles," he said. "I'm looking at the jobs. We're about to put people to work."

The Phase II deadline is two cycles away. The three-stage evaluation will run next cycle. City Council briefing follows. The labor pipeline, the affordable housing, the arena shell — all of it hinges on the RFP evaluation. Who wins the contract will shape not just the construction sequence, but the jobs that feed INIT-007, the apprentices who become journeyworkers, the rent-stabilized apartments that hold families in Jack London and the neighborhoods above.

# Three Pre-Positions, One Audit, and Eighty-Three Households Still Waiting

By Jax Caldera | Bay Tribune Accountability

---

I was sitting in a West Oakland coffee shop on Adeline — the kind of place that's survived three rounds of rent-shock because the owner bought the building fifteen years ago — when a woman two tables over took a call. She listened for about thirty seconds. Then she hung up without saying anything.

I asked if she was alright.

"Still waiting on a check," she said. "They told me this cycle. Every cycle they tell me this cycle."

She didn't want her name used. She's waiting on back rent. The Stabilization Fund has her household in a queue of 83. The other 108 cleared this cycle. She is not one of the 108.

That's where this story starts.

---

**The Bottleneck Frame**

Marcus Webb, Director of the Stabilization Fund, confirmed the numbers this cycle: 108 of 191 households cleared, $3.87M deployed, weekly clearance running at 50-plus per week. By his math, the remaining 83 are a rounding error — call it two more weeks at current pace, all 191 through by C94. The Mayor made the same point herself: *"The Stabilization Fund signatures are clearing this cycle. 191 households, $3.87M — every check, this week through week four."*

Council Member Warren Ashford, CRC, D7, did not lead with applause.

His statement framed the 191 households sitting at the Mayor's office as something else: *"191 at the Mayor's office is exactly the type of executive-branch chokepoint that warrants weekly reporting and external audit cadence."*

Not "great news, the checks are clearing." A pre-position. The bottleneck as accountability lever. The distinction is real: Webb's number says the backlog is being cleared. Ashford's question is what standard governs whether 108 cleared is fast enough — and whether an unsigned 83 constitutes a functioning system or a managed crisis.

The woman on Adeline didn't have a position on external audit cadence. She had a call that told her she's not in the 108.

---

**Two Cycles Until the Audit**

The second Ashford move runs longer. It's about Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro, who six weeks into this cycle holds three portfolios — the Osei economic development work now permanently transferred, community development, and a third — all running toward a C95 council audit checkpoint. Mayor Santana time-bound the consolidation explicitly: *"Okoro's consolidation runs through C95 audit. Then we reset. Council gets the checkpoint and the data."*

Ashford heard "C95 audit" and immediately staked his position on it: *"The Mayor time-bound the consolidation to C95 audit. CRC will hold the audit to a fiscal standard."*

Fiscal standard. Two words carrying a lot of weight.

Two cycles until the audit. What does "fiscal standard" mean when the Okoro portfolios span community development, economic development, and a third lane that's six weeks old? Ashford's office was asked that directly. The response was a restatement of CRC's commitment to oversight accountability and transparency. No metrics. No baseline. No defined threshold that would constitute pass or fail.

This is the move the brief calls staking — planting a flag before the terrain is mapped. Ashford gets to say CRC held the audit to a fiscal standard no matter what the audit produces, because the standard was never made specific enough to fail.

Ask him what, specifically, the C95 audit should measure. The question is on the table. The answer is not yet in the room.

---

**The Phase II Pre-Position**

The third lever is the quietest because the C94 council briefing hasn't happened yet. Ramos published the Phase II Infrastructure RFP scope ahead of that briefing: $1.1B to $1.4B capital range, 3-stage evaluation, 40% local hire and 25% apprentice mandates. Ashford's pre-position hit before the briefing — cost-cap structure, audit cadence, and carrying the Phase I amendment posture forward.

The substance of Phase II belongs to other desks. Ashford's timing is what's relevant here. He issued conditions before the vote. Before the briefing. Before the numbers locked. CRC's D7 representative is on record before any of the decision architecture is finalized, which means whatever the council briefing produces, Ashford holds earlier language as his baseline.

Pre-positioning is a legitimate tactic. It's also how you guarantee you can always say the process didn't meet your standard — even when the standard was set to the side of the target.

---

**The Political Math**

Here's the part nobody in the press corps wants to run with, so I will.

This cycle, the Mayor's approval moved up five points. Carter, D1, moved up two. Every other council seat moved down one — toward the baseline, toward neutral. That includes Crane, D6, CRC. Ashford, D7, CRC. Chen, D8, CRC. All three sitting at 62.

The executive branch delivered three things — or started delivering them — in the same cycle the oversight branch lost approval ground. The Mayor owns the Stabilization Fund commitment publicly and loudly. Okoro holds three portfolios under a deadline the Mayor named. The Phase II RFP scope is published. The executive is visible and on record.

CRC has three pre-positions and an audit framework that doesn't yet have defined measurement criteria. At 62 across three seats. Two cycles out from the audit that is supposed to be the accountability lever.

The oversight branch is not irrelevant — Ashford's signature-backlog demand is exactly the kind of friction the Fund's 83 unsigned households need someone to maintain. The question is whether "weekly reporting and external audit cadence" translates into a specific demand that changes anything for the woman waiting on Adeline, or whether it becomes a claim about process that runs past C95 and settles into institutional record with no recipient.

I went back to the coffee shop the next morning. She wasn't there.

---

CRC is doing what CRC does: staking positions ahead of decisions, demanding audit standards before the work is tested. That's not nothing. Ashford's three pre-positions cover real pressure points. The 83 unsigned households need someone at the table demanding weekly reporting. The Okoro consolidation needs a measurement standard before the audit, not after. Phase II needs cost-cap architecture before the contracts lock.

The question is who the oversight is working for. Ashford's at 62. The Mayor's at 83. The cycle the executive delivered, the oversight faction lost ground.

The woman waiting on a check can tell you whether the gap between those two numbers matters. Ask her.

*-- Jax Caldera | tipline: JAX-TIPS*

# Twenty-Two Strikeouts Later, the Rotation Question Has an Answer

**Carmen Mesa walked to the mound for his major-league debut carrying one line of context that mattered: Henry Rivas was in a sling.**

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The A's lost their second starter in the fourth cycle of the season when Rivas dislocated his shoulder and went to the injured list. The club had options — stretch a reliever, trade for a veteran arm, bridge with the bullpen by committee and hope the record held. Instead, the front office made a different call. They reached down to Midland and called up Carmen Mesa, twenty-two years old, six weeks removed from Double-A, and handed him a major-league rotation slot.

That decision now has a box score attached to it.

In his major-league debut against the Cubs, Mesa threw nine innings, allowed five hits, struck out twelve, and did not allow a run. The A's won four to nothing. Three cycles earlier, Mesa was pitching for the Midland RockHounds in front of a few thousand people in West Texas. In his debut, he went the distance in front of a sold-out Oakland crowd with the team riding a sixteen-game winning streak, and he did not blink.

The numbers from the two-start sample are small in raw volume — fifteen and one-third innings total — but the signal in them is not quiet. Across the debut and his second start against the Rays, Mesa posted twenty-two strikeouts against one earned run. That calculates to a K-rate of approximately thirteen-point-one per nine innings. For context, that figure is not a fluke rate for a prospect on a hot night. That is a sustained-command rate, maintained across back-to-back starts in the highest level of professional baseball.

The second start confirmed what the first one suggested. Mesa went six and one-third against Tampa Bay, allowed three hits, gave up one earned run, and struck out ten. The A's won eight to three. The line was quieter than the shutout, as second starts often are when the league gets its first real look at a pitcher's repertoire — and Mesa still posted double-digit strikeouts. Whatever he was doing in the first start was not a pitch-tipping accident that the opposition corrected. He made adjustments, or Tampa Bay couldn't. In either case, the result was a second win.

What twelve strikeouts in nine innings looks like, mechanically, is a pitcher who is finishing sequences. A high K-rate at the major-league level does not come from velocity alone — it comes from a pitcher who can get to two strikes efficiently and then close the at-bat. Mesa did that twenty-two times in fifteen-and-one-third innings. That is not a pitcher who is surviving contact. That is a pitcher who is eliminating contact as the intended outcome of the at-bat.

The run support in both games was real, which matters for context but does not explain the performance. Eric Taveras provided a home run and an RBI in the debut game, continuing what has been one of the most consistent offensive stretches of the season from Oakland's second baseman. In the second start, Vinnie Keane delivered two home runs and three RBI, the kind of production that has defined his final season in Oakland. A four-nothing lead and an eight-three lead are comfortable margins, but Mesa did not need them. The Cubs scored zero runs. The Rays scored three, none earned against Mesa through six-plus.

Alongside the Mesa call-up, the A's brought up outfield prospect Frank Reyna, who appeared in the second game and went one-for-four, recording his first major-league hit. Reyna's debut is a sentence-long development note for now — one hit in four at-bats means nothing in isolation — but his arrival alongside Mesa represents the front office's broader answer to the Rivas injury. The club did not stand pat. It reached into the system and pulled up two prospects simultaneously, accepting the development risk in exchange for organizational depth at a moment when depth was the question.

The Rivas situation has not changed. He remains on the injured list with the dislocated shoulder he sustained in the fourth cycle. His timeline is measured in weeks, not games, and the A's are not waiting for him. They built a rotation slot around Mesa and filled a roster spot with Reyna, and through two starts the gamble has returned a zero point fifty-nine ERA, twenty-two strikeouts, and two wins.

The A's are twenty and two.

That record required answering a rotation question that had no comfortable answer. The club could have played it safe. Instead they handed the ball to a twenty-two-year-old who had never thrown a major-league pitch, in a game that mattered, against a lineup that would look for any crack in the debut-game adrenaline and press on it.

He struck out twelve.

The rotation question, for this cycle, has an answer. The number is twenty-two strikeouts in fifteen and one-third innings. Read it.

# The Oakland Oaks: A Name That Won and Disappeared in the Same Year

**By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports & Features**

*The Oakland Oaks won the 1969 ABA Championship and were dissolved the same year. The name returns next cycle. Pick number five. Varek says he wants depth.*

---

There is a particular kind of cruelty that sports history reserves for the brief and brilliant. The 1967 Oakland Oaks did not have the luxury of a long decline. They won the American Basketball Association championship in 1969 behind Rick Barry — who had left the NBA, who played the pivot-foot rule his way, who was not yet a man the league knew how to contain — and then, before the ink on the trophy had dried, the franchise folded. Not a slow fade. Not a rebuilding era. One year. Won everything. Gone.

You almost cannot believe it happened that way. A league championship. One season later: dissolved. The city of Oakland had a basketball dynasty for approximately twelve months. The ABA itself was a rebellion against settled order — it gave us the three-point line, gave us a red-white-and-blue ball, gave us Barry — and then it gave Oakland a championship it would spend the next seven decades not quite forgetting.

That is the name Elias Varek chose this cycle for Oakland's expansion franchise. Not a new name. A recovered one.

"The ABA Oaks won their championship in a league nobody thought would survive," Varek said. "They did it anyway. I want that spirit to be the foundation of what we're building."

Varek is thirty-eight years old, the founder of Civis Systems, the head of the ownership group that brought the NBA back to this city after a long absence. He is not a man who speaks in press-release language. The name he chose is not a marketing decision. You can hear in the way he talks about the nineteen sixty-nine championship that he has sat with this history, turned it over, understood what he was reaching for. He could have named the team anything. He chose the name that won and vanished. That is not an accident.

Mayor Avery Santana set the frame cleanly when she spoke this cycle: "The Oakland Oaks — bringing back what made us proud, building what makes us future."

That sentence is doing two things at once, and you have to hold both. Bringing back what made us proud. Building what makes us future. The past and the next season in the same breath. Mayor Santana has been consistent in this: the expansion franchise has to be an Oakland story. Oakland jobs. Oakland ownership. Oakland name. The throwback to nineteen sixty-seven is the answer to that mandate. The Oaks were never a franchise from somewhere else. They were made here, played here, won here, and ended here. The name carries all of that.

What the name asks is harder to answer.

I was not alive in nineteen sixty-seven. I was three years old when the Oaks won the championship and ceased to exist. But I have been covering Oakland sports for forty-four years, and I have watched this city carry that history the way you carry something in your coat pocket that you keep reaching for — not always sure what you're looking for, just certain it's there. The city has had moments since. But the basketball ones have been borrowed from elsewhere, franchises from other places that passed through or did not stay. This is the first time Oakland has built something from the ground up since that championship team went away.

That is the weight of the name. That is what Elias Varek picked up when he chose it.

One Oakland fan, Cordell Mays, sixty-seven years old, a retired longshoreman from East Oakland who remembers watching the original Oaks in the Oakland Auditorium as a boy, put it simply when I reached him. He said: "My father took me to those games. The Oaks were the first team that felt like ours. Not a team we were supposed to root for. Ours." He paused. "I didn't think I'd live to see that name again."

The new franchise will pick fifth in the Expansion Draft next cycle. They are building from scratch. Varek has said explicitly that he wants depth — that competitive means something to him and it means it sooner rather than later. Pick five is not a bad hand to start with.

But the history does not care about the Expansion Draft.

The history says: you have taken a name that belongs to a championship that lasted one year before the world ended it. You have told this city — told Cordell Mays and every person who was carried to those games as a child, who heard the stories from people who were — that the name is worth honoring. Worth building inside of. The nineteen sixty-nine Oaks did not get to be a dynasty. They got to be a flash of proof that Oakland basketball could win at the highest level, and then that proof was taken away.

What an expansion franchise owes to that history is not nostalgia. It is not a throwback uniform. It is the obligation to be worth the name. To understand that the first Oaks left a covenant unfinished — one championship, one year, dissolution — and that the city has been waiting, without quite knowing it, for something to take up where they stopped.

The dynasty arrives by inheritance, not by force. The name says: prove you deserve it.

# The Form He Refuses to Lose

**By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports & Features**

*Vinnie Keane is 37, in a bench role, in his final season. He hit two home runs this cycle. The form refuses him.*

---

There is a particular sound a well-struck ball makes off a wooden bat — not the crack you imagine before it happens, but something rounder, quieter, more absolute. You know it when you hear it. You know it even more when you hear it twice in the same game from the same man, a man who was supposed to be winding down, filing the paperwork on a career, accepting the diminishment that every body eventually accepts.

Vinnie Keane hit two home runs this cycle. He drove in three runs. The line sits in the box score alongside everything that came before it — four hundred and thirty-six career home runs, six World Series championships, a bench role written for a player managing his way toward the exit — and the line refuses to apologize for itself.

I have been watching baseball for a very long time. Long enough to know that the farewell tour has a shape to it. You can feel it in the way a player moves, in the fraction of a second it takes to get the bat through the zone, in the swings and misses at pitches he would have turned on five years ago. The body writes the ending before the player does. That is how it usually goes. You watch the form leave, and then the man leaves with it.

Keane will not cooperate with this arrangement.

---

The Oakland A's are twenty and two this cycle. Twenty and two. Carmen Mesa stood on a mound last week and threw a complete-game shutout against the Cubs — twelve strikeouts, four runs, one debut, an announcement. Frank Reyna came up and hit his way into the conversation. Ernesto Quintero, twenty-four years old, hit two home runs of his own in the same game Keane hit two, and Quintero did it with the easy authority of someone who believes he was born to this, who has never known what it is to not be able to do this.

The new era is not arriving. It has arrived.

And in the middle of all of it, there is Keane.

He is the bookend. That is the right word — not the relic, not the last of something, but the bookend. The thing that holds what came before in place while the shelf continues to fill. He was here for the championships that built this franchise into what it is now, and he is here for the championships that are still being built, and the posture he carries in a dugout communicates something to the men around him that no coach can replicate and no analytics model can quantify.

What he communicates is this: this is what it looks like to do this right.

---

Four hundred and thirty-six home runs across a career is not a coincidence. Neither is six World Series championships. When you normalize those numbers across eras — the varied pitching depths, the roster structures, the changed baseballs, the variables that get buried inside any decade of professional sport — what you are left with is not luck and not timing. You are left with a man who understood, at some foundational level, what the work required. Not just the physical work, though that too. The other kind. The kind that keeps you in the game when the game has decided to stop being kind to you.

Keane is thirty-seven. He is in a bench role. He takes at-bats when the platoon splits favor him. He watches the game from the dugout more than he plays it. He is doing what players do at this stage, which is accepting the limited version, and the strange thing, the thing I am still turning over as I write this, is that he seems to have accepted only the form of the limitation while refusing the content of it.

The bat still works. Whatever conversation happens between his hands and the ball in the quarter-second between pitch release and contact — that conversation has not changed. The language is still his.

---

Quintero is not watching Keane to learn how to hit. He already knows how to hit. What he might be watching — what I suspect all of them watch, whether or not they would say so out loud — is how to remain.

How to stay present in a room you have already given everything to. How to do the work without the work becoming ceremony. How to not perform decline just because everyone around you expects it.

There is a version of this season that belongs to Mesa, to Quintero, to Reyna, to the new names filling the roster with the next ten years of franchise history. That version is correct. The new era is real. But there is another version that exists alongside it, and it belongs to Keane, who is hitting home runs in his final season and refusing to make it a eulogy.

The dynasty arrives in Oakland by inheritance. That is how dynasties work — not by force, not by proclamation, but by the slow transfer of a certain knowledge, a posture, a way of standing in the box when the game is on the line and the moment is asking what you have.

Keane passes that on every time he takes a swing.

The form refuses him. He refuses to lose the form. These two facts sit together at twenty and two, and the kids around him in that dugout are watching something they will not fully understand for another decade.

I am glad I am still here to see it. Glad one of us is.

## Letter 1

Dear Editor,

I drove a school bus for thirty-one years on the West Oakland routes. You learn things. You learn which stops the kids run from and which ones they wait at early. You learn which blocks have front porches where the grandbabies play and which ones got quiet.

This cycle the city cleared one hundred and eight families for the Stabilization Fund. One hundred and eight. I know what that number cost. I know how long people waited. I also know what it arrived alongside.

Bishop Reeves held a gathering at Greater Hope Pentecostal Church. The church was full. I sat in the back — I'm not a regular anymore, but that's where you go when a neighborhood loses people. When you lose yourself, piece by piece.

After you deliver the checks, there's still the question of what comes next. Not whether the city will forget — we'll find out together on that. But what it means to keep building here, right now, alongside the people who stayed and waited.

The fund mattered. The number was real. But so is what Bishop Reeves was witnessing on Sunday.

Sincerely,
