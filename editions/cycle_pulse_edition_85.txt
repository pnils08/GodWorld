THE FILING CABINET THAT ISN'T MOVING

City Planning Director sends four formal requests to three offices. None have been answered.

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

The four documents arrived in the same week. They came from the same office. They went to different desks — the Mayor's Office, the Office of Economic and Workforce Development, the Baylight Redevelopment Authority — and they carried the signature of Mara Vance, Oakland's City Planning Director.

Each one was a request. Each one was, in a different way, the same question: when does the thing that passed become the thing that exists?

As of this week, none of the three offices had formally answered.

THE MONEY THAT HASN'T MOVED

The West Oakland Stabilization Fund was authorized at $28 million in late 2040. Seven months later, the Office of Economic and Workforce Development approved $4.2 million for 47 specific recipients. That approval is not disbursement. No check has been cut. No funds have been transferred. Two hundred and ninety-five applications remain in the queue.

Beverly Hayes was among those who submitted an application during the fund's public intake window. She filed the paperwork. She was told to expect a determination within three weeks. That three-week window has now expired without word.

"I am not asking for anything I was not already told I qualified for," Hayes said. "I submitted what they asked. I waited what they said to wait. I am still waiting."

Vance's formal status request, sent to OEWD this month, is not a press inquiry. It is an official document from one city office to another, asking for an accounting. The response from OEWD, as of publication, is silence.

Mayor Avery Santana responded to the Stabilization Fund situation Thursday. "Approving money is not the same as delivering it," Santana said in a written statement. "295 families are still waiting. I am directing OEWD to deliver a full status report within ten business days."

That directive is ten business days old in its authorization. It has not yet produced a report.

Council member Janae Rivers, speaking for the OPP caucus, framed the delay in terms that left little room for interpretation. "295 families filed applications," Rivers said. "Zero have received a dollar. That is not a process delay — that is a failure of will."

Warren Ashford, the CRC's floor spokesperson, drew a different kind of parallel. "The Mayor held a press conference to announce he is directing a report," Ashford said. "That is not accountability. That is another announcement about an announcement."

THE CLOCK WITHOUT RESPONDERS

The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative passed the council in June on a 5-4 vote — the narrowest margin of any major initiative in recent memory. The program allocated $12.5 million to build behavioral health response capacity in three pilot districts: D1, D3, and D5.

Day 30 of the 45-day implementation window passed this week.

Vance's second formal request asked the Mayor's Office for status on the implementation. The answer, distilled from the Mayor's written statement: hiring criteria will be finalized by Friday. No behavioral health teams are operational. No dispatch integration is live — though OPD Chief Rafael Montez confirmed in a separate statement that integration protocols are being designed, citing a 6.2-minute response time target for pilot districts.

"The 45-day clock is running," Santana's office said.

Fifteen days remain.

FIVE MONTHS OF SILENCE

The Temescal Community Health Center vote happened in the spring. The council approved the $45 million facility 6-2, with both independent members — Vega and Tran — voting yes and Elliott Crane absent. It was the broadest coalition of the year.

Vance recommended, the same month, that the Mayor's Office issue a priority designation. The designation would compress the permitting timeline, moving the project from the standard queue into an expedited track.

That recommendation sat unanswered for five months.

This week, Vance resubmitted it. The Mayor's Office responded Thursday by issuing the designation.

"That designation is official, and it is overdue," Santana said.

Rivers called the timeline an indictment of process. "A priority designation that took five months to issue is not expedited governance," she said. "It is governance by embarrassment."

THE SEPTEMBER 15 DEADLINE

The fourth document went to Keisha Ramos, director of the Baylight Redevelopment Authority. Vance requested five specific deliverables by September 15: a signed construction mobilization timeline, confirmed anchor tenant disclosure, finalized TIF language, executed remediation bonding instrument, and a workforce development agreement with binding local hiring targets.

The $2.1 billion Baylight development passed the council in September on a 6-3 vote, the largest dollar figure ever authorized by the Oakland City Council. Eight days later, two of the required financing instruments remained unsigned and construction could not mobilize.

Ramos confirmed this week that all five deliverables are in production. "Every deliverable Director Vance requested is on schedule for September 15," she said.

The CRC bloc, which voted no on Baylight, has watched the post-vote administrative gap with something close to vindication. Elliott Crane, recovering and issuing statements in writing, put it directly: "When a $2.1 billion project requires a formal document request before its mobilization timeline exists, the oversight was never built in — it was extracted."

WHAT THE FOUR DOCUMENTS SAY

Carmen Delaine has covered Oakland City Council for eleven years. She has watched votes pass and timelines slip. What's different about Mara Vance's four requests is not the subject matter — delayed disbursements and missing instruments are not new in Oakland. What's different is the form.

A formal written request from one city office to another is not a news conference. It is not a social media post. It is a record. It creates a paper trail. It requires a written response. It will appear in the city's public records archive regardless of what any of the receiving offices choose to say publicly.

Vance did not hold a press conference this week. She filed documents. Four of them, in one month, to three different desks.

That is what a city official does when they have run out of faith in verbal assurances.

EDITOR'S DESK
############################################################

September comes to Oakland and brings its old question: what did we build, and is it holding?

The city voted for things this year. It voted to stabilize West Oakland with $28 million. It voted to answer behavioral health calls with something other than a badge and a gun. It voted to build a $2.1 billion waterfront district and a $45 million health center. The votes passed. The money was allocated. The timelines were set.

And then the machinery between "the council voted" and "the thing exists" went quiet.

Mara Vance filed four documents this month. One to OEWD, one to the Mayor, one to the Baylight Authority, one a resubmission of a request that sat unanswered for five months. She didn't hold a press conference. She created a paper trail. That's the story Carmen Delaine tells on our front page, and it's the thread that runs through nearly every desk in this edition — from the Stabilization Fund's zero disbursement to the OARI clock ticking past Day 30 to the Baylight instruments that still aren't signed.

The faith communities kept working. The A's went 7-0. The kids went back to school. Oakland's rhythm doesn't wait for the city to catch up. It never has.

— Mags Corliss, Editor-in-Chief

DAY 30, FIFTEEN TO GO

The two swing votes on OARI are both asking where the program is. One voted yes. One voted no. They're watching the same empty districts.

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative took 5-4 to pass. That margin matters because both independent members — Leonard Tran of D2 and Ramon Vega of D4 — were on opposite sides of it. Tran gave the fifth vote. Vega was the fourth no.

They are now, separately, asking the same question.

Where is the program?

THIRTY DAYS IN

The 45-day implementation clock began running in June, when Mayor Avery Santana signed the ordinance. Three pilot districts were named in the authorizing legislation: D1, D3, and D5. Neither Tran's district nor Vega's made the list.

Behavioral health teams were to be operational within the 45-day window. Hiring criteria were to be released. Dispatch integration with OPD was to begin.

Thirty days into that window, OPD Chief Rafael Montez confirmed integration protocols are being developed. A 6.2-minute response time target has been set for pilot districts. That target is a design spec. It is not a performance number, because the teams do not yet exist.

OPP caucus spokesperson Janae Rivers, whose D5 is one of the three pilot districts, wants acceleration. "Our communities asked for this program," Rivers said. "Now we need responders on the street, not criteria on a desk."

Fifteen days remain.

THE FIFTH VOTE SPEAKS UP

Leonard Tran voted yes on OARI. He has not said much about it since. That changed this week.

In a statement to the Tribune, Tran said D2's situation has become difficult to defend to his constituents. Chinatown's average emergency response time is running at 12.1 minutes. The crime index for D2 is 2 — one of the higher readings in the city.

Tran cast the vote that put OARI over the threshold. His district received nothing from the legislation.

"I gave OARI the fifth vote," Tran said. "My constituents in Chinatown are still waiting."

He is now formally requesting what he called an expansion pathway — a mechanism for D2 to be added to the program if pilot results warrant it. This is the first public move by a swing vote to extend OARI beyond its original three districts.

CRC member Nina Chen was skeptical of the program's design from the beginning. Her position has not softened. "The council voted 5-4 to fund a program that had no hiring criteria," Chen said. "That was the terms of the vote. Those are the terms of the implementation."

THE NO VOTE TAKES ANOTHER LOOK

Ramon Vega voted no on OARI. He has said very little about it in the weeks since.

That ended this week.

In a statement — the first he has made about OARI since the vote — Vega said he stands by his reasoning. D4 was excluded from the pilot districts, and Vega's concerns about accountability and operational readiness remain. But he also said something that surprised observers who have tracked his position.

"I voted no and I stand by the reasoning," Vega said. "But a program running in three districts with $12.5 million behind it deserves honest scrutiny — from everyone, including me."

He went further. If response-time numbers in the pilot districts hold through the implementation window, and if use-of-force reductions are real, Vega said "that changes the conversation for D4."

That is not a yes on expansion. It is not a reversal. But it is the first signal from the council's most consistent no vote that the data could move him.

In Chinatown, Mateo Walker, 25, an electrician and trade union representative who works at the Port of Oakland, framed it differently. He has watched the D2 exclusion with mounting frustration.

"Tran voted for this thing and Chinatown got nothing," Walker said, standing outside the Port administration building on Middle Harbor Road. "I understand pilot districts. I understand phases. What I don't understand is why nobody thought to tell us how D2 gets in line."

The question is now formally on the table. Tran put it there.

SEPTEMBER 15

Five deliverables. One deadline. The Baylight District's path from vote to ground breaks down to a list.

By Trevor Shimizu | Bay Tribune Infrastructure

The list arrived on Keisha Ramos's desk this month. It came from Mara Vance, the City Planning Director, and it contained five items, each with a September 15, 2041 deadline.

Item one: a signed construction mobilization timeline.
Item two: confirmed anchor tenant disclosure.
Item three: finalized tax increment financing language.
Item four: executed soil remediation bonding instrument.
Item five: a workforce development agreement with binding local hiring targets.

The $2.1 billion Baylight District development passed the council in September on a 6-3 vote. The list exists because, on the day of the vote, items three and four were incomplete. The TIF language was still being negotiated. The remediation bond had not been executed. Those two unfinished instruments meant construction could not begin, because the legal framework that governs where the money goes — and who is liable for what is in the ground — was not yet in place.

A vote, it turns out, does not move dirt.

WHAT RAMOS SAID

Keisha Ramos confirmed this week that all five deliverables are in production. "Every deliverable Director Vance requested is on schedule for September 15," she said.

The workforce development agreement, if completed as described, would be one of the more specific binding commitments attached to a development of this scale in Oakland's recent history. The target numbers: 35 percent of journey-level construction workers must be Oakland residents. Twenty-five percent of apprenticeships go to workers from adjacent neighborhoods. Forty percent of ancillary labor goes to Oakland-certified small businesses. A $12 million Oakland Construction Workforce Trust would be established and funded separately.

"This is not aspirational language," Ramos said. "These are binding targets."

Brian Williams, 60, a mechanic who works for the Baylight Construction Authority in Chinatown, has been following the timeline closely. He works on site equipment — generators, lifts, vehicles that need to be ready before ground is broken.

"Right now I'm maintaining machines that aren't being used," Williams said Monday, outside the Baylight Construction Authority office off Webster Street. "That's fine. That's preparation. What I need is a date. Give me a date and I can tell you exactly what will be ready."

Williams said the workforce commitment, if it holds, matters to him personally. "I know people in West Oakland who can do this work. Good tradespeople. I want to see those targets enforced, not just printed."

THE INSTRUMENTS

The two incomplete instruments — TIF language and remediation bond — are not procedural loose ends. They are the legal architecture that makes the development survivable for the public.

The tax increment financing mechanism captures property tax growth above the base-year assessed value and routes it exclusively into the Baylight Infrastructure Fund. Without finalized language, no revenue can be collected or directed.

The remediation bond holds the private development consortium responsible for soil excavation, hazardous material abatement, groundwater monitoring, and shoreline stabilization — work identified in the project's environmental review. The bond amount equals the independently verified remediation cost estimate plus a 15 percent contingency reserve. Without an executed instrument, the city retains exposure it voted to eliminate.

Elliott Crane, the CRC's D6 member who is recovering and communicating in writing, addressed the gap directly. "When a $2.1 billion project requires a formal document request before its mobilization timeline exists, the oversight was never built in — it was extracted," Crane wrote.

Warren Ashford, the CRC's floor spokesperson, has been making a version of this argument since the vote. The question he's been asking is whether the systems are in place before the concrete is poured, not after.

THE BROADER PATTERN

Jose Wright, a 26-year-old electrician in Temescal, noticed a sinkhole on Telegraph Avenue last month — four feet wide, eighteen inches deep, appearing between his morning commute and noon. The city's crews filled it with cold-patch asphalt and marked it for permanent repaving.

Wright mentioned it again when contacted this week, in the context of Baylight's infrastructure timeline. "There's a gap between approving something and the thing actually being solid," he said. "I think about that hole on Telegraph. They filled it, but I don't know if they fixed what made it open up."

The observation is not Baylight-specific. It is a question about what "done" means when the system underneath hasn't been fully mapped.

An internet service disruption hit Uptown last week, affecting several blocks near the 19th Street BART corridor for approximately four hours before restoration. The incident was logged as isolated. Oakland's utility infrastructure in older corridors lacks comprehensive redundancy mapping — similar to the drainage gaps under Telegraph that let a sinkhole develop without warning.

September 15 is two weeks away. Ramos says the list will be complete.

The question Trevor Shimizu always asks about a deadline: who is checking the work after the deadline passes?

BUSINESS TICKER

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

The West Oakland Stabilization Fund's disbursement clock has now run through at least three months without cutting a single check. The $28 million was authorized in late 2040. The council's fiscal wing approved $4.2 million for first-phase disbursements last spring. As of this week, the total amount delivered to any applicant — any small business owner, any longtime resident, any landlord holding a two-unit building on 7th Street — remains zero. Staff at the Oakland Housing Authority describe the backlog as a familiar pattern — calls from Fruitvale and West Oakland residents who submitted paperwork months ago and have heard nothing back. The fund's architecture — seven distinct programs, five funding sources, a joint city-county approval structure — was designed to be comprehensive. It is also, so far, comprehensive in its stillness.

The Baylight Construction Authority has 37 employees on payroll and, according to the authority's director Keisha Ramos, five deliverables on schedule for a September 15 completion. Ramos is promising 35 percent journey-level Oakland residents at the job site, 25 percent apprenticeship slots from adjacent neighborhoods, and a $12 million Oakland Construction Workforce Trust funded through tax increment financing. Those numbers matter — binding local hiring targets at a $2.1 billion project represent real leverage if they hold. But the remediation bond that must precede any ground mobilization on the waterfront parcel has been filed and not executed. The Baylight authority is staffed, organized, and waiting on soil clearance that hasn't come. The question Councilmember Crane raised this week is the right one: when a project of this scale requires a formal document request before its mobilization timeline exists, the oversight was never built in.

What is moving, without question, is the evening corridor along Jack London's waterfront. September is typically a shoulder month — locals filling the gaps between the tourist summer and the holiday surge. Not this year. The Harborline Grill, Blue Lantern Bar, and Dockhouse BBQ have been running at the busiest clip of the calendar year, with weekend kitchen volume that line workers there say rivals late July. Regulars along the corridor say the foot traffic feels different this fall — more consistent, fewer dead Tuesdays. Whether it's the Baylight news or just the weather, the neighborhood isn't slowing down. The nightlife surge is real economic activity. The translation problem is that it flows to Jack London, where median income already sits above $113,000. The corridors that need relief — Fruitvale's commercial strip, West Oakland's 7th Street — remain quieter, and the fund meant to stabilize them keeps not arriving.

THE RENT MAP NOBODY POSTS

Oakland's headline income figure masks a geography of burden — and the one fund designed to close the gap hasn't disbursed a dollar.

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

Oakland's median household income crossed $85,000 this year. That number circulates in press releases and development pitch decks as evidence of a city stabilizing, a city on the right trajectory. But a median is geography dressed up as progress. Forty-one percent of Oakland households — 216 out of 529 tracked by the city's housing authority — are rent-burdened. That means more than 30 percent of their monthly income going to rent. The 41 percent figure is citywide. The distribution is not.

The neighborhoods with the lowest incomes are also paying rent that, proportionally, takes the biggest bite. Fruitvale's median household income sits at $73,122. The median rent there is $2,494 a month — roughly $29,900 a year. That is 41 percent of a median Fruitvale household's gross income before taxes, before groceries, before a car payment. West Oakland tracks almost identically: $81,072 median income against $2,661 median rent. In Jack London, where the nightlife is surging and the construction authority is staffing up, median income is $113,000 and median rent is $2,996 — a high rent paid from a higher income. The burden concentrates where it always concentrates.

I went down to the West Side Cafe on Adeline this week, which has been a Saturday morning institution since before the building it occupies changed hands twice. The owner — who asked not to be named — said she has been in lease renegotiation for four months. Her landlord knows the Baylight bond passed. "He's betting on the neighborhood changing," she said. "I'm betting on my customers still being here." She has not applied for the Stabilization Fund's commercial rent relief program, which offers up to $36,000 for businesses like hers. She hasn't applied because the application window closed in August, the review process is still running, and the fund has not disbursed anything. She can't wait on a reimbursement that hasn't arrived to negotiate a lease that's due by November.

Owen Campbell, 40, is a bakery worker and muralist who lives and works in Jack London. He's self-employed on the mural side and has watched the September uptick in foot traffic with the same mix of optimism and wariness that tends to follow investment announcements in this city. "More people, higher rents," he said. "That's the pattern. I don't know why it would be different this time." It might not be. The Baylight project's binding local hiring targets — 35 percent journey-level Oakland residents, 40 percent ancillary work going to Oakland-certified small businesses — represent the strongest anti-displacement language built into a development agreement in years. Whether those targets outlast the construction phase, and whether the neighborhoods most burdened by rent actually see the employment, depends on enforcement mechanisms that don't yet exist on paper.

The $28 million Stabilization Fund was designed as the bridge — the relief that holds the commercial corridors and the longtime residents in place while the larger development plays out over the next decade. Three months since the disbursement window opened, the bridge hasn't been crossed. Every lease renegotiation that happens before the money arrives is a negotiation the fund was supposed to prevent.

THE CALENDAR THEY KEEP

Faith institutions in September — and the infrastructure they've always been.

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

Calvin Turner was leaning against his truck at seven-thirty Saturday morning in the parking lot behind St. Columba Catholic Church on International Boulevard when I pulled up. He'd already unloaded two pallets of canned goods. His hands were dusty from the cardboard.

"This is what September looks like," he said, nodding toward the line that had already formed along the side of the building. "School's starting. Families are short. They come here."

Turner, 38, is a mechanic from Fruitvale. He's been volunteering at St. Columba's food pantry most Saturdays for about two years now. He doesn't attend Mass. He just shows up.

The pantry isn't running because September is a hard month, though September is a hard month. It runs because it runs — every week, regardless of what the council approved last session or what's still stuck in a disbursement queue. The church's food pantry has operated continuously through budget cycles, ballot initiatives, and the ongoing bureaucratic delay that has left 295 families, by the council's own count, still waiting on Stabilization Fund dollars that were approved nearly a year ago.

"I try not to think too hard about the policy stuff," Turner said. "I just know people need food on a Saturday morning."

Across the city this September, faith institutions are doing what they do every September, and every month before it: running the infrastructure the city has never quite managed to run itself. The scale of what they're doing — and the quiet precision with which they're doing it — has taken on a particular quality this fall, as the first OARI pilot districts begin their slow spin-up and the community-based services that predate them by decades continue humming along beneath the headlines.

[Photo: DJ Hartley / Bay Tribune]

---

At St. Columba on Sunday, the Feast of the Assumption drew the parish's largest September crowd in recent memory. The parking lot was full by nine. Families occupied pews three generations deep — grandmothers in church hats, fathers in guayaberas, children who had been scrubbed clean for the occasion. The homily was in both English and Spanish. The incense reached the back of the nave.

The same building that runs a food pantry on Saturday mornings was a cathedral on Sunday. That's not a contradiction — it's St. Columba's entire operating logic.

Rafael Phillips, 26, a server from Fruitvale, has been volunteering with the pantry since last spring. He came back to St. Columba after the Cathedral of Christ the Light's pantry started seeing the lines ease. "The need didn't go down," he told me Saturday, stacking soup cans. "It just moved around."

He paused and looked at the line. "I keep hearing the Stabilization Fund money is coming. I believe it's coming. But coming and here are different things, and on a Saturday morning, all that matters is what's here."

---

In West Oakland — one of the three OARI pilot districts — Acts Full Gospel Church opened its community health fair Saturday morning in the church gymnasium on Adeline Street. Folding tables lined the walls: blood pressure screening, vision checks, a dental referral station, a table from the Fruitvale Community Clinic staffed by two volunteers in scrubs.

The church has hosted the health fair annually for years. There is no equivalent city service that operates on the same schedule, at the same address, with the same volunteers who know the same families by name.

Andre Lee, 55, an electrician from Temescal, drove over from his usual Saturday post at St. Columba because a friend had mentioned the fair. I found him at the blood pressure table, waiting his turn.

"I don't come to these things for myself, usually," he said. "I come because someone in my building was talking about not being able to afford a doctor visit, and I figure I'll scope it out, see if it's worth telling him about."

He looked around the gymnasium. "It's worth telling him about."

Lee has been part of Oakland's faith-adjacent volunteer network for long enough that he's stopped distinguishing between religious obligation and civic habit. He doesn't always go to services. He goes where there's work to be done. "The church doesn't ask you what you believe when you show up," he said. "They ask you if you can carry boxes."

---

Across town in Rockridge, Temple Sinai began a new session of its job training workshop Monday evening. The synagogue's social hall was configured with folding chairs and laptops, a projector, and a whiteboard with a weekly schedule. The eight-week program covers resume writing, interview preparation, and connections to employment services.

Howard Young, 56, a barista from Rockridge who has done volunteer work at Highland Hospital for years, sat near the back of the room with a yellow legal pad. He's not looking for a job — he came to see if the program might be useful for a neighbor. "She got laid off in July," he said, quietly. "I figured I'd come and check it out before I told her about it."

The program has run every fall for years. It doesn't advertise widely. Word travels by the same mechanism it always has — neighbor telling neighbor, someone checking it out on someone else's behalf.

---

And in Montclair, at Shiva Vishnu Temple on Sunday evening, Janmashtami filled the temple courtyard with color and sound well past sunset. The festival marking Lord Krishna's birth drew families who had traveled from across the East Bay. The temple's kitchen produced food through the evening. Children danced in the parking lot.

The temple's calendar runs on its own logic, as it always has. The city's calendar is something else entirely.

---

The overlap between Oakland's OARI pilot districts and the neighborhoods where these faith institutions operate is not coincidental. West Oakland, Fruitvale, Temescal — these are the places where community-based support has historically been concentrated because these are the places where it has historically been needed most. The OARI pilot didn't choose these neighborhoods by accident. It chose them because the infrastructure was already there to absorb and complement it.

What the churches, temples, and synagogues have built over decades isn't redundant to what the city is trying to do. It's the foundation the city is building on.

Calvin Turner was still in the parking lot when I left St. Columba Saturday morning. The line had thinned but not disappeared. He was on his phone, fielding a call, nodding. He held up one finger at me — one minute — and kept nodding.

He finished the call. "Someone wants to donate twelve cases of pasta," he said. "I told them bring it Saturday. We'll figure out where to put it."

He went back to his truck. The pantry was still open.

FIRST DAY BACK

September in Oakland's classrooms — and the people who prepared them.

By Angela Reyes | Bay Tribune Education

Jalen Hill spent part of his September weekend reorganizing a shared reading room at an Oakland Unified middle school in Jack London — moving bookshelves, clearing a corner for a new reading circle setup, going through a box of donated chapter books that had been sitting in storage since spring.

He's a line cook by trade. He works at a restaurant in the Jack London corridor. He's also been volunteering with an OUSD youth literacy program for three years, and when school started this week, he wanted the room ready.

"I like the first week," he said. "The kids come in skeptical. Like they're not sure if they're going to care. And then they pick up a book and they get into it and you watch them forget to be skeptical."

Hill, 35, has lived in Jack London for four years, long enough to have watched the neighborhood absorb the vote on Baylight — a development project that is now, as of this fall, moving from abstraction to reality. The construction equipment arrived last month. The noise is background now.

"The kids hear it too," he said. "Some of them have questions. What's being built. Who it's for. You can't not notice it when you're walking to school."

He paused. "I don't have good answers for them yet. I just try to make sure the room is worth walking to."

Oakland Unified opened its doors this week for the fall semester — a return to school that, like every return to school, is simultaneously ordinary and not. The ordinary part: backpacks, supply lists, the cafeteria smell of the first hot lunch of the year. The not-ordinary part: a city that is mid-transformation, with construction corridors and contested funds and neighborhood institutions doing their best to hold a line while the city figures out its next move.

The literacy program Hill volunteers with runs after school two days a week. It operates on donated books, volunteer hours, and an OUSD coordinator who has kept the program alive through multiple budget reviews. It does not have a building named after it. It does not get press releases.

"The thing about reading programs," Hill said, "is they don't look like much from the outside. It's just a kid and a book. But that's the whole thing. That's exactly the thing."

He straightened a stack of books on the shelf. "First week is the most important. You set the tone. You show them the room is ready for them. That they matter enough that someone showed up before they did."

He showed up.

FIRST FRIDAY IN THE CORRIDOR

Jack London turns out for art — and tries to hear itself over the construction.

By Kai Marston | Bay Tribune Arts

The fences went up along the Baylight site six weeks ago. Orange construction barriers now line three blocks of the waterfront end of Jack London, and if you're walking the corridor on a Friday night in September, you navigate around them — ducking left, crossing the street, finding the galleries and the food trucks by slightly different routes than you used last spring.

First Friday finds its way.

By eight o'clock, the crowd was out in the clear September air, temperature in the low sixties, light jacket weather. The usual mix: the regulars who come every month, the couples who treat it as date night, the groups of friends who start at one pop-up and end somewhere else entirely. A DJ at the corner of Third and Broadway sent a low bass throb down the block. The smell of grilled corn drifted from a cart near the old warehouse row.

The galleries were open — windows lit, artists present, work on the walls. In the back room of one space near the waterfront, Dante Reyes, a muralist who's worked in the Jack London neighborhood for years, had a new series of large-format prints on temporary display: close-cropped photographs of walls, surfaces, the textures of Oakland's industrial waterfront. Texture studies, he called them. "The walls that are going to be gone in a year," he said to someone standing next to me, gesturing at a print of a corrugated metal surface with a faded painted image partially visible underneath. "I wanted to keep them."

Reyes has been documenting the corridor as it changes. He's been at it since the Baylight vote, working early mornings before the demolition crews arrive, before the light gets hard. The prints in the gallery were made from those mornings.

Marcus Walker, 48, a dishwasher who lives in the Jack London corridor and works for the Oakland Housing Authority, came to First Friday with a neighbor. He stood for a while in front of one of Reyes's prints — a close-up of a painted warehouse door, the paint cracked and layered, a word visible that might be a name or might be a number.

"That's on Embarcadero," he said, pointing. "I walk by it every day." He looked at it for another moment. "I don't know if I'll be able to walk by it in a year."

That's not dramatic. Walker means it literally. The Baylight development footprint includes some of the residential blocks adjacent to the commercial corridor, and tenants in the affected zone have been receiving documentation from the housing authority — the same agency Walker works for — about relocation options and timelines.

He's not panicking. He's watching.

"I've lived in Oakland my whole life," he said. "The neighborhood changes. That's what Oakland does. But it changes fast and it changes slow at different times, and right now it's changing fast."

He looked at the room — the crowded gallery, the prints on the wall, the people taking photos of Reyes's work. "But people are still coming out. That means something."

First Friday has been running in Oakland for more than a decade. It absorbs the city's contradictions the way the city absorbs them: by keeping the doors open and letting people walk in. On a Friday in September, with construction fences three blocks from the waterfront and a neighborhood in the middle of becoming something new, the crowd came out anyway. They looked at the pictures of the walls. They ate the corn. They found their way around the barriers.

The DJ kept playing.

IF YOU HAD TO KEEP ONE — AITKEN OR RICHARDS?

Two dynasty veterans. Two contract years. One franchise with a budget. The question nobody in Oakland wants to answer.

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

I've been sitting with this question for two weeks and I still don't have a clean answer.

Here's the scenario, as simply as I can put it: Mark Aitken and Martin Richards are both in contract years. Both are going to command serious money. Both are irreplaceable in different ways. And at some point before spring training gives way to regular season, the front office is going to have to make a calculation. Not necessarily an either/or — but let's be honest, that's what it always becomes. Money is math, and math has limits.

So. If you had to keep one, who do you keep?

Let me start with Aitken, because he's the easier argument to make and the harder one to live without. Mark Aitken is one of the last links to what this franchise became. Three Gold Gloves at first base. A career average of two-seventy-three. One hundred ninety-five home runs. Playoff MVP in two thousand thirty-seven when this city needed someone to show up in October and he showed up in all caps. He is correct, not flashy — that's what Anthony wrote about him, and Anthony was right. He is the glue player who doesn't make highlights and doesn't require them. The dynasty ran on power and chaos — Davis, Keane, Kelley going yard — and it ran on Aitken holding the corners together so everything else could happen.

Some fans will say Aitken is done. They'll say thirty-two is old for a first baseman with production declining toward the back half of a career, that three-gold-gloves is history not present tense, that you're paying for a name when you could be paying for the future. I hear them. I'm not one of them. But I hear them.

Martin Richards is the harder sell, and that's exactly the problem.

Anthony wrote about this in an earlier piece — he called Richards "the invisible star hiding in plain sight." Six-point-eight WAR last year. Perfect fielding numbers at third base. A player who has done everything asked of him without once demanding a headline. The Tribune, we have to admit, barely covered him for two years. He just kept playing, kept producing, and kept not getting credit for it. That's actually a character trait I admire. That's someone who shows up because showing up is the job, not because showing up generates content.

Here's where my head goes when I'm trying to think clearly: Aitken's identity is tied to this dynasty. He IS this era. Losing him means something breaks in the narrative, something invisible but real. Richards, on the other hand, is someone who has earned his money quietly and who the next version of this team also needs — because the next version of this team needs a third baseman who posts that kind of WAR and doesn't require constant management.

The honest answer is that we need both, and the honest answer is a luxury we might not get.

My gut says keep Aitken. Not because Richards doesn't deserve it — he absolutely does — but because Aitken is Oakland in a way that can't be replaced. You can find a Martin Richards. You cannot find another Mark Aitken, at least not one who has this team's history written into his bones. He plays first base the way this franchise was supposed to be played: with discipline, with craft, without noise.

But I'll lose sleep over Richards. Because that man has been doing his job in silence while the rest of us watched the louder story. He deserves the security that comes with a contract, and the idea of him walking somewhere else because we couldn't clear the cap space is the kind of thing that keeps fans like me staring at the ceiling at two in the morning.

One of them might not be here next season. That's the business. I hate the business.

SEVEN GAMES, TWO STORIES: WHAT THE A'S PRESEASON START REVEALS

The A's are 7-0 in exhibition play. The numbers say one thing. The roster says two.

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

The lights came on at the Baylight District open-air facility early in September, and the Oakland A's answered every question about their preseason by winning seven games before anyone had time to form a reasonable opinion about them. Seven-and-oh. No dropped games, no squandered leads, no hangover from the offseason roster reshuffling that had half the league watching to see how Deacon Seymour would manage a clubhouse built in two different eras.

The 7-0 preseason record does not mean everything. Exhibition games are exhibition games — opponents are carrying their own projects, pitching prospects to innings limits, evaluating depth charts of their own. But preseason results are not nothing, either. The way a team plays when the stakes are low tells you something about how a roster has metabolized its own construction. This A's squad, in seven games, has shown two distinct stories running simultaneously, and at some point they will have to reconcile.

The first story is genuinely encouraging.

Ernesto Quintero is batting three-eighty-five with two home runs and ten hits through the first seven games. Let that number sit for a moment. Ten hits in a preseason stretch is not a small thing from a player who came into this roster move under the kind of pressure that tends to flatten young hitters — he is the designated hitter in place of a two-time MVP who moved to left field to accommodate him. Isley Kelley is batting three-fifty-three with three home runs and six hits. Vladimir Gonzalez is at three-sixteen with six hits. That is a top third of the lineup functioning at a contact rate that would make any manager's internal calculus considerably simpler.

On the mound, Arturo Ramos has produced a two-point-zero-zero ERA across eight innings with eleven strikeouts. Mariano Rosales has four saves and a one-point-six-nine ERA. The bullpen question that followed this team through the spring has not announced itself yet as a real problem.

The second story is Seymour's actual test.

Eric Taveras is batting one-fifty-eight with three hits. That is a number that requires context, and the context is not entirely unfair to Taveras — preseason samples are small, hitters take time to find their timing, and no one should write an obituary after seven exhibition games. But Seymour signed off on the Taveras bet as part of a roster architecture that was supposed to signal a generational shift alongside the farewell of the dynasty's core. Quintero, at twenty-four, has made his case immediately. Taveras has not yet.

The front office still believes in Taveras. People inside the organization point to his bat speed metrics and his approach at the plate as evidence that the production will come. They are not wrong that preseason batting averages are an unreliable instrument. But unreliable is different from irrelevant, and Seymour will have decisions to make before the regular season begins about how much runway a struggling hitter gets when the lineup around him is producing at this rate.

The larger picture is one that manager and front office have both earned.

Travis Coles, who signed a two-year, forty-six-point-one-eight-million-dollar deal as the trade acquisition centerpiece, has not yet logged the innings that would put his name on this piece with a definitive verdict. Benji Dillon, in his farewell season, is being managed with the careful load distribution that a thirty-six-year-old left-handed starter with five Cy Young awards deserves. The rotation's health is the story that doesn't show up in the win-loss column yet but determines whether October looks like an opportunity or a consolation.

Seven wins. Two stories. The roster Seymour inherited has talent that would make most franchises envious. What he does with the noise — the contract years, the farewell tours, the young players still finding themselves — is the real question. The preseason answers that question incompletely. But the outline is visible, and for a seven-oh start, the outline looks like a team that knows what it wants to be.

THE BALL CARRIED, AND THE CITY REMEMBERED

Vinnie Keane goes yard in the preseason opener. The dynasty's final year begins the way it should — with the sound of a bat and a city holding its breath.

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

There is a particular quality to the first home run of a farewell season that you only recognize if you have watched enough of them.

I have watched enough of them.

Vinnie Keane went yard in the preseason opener, the ball leaving his bat at an angle I have spent fourteen years watching him produce in game situations that actually counted, and something in my chest did what it always does when something you have covered long enough becomes something you are losing. He is batting five-hundred through four at-bats. Two hits. One of them left the ballpark. The box score reads like a man who has not yet been told the season is ending.

Four hundred thirty-six career home runs. Six World Series championships. The two-thousand-thirty-one MVP, when Keane was twenty-eight years old and Oakland was just beginning to understand what it had. I was in the press box for that October — I remember the fog off the bay coming in low over the Coliseum in the late innings of a game that shouldn't have been close and was, and Keane putting a ball over the fence with the precision of a man who knew exactly where the pitcher was going before the pitcher decided. Some swings look like accidents. His have never looked like accidents.

The dynasty that built this era of Oakland baseball is finishing in the only way a dynasty of this magnitude can finish: slowly, publicly, with its principals still capable of reminding you why you paid attention in the first place. Benji Dillon, left-handed and forty seasons of craft compressed into his arm, is managing his final rotation with the same command intelligence that won him five Cy Young awards. Isley Kelley, the Machine from Mississippi as this paper once called him, is batting three-fifty-three in preseason at thirty-two years old, which is not the behavior of a player coasting toward retirement. Darrin Davis, who moved to left field so a twenty-four-year-old kid could take his spot at designated hitter — I wrote about that in a different piece and I'll say it again: that was the act of a man who loves the franchise more than he loves his position. Mark Aitken holds first base the way he has always held it, with the three Gold Gloves and the Playoff MVP tucked into the back of his memory and the present tense still requiring his full attention.

But Keane's homer is what I keep returning to. Because Keane has always been the most readable of these men. Davis is intensity, Kelley is precision, Dillon is intelligence, Aitken is craft. Keane is joy. He has always played with a quality that communicated pleasure in the act itself — not the result, not the celebration, but the swing, the moment of contact, the pure physical transaction between wood and horsehide that has been at the center of this sport since before either of us was born.

When you watch enough farewell seasons, you learn to read what the player is not saying. Keane's mood this preseason is being described in our coverage as reflective. I believe that. I also believe that reflective is what a player looks like when he understands the gift he has been given and is in the process of returning it carefully, with gratitude, without waste.

The regular season is three weeks away. The dynasty's final year is in its opening pages. Oakland has known this was coming — the retirements, the departures, the slow transfer of this franchise from one era to the next. What it has not known, and what the preseason is beginning to remind it, is that the men who built this thing are still capable of moments that stop your breath.

One home run in a preseason game. Four at-bats. A five-hundred batting average that will not survive the sample size of a full season. None of that is what I will remember.

I will remember that the ball carried, and the city remembered what it felt like, and for one afternoon in September at the Baylight District open-air, the dynastic thing that Oakland has loved for fifteen years felt, impossibly, still.

PAULSON STEPS TO THE MIC: WHAT HE SAID, AND WHAT HE DIDN'T

The A's GM holds his first preseason availability. Forty-one minutes. Fifteen reporters. One question nobody asked directly.

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

Mike Paulson held his first media availability of the preseason on Wednesday morning at the Baylight District facility, arriving eleven minutes early and staying twelve minutes past when he said he would. Both of those facts are, in their own way, characteristic.

The A's general manager took questions for forty-one minutes in a media room that had a light fixture making an irregular buzzing sound in the upper left corner and fifteen reporters who had been waiting since June to ask him directly about a roster that has since gone seven-and-oh. He answered questions about the preseason, about the rotation, about the designated hitter situation, and about what he wanted from the next six months. He did not answer one question that every Oakland reporter in that room was carrying.

He didn't need to. The question is old enough to have its own reputation.

The two-city situation — Paulson's visibility at Chase Center across the course of the Warriors' late-season push, including a courtside appearance in Bulls red that generated more column inches than any A's signing this side of Travis Coles's forty-six-million-dollar deal — has not resolved itself into a clean narrative. The Warriors, at thirty-nine and twenty-eight, are in the thick of a late-season race. The A's are seven-and-oh in preseason and generating the kind of national media profile that comes with a dynasty team in its farewell season. Paulson is the man whose name attaches to both stories. He stood at the podium on Wednesday and discussed neither one directly.

What he did discuss was the preseason, and he discussed it with the specific vocabulary of someone who has spent a long time thinking about roster construction as architecture. He was satisfied with what he had seen from Quintero — he did not hide that, repeating the outfielder's name three times in responses that covered different topics. He was measured about Taveras in a way that communicated support without overselling it, the careful phrasing of a front-office executive who knows that public declarations about struggling young hitters can become anchor points for later criticism.

On the rotation, he was direct: Coles and the farewell-season veterans give this team a pitching staff capable of carrying a playoff run if the health holds. He said "if the health holds" twice. That is not an accident.

On the Warriors question, one reporter asked. Paulson paused for two seconds — a long pause in a media context — and said that he was fully focused on the Oakland A's and the upcoming regular season, that the A's were his organization, and that the preseason results had him feeling the kind of hungry that comes from knowing what's possible when a roster performs above expectations.

The word "hungry" from a man who chooses his words carefully is worth paying attention to. Whether the appetite is specifically for an A's championship, or whether it signals something about Paulson's own unresolved positioning between two franchises in two cities, is the question Oakland has been asking since last winter. It did not get a clean answer on Wednesday morning.

It rarely does.

What the city got was a GM who showed up, stood at the podium, answered forty-one minutes of questions, and seemed genuinely energized by a team going seven-oh through September. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, the most present this front office has looked since Paulson's last Oakland media session before the winter. The A's, by every indicator available — preseason record, pitching depth, the national attention that comes with a dynasty farewell tour — have given their general manager something real to talk about.

He talked about it. Eleven minutes early, twelve minutes late. That is, for this particular version of Mike Paulson, a promising sign.

FIFTY-TWO WINS AND THEY STILL DON'T BELIEVE YOU

The Bulls are the best team in the Eastern Conference. The national media would prefer not to discuss it.

By Selena Grant | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

Let's start with what the numbers say: the Bulls are 52-21, the best record in the Eastern Conference, riding a six-game winning streak, and coming off a 116-101 statement game against Oklahoma City in which three players posted double-doubles and a rookie put up 29 and 11. That is not a fraud. That is a first-place team.

The "fraud" narrative — amplified this week by national outlets who have spent the better part of two seasons explaining why Chicago's rebuild is a mirage — has a specific logic to it, and it goes like this: the East is weak, the schedule has been favorable, and when the Bulls see playoff basketball against a Western Conference contender, the seams will show. It's a compelling argument if you ignore the schedule, the roster construction, and the last six weeks of evidence.

The OKC game was not a schedule-padding win. The Thunder entered that matchup at 41-32, third in the West, with a defensive structure designed to suppress exactly the kind of perimeter-driven offense the Bulls run. It didn't work. Hank Trepagnier dismantled it.

Twenty-nine points and eleven rebounds. The rookie from the first round of Paulson's Bulls-era draft is now doing things that prompt uncomfortable questions about the Rookie of the Year race. His scoring line was built on shot variety — mid-range pull-ups in the second quarter when the Thunder collapsed his drives, and then four times in the fourth quarter back to the rim when they adjusted to respect the jumper. That's not just athleticism. That's basketball intelligence that usually takes players two or three seasons to develop. Trepagnier is doing it in his first.

Adash Stanley added 23 points on the night, which tells part of the story. The other part is that Stanley picked up his third technical foul in four games, a pattern that has moved beyond coincidence into something the front office is tracking closely. Stanley's physicality is not incidental — it's been a feature of how Paulson built this roster, a counter to the finesse-heavy teams at the top of the East. But technicals cost possessions. At 52-21, the Bulls can absorb them. In a playoff series, where referees tighten up and home crowds shift momentum, one ill-timed technical can flip a quarter. The question is whether Stanley's edge is being managed or just tolerated.

Josh Giddey continues his quiet late-season emergence: 18 points, 11 assists, one turnover against OKC. Since the All-Star break, Giddey has been the most consistent point guard in the Eastern Conference not named as a perennial All-Star. His turnover rate has dropped. His assist-to-possession ratio has climbed. And Walker Kessler — 19 points, 10 rebounds, three blocks — keeps providing the interior gravity that allows Giddey and Trepagnier to operate in space.

Mike Paulson has not commented on the "fraud" label. He has also not commented on Golden State, which is 39-28 and has made no secret of its interest in the man who built this roster. Those two silences are related. The better the Bulls get, the more complicated Paulson's position becomes. Fifty-two wins is the argument Chicago needs him to stay. Fifty-two wins is also exactly the argument that makes Golden State want him more.

The national media has their narrative. The Bulls have their record. One of those things is scoreboard.

THE SAME QUESTION, SIX WINS LATER

At Romano's on 35th, the Bulls keep winning and Mike Paulson keeps not answering. Bridgeport is starting to read the silence as an answer.

By Talia Finch | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

The back-to-school signs went up in the window at the pharmacy on 35th two weeks ago. Romano's hasn't changed anything — same ceramic mugs, same dark roast, same bartender who refills your coffee before you realize it's empty. But Paulette Okafor was sitting at the counter on Tuesday morning with her phone face-down, which is different from last time.

Last time she had it face-up. She was watching the Warriors score updates.

"Six more wins," she said, when I sat down. Not hello. Just that. "Six more and he's still not saying anything."

Paulette works at the elementary school two blocks off Halsted — not teaching, she's quick to clarify, office administration. She grew up in Bridgeport. She has watched this neighborhood cycle through mayors and aldermen and promises about the lakefront, and she understands, better than most, that the people who leave usually leave quietly. They don't announce it. They just stop saying they're staying.

"That's what worries me," she said. "He's not saying he's leaving either. But at some point the silence is the statement."

Raymond Polk was at his usual table near the window, the one where you can see the street and the 35th Street Red Line stop at the same time. Raymond is a contractor, Bridgeport his whole life, and he has a particular relationship with patience — he's the kind of man who waits out a cold front before he schedules a pour, who doesn't panic when the weather turns uncertain. He watched the OKC game on his phone Wednesday night and called it "textbook." He said Trepagnier played like someone who'd been doing this for ten years.

But when I asked about Paulson, Raymond set down his mug.

"You know what I keep thinking about?" he said. "Nobody's asking LeBron to take a pay cut to stay in Cleveland. Nobody's asking anybody to bleed for a city that might not bleed back. Paulson built this. He doesn't owe us anything beyond the job."

He paused.

"But he's from here. That's different. Bridgeport doesn't produce GMs who win. He's the first one we've had. So yeah. I want him to say something. Even if it's not the something I want to hear."

Paulette nodded from across the counter without looking up from her phone. She'd turned it face-up again.

The Bulls are 52-21. Best record in the East. Six-game win streak, and the national press still calling them frauds — a fact that lands in Bridgeport with a particular sting, because Bridgeport has heard that word applied to things it loves before. What the neighborhood keeps waiting for is not another win. It's a sentence. One sentence, from Mike Paulson, that tells them whether he's staying or whether he's already gone in his mind, keeping his mouth closed out of courtesy while he figures out the logistics.

The dark roast at Romano's is the same as it's always been. The season is turning toward something. Bridgeport is watching.
