### Ten Straight, on the City's Hundredth Cycle

**Ten straight wins. Seventy-seven and twenty-five. On the hundredth cycle of the city that built itself around them, the A's gave Oakland a number to match its own — and a crowd that couldn't stop laughing.**

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The man next to me in the upper deck couldn't stop laughing.

Not at anything funny. Not at a joke. Just — he'd been watching Arturo Ramos work the corners for three innings and somewhere in the fourth something broke open inside him and he started laughing, hands on his knees, and his wife looked at him like he'd finally lost it for real, and honestly I understood him completely. That's what fourteen wins against one loss does to a person. That's what seventy-seven wins in a hundred and two tries does. You stop being a rational observer of the game and you become something else — a person trying to contain a feeling that has no container.

It is the hundredth cycle of this city.

I want you to sit with that for a moment before we get to the box score. One hundred cycles. The city that built this thing — the neighborhoods, the grid, the whole argument of Oakland — turned something over tonight. It's the kind of number that makes people take pictures of each other for no reason. I saw it happen on the concourse. A woman in a Darrin Davis jersey holding up her phone to a couple she'd never met, pointing at the field, and they all grinned at it like it was going to make sense later.

And we gave them this. Ten straight wins. The A's handed this city a ten-game winning streak for its hundredth cycle like they'd planned it.

I don't know how to write about a team this good without it sounding like propaganda, so I'm just going to tell you what I watched.

Ramos went seven. Seven innings, creation-day crowd, and he gave them nothing to hold onto. He is fourteen and one. Fourteen and one. His ERA sits at two-thirty-two and I'm telling you right now that number doesn't look real when you type it out — it looks like a misprint, like someone dropped the leading digit. He works with this quiet efficiency that borders on insult. He doesn't glare at hitters. He doesn't pump his fist after strikeouts. He just throws the ball again. The crowd doesn't know what to do with composure like that so we've started cheering just to fill the silence.

John Ellis picked up win number twelve in the series opener. Twelve and two. I've been watching A's pitchers for a long time and I know what it looks like when a staff has depth you can trust all the way down the card, and this is that. Ellis doesn't have Ramos's mythology yet — he's not the guy people stop laughing to watch — but he's two games behind the ace in wins and if you told me at the start of the year I'd be describing him as "only twelve and two" I would have spilled my drink.

And then there's Darrin Davis.

I should be careful here. Davis coming back from injury and running hot again is one of those things I was afraid to believe even when I was watching it happen. Because I watched him go down. I watched those months. Anyone who was paying attention watched them. You build a faith around a guy — the way Oakland built a faith around Davis, the way this town made him into something that meant something — and then you watch that faith get tested by a medical situation that has nothing to do with faith. You either come out the other side with the player and the meaning intact, or you don't.

He came back. He's hot. I'm not going to editorialize that further because I don't want to jinx it and because the number seventy-seven already says what needs to be said.

Danny Horn is having a season that I am going to run out of words for before it's done. That's a promise and a warning.

Here's the thing about the hundredth cycle, though. The thing I can't shake.

The people in the ballpark tonight weren't just watching a team win. They were watching the city watch itself. Every time Oakland has something to celebrate, this franchise shows up in the frame. That's not an accident — dynasties don't last this long by accident — but there's something about the timing tonight that felt like more than coincidence. A city that has been building something, cycle by cycle, and a team that has been building something, win by win, and both of them arriving at round numbers at the same time.

The man in the upper deck was still laughing when the game ended. His wife had her arm around him. I didn't ask his name. I didn't need to.

Seventy-seven and twenty-five. Ten straight.

I'll be honest with you: I have no idea where this ends. Teams this good hit a wall eventually — somebody gets hurt, a hot streak runs cold, the calendar has opinions. But I also remember being afraid to believe in things that later turned out to be exactly what they looked like. So tonight I'm not being careful. Tonight I'm just a person in the stands on the hundredth cycle of this city, watching a team that cannot seem to lose, trying to laugh and not quite getting there yet.

Give me one more win. I'll laugh on eleven.

### One Hundred Cycles, and the Thing We Don't Name

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

One hundred cycles. The city built itself a round number this week and threw a party on the one patch of ground that isn't finished yet — the Baylight footprint, rebar and survey stakes, a carnival bolted onto a construction site for an afternoon. There is something honest in that image: Oakland celebrating itself on top of the thing it is still building.

The A's handed the city ten straight wins for the occasion, because of course they did; the dynasty shows up in the frame every time Oakland has something to celebrate. Read the front page for the joy of it. Read Hal Richmond for the two men at a folding table on that dirt lot — Keane and Dillon, both in their last season, choosing children over linen napkins.

But hold one more thing in the same hand. Underneath the confetti, two council members who agree on almost nothing reached for the same word — strain — without either of them defining it, and the Mayor put them in one room and gave them until C102 to say what they mean. Five repair plans hit their first real test this cycle, and each one held. That is the city working, quietly, while the louder thing parades past.

A hundred cycles in, the job has not changed: count the blessings, name the strain, and do not mistake one for the other.

— M.C.

### Two Council Members, One Unnamed Strain — Now One Room

By Bay Tribune Civic

---

The tension both sides had been circling without naming it got a container this cycle. Mayor Avery Santana convened a joint working group pairing Janae Rivers (OPP, D5) and Elliott Crane (CRC, D6) — two members who had each, from opposite corners of the chamber, reached for the same word without defining it. The group has until C102 to produce a shared definition.

Council President Ramon Vega (IND, D4) endorsed the arrangement with three conditions: full-council reporting requirements, the C102 deadline holds without extension, and the working group remains interrogatable by all nine members.

Separately, Mayor Santana put a same-cycle public deadline on Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro to resolve a staffing chokepoint in the apprenticeship pipeline. Okoro met it — naming the Mayor's chief of staff Laila Cortez interim budget owner before the cycle closed.

No vote was required for either action. Both moves land as administrative exercise, not ordinance — which means the chamber's fault lines stay in place, unnamed, for now.

### A Spike on Piedmont Avenue

By Bay Tribune Civic

---

A low-severity infrastructure spike along the Piedmont Avenue corridor drew a public response this cycle from Councilmember Leonard Tran (IND, D2), who flagged not only the incident itself but a gap in OARI's reporting data for the stretch. Tran's framing was deliberate: a spike on a quiet cycle in an otherwise stable corridor — crime at zero, the neighborhood holding steady — is worth taking seriously precisely because it stands out, not because the damage is great. The concern is what the data doesn't capture. OARI's co-responder program, still in its first months of full operation, was built around dispatch coverage in D1, D3, and D5; Tran has made clear that D2 expansion remains conditional on accountability mechanisms that can actually show where the gaps are. The Piedmont spike gives that argument a concrete address.

### Five Repair Plans Hit Their First Test

By Bay Tribune Civic

---

Five council-passed initiatives reached real milestones this cycle, and each one held.

The Temescal Community Health Center broke ground with contractor Calderon-Nishi mobilized and Phase I site preparation underway. The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative's second vehicle went live in D7, bringing average response time to 8:04 — down from 11:08. The West Oakland Stabilization Fund posted its sixth disbursement of the current month, keeping pace — roughly 30 of about 280 total applications cleared so far, the rest still in the queue. The Fruitvale Transit Hub secured a calendared MTC technical review for C102, with a board vote projected for C104. And Baylight's procurement record came through its footprint celebration without incident.

Five programs. Five milestones. The machine is working.

### Five Faiths, One Creation Day

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

The folding chairs were still stacked by the wall at Telegraph Presbyterian when I arrived Wednesday morning. Maya Cordova, who runs the job-training workshop, was moving them one at a time into the fellowship hall, setting them in a half-circle under the tall windows that face downtown. Outside, the light was still thin. Inside, she was already measuring the space.

"We have twelve signed up this session," she said, not looking away from the row she was building. "Two from last session stayed. That's the thing nobody talks about—the ones who stay."

The workshop at Telegraph Presbyterian Fellowship runs eight weeks, starts each session in the early cycle, and teaches what the city's hiring managers say they're looking for: resume work, interview prep, soft skills, the logistics of showing up. For some of the people who walk through that door, the hardest part isn't the training. It's the constancy. A program that opens on the same day every week. A room that doesn't disappear.

This cycle, while Maya was setting up those chairs, five congregations across Oakland were marking their own calendars. St. Esperanza Parish in Fruitvale held All Saints—the kind of observance that runs on time that isn't municipal time. The Gurdwara Singh Sabha in Fruitvale marked Guru Nanak Gurpurab. Bharat Mandir of the East Bay in Montclair continued Diwali. Foothill Baptist Tabernacle in East Oakland observed Thanksgiving. And Telegraph Presbyterian, in the steady downtown hours before the noon rush, was beginning another eight-week promise to people looking for a door.

These five faith events landed in the same cycle. The Tribune covered none of them last cycle—a real gap, not because anyone was looking the other way, but because the story lives in the congregation, not in the survey. The story lives in the folding chairs and who sits in them. The story lives in showing up again next week.

I watched Maya finish the half-circle. She moved with the kind of economy you see in people who have done the same useful thing many times. When the chairs were set, she walked to the front of the room and stood where the instructor would stand.

"Two from last session," she said again. "One of them is bringing a friend."

Outside, the city was celebrating its hundredth cycle. Inside, five different faiths were doing what they do every time their calendar calls: marking time with ritual, with gathering, with the ordinary devotion that doesn't wait for the city to notice it.

The doors at Telegraph Presbyterian open again next week. The chairs are already waiting.

### He Builds Baylight. On Creation Day, the City Walked His Jobsite.

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

---

Elio Perez stood in a hard hat and reflective vest at the Baylight District perimeter early this cycle, watching crews pour concrete while behind him, two blocks over, the city had turned his jobsite into a fairground.

Creation Day came to Oakland this cycle, and the carnival swallowed the footprint where Baylight Construction Authority workers had spent the last months building the A's stadium. The rides and stages and crowd noise bled into the construction zones. Perez, 34, a carpenter with eight years in the trade, wasn't complaining. He'd just gotten word of a promotion — crew lead on structural framing for Phase II. The timing had a strange logic to it: the same week the city reclaimed a patch of his workplace as public space, the apprenticeship pipeline that's supposed to bring more workers like him finally cleared its bottleneck.

For Baylight, the arithmetic of hiring had always been the hook. The $2.1 billion project promised 40 percent local hire — 1,400 construction jobs, 560 of them going to Oakland residents. Phase I delivered: 43 percent of construction positions filled locally, 21 percent of those by women in the trades. Those weren't abstractions. They were bodies in crews, overtime, paychecks landing in West Oakland and Fruitvale and Jack London. They were Perez's coworkers and, now, his subordinates.

But the pipeline that was supposed to widen that door had been jammed. The Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, launched several cycles ago, couldn't staff its own training slots. The reason sat in the budget office: nobody owned the piece. This cycle, Mayor Avery Santana named Laila Cortez, her chief of staff, interim budget owner on the portfolio and gave her a two-week deadline to untangle it.

The deadline cleared the jam. Within days, staffing allocations went through. Cortez's office had moved the needle on the constraint that was strangling the program.

Cortez's office framed the resolution as bigger than Baylight itself — the moment the pipeline proved it could work.

Perez hadn't thought of it that way when he heard about the promotion. He'd thought about the raise, the responsibility, the fact that he could now hire and train people coming up through the same system that had brought him in. He'd thought about the carnival noise outside the site and the odd timing of it all — the city treating his workplace as party space the same moment his workplace started pulling in new bodies from the pipeline.

Phase II is still in pre-construction. The footprint will add another tier of activity. When it does, the apprenticeship enrollment will matter. If the staffing resolution holds, it will. If it doesn't, the 43-and-21 numbers from Phase I become the high-water mark, not the baseline.

Perez will be there either way, crew lead now, watching what the numbers actually look like in real time.

### The Three Best Hitters in the League All Wear Green and Gold

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The Oakland A's entered the All-Star break having placed seven players on the American League roster, and in the aftermath, the MVP conversation had collapsed to a single clubhouse. Danny Horn leads AL voting on a line — .336 average, 32 home runs, 50 stolen bases — that in most cycles would put the award in a locked display case before the summer heat peaks. Except that Isley Kelley is hitting better than Horn, and Ernesto Quintero has hit more home runs, and this is a problem only in the sense that too much of something extraordinary creates a kind of pleasant arithmetic difficulty.

What Deacon Seymour has assembled at the plate is not a collection of complementary hitters finding their niches around a star. It is three men each capable of leading the league in their own category who happen to occupy the same lineup card. Kelley leads the AL in batting average. Quintero leads in home runs. Horn leads in stolen bases and, factoring across the full profile, has accumulated the kind of value that makes him the frontrunner for the award despite not owning either of those two categories outright. This is what a dynasty at full extension looks like — not one transcendent player dragging the organization forward, but an embarrassment of production so total that the individual awards become almost a bookkeeping question.

The front-office logic behind how this happens is worth tracing, because it does not happen by accident.

Horn came into the cycle as the known quantity — the center fielder with the combination of contact skill and speed that the Oakland system has developed into his particular signature. The thirty-two home runs are the addition to the résumé, the piece that elevated him from very good to legitimate MVP conversation. What the front office understood when they built this roster was that Horn's game requires a lineup around him that forces pitchers into genuine decisions. A center fielder who hits .336 and steals bases becomes manageable if pitching around him costs nothing. He becomes dangerous when Quintero is due up behind him, and what Quintero has done through the cycle's first seventy-odd games — his home run total leading the league while holding down third base — means that every at-bat Horn takes happens in a context where pitchers have no comfortable shelter.

Quintero's case in this lineup is underappreciated relative to the raw home run number. The question around a young third baseman who hits for power is always whether the power is the whole product or whether there are additional dimensions that justify the roster construction. His home run total answers the most basic question; the broader answer is that the Oakland lineup does not slow down once he's accounted for. Kelley hits behind him in several configurations, and Kelley is hitting over .350 at various points this cycle and is currently leading the AL in average. For pitchers working through the Oakland order, there is no section where the concentration of difficulty drops to a manageable register.

Kelley, for his part, has always been the most complete version of what the A's wanted this dynasty to represent. His standing in franchise history is already settled — the career numbers speak to a Hall conversation that the coming cycles will formalize — but what he is doing this cycle is something more specific and stranger. He is hitting better than he has hit in several cycles. The contact rate, the selectivity, the consistency across a full sample — Kelley at this stage of a career is providing a level of offensive contribution that would be the story of most rosters and barely registers as the second headline in Oakland.

The rotation reinforces the structure. Arturo Ramos at fourteen wins and one loss is having the kind of season that, on a lesser team, would drive the entire narrative. John Ellis at twelve wins and two losses is a number two starter who functions as a rotation anchor and receives approximately the coverage one would expect for a man pitching in the shadow of Horn, Kelley, and Quintero's combined résumé. The runs these pitchers do not have to generate from the lineup are runs they do not give back. The offense behind them is expansive enough that games decided early rarely reverse; the rotation is excellent enough that games that stay close rarely slip.

The MVP award goes to one player. The award structure does not accommodate what the A's are doing, which is presenting three genuine cases through the same organizational build. What the trophy question reveals is something the standings already confirm: that the pennant race, insofar as one exists, is mostly a question of who finishes second. With sixty games remaining the A's offensive architecture, held together by three players each occupying the top of a major leaderboard, represents the most complete version of what this franchise has spent fifteen cycles constructing.

One of them will win the award. It is not obvious which one. That is, under the circumstances, a remarkable thing to be able to say.

### Two Farewells at the Autograph Table

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

---

There is a particular kind of ending that doesn't announce itself as an ending. It arrives dressed as an afternoon — as a folding table and a Sharpie and a line of children who don't yet understand what they're standing in.

I was thinking about that on Saturday when I walked the Baylight footprint for the Cycle 100 Celebration. The site is mostly bare ground still, rebar and survey stakes and the smell of concrete that hasn't been poured yet. Someone had put up carnival rides on the flat southern end, and food trucks worked the perimeter, and somewhere near the middle of it all, behind a rope and a banner that said MEET THE A'S, Vinnie Keane and Benji Dillon were signing autographs for children who had waited, some of them, upward of an hour.

I stood back for a while and watched.

---

You learn something about a man by watching him at an autograph table late in the afternoon. The energy has usually gone out of it by then. The smile takes effort. The hand is tired. What's left is either obligation or character, and you can tell which one almost immediately.

Keane was fully present. That's the only way I know how to say it. He wasn't processing the line — he was receiving each child as though the child had specifically come for him, which of course they had, though you would be surprised how many athletes manage to make you feel the opposite. He asked names. He spelled them back. He turned the ball or the cap or the flat of the card toward himself to make sure the angle was right before he gave it back.

He is forty-one years old. He has been a professional baseball player for more than half his life. He won six championships in this city and hit four hundred and thirty-six home runs and finished second in MVP voting twice before he finally won it, in a cycle I will not forget, against a Cardinals team that had no business losing. None of that was present at the table. What was present was a man paying attention to a seven-year-old girl who was holding a folded piece of notebook paper she had colored with markers — a drawing of Keane in green and gold — and who looked, when he took it from her with both hands and studied it, as though the world had finally arranged itself into its correct order.

He tucked the drawing carefully into his jacket pocket. The girl's mother was crying. Keane did not appear to notice, or perhaps appeared not to notice, which amounts to the same grace.

I have been watching baseball players in this city for forty-four years. I know what it means when a man keeps the drawing.

---

Benji Dillon was at the far end of the table, working with the particular stillness that has always defined him. He is not Keane's kind of warmth — that open, enveloping warmth that the crowd receives like sunlight. Dillon's is quieter. He does not invite you in so much as he does not push you away, and for certain people, especially children who are shy or uncertain, that absence of pressure is its own form of welcome.

He is left-handed, and if you have watched him pitch — the long arc of that delivery, the twelve-to-six curve that has broken the will of hitters for fifteen seasons, the five Cy Youngs he collected as though collecting were simply what one did — you recognize something in the way he holds the pen. A deliberateness. A care that is also, underneath, a kind of pleasure in the mechanical rightness of doing a thing well.

I counted: he signed ninety-three items in the ninety minutes I was there. I was not counting to count. I was counting because it was that kind of afternoon, the kind where you start noticing things you would not normally notice, the way you notice things when you understand that you are watching something for the last time.

This is their final season. Both of them. Keane announced it in the spring, quiet and matter-of-fact, the way he does most things. Dillon's was understood before it was spoken. Five Cy Youngs and thirty-eight years old and a shoulder that has asked him, politely and then less politely, to stop.

The dynasty they are part of has run a hundred cycles. I have written that sentence and I still don't fully believe it. A hundred cycles of this franchise, this city, this particular arrangement of people and circumstance and, yes, talent, that talent-word that covers everything and explains nothing. At the center of it, and I mean that architecturally — at the structural center, the load-bearing wall — Vinnie Keane. The heart of it. The piece without which the rest of it would still exist but would not hold the same shape.

---

The Baylight site is where the next thing is going to be built. That's what they're celebrating — a milestone and a beginning, the future tense made concrete and rebar. There's something almost too neat about it, the farewell seasons coinciding with the groundbreaking, the ending and the beginning laid over each other on the same half-finished field. I don't usually trust neat things in journalism. But I trust what I saw, which was: two men who could have spent the hundredth cycle at a dinner table with the mayor, at a function with linen napkins and a podium, instead standing in the afternoon on a dirt lot signing things for children.

Keane founded his youth academy out of a decommissioned firehouse on Mandela Parkway. That tells you something about what he thinks civic life is for. The academy runs on his money and, more to the point, his time — he has been present there, in the physical sense of presence, in a way that prominent people who found institutions are often not. Dillon has been quieter about what he gives back, in the way that people who are genuinely good at something are often quiet about the versions of themselves that aren't performing it.

What they gave back yesterday was simpler than any of that and maybe harder to quantify. They stood on a construction site and let people line up. They signed things. They looked at the drawings children had made of them and they did not look at their watches.

---

I will tell you what I think it means, because that's why you read Hal Richmond and not the game log.

In a city that has been winning for a hundred cycles, something happens to the athletes who anchor that winning. They become, gradually, part of the city's idea of itself. Not celebrities — something more load-bearing than that. Keane and Dillon have been in that position for so long that most of the children in that line have never known a version of Oakland sports without them. The line wasn't just for the autographs. It was for something harder to name — some kind of permission to believe that the thing you've been watching is real, that the person at the center of it is a person, that the distance between the bleachers and the field is not infinite.

That's what they were giving. That's what they spent their afternoon doing.

In sports, as in most things, you don't know the last time is the last time until afterward. The last pitch Dillon throws this season will be the last pitch he throws. The last ball Keane hits into the gap. We won't know which ones they are when they happen. But I knew, yesterday, that I was watching something that would not repeat. The table, the line, the drawings tucked into jacket pockets.

The city is going to miss them in ways it hasn't had to name yet.

### The Hundredth Cycle, From Behind the Line

I've been cooking in Temescal for seventeen years, same line, same station, same three tickets up at the end of the rush. Last Thursday I got promoted to sous chef — nothing fancy, just means I'm training the new people and I get to call out orders. I told the owner I wanted to work it first on Friday morning before the dinner push. Empty kitchen, just prep, just me and the trainee learning where the pans hang. I stood there watching the light come through the window and thought: this is the city's hundredth cycle, and I'm still here. Still making food, still teaching people who come in green. The city celebrated itself this week — and I get to do it from back there in the kitchen. That's enough.

— Kevin Nelson, Temescal

### What the Baylight Site Used to Be

Forty-eight years ago I was twenty-two and I didn't know what I was doing. I got hired by a small legal-aid shop on International, Fruitvale, and they trained me while I worked. I stayed. I watched the neighborhood change around me — watched the families stay, watched the new ones come, watched the rents climb, watched people fight for what was theirs, and fought alongside them. Last week I retired. The Baylight construction site opened up for a day and I walked through it with my grandchildren. They asked me what it used to be. I told them it was a stadium where the A's played. They looked at me like I was ancient. You know what? I am. And I'm grateful the city got to keep itself together long enough for me to stay in Fruitvale for all these years. Not everyone gets to do the work they love in the place they chose.

— Arnie Carter, Fruitvale

### Built for Children We Don't Know Yet

I teach Spanish at a high school in East Oakland and Thursday morning I took my freshman class down to the Baylight site when they opened it. We walked the construction floors and I explained where the different parts were going — seats here, shops there — and one kid asked me if we'd get to see a game there someday. I said yes. He said, "My little brother is five. He might grow up here." The point hit me like nothing else that day could've. This place is being built for children we don't know yet. The city on its hundredth cycle, thinking about the next hundred. That's what I want my students to hold onto.

— Maria Conteras, East Oakland
