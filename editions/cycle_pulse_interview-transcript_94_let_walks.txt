# Interview Transcript — Mike Paulson (GM, Oakland A's)

**Cycle 94 | Paulson mode | Reporter: Hal Richmond | Subject: Mike Paulson**

Theme: The let-walks. Kelley's farewell going seven for nine the cycle the feature aired. Keane and Aitken and the question of Martin Richards. The youth chain that funds the walks. And the phone call Varek named on the record at a tech-expo panel — the one that's been hanging in public air since this cycle.

Setting: The Tribune offices, late afternoon. Richmond carries a notebook he doesn't open. Coffee that's gone cold. On the record.

---

**HAL RICHMOND:** Mike — thanks for coming up. I want to start with Isley.

The producers spent — what, eight months? — assembling the feature. Archival footage from the basement. The thirty-two acceptance, the thirty-four October, the thirty-five curtain call. Teammates trying not to cry on camera and mostly failing. We aired it this cycle. The same cycle Isley went seven for nine against Baltimore in a series this team lost for the first time since the opener.

The posture in that feature was farewell. The posture in the front office — what I can read of it from out here — has been farewell. The contract runs through this season and the signal has been clear enough that I built a tribute around it.

But that's a man currently hitting .777 against major-league pitching across nine at-bats. That's not a man saying goodbye.

So I want to ask you straight, Mike. Is the posture still where it was three cycles ago? Or is what we just watched the kind of thing that moves a decision?

---

**MIKE PAULSON:** What a great job on the Isley production that aired — and such great ratings speak to who Isley is and has been for Oakland.

I think the table needs to be reset on the nature of this season. It is true we are running a farewell campaign for Vinnie Keane, as he is set to retire. Benji Dillon came back on a one-year deal to make one last run with Vinnie.

As for everyone else — Benji hasn't made any statement on this being his last year and will not have contract talks during the season. Same situation with Mark Aitken, Martin Richards, and Isley Kelley. Obviously in a perfect world we want everyone back next season, but reality needs to come into play. We have an overabundance of minor-league talent ready to break out, as you see with the young guys on the current roster.

I took a bit of issue with the reporting that it's been decided. As I'd think I'd know first, as the person deciding.

It's a good problem to have. But hard decisions ultimately will have to be made to cement the A's future.

---

**HAL RICHMOND:** Let me stay with that for a minute, because you just put something on the record I want to make sure I hear correctly.

Benji Dillon came back on a one-year deal to make one last run with Vinnie. That's a sentence I haven't seen in print. Vinnie's farewell campaign is a thing this city has been allowed to know is happening. Dillon's role inside it — a one-year deal, deliberate, to ride with him — that's a piece of dynasty architecture I think readers would want to know. Two pitchers and a designated hitter making a particular decision together about how they leave a clubhouse. That's not roster construction. That's something else.

I'll come back to that.

The reset you just offered is a real one and I want to honor it on the record. What you're telling me is that the only confirmed departure is Vinnie. Dillon, Aitken, Richards, Kelley — no statements, no contract talks during the season, the door isn't closed and isn't open. That's a different room than the one Slayer and I have been writing from this cycle.

I want to ask you about the gap, Mike. Because what you're describing as "I'd know first as the person deciding" — that's true. You would. But the posture I've been reading from the front office, the posture Slayer read from the front office, the posture readers have been digesting for three cycles — that has shape. It's quiet, but it's not nothing. You commit money forward to Coles and Reyna and the next thing, you call up Reyna and let him have a two-homer night, you let a TV crew spend eight months building a Kelley retrospective and you don't ask them to slow down — and Oakland reads that posture as a decision being made even if nobody in the building has signed paper on it yet.

So help me understand it, Mike. Is the posture I'm reading a thing I'm misreading? Or is the posture real, and the decision just hasn't closed?

---

**MIKE PAULSON:** Understandable. I appreciate the conclusion one can make thirty games into a season, but it's not a hot-take decision on my end as GM. The outcome of the season plays a role. The goal is to win the championship — what are the factors of it happening or not happening.

Preliminary discussions took place on contracts, and we have decided to wait until after the season. We respect the right to do so. And will be ready, hopefully after a parade.

---

**HAL RICHMOND:** After a parade.

That's a sentence with weight. I want to leave it sitting here for a second before I move on, because it's been a long time since a GM said that to me in this room. The last parade was thirty-five. The men we're talking about — Vinnie, Isley, Mark, Benji, Martin — were the ones who walked the floats. Whatever else this conversation is about, you just put on the record that the goal of this season is to give them one more.

I'll take that to print.

And it's the reason I want to ask you the next thing, Mike. Because two stories landed in the same paper this cycle. One was about your players. The other was about you.

At a tech-expo panel — and I watched the clip — Elias Varek answered a question about whether his ownership group would recruit you to run the Oaks. He answered it with five words, on the record. "I will absolutely be making that phone call." Velez ran it on the front page. The question was framed as a joke. The answer was not a joke.

You've now told me, in this room, that you're walking toward a parade with the men who built this franchise. The question I have to ask the city you're walking with is: has Varek made the call? And if he hasn't — when it comes, are you the man who answers it, or the man who tells him to wait until after the season the way you just told me Benji and Isley and Martin will?

---

**MIKE PAULSON:** What a great man Elias is, and what a wonderful thing it is that Oakland is where he chose for his legacy. I've had the pleasure of meeting Elias on a few occasions. Civis Systems' impact I tie as one of the main reasons Oakland is the destination it is now.

I have not received that call.

After the success I was able to have with the Bulls last season — in my first attempt — it did show the system translates. The system can be somewhat autonomous. The A's are best in the MLB even with my time split with the NBA. But the idea of an expansion franchise is a different animal.

---

**HAL RICHMOND:** Mike — I want to ask you one more before I let you out of this chair.

You said two things in this room that I'm going to print next to each other. You said the system can be somewhat autonomous — autonomous enough that you could run the Bulls last season and the A's are still the best team in baseball. And you said an expansion franchise is a different animal. Those are both about *how* you do this work. They're not about why.

The thing I keep coming back to is what your card says about you in the archive — that you tie community-building as your primary legacy. And what I watch your veterans do. Vinnie running the academy on Mandela Parkway that's going to outlive his bat. Mark sitting in city meetings as Player Rep and Community Liaison while he plays first base on a contract through forty-three. Isley letting a film crew into the basement for eight months. Martin showing up at third for six and a half years and never asking anybody to write about him.

That's not a system. That's a culture you grew. And the men inside it grew it with you.

So here's the question I want to ask, Mike, and then I'll get out of your way. When you talk about the system translating — does the *culture* translate? Or is the reason expansion is a different animal that you can hand somebody Civis Systems' data and a stadium and a brand, and you still can't hand them the thing that made Vinnie open an academy and Mark go to city meetings and Isley walk into the basement footage room?

What does the parade look like, from the GM's chair, when the floats roll past Mandela Parkway and the academy is still there and the players who built it are walking next to Reyna and Coles and Mesa for the last time?

---

**MIKE PAULSON:** First off — Hal, I've been reading your work since I started living sports as a kid.

As far as culture goes: the system travels and the culture adapts. To your point, Vinnie Keane is a one of one.

Over the years with the A's, the core that comes to mind is mainly positional, and really only Dillon is the pitcher. So on one hand we settled nicely on position players, and pitching we had to adapt. As you adapt, you lock on to the culture core.

The parade is through the new paved streets of the Baylight District, through Civis Field — The Town.

---

**HAL RICHMOND:** Mike — thank you. Print run is Wednesday.

I'll walk myself out.

---
