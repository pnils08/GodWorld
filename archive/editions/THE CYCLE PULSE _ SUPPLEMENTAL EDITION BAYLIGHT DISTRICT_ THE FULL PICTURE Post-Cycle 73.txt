OAKLAND SEES ITS FUTURE: FIRST RENDERINGS OF BAYLIGHT DISTRICT RELEASED

$2.8 billion waterfront vision takes shape as planning review begins

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic

OAKLAND — For three years, it was a phrase. A promise. A political football.

Now it’s a picture.

The A’s ownership group released the first official renderings of the Baylight District this week, and Oakland finally sees what “coming home” looks like: a 35,000-seat stadium rising from the waterfront at dusk, its lights reflecting off the harbor. Residential towers framing the skyline. A marina dotted with sailboats. The San Francisco skyline shimmering across the Bay in the distance. Crowds filling a central plaza that didn’t exist a rendering ago.

It’s not real yet. But it looks like it could be.

“The Baylight District represents Oakland’s claim on its own future,” the project narrative reads. “A waterfront transformed from industrial remnant to civic anchor.”

The numbers are staggering: 65 acres. $2.8 billion in private investment. 3,200 residential units — 56% of them affordable. 485,000 square feet of retail and commercial space. A 180-slip marina. Twelve acres of public parks. A new ferry terminal connecting Oakland to San Francisco, Alameda, and Richmond.

And zero public funding required.

The financing structure that Mike Paulson outlined in his presser is now detailed in black and white. The ownership group is pursuing tax exemptions and infrastructure credits rather than direct taxpayer investment. The stadium, the towers, the retail — all funded by private capital. The environmental remediation of the harbor land is bonded by ownership.

“The political question shifts,” the project document states, “from ‘should taxpayers fund this?’ to ‘what does Oakland require in return for the land and the permits?’”

The answer is a community benefits package unprecedented in Bay Area stadium history: 45% local hire for construction, 35% for permanent jobs, 30% of retail leases reserved for Oakland small businesses, 100% public waterfront access, and a $2.5 million annual community fund overseen by a citizen-majority board.

The City Planning Committee begins review in Cycle 74. Environmental impact studies will run through Cycle 82. Public comment opens in Cycle 78. If all approvals hold, construction starts Year 3. Opening Day happens Year 4.

The A’s come home.

A STADIUM ON THE WATER — AND A NEIGHBORHOOD AROUND IT

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

The first thing you notice is the light.

The Baylight District rendering captures the development at twilight — that golden hour when the Bay turns copper and the city starts to glow. The stadium sits at the heart of the frame, its open-air bowl illuminated, the field a perfect green rectangle visible from above. You can almost hear the crowd.

But look past the stadium. That’s where the vision lives.

**The Towers:** Four residential high-rises (18-32 stories) frame the stadium like bookends. Their balconies face the water. Their ground floors open onto retail promenades. These aren’t just apartments — they’re the reason the district functions on non-game days.

**The Plaza:** The central gathering space in the foreground pulses with pedestrian traffic even in a static image. Palm trees line walkways. People cluster near what appears to be an outdoor dining area. This is the “Central Plaza” from the project documents — one of three major public spaces designed for year-round programming.

**The Marina:** Boats fill the harbor — sailboats, yachts, smaller vessels. The 180-slip marina includes space for 40-80 foot boats, plus a dedicated kayak and paddleboard launch. The 1.2-mile waterfront promenade curves along the water’s edge, connecting it all.

**The Bridge:** A pedestrian crossing spans the marina, linking the retail district to the waterfront parks. Behind it, the Bay Bridge is visible in the twilight haze — a reminder that this isn’t fantasy architecture. It’s Oakland.

**The Skyline:** San Francisco glimmers across the water. The stadium’s orientation is deliberate: home plate faces the Bay, outfield views toward the city. Every seat in the upper deck will see that skyline.

The rendering does what renderings do — it sells a dream. But the details match the documents. The scale feels right. The integration of stadium, housing, retail, and waterfront isn’t conceptual anymore.

It’s a picture you can walk into. In three years, Oakland might.

Names Index: Maria Keen (Reporter)

THE PIPELINE: HOW OAKLAND KIDS GET BAYLIGHT JOBS

City unveils Youth Apprenticeship Program tied to stadium construction

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

The 45% local hire commitment is a number on paper. The Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline is how it becomes real.

The city unveiled a comprehensive workforce development program this week, designed to train Oakland residents — specifically young people aged 16-24 — for careers in construction trades. The explicit goal: ensure that when Baylight shovels hit dirt, Oakland workers are first in line.

“The 45% local hire commitment means nothing if Oakland doesn’t have 45% ready to work,” the program document states. “The Pipeline makes sure they are.”

**THE STRUCTURE**

Three tracks, three entry points:

*Track 1 — High School Pathway (Ages 16-18):* After-school and summer intensive programs at five OUSD schools: Oakland Tech, Skyline, Fremont, Castlemont, and McClymonds. Students complete an 18-24 month program earning pre-apprenticeship certificates, OSHA-10 safety credentials, and industry certifications.

*Track 2 — Young Adult Pathway (Ages 18-24):* Full-time paid training — 32 hours per week at $600/week stipend — for 12 months. Graduates receive registered apprenticeship placement and union referral.

*Track 3 — Direct Placement (Ages 18+):* Job matching with Baylight contractors for graduates of Tracks 1 and 2, or those with equivalent credentials.

**THE TRADES**

All participants rotate through four or more trades before specializing:

- Carpentry (4-year apprenticeship, $52/hr journeyman wage)
- Electrical (5-year, $58/hr)
- Plumbing/Pipefitting (5-year, $56/hr)
- Ironworkers (4-year, $54/hr)
- Laborers (2-year, $42/hr)
- Operating Engineers (3-year, $55/hr)
- Sheet Metal (5-year, $54/hr)
- Cement Masons (3-year, $46/hr)

Training partners include NorCal Carpenters, IBEW Local 595, UA Local 342, and other Building Trades Council affiliates.

**THE FUNDING**

$12.5 million total from public-private sources:

- City Workforce Development Fund: $3.2M
- State Career Technical Education Grant: $2.8M
- Peralta Community College (in-kind): $1.5M
- A’s Ownership Group: $2.0M (tied to Baylight CBA)
- Building Trades Training Trust: $1.2M
- Federal Apprenticeship Grant: $1.8M

**THE TARGETS**

By Year 3 (construction start):

- 280 high school students enrolled
- 200 young adults in training
- 180 pre-apprenticeship completions
- 150 registered apprenticeship placements
- 400+ direct Baylight job placements

Demographic targets include 70% Oakland residents, 50% from high-unemployment zip codes (West Oakland, East Oakland, Fruitvale), 25% women (stretch goal — current trades average is 4%), and 15% formerly incarcerated or system-involved youth.

**THE SUPPORT**

Training alone doesn’t create retention. The Pipeline includes:

- $600/week stipends during training
- Tool purchase grants (up to $1,200)
- AC Transit passes and gas cards
- Childcare subsidies
- Dedicated case managers (1:40 ratio)
- Financial literacy courses
- Driver’s license prep and fee assistance

**THE TIMELINE**

The program launches Year 3, Q1 — eighteen months before Baylight construction begins. A 17-year-old at Oakland Tech today could be a second-year electrical apprentice pulling wire at the stadium site by Year 4.

That’s not abstract benefit. That’s a career with a pension and a union card.

THE APPROVAL GAUNTLET: WHAT HAPPENS NEXT

Eight bodies, ten cycles, one vote

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic

The rendering is beautiful. The numbers are impressive. The community benefits are unprecedented.

None of it matters until the votes come in.

The Baylight District faces an approval pathway that will test every coalition Mayor Santana has built. Here’s the gauntlet:

**CYCLE 74 — Planning Committee Review**

The City Planning Committee conducts initial review of land use, zoning, and development standards. This is procedural but not automatic. Committee members will scrutinize density, traffic projections, and environmental baselines. A negative recommendation here doesn’t kill the project, but it creates political headwinds.

**CYCLES 75-82 — Environmental Impact Review**

State CEQA requirements plus Oakland Environmental Services review. This is where projects die slow deaths. Traffic studies. Air quality modeling. Harbor ecosystem assessments. Soil contamination reports. Each study is a potential delay. Each delay is a potential exit ramp.

**CYCLES 78-80 — Public Comment Period**

Community forums across Oakland neighborhoods. This is where the political temperature gets measured. Support from Jack London and Downtown is assumed. The question is whether West Oakland, Fruitvale, and East Oakland — neighborhoods that have watched development promises come and go — show up in favor or opposition.

**CYCLE 83 — Final Council Vote**

Nine council members. Five votes needed.

Current landscape: Mayor Santana is the project’s champion. The Oakland Progressive Party caucus (led by Janae Rivers) has expressed cautious support contingent on community benefits enforcement. The Conservative Reform Coalition (led by Elliott Crane, currently hospitalized) has raised concerns about tax exemptions reducing city revenue.

Council President Ramon Vega has not publicly committed.

**THE WILD CARDS**

- Marcus Osei’s absence: The Deputy Mayor for Economic Development was the city’s lead negotiator. His hospitalization after a traffic collision removes a key advocate from the table. Laila Cortez is handling interim duties, but institutional knowledge takes time to transfer.
- Elliott Crane’s absence: The Business Caucus Leader’s injury removes a potential swing vote — or a potential opponent — from active lobbying.
- Migration drift: At -45 this cycle, Oakland is losing residents faster than any point in recent memory. Does the promise of future jobs and housing outweigh present-day departures? Or does the timeline (Year 4 opening) feel too distant for a city bleeding population now?

**THE BOTTOM LINE**

The project has momentum. Private financing eliminates the taxpayer-subsidy debate. Community benefits are locked into the agreement structure. The rendering gives people something to believe in.

But eight cycles of review is a long time. A lot can change between now and Cycle 83.

THE PICTURE AND THE PROMISE

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports — Senior Historian

I’ve seen stadium renderings before.

I’ve seen the gleaming towers and the happy crowds and the sunset light that makes everything look like a postcard from a city that doesn’t exist. I’ve watched those renderings get filed away when the votes didn’t come, when the financing collapsed, when the politics shifted.

Renderings are promises made in pixels. They’re easy to make and easier to break.

But I’ve also seen what happens when the promise holds.

I was here when the Coliseum opened in 1966. I was here when the Raiders left, came back, and left again. I was here when the A’s became a dynasty and when the dynasty started to crack. I was here when Las Vegas made its offer and Oakland said no — or couldn’t say yes fast enough.

The Baylight rendering shows a stadium on the water. Home plate facing the Bay. The San Francisco skyline in the distance. Boats in the marina. People in the plaza. Light everywhere.

It’s beautiful. It’s also just a picture.

What makes it different this time? The money, for one. Private financing means no bond measures, no ballot fights, no “why should my taxes pay for millionaires” debates that have killed stadium deals from Oakland to St. Louis. The A’s are asking for tax exemptions, not tax dollars. That’s a different conversation.

The community benefits matter too. Fifty-six percent affordable housing in a city where affordability is a crisis. Forty-five percent local hire when unemployment in some zip codes approaches 15%. A citizen oversight board with actual authority. These aren’t afterthoughts — they’re load-bearing walls.

And the Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline might be the most important document in the entire package. A rendering shows you a stadium. The Pipeline shows you the 17-year-old at Oakland Tech who becomes the electrician who wires it. That’s not a picture. That’s a person.

I’ve been covering Oakland sports for forty years. I’ve learned not to trust renderings.

But I’ve also learned that sometimes — not often, but sometimes — the picture and the promise turn out to be the same thing.

I want to believe this is one of those times.

JOBS FOR WHO?

By P Slayer | Fan Columnist

Let me tell you about my cousin Darnell.

Darnell is 22. He lives in East Oakland. He graduated from Castlemont in 2022 with decent grades and no idea what to do next. He’s worked warehouse jobs, delivery gigs, a stint at a fast food place that laid him off when hours got cut. He’s not lazy. He’s not stupid. He just never got connected to anything that connected to something else.

The Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline document says Castlemont is one of the five schools in the program. It says young adults aged 18-24 can enroll in full-time paid training — $600 a week, 32 hours, twelve months. It says graduates get registered apprenticeship placement with Building Trades unions. It says 50% of slots are reserved for high-unemployment zip codes.

Darnell’s zip code qualifies.

The document says a laborer apprenticeship is two years, journeyman wage $42 an hour. Electrical is five years, $58 an hour. By Year 4, when that stadium in the rendering opens, Darnell could be pulling wire or laying concrete or operating machinery — with a union card and a pension plan and a path to the middle class that warehouse gigs will never provide.

That’s the promise.

Here’s my question: Will anyone tell Darnell?

Because these programs exist in documents and press releases and government websites that nobody in East Oakland reads. They require applications and intake processes and case managers who are overworked and underfunded. They assume people know where to look.

The Pipeline targets 400+ direct Baylight placements by Year 3. That’s not a big number for a city this size. That’s less than one graduating class at one of those five schools. If the outreach works, if the support services hold, if the training quality delivers — it could change lives.

But the history of workforce programs in Oakland is a history of good intentions and mediocre execution. The documents always look great. The follow-through is where things fall apart.

I want this to work. I want Darnell to get a call, show up to an intake session, start a career. I want the Baylight District to be the thing that finally connects Oakland kids to Oakland jobs.

But wanting isn’t enough. Someone has to make sure the Darnells actually hear about it.
