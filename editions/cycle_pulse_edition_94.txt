# Civis Systems Field — The Town

**Civis Systems names the A's new ballpark, Varek answers a joke about hiring Mike Paulson with five words on the record, and one private network now shapes Oakland's two pro franchises and the district they'll play in.**

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

---

Elias Varek calls his company a "systems intelligence" firm. It models transit patterns, construction bottlenecks, emergency response routes, utilities. Builds digital mirrors of how cities actually move.

At a tech-expo panel this cycle, someone asked whether Varek's ownership group—the new backers of Oakland's NBA expansion team—would recruit Mike Paulson, the A's general manager, to run the Oaks front office too.

Varek laughed. "I will absolutely be making that phone call."

It's not idle posturing. The three largest professional-sports bets in Oakland this cycle—the Baylight A's ballpark nearing completion, the Oaks expansion announcement, and this week's naming of that ballpark as Civis Systems Field—all flow through one man's private network. And that network isn't based on sports. It's based on what Varek has spent a decade building: a view of how cities work.

Civis Systems, founded by Varek and headquartered in West Oakland, sells what urban planners have always lacked—a complete, real-time model of civic infrastructure. Forty employees. Sixty million in annual revenue. Already embedded in Baylight Phase II planning. The firm has become indispensable to how this city schedules growth.

The ballpark naming—"Civis Systems Field — The Town"—codifies what locals already call it. The nickname resonates on purpose. Varek didn't name it after his company to advertise. He named it The Town to anchor a neighborhood, to give the A's ballpark (and eventually the Oaks arena, blocks away) a civic identity. The brand isn't the firm. It's the district.

But the naming reveals something sharper: one person's company infrastructure is now the city's naming infrastructure. Varek's systems model every inch of the Jack London corridor. His firm evaluated where an NBA arena could sit, what traffic patterns it would create, how it meshes with a baseball stadium five blocks north. Paulson, as A's GM, has watched the same infrastructure decisions that shape the team's neighborhood. The joke question at the panel wasn't actually a joke. It was the simplest way to ask: are the ownership ecosystems already merged?

Paulson won't comment. Varek's spokesman declined to clarify whether any recruitment has happened. But the architecture is visible. An ownership network that also controls the infrastructure modeling that lets an ownership network make site decisions. A founder whose day job is understanding how cities move, and whose night job is shaping where Oakland's top two sports franchises play.

The Oaks aren't the Varek plan. They're the Varek plan's commercial expression. When a privately held tech firm becomes the lens through which a city sees itself—becomes the naming authority, the arena-site evaluator, the man making calls about recruiting the A's GM—the question stops being sports. It becomes: who owns the view of how Oakland works?

Locals are already comfortable calling it The Town. It's unclear if they're naming a ballpark or a whole ecosystem.

# What The Town Looks Like

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

This cycle a private firm gave the A's new ballpark its name, and Oakland gave the ballpark its real name back. Civis Systems Field — The Town. Elias Varek's company built the model this city uses to plan its own infrastructure; this cycle that company also became the entity stamped on the largest civic structure rising in Jack London. At a tech-expo panel, Varek answered a joke about hiring the A's general manager to run the Oaks too with five words on the record: "I will absolutely be making that phone call." The joke was the most direct map anyone has offered of how much of Oakland's professional sports ecosystem now passes through one ownership network.

Read Jordan Velez on what that consolidation looks like up close. Read Carmen Delaine on the Transit Hub vote that finally happened — three cycles, one Mam-language scope addition, an 8-0 vote on the record that wasn't there last cycle and is the texture of what changed between them. Read Maria Keen on what 191 Stabilization Fund authorizations cleared and what the block outside Beverly Hayes's window still feels like. Read the Adams Point sanctuary that opened its doors without being asked. Read Hal Richmond on a television feature that arrived in time to watch its subject go seven for nine. Read P Slayer on the player we forgot to count.

The city moved this cycle. So did the texture of who owns what.

— M.C.

# The Vote That Was There

**Three cycles after Carmen Solis stood at public comment, the deliverable arrived: a twelve-page scope addition with budget figures and a start date. The vote was 8-0.**

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

---

The stack of printed comments was three inches thick. Soria Dominguez set it on the dais table before the session opened and didn't move it for two hours.

She'd spent the week between cycles pulling every written submission from the Mam-language input sessions, cross-referencing them with the professional interpretation log, and producing a twelve-page scope addition that attached to the Transit Hub Phase II application: multilingual wayfinding, $85,000 to $120,000 budget range, professional interpretation services beginning in the third week of this cycle. Every commitment sourced, every dollar tied to a line. It was, in the language of city planning, a deliverable. In the language of the Fruitvale community-input process that produced it, it was the answer to a specific question asked three cycles earlier and not answered until now.

Carmen Solis had asked that question. She'd stood at the public comment podium last cycle and laid out what was missing from the Transit Hub framework: no Mam-language access, no professional interpretation, no path for the community that had organized the most sustained input of any group in the process. The record from that session was the beginning of a thread. Soria Dominguez's stack of papers was the end of it.

---

District 2's Leonard Tran had held a conditional vote last cycle. He said so plainly in committee — he would not support Phase II until the multilingual access question had a real answer, not a commitment to revisit. When the C93 council session collapsed without reaching the item, Tran's condition remained open. It was, as this desk noted at the time, a vote that wasn't there.

What changed was the documentation. Not a promise — a scope addition with budget figures and a start date. Tran confirmed his yes vote before the session opened. By the time Council President Ramon Vega called the item, the math was already done.

Vega named the procedural path clearly: the scope addition would be incorporated into the application before it advanced to the regional transit authority. Not as an amendment to be negotiated later. As part of the base document. It was the kind of procedural specificity that makes a vote durable.

---

The final count was 8-0, with one council member absent.

For a $230 million infrastructure project three cycles in the making, the margin was less surprising than the texture of what produced it. The Transit Hub has moved through committee hearings, funding framework negotiations, and a community benefits agreement process that is still ongoing. What held the vote last cycle wasn't a policy disagreement — it was a gap in documentation that one planning department official and one community advocate had identified and, between cycles, closed.

Roberto Iglesias, the Fruitvale taquería owner who has run his shop on the same stretch for eleven years and was seated on the Transit Hub Oversight Committee last cycle, watched the vote from the third row of the chambers. He didn't speak during public comment. He'd said what he needed to say over the previous three cycles of process.

"The project was always going to pass," he said after the vote. "The question was what it was going to be when it did."

---

Vega closed the session with brief remarks. The next steps were administrative — application submission, regional coordination, the first construction phase timeline that now has a real start point attached to it. He didn't editorialize. He moved to the next item.

That procedural rhythm is the part this desk covers that rarely makes it into the headline: the meeting that happens after the meeting, the application that goes out the door, the scope addition that gets incorporated as base text rather than side agreement. Three cycles of community input, one deliverable, one vote. The work that was done between cycles is what made the vote that was there.

# West Oakland: Cleared and Still

**Webb cleared 191 Stabilization Fund authorizations. Beverly Hayes got her check. The block outside her window hasn't moved.**

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Neighborhoods

---

Beverly Hayes was sitting on her front stoop on Telegraph, a mug of coffee in one hand and a stack of mail in the other, when I asked her what "cleared" meant.

She'd been waiting for an answer to that question for three cycles now—ever since the Stabilization Fund money came through, ever since she got her notice that the relocation assistance would cover her move, ever since the mayor stood at the council podium and said the backlog was gone. One hundred ninety-one authorizations processed. Forty-eight of them for people like her, people whose landlords had been squeezing them out, people who needed the city to say: we see you, and we're going to help.

"It means I got the check," she said, and laughed—not a happy laugh, the kind you make when a joke lands but doesn't feel funny. "Doesn't mean the building next door is open again. Doesn't mean the retail on this corner feels like it did two years ago."

Beverly has been the community director at West Oakland Community Center for six years. Before that, tenant rights advocacy. Lived here longer than that. She knows this neighborhood the way you know a person you've stopped trying to understand and started just accepting—all the contradictions held at once. The pride. The exhaustion. The storefronts that are still dark, the sidewalks that are still quieter than they should be.

The mail she was holding was the Stab Fund paperwork, finally processed. She's not moving yet. Doesn't know when she will. The money is real now, but the neighborhood still feels the same way it did two cycles ago, when nothing was cleared yet—like something is holding still, waiting for the next thing.

"The city says we cleared it," she said. "But clearing a backlog and clearing a street are two different things."

I walked with her down Telegraph, past the boarded windows and the hand-painted signs advertising the businesses that are still there. Past the coffee shop where the morning regulars know each other by name. Past the community center itself, where Beverly will keep showing up every morning, whether the fund cleared or not.

"I'm not angry," she said as we turned the corner. "I'm just tired. And the neighborhood is too."

That's what cleared feels like in West Oakland right now—like a backlog has moved from one column to another, and the neighborhood is still standing on its stoop, waiting to see what happens next.

# Adams Point Opens Doors

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Neighborhoods

---

The Tuesday morning light came through the stained glass at the east end of Adams Point United Methodist, filling the sanctuary in blocks of blue and amber. Rev. Daniel Han stood in the aisle, watching two teenagers move a folding table from the fellowship hall. One of them—a girl in a green jacket—asked him something I couldn't hear. He nodded, smiled, pointed toward the kitchen.

"We opened up this cycle because we looked around and saw people who needed to know we were here," he said when he came back to where I was standing. "That's not complicated."

It's the second cycle in a row the church has set up community support—a place to sit, hot water for tea, food donated by neighbors and congregants, someone to talk to if you need to. The Methodists have been at this corner in Adams Point for 132 years. The building knows what it's supposed to do.

The girl in the green jacket—I never got her name—was helping because her grandmother asked her to. Her grandmother had called the church on Monday morning. By Tuesday at ten, they had moved tables and stacked chairs and organized three boxes of canned goods a neighbor had left on the doorstep. Rev. Han said the neighbor hadn't been a member in years. He'd just heard the church was opening and wanted to be part of it.

That's how it happens here. The decision gets made quietly. The word spreads. People show up to help, or to ask for help, or both. The Methodist congregation is three hundred strong. On Tuesday morning, it felt much smaller—maybe thirty people coming through over a few hours—but the light through the stained glass made everything feel like it was the right size.

"We're progressive, we're affirming, we're open," Rev. Han said. "But sometimes I think people just need to know they can walk through the door. This is the cycle for that."

The girl in the green jacket came back to the fellowship hall with a stack of cups. She was moving fast, purposeful. Her grandmother watched from a chair in the corner, knitting something small and blue. Neither of them spoke. They didn't need to.

By noon, the tables were set. The kettle was hot. The door was open.

# Kelley In Focus, Kelley Catches Fire

**The television feature aired this cycle. The same cycle Kelley went seven-for-nine against Baltimore in the A's first series loss.**

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Senior Writer

---

There is a particular cruelty to good timing.

The producers of *Isley Kelley: In Focus* spent — you can imagine this — months assembling the thing. Archival footage, the kind that sits in boxes and on hard drives in stadium basements. The 2032 MVP acceptance. The 2034 postseason, when he carried a lineup that had no business carrying itself that deep into October. The 2035 curtain call in the ninth inning of a game that had already been decided, the crowd asking for one more look simply because they needed it. Interviews with teammates who tried not to cry on camera and mostly failed. A television feature, built as a tribute to what has been.

It aired this cycle. The same cycle Isley Kelley went seven for nine across a series against Baltimore — one home run, five runs batted in — in a series the A's lost for the first time since the season opened.

You cannot plan that. You can only watch it happen and wonder what it means.

---

What it means, I think, is this: we have been rehearsing a goodbye we do not yet have to say.

Three times this franchise has given Isley Kelley the Most Valuable Player Award — twenty thirty-two, twenty thirty-four, twenty thirty-five. Four hundred and forty-seven home runs. Two thousand, three hundred and ninety-eight hits, which is twenty-six short of a number that means something, a number people will talk about when they assemble the career in retrospect. Ninety-two point one career WAR. These are the canonical measurements of a player the franchise has signaled it will not bring back after this season. The contract runs through twenty forty-one. After that, the front office has made its posture clear. The truesource numbers support the decision, probably. The heart does not care about truesource numbers.

I have been covering baseball in this city for forty-four years. In that time I have watched a great many men play their last games without knowing they were their last games. That is the common experience — the body gives out, or the roster moves without sentiment, and one morning a player is no longer a player and neither he nor the crowd was properly prepared. What is unusual about this cycle, this franchise, is the foreknowledge. We have been given time.

*Isley Kelley: In Focus* is a document of what that time contains. It is a television feature, which means it has a shape, a beginning and a middle and an end, a score underneath the old footage that signals when to feel things. It is the kind of production that arrives when a career has achieved sufficient magnitude to warrant the retrospective treatment while the man is still playing. That is the point I want to stay with: still playing. Still hitting. Still going seven for nine against a Baltimore staff that had every statistical reason to believe it could handle him.

The feature aired. He caught fire. The timing is not a cruelty. It is a correction.

---

Somewhere in those nine at-bats, the television narrative and the actual season folded into each other and produced something neither could produce alone. You were watching a man whose career was being assessed in past tense while he was doing the thing the career is made of, in present tense, in real time. The bat working exactly as it has always worked. The swing still that swing — the slight pause, the hands staying back, the transfer of weight that has looked the same since he was twenty-two years old and nobody was building television features about him yet.

I noticed, watching the numbers from Baltimore come in, that I kept forgetting which tense I was supposed to be using. This is appropriate. He has not finished.

---

Beneath him in the lineup, in the late innings of this same series, Vinnie Keane is riding a streak of his own — eight straight games with a hit, which is the kind of quiet thing that gets noted in parentheses when the headline belongs to someone else. I note it here because Keane and Kelley are both in their final season with this franchise, and watching them in this configuration — one catching fire, one sustaining a quiet flame — you understand something about how dynasties actually operate. The accumulation of parallel excellences that the box score can only partially capture.

But the television feature was about Kelley. And Kelley gave the television feature something it could not have scripted.

---

Two thousand three hundred and ninety-eight hits. Twenty-six short of a number.

I want to be careful not to make that number load-bearing in a way it isn't. Baseball history is full of players who finished careers with totals that ended in odd numerals and were remembered clearly anyway. The career is not the milestone. The career is what the career actually was.

What this career actually was, is: three MVP awards won against competition that was trying to prevent him from winning them. A postseason record built in October games that meant something. A presence in this lineup that changed what pitchers were willing to do against the men batting around him. The kind of player who altered the shape of opposing pitching staffs for a decade.

The television feature assembled that. Cut it together. Put music under it.

And then he went out and played baseball.

---

There will be a time — later in this season, or at its end — when the farewell tone is the only appropriate tone. When the present-tense corrective no longer applies, when the bat has truly been set down. I will write that piece when that time comes. I have been writing farewell pieces for this franchise since before some of the current players were born, and I know how to do it.

This is not that piece.

This is the piece about a television crew spending months building a document of a career and then watching the subject of that document go seven for nine against a live major league pitching staff. This is the piece about the particular strangeness of a farewell season in which the goodbye keeps getting interrupted by the game itself.

Forty-four years of watching baseball. I have learned to be grateful for the interruptions.

The bat is still working. The series was lost. He caught fire anyway.

Pay attention while you still can.

# The Let-Walks Coming

**Kelley gets his sendoff. Aitken's contract runs through 2043. Martin Richards — six and a half years, .297, perfect at third — is the player we forgot to count.**

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

I've been trying to figure out how to write this piece for two weeks. I keep starting it and stopping. I keep thinking I'm being a baby about it, that this is how the game works, that I know better. I do know better. It doesn't help.

Here's what I know: Isley Kelley and Martin Richards are almost certainly playing their last season in green and gold. The front office isn't going to say that out loud. They don't have to. You can read it in how they're operating — the youth push, the money committed forward, the quiet. When contracts expire and ownership is this focused on tomorrow, the guys whose tomorrow has already come tend to walk.

And I don't want to talk about Kelley. That's Hal's territory and Hal will do it proper, the way it deserves. Kelley gets forty-four years of Hal Richmond loving this franchise with his whole chest, and that's exactly right. Kelley has earned that.

I want to talk about Martin Richards.

---

Martin Richards is thirty-one years old and he has played six and a half seasons at third base for the Oakland A's. In that time he has won no awards. He has been named to no All-Star Games that I can remember making noise about. There is no moment in the last six cycles that you would describe as a Martin Richards moment — no walk-off, no series-defining play that got replayed until you could reconstruct it in your sleep.

He hit .297 this season. He posted a 6.8 WAR. His fielding percentage is perfect. Perfect. As in, the number you type when you want to show someone what the ceiling looks like.

I looked it up and I cannot find a single dedicated piece about Martin Richards in the Tribune archive. Not one. We covered the pitching staff seventeen different ways. We wrote Kelley features and Aitken civic profiles and Danny Horn streak pieces. We did not write Martin Richards.

That's on us. That's on me.

---

Here's the part that makes me want to put my head through a wall: the front office isn't wrong about the youth.

Frank Reyna is twenty years old and hits from both sides of the plate and has the kind of gap-to-gap power that makes scouts put down their coffee and pick up their phones. Travis Coles is twenty-one years old with a 1.13 ERA and he is what happens when a franchise just gets lucky enough to draft someone who is, genuinely, that good. Carmen Mesa threw a complete game shutout as a rookie. Ernesto Quintero is twenty-four and has the kind of power projection that makes front offices think about the next ten years instead of the next two.

The depth is there. Or something close to enough. The argument for letting Richards walk — for letting Kelley walk — is not delusional. It is actually coherent roster construction. The money has to go somewhere and it's going to Coles and Reyna and the next thing.

I know this. I accept it in the analytical part of my brain.

---

But here's what the analytical part of my brain can't account for: Martin Richards has been perfect, quietly, every single day, for six and a half years. He has never made a throwing error that cost us a game I remember. He has never popped up to end an inning in a moment that made me want to throw something. He has just been there, at third base, doing the work, and we — the fans, the writers, the people who are supposed to be paying attention — never gave him the flowers while he was standing right in front of us.

And now he's going to walk.

He's going to walk and some other team is going to figure out what he is. Some other team is going to build around that 6.8 WAR and that .297 average and that perfect fielding percentage and they're going to wonder how Oakland let him go. And we're going to be watching Reyna develop and Coles pitch and Mesa go out there every fifth day, and it'll be good, it might even be great, but somewhere in there there'll be a moment when a ball gets through the left side of the infield and I'll think: Martin Richards would have had that.

---

The franchise face question is not really about faces. It's about what you're willing to count. Kelley is a face because we all decided to count him — the three MVPs, the 447 home runs, the two rings, the number they're going to retire with the city watching. We counted him out loud, repeatedly, for fifteen years.

Richards is the guy we didn't count. Not because he wasn't worth counting. Because he did his job so well he became part of the scenery, and we forgot that scenery is made of something.

I'm not saying the front office is making a mistake. I'm saying there's a cost to how we do this that we don't put on the record. We let Richards walk and we write the Reyna profile and the Coles contract explainer and that's what the Tribune looks like next spring. And somewhere in Jack London Square somebody asks who plays third base now and we explain the youth movement again and it makes sense, it really does, it's all going to be fine.

But we should have written the Richards piece when we had time. We should have counted him out loud before the number ran out.

---

The let-walks are coming. Kelley gets his sendoff, the way he should. And Martin Richards — six and a half years, perfect at third, .297 — is probably going to leave quietly, the way he did everything else.

Oakland does not deserve to forget him this fast.

---

*P Slayer has been covering the A's since before most of the current roster was born. He lives in his feelings about it.*

# Reyna's Two-Homer Night

**A's beat Atlanta 6-4. Travis Coles works seven, gets to five wins. Frank Reyna goes deep twice. JR Rosado adds a solo home run on the way to settling in.**

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune A's Beat

---

Frank Reyna walked to the plate in the fourth inning with a runner on first, the A's trailing by a run, and hit a fastball into the left-center gap so cleanly that the center fielder didn't bother moving. The ball carried, cleared the wall, and landed in the third row. Two outs, two runs, lead erased. Reyna stood at second base — briefly, for the half-second it took to realize the ball had gone out — and then jogged the bases with the deliberate calm of someone who had been doing this for years and not someone who had his first major league hit earlier this cycle.

The Oakland A's beat the Atlanta Braves six to four at the Dimond on Wednesday behind a performance from Reyna that will be cited by minor league instructors for a long time: three hits, two home runs, two runs batted in, from a twenty-year-old right fielder who came up from Double-A Midland as an afterthought to a crowded outfield depth chart.

That the A's have the luxury of treating a two-homer night from a twenty-year-old as structural depth rather than headline news says something about where this franchise is right now. Reyna is the reinforcement. The centerpiece of the evening, the win itself, was Travis Coles.

Coles threw seven innings, allowed three earned runs on five hits, and struck out nine Braves hitters in a performance that was, by his own recently established standards, moderate. His season ERA sits at one point one three. He has won five games. He is twenty-one years old and in the second year of a forty-six million, two-year contract that looks more reasonable every start. Against Atlanta he worked efficiently through the middle innings without the electric stuff of some earlier starts, which is its own kind of development — a starter learning to win games when he is not at his best.

JR Rosado — Left Fielder, Las Vegas Aviators (AAA), recently called up — added a solo home run in the sixth that pushed the lead to three and removed whatever suspense remained. Rosado had been quiet since the call-up and arrived at the at-bat with the look of a hitter pressing; the home run appeared to settle something. He finished the night one for three.

The Braves scored twice in the eighth against the bullpen, which made the final score less comfortable than the game felt, but the A's entered the ninth ahead by two and stayed there.

The box score from a Wednesday game in the middle of a long stretch of home dates does not carry the same weight as a playoffs box score. But there is a version of the box score from this game that will be preserved in clubhouse memory for a while: Reyna, 3-for-4, two home runs, a twenty-year-old in his first full cycle with the club performing at a level that requires no qualifier. Coles, seven innings, five wins, one point one three. Rosado, a home run when the team needed one.

The A's are building something, which is not news. But the shape of what they are building — the youth at the edges reinforcing the core, the pitching holding while the offense develops — became a little clearer on Wednesday night.

Dear Editor,

I heard the Stabilization Fund cleared 191 households. I heard it this week at the clinic and I was happy for my neighbors. My landlord hasn't called in three months, so maybe I'm in that clearance. But walking down International yesterday, I didn't see it. The storefronts are still dark. Mrs. Chen's corner store is still empty. The lot where the bodega was — still just a lot. The money came through and then what? If we cleared 191 people from eviction, who's moving back? Who's opening the shops? The newspaper says the neighborhood still feels stuck, and that's exactly right. Stuck is the word I have. The money didn't move us forward. It held us in place.

— Keisha Morris, 51, West Oakland, counselor

---

Dear Editor,

I own a restaurant in Fruitvale and I watched the council vote 8-0 to pass Phase II, and I was relieved. But Carmen Solis was right to push on the Mam-language access. My customers speak Mam. They shop on the avenue. They have kids going to school here. If the Transit Hub moves people and changes this neighborhood — and it will — but we don't translate the information, don't translate the new systems, we're leaving our people stranded. The vote was 8-0 because Carmen made them see it. I'm grateful that's on the record. I'm grateful my community didn't get overlooked again because someone was too tired to ask the right question.

— Miguel Santos, 54, Fruitvale, restaurant owner

---

Dear Editor,

I've been coming to Dario's Bar on Telegraph since 1998. Wednesday night the place was louder than I've ever heard it. Reyna hit two home runs — REYNA, not one of the stars — and the whole block knew it. The team's having something this season that feels real. Not just winning. Something that makes people want to be around each other. That matters. That's the thing you can't measure in stats. That's the thing I've been waiting twenty-eight years to feel again.

— David Okonkwo, 62, Lake Merritt, retired insurance adjuster
