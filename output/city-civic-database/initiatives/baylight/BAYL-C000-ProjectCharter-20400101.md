**Oakland Waterfront Redevelopment Initiative**  
*Status: Preliminary Agreement Reached — Cycle 73*

-----

## I. Project Overview

The Baylight District is a 65-acre mixed-use waterfront development anchored by a 35,000-seat baseball stadium designed to return the Oakland Athletics to their permanent home. The project transforms underutilized harbor land into a year-round destination combining professional sports, residential living, retail, dining, and public recreation.

**Target Opening:** Year 4 (Opening Day)  
**Current Phase:** Planning Committee Review (Cycle 74)

-----

## II. Financing Structure

**100% Private Capital** — No public funding required.

The ownership group is pursuing tax exemptions and infrastructure credits rather than direct public investment. This structure eliminates the political volatility that has derailed stadium negotiations in other markets.

|Component                |Funding Source                                |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
|Stadium Construction     |Ownership Group / Regional Investors          |
|Residential Towers       |Private Development Partners                  |
|Retail & Commercial      |Lease Commitments / Tenant Investment         |
|Marina & Public Spaces   |Development Fund + City Infrastructure Credits|
|Environmental Remediation|Ownership Group (bonded)                      |

**Estimated Total Investment:** $2.8 billion

## III. Development Components

### Stadium

- **Capacity:** 35,000 (expandable to 42,000 for concerts/events)
- **Design:** Open-air, waterfront-facing with retractable roof panels
- **Field Orientation:** Home plate facing the Bay; outfield views toward San Francisco skyline
- **Premium Seating:** 45 luxury suites, 4,200 club seats
- **Concourse:** 360-degree open concourse with sightlines to water

### Residential

- **Total Units:** 3,200
- **Affordable Housing:** 1,800 units (56% of total) — deed-restricted
- **Market Rate:** 1,400 units across 4 high-rise towers (18-32 stories)
- **Unit Mix:** Studio (15%), 1BR (40%), 2BR (35%), 3BR (10%)

### Retail & Commercial

- **Total Commercial Space:** 485,000 sq ft

|Category          |Square Footage|Tenant Count (Est.)         |
|------------------|--------------|----------------------------|
|Restaurants & Bars|145,000 sq ft |35-40 venues                |
|Retail Shops      |120,000 sq ft |50-60 storefronts           |
|Entertainment     |85,000 sq ft  |8-10 venues                 |
|Office/Flex Space |95,000 sq ft  |Co-working + small business |
|Grocery/Market    |40,000 sq ft  |1 anchor + specialty vendors|

**Ground-Floor Priority:** Local Oakland businesses receive first-right on 30% of retail leases.

### Marina & Waterfront

- **Marina Capacity:** 180 slips (40-80 ft vessels)
- **Public Boat Launch:** 2 ramps
- **Waterfront Promenade:** 1.2 miles continuous pedestrian/bike path
- **Public Plazas:** 3 major gathering spaces (Central Plaza, Harbor Green, Stadium Walk)
- **Kayak/Paddleboard Launch:** Dedicated non-motorized access point

### Parks & Open Space

- **Total Public Green Space:** 12 acres
- **Waterfront Parks:** 9 acres along harbor edge
- **Urban Plaza/Hardscape:** 3 acres (event-capable)
- **Tree Canopy:** 850+ new plantings (native species priority)

-----

## IV. Infrastructure & Access

### Transportation

- **BART Connection:** Dedicated pedestrian bridge to existing Lake Merritt station (0.4 mi)
- **Ferry Terminal:** New terminal with service to SF Ferry Building, Alameda, Richmond
- **Bus Rapid Transit:** AC Transit priority lanes on perimeter roads
- **Parking:** 4,500 structured spaces (below-grade and peripheral); 2,000 game-day overflow
- **Bike Infrastructure:** 600 secure bike parking spaces; Bay Trail integration

### Utilities

- **Power:** Microgrid with 40% renewable generation (rooftop solar + battery storage)
- **Water:** Recycled water system for irrigation and non-potable uses
- **Stormwater:** Bioswale network; zero runoff to Bay during typical storm events

## IX. Project Narrative

The Baylight District represents Oakland’s claim on its own future — a waterfront transformed from industrial remnant to civic anchor. The stadium is the centerpiece, but the district is designed to function year-round: restaurants and retail that serve residents and visitors whether or not there’s a game, public spaces that belong to the city regardless of what happens on the field.

The fully private financing model is deliberate. No bond measures. No public debt. The political question shifts from “should taxpayers fund this?” to “what does Oakland require in return for the land and the permits?” The community benefits package is the answer: housing, jobs, waterfront access, local business priority.

Opening Day, Year 4. The A’s come home.
