### Gone Dark: Three Cycles Without Power, and a City That Filed It Under Maintenance

**A grid failure on the Koreatown-Northgate edge has left KONO running on generators and candlelight for three cycles. City Hall has handled it as a utility ticket — never as the emergency a blacked-out district is. The corridor adapted in the dark. The rest of Oakland barely noticed it had gone quiet.**

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

---

The lights went out on KONO three cycles ago, and as far as City Hall is concerned, the neighborhood is still waiting on a work order.

It started at the substation that feeds the eastern edge of the Koreatown-Northgate corridor — the District 7 stretch where the barbecue houses and the late markets and the auto shops sit shoulder to shoulder. The power dropped and never fully came back. Pockets of the neighborhood have been dark ever since: storefronts on generators, apartments by candlelight, the corridor's neon signs cold for the first time anyone there can remember.

Michael has been photographing it. A KONO resident who works the corridor with a camera — he asked that his last name stay out of print — he has spent three cycles shooting a neighborhood lit by headlights and hurricane lamps. "It's beautiful and it's wrong at the same time," he said. "People are out on the sidewalk because their apartments are dark, talking to neighbors they never talked to. And the whole time you're thinking — somebody's supposed to be fixing this. It's been cycles."

The neighborhood improvised the way neighborhoods do. A church near 23rd threw open its doors as a charging station, extension cords snaking across the pews. The night market that runs the corridor switched to lantern light and kept running. The Korean grill houses, gas-fired, never missed a service — they became the warm rooms where the block gathered once the sun went down.

"We made it work because we always make it work," said Sahana, who has called KONO home for years. "But making it work isn't the same as somebody coming to help. Nobody came to help."

That is the part that should trouble City Hall. The outage was logged — as a utility matter, a maintenance item, the kind of ticket that waits its turn in a queue. What it was never logged as was an emergency: a whole district of Oakland sitting in the dark, cycle after cycle, while the city's attention stayed elsewhere. Oakland's sense of how KONO was faring went dark right along with the streetlights, and nobody downtown treated the silence as a siren.

The one thing that did move was people. Quietly, a few families at a time, KONO residents have decamped to relatives in brighter parts of the city — gone for now, meaning to come back when the power does. How many, and whether they all return, nobody can say. It is the only trace of the neighborhood that reached City Hall at all: not a crisis report, just the soft arithmetic of people leaving a place that had stopped working.

The Tribune asked City Hall when the KONO outage was first reported and why a multi-cycle blackout never rose above a routine ticket. No answer by press time. We asked the District 7 council office whether Warren Ashford, who represents the neighborhood, had been briefed. That went unanswered too.

Amal, another KONO resident, put it flat: "If the lights were out three cycles on the nice side of town, you think it'd be a work order? Somebody'd have been out here that first night."

That's the whole of it, in a sentence.

This paper won't pretend it knows everything happening on KONO's dark blocks — the crime, the businesses hanging on, the families weighing whether to stay. Three cycles of silence cost the city that picture, and cost this paper it too. But the cause is no mystery and no act of God. It is a neighborhood that lost power, and a city that decided, by never quite deciding, that the matter could wait.

The question for the people who run Oakland isn't complicated: how does a whole district sit in the dark for three cycles, and whose job is it to turn the lights back on?

So far, nobody's answered.

### The Neighborhood That Wasn't There

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

There is a particular silence a city makes when it stops paying attention to one of its own corners. Not the hush of a quiet street — the silence of an empty chair at a table where someone used to sit. KONO, the stretch east of Koreatown, has gone three cycles now without Oakland seeming to notice it's there. No one downtown can tell you how its people are faring. And the hardest discipline in this work is refusing to fill that silence with a story we'd find easier to tell.

So we didn't. Luis Navarro's front page sits with the silence and asks the only honest question it raises: how does a whole neighborhood go missing from its own city, and whose job was it to notice? Inventing a crisis to fill the hole would be its own kind of lie — and there are real people on those blocks who deserve better than to be turned into a story before anyone has even checked on them.

The rest of Oakland reads warm this fall. Downtown saw more trouble than it's used to — worth a closer look, not a panic. The city sent its crisis teams into the neighborhoods that wait longest when they call for help. The congregations carried the season the way they always have. A salon closed after thirty-two years; a health center finally got its ground; a young builder put a crew together. The city quietly turning back toward itself.

But I keep coming back to KONO. A neighborhood you can't see is a neighborhood you can't help. Somebody at City Hall should want to know why the lights went out on it — not for the record, but for the people living under them.

— M.C.

### The Sharpest Move in Five Cycles: Downtown Violent Crime Ticks Up

By Sgt. Rachel Torres | Bay Tribune Civic Desk

---

For five straight cycles, violent crime in Downtown barely twitched — the same low count, week after week, the kind of steady that stops drawing attention. This cycle it moved.

It is not a wave. Twelve incidents, and patrol still answering in under ten minutes — among the fastest response anywhere in the city. What caught the eye wasn't the size of the number; it was that Downtown almost never does this. The floor it had been sitting on for five cycles shifted, and a step up off a long flat line is worth a second look even when the number itself stays small.

D2 Councilmember Leonard Tran, who represents the district, said the number deserved acknowledgment without alarm.

"Downtown is where the event corridor runs and where the Telegraph pantry operates. It pulls foot traffic, and foot traffic creates friction," Tran said. "One cycle doesn't make a trend. It means we pay attention."

OPD's presence in the district has kept response times competitive, and nothing in the current reading suggests that's changed. The faster tier of response is holding. What moved is incident volume — enough of a step from where it had been sitting to register as the district's sharpest single-cycle change in recent memory.

This is the busiest pedestrian stretch in the city — concentrated retail, the event corridor, the services that pull people in from everywhere. More people in close quarters means more contact between them, and more contact occasionally means more trouble. The neighborhood mood is still good and the shops are still busy, which is part of why this cycle stands out: the backdrop doesn't explain it.

City officials said they are aware and watching. No additional officers have been assigned.

For now it is a small thing in a district with five cycles of calm behind it. Whether this was a bad week or the start of something is a question the next stretch will begin to answer.

### OARI Goes Citywide Into Oakland's Slowest-Response Districts

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

The city's alternative crisis-response program is now operating in every corner of Oakland where emergency response runs slowest.

That is the honest description of what the council authorized last cycle, 6-2-1, when it voted to take the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative citywide. OARI began as a pilot. The districts where it now operates at full strength — among them West Oakland, East Oakland, Fruitvale, and Temescal — are the ones where the city's emergency response has been running worst.

In West Oakland, average response times run past thirteen minutes. In East Oakland, longer still — the slowest measured in the city. Fruitvale and Temescal trail not far behind. The program's stated target is six minutes. The gap between where the numbers are and where the plan says they should land is not a rounding error. It is the bar OARI now has to clear.

"I've called for help and waited," said Dara, a forty-one-year-old home care worker who lives near 32nd and West Grand in West Oakland. "Waited and waited. Whatever they're doing different, I'll believe it when someone shows up fast."

The city has a plan. Eighty-one co-responders, phased in over twelve weeks, riding alongside the existing emergency dispatch across the new districts. The timeline and the headcount come from City Hall — the plan on record, as the administration laid it out. What it hasn't yet met is the only test that matters: whether someone in crisis waits less time for help.

That answer comes next cycle. Until then, it's a promise, not a result.

Mayor Avery Santana signed the expansion authorization. The six members who voted yes — Carter, Delgado, Rivers, and Mobley from the OPP; Tran and Vega as independents — framed it as the necessary next step. Vega's yes came with conditions attached to future funding.

The two no votes, Ashford and Chen of the Civic Reform Coalition, have questioned the program's readiness throughout. Crane, also CRC, was absent. Their position is not without a factual foundation: a program designed to move faster than what it replaces, deploying into the four districts where response has been slowest, is carrying the city's highest-stakes burden from day one.

Joyce Tanner, fifty-five, has lived on Fruitvale Avenue for more than twenty years. She watched the pilot operate in other parts of the city before it came to her neighborhood. "I wanted to see it work somewhere first," she said. "I think I saw enough. But the proof is still going to be: does somebody come, and does somebody come quick."

That is the only proof that matters now. The council has made its decision. The mayor has signed it. The co-responders are being hired and dispatched. West and East Oakland, the two districts carrying the worst response burden in the city, are now inside the program's mandate.

Whether any of it shortens the wait is something the next cycle will start to show — measured the only way that counts, in how fast the door opens after someone calls.

### Fourth Deadline Held: Health Center Closes on Its Temescal Parcel

By Dr. Lila Mezran | Bay Tribune Civic Desk

---

The Temescal Community Health Center cleared its fourth straight deadline this cycle, closing escrow on the parcel at Temescal Avenue and 47th Street where the building will eventually stand. It is a procedural milestone, not a ribbon-cutting — the site is secured, not built. Groundbreaking authorization is the next gate, with construction still cycles away.

But for a project that has missed nothing — architect contract, site declaration, state licensing application, and now the land itself, four deadlines in four cycles — the pattern is its own kind of news. Temescal is the neighborhood the city approved this $45 million center to serve. The center won't change anything there until it opens. What changed this cycle is that the ground under it is finally, legally, the city's to build on.

The clock the project is racing — its state licensing window — keeps running. So far, it hasn't lost a step.

### The Network That Holds: Oakland's Congregations Carry the Season

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

The smell of simmering dal hit me when I walked into the Bharat Mandir of the East Bay on Thornhill Drive at 7 p.m. on a Thursday, Halloween week, and I knew what I was walking into — not just a temple service, but the kind of evening where a city feeds itself without waiting for permission or policy or outside money to arrive.

Diwali was coming. The temple had invited its Montclair neighbors — the people who walk past on their evening routes, the ones who've never stepped inside but have watched the place brighten the block every Diwali. The kitchen was already three dishes deep. Someone's grandmother was directing traffic by the stove.

But this wasn't the only faith gathering carrying the season this cycle. Across Oakland, five congregations — Telegraph Presbyterian in Downtown, Foothill Baptist Tabernacle in East Oakland, the Cathedral of the Living Word at Lake Merritt, St. Esperanza Parish in Fruitvale, and the Bharat Mandir here in Montclair — were running the quiet infrastructure that holds a city together when everything else is still figuring out how to reactivate.

Telegraph Presbyterian had opened its food pantry again, stocked and steady on Saturday mornings. Foothill Baptist and the Cathedral of the Living Word were launching job-training workshops for the new season. St. Esperanza was marking a milestone in its seniors' lunch program — the kind of gathering that happens in the parish hall every week and doesn't make the news, but when you're 67 and you've been eating lunch in the same room with the same twelve people for three years, that continuity is everything.

The texture wasn't desperation. The mood across these neighborhoods this fall ran warm — Montclair, Downtown, East Oakland, Lake Merritt, and Fruitvale all turning toward the gatherings that hold them, the way a city does when the nights start coming early. The faith communities weren't scrambling to fill a gap the city had abandoned. They were the steady thing the city could count on.

At the Bharat Mandir, a woman I didn't recognize was arranging flowers for the altar. She'd wandered in one evening just to see the place, she said, and the temple had asked if anyone had two hours to spare. Now here she was the night before the observance, arranging marigolds. The temple had been there, and it had asked, and that's how networks hold.

The five gatherings that marked this cycle — the food pantry opening its doors steady, the workshops taking sign-ups for new cohorts, the seniors lunch marking its milestone, the Diwali observance drawing neighbors in from the block — weren't separate stories. They were the same story, told five times across five neighborhoods. They were a city learning again how to carry its own, one congregation at a time, one Saturday morning, one job training session, one lights ceremony, one seniors lunch, one dal simmering in a temple kitchen on a Thursday night when someone from inland California walked in and decided to stay.

That's the network. That's how it holds.

### Eight Bidders, One Waterfront: Baylight Phase II Heads to Evaluation

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

---

Eight construction firms put in bids this cycle to build the second phase of the Baylight District, and the city has begun weighing them on cost, schedule, and who can actually deliver. It is the first real competition over the Jack London and Downtown waterfront project since Phase I wrapped, having beaten its own local-hiring targets.

Phase I reported 43 percent of its workforce drawn from Oakland residents, exceeding the community benefits agreement target, with women comprising 21 percent of trades positions across the construction timeline. The apprenticeship pipeline feeding that labor — run through the city's youth program — is the infrastructure Phase II evaluators will examine alongside cost, timeline, and logistics proposals from the eight firms.

"We're looking at who can deliver on the commitment to local hiring while meeting the infrastructure timeline," said Thomas Reeves, 42, a general foreman who managed Phase I excavation work at the waterfront. Reeves grew up in the Fruitvale district and worked three years on the initial project. "Phase II is bigger — you need not just the crew size but the pipeline maturity to back it up."

The eight bidders now move into the evaluation, which will run through next cycle, with Phase II mobilization expected pending final city council authorization.

Downtown's shops held strong all through Phase I construction, and the Jack London waterfront has stayed busy and upbeat around the build.

The Phase II scope centers on mixed-use development with public waterfront access on the Jack London side. The evaluation committees will weigh each bidder's experience on comparable projects alongside cost, timeline, and logistics.

The apprenticeship pipeline has run without interruption since Phase I commenced. Whether the eight bidders can replicate Phase I's local-hire performance while scaling the operation to Phase II's footprint is the central question the evaluation will answer — and the measure by which the community will hold the next phase accountable.

### Varek Talks, Paulson Dodges: The Oaks Question Lands in Minnesota

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The Oakland Oaks expansion timeline has been public knowledge for cycles now — draft in C99, the NBA draft in C100, free agency opening in C102, with a coaching and advisor search already underway behind closed doors. What had not happened, until this cycle, was Elias Varek saying Mike Paulson's name out loud.

Varek, the thirty-eight-year-old founder of Civis Systems and the established owner of the incoming Oakland Oaks franchise, spoke publicly this cycle about Paulson and where the organization is headed. The remarks were measured and deliberate — the kind of public statement a man makes when he has decided the courtship should no longer be conducted entirely in private. Varek, who built Civis into one of the more consequential technology firms in the city, has operated the Oaks ownership from a position of patience since the franchise was awarded. That patience, it appears, has a timetable now, and he is willing to say so on the record.

The question eventually found Paulson — in Minnesota, after an Oakland A's loss, in a visiting clubhouse that had already absorbed back-to-back series defeats against the Twins and the Rangers. A reporter asked him about the Varek call. The A's general manager, who had managed one of the best starts in franchise history only to see the club go two-and-four across the last six, gave the assembled Minnesota press corps exactly two words: "we spoke."

There is a version of that answer that is diplomatic, and a version that is evasive, and a version that is simply the only honest thing a man can say when he is not ready to say anything more. Paulson offered no indication which version applied.

The A's sit at thirty-eight wins and ten losses, a record that would be the story in almost any other week. The skid — consecutive series losses for the first time in a run that had otherwise felt frictionless — is recent enough that the front office has not had time to frame it publicly. Whether the losses in Minnesota and against Texas represent anything structural in the rotation or simply the variance a full season holds is a question Paulson's staff will be working through now. What is not a question is the broader architecture of his situation: the A's are deep in a championship window, the Oaks are on a calendar that begins in earnest at C99, and an owner who funded his franchise with Civis money and built a local operation from nothing is now comfortable enough to say Paulson's name in front of cameras.

None of that constitutes a news event on its own. Varek's ownership has been settled for cycles. The advisory conversations between an expansion franchise and one of the most accomplished general managers in the sport are not a secret to anyone who covers Oakland sports. What makes this cycle different is the transition from private conversation to public statement — Varek speaking, Paulson answering with the minimum possible words in a room that was not designed for his comfort.

The coaching hunt will produce a name eventually. The C99 expansion draft will require a personnel structure around whoever runs the floor. Whether Paulson shapes any of that in an official capacity, or whether "we spoke" is the full extent of his on-record contribution to the Oaks story, will not be resolved inside a visiting clubhouse after a three-run loss. But the question has now been asked publicly, and the man it was asked about has confirmed, at least, that the conversation happened.

Two words. For now, that is all Oakland gets.

### First Wobble: The 38-10 A's Drop Two Straight Series

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

I keep going back to the math and it keeps not helping.

Thirty-eight and ten. That's where we are. That's the best record in baseball, and it still feels like something broke last week, and I don't fully know what to do with that.

Two of three to the Twins. Two of three to the Rangers. Back-to-back series losses for the first time all year. And here's the part that won't leave me alone: four of those six losses came by a single run. An average of one run. The margin between the A's and ordinary in those games was so thin it barely had a name.

This isn't collapse. I know the difference. I've watched collapse in this city. I watched it from the bleachers with my stomach in my feet, and this isn't that. This is a wobble. A stumble. But I'm forty-one years old and I have been following this franchise long enough to know that wobbles can mean things, and they can also mean nothing, and figuring out which is which while you're inside them is basically impossible.

So let me tell you what I actually know.

I know that Isley Kelley doesn't lose the tight ones easily. I've watched him manufacture runs in games that had no business being won, grind at-bats that turned innings, do the quiet terrible work of keeping a lineup alive when the lineup wants to quit. If we're going one-run the wrong way in the late innings, I want to know why, and I want the answer to be something fixable.

I know Danny Horn can carry this offense when his bat is right. He's had the kind of first half that makes you feel safe. But safety is a feeling, not a fact, and last week the facts said the bats went quiet precisely when they couldn't afford to.

I know Ernesto Quintero at the plate in a close game is a statement. Has been all year. Four losses by one run is four games where statements didn't get made.

Here's what I keep arguing with myself about, and I don't have a clean answer.

Part of me says: this is the schedule grinding on a great team. This is two quality opponents catching the A's in a stretch where the margin for error compressed and the A's didn't adjust fast enough. You go thirty-eight and ten in the first third of the season, you're going to hit patches where the game gets tight and the breaks go the other way. That's baseball. That's not prophecy.

The other part of me — the part that grew up in this city, the part that has seen this franchise chew through hope like it was nothing, the part that can't help fearing the thing that hasn't happened yet — that part says: watch how they respond. This is the first real test. Not of talent. They have the talent. Of something harder to name. The will to win ugly when winning ugly is the only option left.

Two wins averaged four and a half runs. Two wins where we weren't in the margin, where we could operate, where the game had room. We know who this team is when the game has room.

Thirty-eight and ten says we don't know yet who they are when it doesn't.

I want to find out. I want it to come back the answer I believe it will. But I'm not resolving this in print before they resolve it on the field. You can call that caution. I've been surprised before.

Oakland, we're fine. Probably.

Check back with me after the next six.

### The Shortstop and the Pulpit: Isley Kelley's Week

By Simon Leary | Bay Tribune Sports

---

Isley Kelley leads the major leagues in batting average. This cycle he also stood at the front of a congregation.

The A's shortstop — a devout Baptist with a theology degree who has spoken often about wanting to serve his faith in his lifetime — took an honorary turn as guest priest at Foothill Baptist Tabernacle, welcomed by Dr. Ophelia Brenner. A locally produced documentary on Kelley ran the same week.

It is an unusual line in a season already full of them for Kelley, who has carried the A's lineup as much as anyone through the first third of the year. The man hitting .345 spent part of his week in vestments, not a batting cage. In a clubhouse full of strivers chasing the franchise's next ring, Kelley has been quietly chasing something else alongside it. This cycle the two showed up in the same week.

### Re: The Temescal Health Center

I closed my salon on Telegraph this cycle. Thirty-two years cutting hair in Temescal, watching kids come in with their parents' old style and leave talking about what they want to be. The neighborhood held me. Now I'm done — and the word is the Temescal Health Center finally has its ground, the parcel secured after all this waiting. It isn't built yet. But it's real now in a way it never was, and I think about all those years we had nowhere close to go for basic care. I didn't think I'd see it even start. I'm starting to. That's grace.

— Lingo Kim, Temescal

### Staying Is Starting to Look Like Something

I'm new here. Landed in Laurel this cycle and found real work in a kitchen — line cook, not fancy, but real work, not gig work. Five of us on the line are from this part of the city and we're all staying put. The Oaks owner is new, the Baylight build is real, people are coming back. I came back too. I'm staying because staying is starting to look like something.

— Patrick Hill, Laurel

### Building in My Own City

My work grew this cycle. I run my own small construction outfit out of Fruitvale, and for the first time I've got a crew under me on a real build — housing, in my own city, for people like me. I grew up thinking the only work around was the kind that breaks you and pays you nothing. Building this outfit from the ground up, hiring guys off my own block — it changes what you let yourself want. I'm letting myself want this.

— Shawn Neighbor, Fruitvale
