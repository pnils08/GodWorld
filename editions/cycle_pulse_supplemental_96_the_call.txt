THE PAVING OF A DYNASTY
As Elias Varek prepares to bring the Oakland Oaks to the Baylight District, a $14 million retail recovery fund is laying the groundwork for a West Oakland renaissance.
By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business
============================================================

Lena Vance, 54, stands behind the counter of Vance’s Vintage on San Pablo Avenue, her hands smoothing the lapel of a wool coat that probably saw its first winter in nineteen seventy-eight. Outside, the morning fog is lifting, revealing a street that feels like it’s holding its breath. For three decades, this stretch of the San Pablo corridor has been defined by what was missing — the boarded-up windows, the missing foot traffic, the capital that always seemed to flow toward the hills or the waterfront, never here.

But this morning, Lena isn’t looking at the vacancies. She’s looking at a notification on her tablet. A micro-loan of fifteen thousand dollars, part of the Mayor Avery Santana’s new fourteen million dollar West Oakland Retail Recovery initiative, has just been cleared for her storefront’s seismic retrofitting and facade improvement.

"It’s not just about the paint," Lena says, her voice steady. She’s lived in West Oakland her entire life. Her father worked the docks; she remembers the smell of salt and diesel on his jacket. "It’s about the gravity. For years, the gravity in this city pulled away from us. Now, you can feel it shifting. You can feel the weight of the future landing right on our doorstep."

The "weight" Lena is referring to is more than just a municipal grant. It is the logistical and financial momentum of a dynasty in the making. As Mayor Avery Santana has officially signed off on the OARI (Oakland Alternative Response Initiative) citywide rollout and the second phase of the Stabilization Fund, the narrative of West Oakland is being rewritten in real-time. The fourteen million dollars allocated for the next eighteen months isn't just a recovery fund; it's the paving of the road that leads directly to the Baylight District (INIT-006) and the future home of the Oakland Oaks.

Follow the money, and you’ll find it leads back to the same intersections where Civis Systems (BIZ-00052) is already planting its flag. Elias Varek, the founder of Civis and the public face of the Oaks, isn't just building a stadium. He’s building an ecosystem. When Varek confirmed "The Call" to Mike Paulson this cycle, he wasn't just talking about a front-office hire. He was signaling that the billion-dollar machine of professional sports is now inextricably linked to the survival of small businesses like Vance’s Vintage.

I spoke with three other owners along San Pablo this week to understand how this capital is actually hitting the pavement. This isn’t the broad-stroke stimulus of years past. This is surgical. The fourteen million dollar fund is divided into three tiers. Tier 1 covers immediate structural repairs—roofs, seismic bracing, the invisible bones of a commercial district that have been decaying since the nineties. Tier 2 is for "digital integration," providing the hardware and training for small shops to sync with the city’s rising smart-grid. Tier 3 is the facade work—the "Civis-standard" aesthetics that will define the visual transition from the old West Oakland to the new Baylight horizon.

"They aren’t just giving us money," says Javier Mendez, 41, who runs a print shop two blocks from Lena. Mendez received a Tier 2 grant of twenty-two thousand dollars. "They’re giving us a seat at the table. Or at least, they’re making sure we look like we belong at the table. If I don’t have the same high-speed uplink as the stadium, I’m invisible to the people walking through here in two years. This loan ensures I’m part of the map."

The "map" Mendez mentions is literal. Part of the recovery initiative involves the installation of "Civis-standard" smart-curbs along San Pablo. These aren’t your grandfather’s concrete gutters. They are equipped with environmental sensors, high-fidelity lighting that adjusts to pedestrian density, and integrated delivery zones for the autonomous fleets that already service the Jack London docks. By bringing this infrastructure to the San Pablo corridor, the city is effectively extending the Baylight District’s nervous system into the heart of the residential neighborhood.

The connection to Civis Systems is more than metaphorical. Civis Systems Field — The Town, as the new stadium is officially named, will be the physical anchor of this transformation. But a stadium without a thriving neighborhood around it is just an island of concrete. Varek knows this. By aligning his corporate interests with the Mayor Avery Santana’s retail recovery efforts, he is effectively subsidizing the "goodwill" backdrop for his franchise. 

The fourteen million dollar initiative is focusing heavily on "corridor connectivity." In plain English, that means making sure that when fans walk from the West Oakland BART station or the new parking structures toward the Baylight gates, they aren't passing through a ghost town. They’re passing through Lena Vance’s shop. They’re stopping for coffee at the new spots popping up near Foothill. 

But there’s a question that needs to be asked: who gets the first contract? When the facade improvements begin, when the new street lighting goes in, when the smart-curbs are installed, which firms are doing the work? Jordan Velez has always been skeptical of the word "development," and for good reason. My father saw the first wave of redevelopment in the seventies. He saw the contracts go to firms in San Francisco and San Jose, while the men on our block stood in line at the union hall.

This time, the city is mandating a forty-three percent local hire benchmark, a figure already being met in Phase I of Baylight. But the retail recovery fund goes further. It requires that fifty percent of the micro-loan recipients use contractors headquartered within the West Oakland or Fruitvale corridors. It’s an attempt to keep the money circulating within the neighborhood like blood through a limb.

A fourteen million dollar fund is a drop in the bucket compared to the two point one billion dollar projected cost of the Baylight District. But for a neighborhood that has survived on grit and memory, that drop is a flood. The micro-loans are being disbursed through the West Oakland Community Development Corp, and the oversight is rigorous. Applicants have to prove not just financial need, but a "commitment to the corridor's long-term character."

"Varek didn't just call Mike Paulson," says Marcus Thorne, 42, who runs a small logistics firm out of a shared workspace three blocks from the Civis headquarters. "He called the whole city. He’s telling us that the Oaks aren't just a team; they’re a landlord. They’re a developer. They’re the new boss. But if the new boss is paying for the new roof on my garage, I’m going to take the call too."

The tension in Thorne’s voice is common. There is excitement, yes—a palpable buzz that has pushed nightlife activity to a nine out of ten this cycle—but there is also the skepticism of a workforce that has seen "development" turn into "displacement" before. Jordan Velez knows this rhythm. My father worked those same docks Thorne’s trucks service now. I saw what happened when the shipping lines consolidated. I saw the empty lots grow where there used to be families.

The difference this time is the Baylight Bridge. The physical transition from the old industrial bones of the waterfront to the glass-and-steel promise of Civis Systems Field is being built with local hands. If the forty-three percent hire rate holds, and if the fourteen million dollars in retail recovery actually stays in the pockets of people like Lena Vance and Javier Mendez, then the Oakland Oaks won't just be a team that plays in West Oakland. They’ll be the team that West Oakland built.

The "Prosperity City" model depends on more than just construction jobs. It depends on the velocity of money. A micro-loan to a vintage shop on San Pablo creates a ripple effect. Lena Vance hires a local contractor to fix her roof. That contractor buys supplies from a local hardware store. The hardware store owner takes his family out to dinner at a restaurant in the Jack London corridor. 

This is the economic feedback loop that the Mayor and Elias Varek are betting on. They are trying to manufacture a middle class where one was hollowed out. It is a bold, expensive, and deeply complicated experiment in urban engineering. And it all starts with fifteen thousand dollars and a wool coat from nineteen seventy-eight.

But we must look at the fine print. The micro-loans are not grants. They are low-interest loans, deferred for the first twenty-four months, but eventually, they must be repaid. The city is betting that by the time those repayments start, the Baylight District will be generating enough foot traffic to triple the revenue of these small shops. It is a high-stakes gamble on the future of West Oakland. If the stadium isn't finished on time, or if the "Oaks effect" doesn't materialize, these small business owners could find themselves underwater, owing the city for improvements they can no longer afford.

"I know the risk," Lena Vance says, looking out at the San Pablo traffic. "I’ve lived with risk every day since I opened this door. But for the first time, the risk feels like it’s leading somewhere. For the first time, the city is betting on me as much as I’m betting on them."

As the sun sets over the San Pablo corridor, the "festive-spooky" mood of Halloween is beginning to take hold. There are orange lights in the windows of shops that were dark last year. There are people on the sidewalks, checking their phones for the latest news on "The Call," wondering if Mike Paulson will really leave the Coliseum for the paving stones of the future.

The Oaks are coming. The road is being paved. And for the first time in a generation, the people of West Oakland aren't just watching the rollers go by. They’re the ones holding the contracts.

THE SYSTEM AND THE SOUL
After forty years in the press box, Hal Richmond reflects on the gravity of "The Call" and the existential dilemma facing the architect of Oakland’s greatest dynasty.
By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports
============================================================

When you’ve written about the same team for nearly four decades, you learn to read the silence of the Coliseum as clearly as the roar. There is a specific kind of quiet that settles over the concrete during the early morning hours of October, before the gates open and the ghosts of championships past begin to stir. It is the sound of time passing, of eras shifting beneath our feet like tectonic plates. And this year, in the autumn of two thousand forty-one, that silence feels heavier than usual. It feels final.

I remember standing on the concourse in nineteen eighty-nine, watching the confetti fall for a team that felt like it would live forever. I remember the lean years of the early thousands, and the rebirth that followed. But nothing in my forty years of covering Oakland baseball has felt quite like this. We are living in the shadow of "The Call."

Elias Varek, the man who would be the king of the Baylight District, has finally reached out to Mike Paulson. The confirmation came this cycle, a quiet admission that the recruitment of the architect of the A’s dynasty is no longer a matter of speculation. It is an active pursuit. And for Mike Paulson (POP-00527), the man who has spent nearly two decades building the most efficient winning machine in the history of the sport, it is the ultimate dilemma.

To understand the weight of this moment, you have to understand the lineage. Mike Paulson didn’t just inherit a front office; he inherited a philosophy. He is the direct descendant of Sandy Alderson and Billy Beane, the men who first proved that you could measure what others only felt. Alderson was the strategist, the man who brought military discipline to the scouting room. Beane was the disruptor, the one who realized that the old scouts were looking at the wrong things—at the shape of a jaw or the sound of a bat, rather than the cold, hard reality of on-base percentage.

But Paulson took it further. He built a "System" that didn't just find value in the margins—it manufactured it. If Alderson built the foundation and Beane built the engine, Paulson built the artificial intelligence that runs the whole factory. Under his watch, the A’s haven’t just won; they’ve dominated, turning the Coliseum into a fortress of data-driven excellence. He didn't just find the undervalued players; he developed them in a way that made their production feel inevitable.

But there is a ghost in the machine. In a two thousand forty-one interview, Paulson famously told me, "The system travels, the culture adapts." It was a statement of cold, professional confidence. He was saying that his logic—his algorithms, his scouting patterns, his developmental pipelines—could be picked up and moved to any building, any city, any era. It is the ultimate expression of the modern front office: the belief that greatness is a portable asset.

And yet, as I watch the A’s take the field this autumn, I find myself wondering if Paulson truly believes that. Because if the system travels, why hasn't it accounted for the "Let-Walks"?

That is what the fans call them now. The core of this dynasty, the men who have defined the "Oakland Way" for the better part of a decade, are all standing on the precipice of free agency. These aren't just names on a roster; they are the living, breathing evidence of a culture that Paulson’s system supposedly created, but which has now outgrown the spreadsheets.

Take Isley Kelley (POP-00019). The shortstop with the theology degree and the MVP swing. I watched him as a rookie in two thousand thirty-two, a kid who looked like he was playing at a different speed than everyone else. But it wasn't just the range or the arm; it was the way he carried himself. He spent this very cycle serving as an honorary priest at Foothill Baptist, praying with the same people who scream his name from the bleachers on Tuesday nights. You can’t put that in a model. You can’t calculate the value of a man who makes a city feel like it’s being looked after.

Then there is Mark Aitken (POP-00003), the first baseman who has become the community’s beating heart. Aitken is the player representative, the man who spends his off-days at the West Oakland food banks and the youth clinics in Fruitvale. He resides in Jack London, a few blocks from where the new Baylight towers are rising, but his roots are in the old soil. When the A’s win, Aitken is the first one at the rail, high-fiving the kids who will remember that moment for the rest of their lives. That’s the culture.

And what about Martin Richards (POP-00031)? The veteran third baseman who has seen every parade, who has been the steady hand in the clubhouse when the system’s projections failed and the pressure of October mounted. Or Benji Dillon (POP-00018), the five-time Cy Young winner who signed a one-year deal just to "ride with Vinnie" for one final season. Dillon is a lefty with a spin rate that hears echoes of old giants, a man who knows that in baseball, as in life, some things are only worth doing if you do them with the right people.

These four men are the "Let-Walks." According to the system, they are at the age where their value is declining. The data says it’s time to move on, to replace them with cheaper, younger versions of themselves. And that is exactly what the Oaks represent.

The Oakland Oaks, Varek’s new venture, are looming in the distance like a gleaming city on a hill. They represent the future—the Baylight District (INIT-006), the state-of-the-art Civis Systems Field, the promise of a franchise built from the ground up with unlimited resources. It is the perfect laboratory for Paulson’s system. If he takes "The Call," he will have the chance to build a dynasty without the constraints of the Coliseum’s crumbling concrete or the A’s modest budget. He will be the architect of a new world.

But what happens to the soul he leaves behind?

I remember a conversation I had with Mike Paulson back in two thousand thirty-five, during the height of the second championship run. We were sitting in his office, the walls covered in charts that looked more like celestial maps than scouting reports. I asked him then if he ever worried that the numbers would eventually swallow the story.

He looked at me with that calm, measured gaze and said, "Hal, the numbers are the story. They are the fingerprints of the work. If you build the right system, the story takes care of itself."

It was a beautiful answer, but today, in two thousand forty-one, it feels incomplete. Because the story of the A’s dynasty isn't just the wins and the losses. It’s the sound of the line in West Oakland. It’s the way the city smells like beer and asphalt and history on parade days. It’s the relationship between a player like Isley Kelley and the people he prays with on Sunday mornings. That isn't part of the system. That is the culture. And culture doesn't adapt; it grows. It takes root. It becomes part of the dirt.

Beane’s "Moneyball" was about finding the hidden value in players. Paulson’s "System" is about finding the hidden value in the city itself. He realized that a winning team is the ultimate urban development tool. But by making the team a tool, he has risked making the players interchangeable. If Kelley leaves, the system says the next kid will step in and provide eighty percent of the production for ten percent of the cost. And the machine keeps humming.

But the machine doesn't pray at Foothill Baptist. The machine doesn't reside in Jack London. The machine doesn't sign autographs until the lights go out.

If Paulson leaves for the Oaks, he will take his system with him. He will find new versions of Kelley and Aitken and Dillon. He will find the next Ernesto Quintero (POP-00050) and build a new machine around him. And he will probably win. He is too good not to. But he will be building on paving stones, not dirt. The Baylight District is a miracle of modern urban engineering, a "Prosperity City" success story that is bringing billions of dollars and thousands of people to the waterfront. But it is new. It is clean. It hasn't had the time to absorb the sweat and the heartbreak and the joy of a forty-year history. It doesn't have the ghosts.

And that is the true dilemma of "The Call." It isn't just about a job or a salary or a stadium. It’s about whether a man can leave his legacy in the dirt and still be the same man when he stands on the glass.

I’ve seen GMs come and go. I’ve seen owners who treated the team like a toy and owners who treated it like a treasury. But Mike Paulson is different. He is the first one who truly understood that Oakland baseball is a conversation between the past and the future. He bridged the gap between the traditionalists and the analysts. He made the data feel like poetry.

But even the best poets eventually run out of lines.

As I walked through the Jack London corridor this cycle, the festive-spooky mood of Halloween was everywhere. Orange lights flickered in the windows of the new lofts, and the smell of roasting coffee mingled with the salt air. It is a city that is growing, thriving, looking forward. The migration numbers are up, the economy is stable, and the excitement for the Oaks is palpable. You can feel the gravity shift.

But if you go out to the Coliseum, past the new construction and the smart-curbs, you can still feel the old gravity. You can still feel the weight of the "Let-Walks." You can see Vinnie Keane (POP-00001) in the dugout, his final season winding down like a clock that everyone is afraid to stop. Vinnie is the ultimate "one of one." The system has no comp for him. He is the sun around which this entire universe has orbited. When he goes, the gravity of the Coliseum might just collapse entirely.

You can see Mark Aitken signing autographs for kids whose parents were kids when I started this beat. That continuity is what makes a team a "legacy." It’s what transforms a business into a civic institution.

If Mike Paulson takes "The Call," he will be choosing the system over the soul. He will be betting that the architecture of winning is more important than the foundation of history. And in the world of two thousand forty-one, maybe he’s right. Maybe the future belongs to the portable, the efficient, the clean.

But after forty years, I’ve learned that baseball is a game of paper cuts as much as percentiles. It’s the little things that stick to you—the smell of the grass after a summer rain, the sound of a ball hitting a glove in a quiet stadium, the way a city holds its breath when the bases are loaded in the ninth. Those things don't show up in the algorithms. They don't travel. They are the "un-measurable" that Beane ignored and Paulson mastered.

As the A's prepare for what everyone acknowledges is their final season in this form, I find myself thinking about a quote from a player whose name has long since been lost to time, but whose words have stayed with me. He said, "You don't play for the team on the jersey. You play for the dirt you're standing on."

Mike Paulson is about to choose new dirt. It will be cleaner dirt, more expensive dirt, dirt that has been analyzed and optimized and integrated into a billion-dollar development plan. But it won't be the same. He will be the first GM to build a dynasty in a "Prosperity City," and he will do it with the same cold brilliance that has defined his career.

We are watching the end of an era. Not just a dynasty, but a way of being. The transition from the Coliseum to Baylight is a physical manifestation of the shift from the twentieth century to the twenty-first. It is the triumph of the plan over the happenstance. It is the realization of Varek’s vision, a vision that includes a stadium as an "urban intelligence" hub.

And yet, as I sit here in the press box, watching the sun set over the Oakland hills, I can’t help but hope that some part of the soul stays behind. I hope that even if the system travels, the memory of what we built here—in the dirt, in the noise, in the heartbreak—remains. I hope that when the Oaks take the field at Civis Systems Field, they still hear the echoes of Isley Kelley’s prayers and Mark Aitken’s laughter.

Because a city without its ghosts is just a collection of buildings. And a team without its soul is just a business.

The Call has been made. The answer is coming. And for those of us who have lived our lives in the rhythm of Oakland baseball, all we can do is wait for the silence that follows. We will be there, in the new press boxes and the new bleachers, waiting to see if the system really can manufacture a soul.

THE VIEW FROM WEST OAKLAND
From the heat of the Blue Lantern’s kitchen to the festive-spooky streets of the corridor, Mason Ortega shadows the workers and fans who are witnessing their neighborhood’s transformation.
By Mason Ortega | Bay Tribune Culture
============================================================

I spent three shifts shadowing the line at the Blue Lantern Bar in West Oakland this cycle. If you want to know how a neighborhood is actually handling the news of "The Call," you don't go to a press conference. You go to the place where the heat is real, where the tickets don't stop, and where the people making the food have been living the story long before the reporters showed up.

The kitchen at the Blue Lantern is a tight, high-pressure box that smells like rendered fat, charred peppers, and the kind of history you can only earn through decades of service. Oscar Ruiz, 52, is the owner, but tonight he’s working the middle station. He’s been in West Oakland for forty years. He’s seen the neighborhood ebb and flow, but he says he’s never felt a rhythm quite like this one.

"It’s the sound," Oscar says, his hands moving in a blur as he plates a row of sliders. He doesn't look up. He doesn't have time. "Listen to the line. It’s not just the fans cheering. It’s the vibration of the street. You feel it in the floorboards. The city is moving, man. It’s moving toward us."

The "sound" Oscar is talking about is the low-frequency hum of construction from the Baylight District, mixed with the high-pitched excitement of a neighborhood that just found out one of its own—Elias Varek—is effectively buying the future. When Varek confirmed he’d made the call to Mike Paulson, the Blue Lantern went from a busy local spot to a mandatory stop on the "Oaks Trail."

The heat in the kitchen is a constant ninety-four degrees. The ventilation is struggling with the sudden surge in volume. This cycle, the nightlife activity in West Oakland hit a nine out of ten, and you can feel every decimal of that increase on the line. The tickets are coming in faster than the printer can spit them out. Burger, medium-rare. Wings, extra crispy. Nachos, hold the jalapeños.

I watched Oscar shadow his lead cook, a kid half his age who’s only been in the neighborhood for six months. They move like a choreographed unit, avoiding the "paper cuts" of a mismanaged rush. But beneath the routine, there’s a tension.

"We grew up A’s fans," Oscar says, wiping sweat from his brow during a thirty-second lull. "My dad took me to the Coliseum when the seats were still orange. Leaving that... it feels like leaving a limb behind. But then you look at what Varek is doing. You look at the micro-loans hitting the shops on San Pablo. You see the orange lights in the windows for Halloween. It’s hard to stay mad when the lights are finally coming back on."

Outside the kitchen, the "festive-spooky" mood of late October has taken over the corridor. It’s a specific Oakland vibe—part celebration, part edge. The migration numbers are up, and you can see it in the faces at the bar. There are the regulars who have been drinking here since the nineties, and then there are the new arrivals, the "Baylight Pioneers" who are moving into the lofts and looking for something authentic.

Jada Silas, 28, is one of those regulars. She’s wearing an A’s jersey with "Keane" on the back, but she’s also got an "Oakland Oaks" pin on her lapel. It’s a common sight this cycle—the divided loyalty of a city in transition.

"I’m here for the A’s until the last out," Jada tells me, her voice competing with the sound of a game on the TV and the roar of the crowd. "Vinnie Keane is my hero. I cried when he hit that walk-off last month. But I live two blocks from where the new stadium is going. I see the cranes every morning. I see the jobs coming back. You can’t ignore that. You can’t hate the guy who’s trying to save your block."

The "shadowing" experience at the Blue Lantern is a lesson in the mechanics of a "Prosperity City." It’s not just about the big announcements; it’s about the capacity of a neighborhood to absorb that prosperity without losing its soul. Oscar Ruiz is betting that he can handle the volume. He’s betting that the Blue Lantern can stay a "West Oakland bar" even as it becomes a "Baylight destination."

But the line doesn't care about the long-term. The line cares about the next ticket.

"Forty tickets up," Oscar yells, and the rhythm resets. The sound of the line—the clatter of pans, the hiss of the grill, the barked orders—drowns out the conversation. This is the reality of the transformation. It’s work. It’s heat. It’s the constant pressure of a city that is growing faster than its infrastructure.

I spent three shifts watching this motion. I saw the way the cooks' bodies adjusted to the increased pace, the way the routine shifted to accommodate the new "Oaks" crowd. It’s a behavioral echo of the larger city. Oakland is adjusting its stance. It’s bracing for the impact of a new dynasty.

As I walked out of the Blue Lantern after my final shift, the cool October air felt like a benediction. The street was alive with the festive-spooky energy of the season. There were families out, checking out the Halloween decorations, and groups of fans heading toward the waterfront. The sound of the construction was still there, a steady heartbeat beneath the noise of the neighborhood.

West Oakland is the epicenter of "The Call." It is the place where the system meets the street. And if the view from Oscar Ruiz’s kitchen is any indication, the street is ready. They’re tired, they’re hot, and they’re working harder than ever. But for the first time in a long time, they aren't just surviving the rush. They’re owning it.

"Come back next cycle," Oscar shouted as I left. "We’ll have the new menu ready. Varek-style. High-energy, high-flavor."

He was laughing, but his hands were already moving toward the next ticket. The line never stops. And in West Oakland, the line is just getting started.

THE SHADOW ROSTER
As the Oakland Oaks prepare for the expansion draft, the math of the transition reveals a fundamental conflict between the value of the present and the potential of the future.
By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports
============================================================

The first thing that jumps out when you look at Ernesto Quintero's Statcast profile is the sheer volume of the contact authority. Through the first fifty-two games of the season, the A’s rookie third baseman (POP-00050) is not just leading the league in home runs with fifteen; he is doing so with an underlying process that suggests his production is sustainable, if not actually suppressed by his current environment.

Quintero’s average exit velocity on batted balls currently sits at ninety-four point eight miles per hour, placing him in the ninety-eighth percentile of all major league hitters. More importantly, his maximum exit velocity—a metric that scouts use to gauge raw physical ceiling—was clocked at one hundred twelve point four miles per hour during a late-cycle double against Houston. For a twenty-one-year-old, those numbers are not just impressive; they are an anomaly.

But the real story is in the expected metrics. Quintero’s xSLG (expected slugging percentage) is currently point five hundred eighty, while his actual slugging percentage is point five hundred forty-one. This forty-point gap suggests that despite his fifteen home runs and point three hundred twenty-one batting average, Quintero has actually been "unlucky" on several well-struck balls that were knocked down by the Coliseum’s marine layer or tracked down in the expansive gaps.

When you compare this profile to Vinnie Keane (POP-00001), the "one of one" whose final season has become a city-wide vigil, the analytical contrast is stark. Keane, at forty-two, no longer possesses the raw exit velocity of his prime. His average velocity has dropped to eighty-nine point two miles per hour, and his barrel rate has dipped below the league average for the first time in his twenty-year career. 

Yet, Keane’s xwOBA (expected weighted on-base average) remains elite because of a plate discipline profile that has actually improved with age. He is chasing less than fifteen percent of pitches outside the zone—the lowest rate in the Statcast era. Vinnie Keane isn't beating you with force anymore; he is beating you with geometry and patience. He is a trajectory-locked hitter who knows exactly which pitches can be manipulated into the holes.

This is the "Shadow Roster" dilemma facing the Oakland Oaks as they approach the expansion draft. The Oaks, led by the potential recruitment of Mike Paulson, must decide whether they are building a team to win in the Baylight District immediately or if they are building a long-term developmental engine.

The math of the expansion draft is brutal. With the number five pick, the Oaks will have access to a pool of protected and unprotected players that is historically shallow. The current projection models suggest that the "average" player available in the number five slot will be a twenty-six-year-old with a career WAR (Wins Above Replacement) of one point two and an OPS-plus of ninety-eight. In other words, a replacement-level starter or a high-end bench piece.

However, the "Expansion Prototype" that the Oaks’ analytics department is likely targeting is not the established veteran, but the "Quintero-lite." They are looking for hitters with elite max exit velocities whose actual production is suppressed by high ground-ball rates or unfavorable park factors. These are the "Breakout Candidates"—players whose contact authority suggests that a simple adjustment in launch angle (moving from a five-degree average to a fifteen-degree average) could unlock twenty home runs of surplus value.

The Oaks are not just drafting players; they are drafting "potential energy."

This brings us back to the "Let-Walks." If Mike Paulson takes "The Call" and moves to the Oaks, he will be leaving behind a core of veterans whose value is almost entirely captured in their current environment. Isley Kelley (POP-00019), Mark Aitken (POP-00003), and Martin Richards (POP-00031) are all entering the phase of their careers where the regression curves begin to steepen. 

According to the PANDAS (Autocorrelation Analysis) model, players of their profile—high-contact, moderate-power veterans—tend to see a significant drop in barrel rate once they cross the thirty-two-year-old threshold. For an expansion team like the Oaks, signing these veterans would be a "sentimental tax." Their value to the A’s culture is immeasurable, but their value on an expansion roster—where every dollar and every roster spot must be optimized for future growth—is questionable.

The number five pick math suggests that the Oaks could find more long-term value in a twenty-two-year-old pitcher with a high spin rate and a vertical break profile that projects as a future number two starter, rather than a thirty-four-year-old first baseman with a legendary clubhouse presence. 

The analytical bluntness of this approach is exactly why Elias Varek is pursuing Paulson. Paulson’s system is designed to ignore the sentiment and focus on the "Shadow Roster"—the theoretical team that exists in the spreadsheets two years before the first pitch is ever thrown.

But there is one metric that the spreadsheets haven't mastered: the Vinnie Keane effect. Statcast can measure the velocity of the ball leaving his bat, but it can’t measure the gravity he exerts on a pitcher’s decision-making process. It can’t measure the way a rookie like Ernesto Quintero develops faster because he is standing in the same dugout as a man who has seen every pitch ever thrown.

The Oaks will have the resources of Civis Systems (BIZ-00052) and the logistical advantage of the Baylight District (INIT-006). They will have the "Prosperity City" momentum and the backing of a billionaire founder. But they will start with a zero-base culture. They will be drafting against the ghosts of the Coliseum.

If the Oaks target the "Shadow Roster" correctly, they will find the next generation of Quinteros. They will find the pitchers whose arsenals are "shape-locked" but underperforming. They will use the #5 pick to secure a cornerstone that the A’s can’t protect because of their own roster constraints.

But as the A’s dominate the league with a forty-and-twelve record, the analytics department is forced to acknowledge a difficult truth: the "Final Season" version of this dynasty is the most efficient version they’ve ever built. They are producing runs at a rate that is twelve percent higher than the league average, despite having one of the lowest payroll-to-win ratios in the history of the sport.

Ernesto Quintero is the fuse, and Vinnie Keane is the fire. Replacing them isn't a matter of drafting the right exit velocity profile. It’s a matter of building a system that can survive without the "one of one."

The math of the expansion draft is a controlled burn. You clear out the old growth to make room for the new. The Oaks are preparing the ground. They are looking at the spin rates and the launch angles and the expected weighted averages. They are building a team out of decimals.

But when the first pitch is thrown at Civis Systems Field in two years, the only metric that will matter to the fans in West Oakland is the one that doesn't have a Statcast equivalent: the win.

And as any scout will tell you, the hardest thing to find in an expansion draft isn't a hundred-mile-per-hour fastball or a forty-degree launch angle. It’s the guy who knows how to make the last out.
