**Steel and Schedule**

*The Baylight waterfront rises on compressed timelines. Five September deliverables loom. The workers who build it know what's at stake.*

By Trevor Shimizu | Bay Tribune Infrastructure

6:47 a.m., Jack London Square. The sun catches the crawler crane's boom before it catches the water.

Marcus Ibarra stands on the observation platform at Pier 3, coffee in hand, watching the steel go vertical. Behind him, the Baylight district sprawls across fourteen acres — a grid of concrete pours, cable runs, and wooden formwork that will become the waterfront Oakland has promised itself for three decades. The project is two years old. It is supposed to be finished in fourteen months. It will not be finished in fourteen months.

"Look at the schedule," Ibarra says, not looking away from the crane. "That's what matters now. Not the vision. The schedule."

Baylight is a $2.1 billion project. That number moves around in the trades like a rumor — sometimes it's $1.9 billion, sometimes $2.4 — but the scale is constant. The site itself is 14.2 acres of the waterfront that used to be port overflow, container staging, and the kind of space a working city stops noticing. Now it is the most expensive hole in Oakland.

The August 2041 update to the project timeline identified five critical deliverables converging in September: the Phase 2 structural steel completion (target September 15), the water-main relocation to feed the district's hydrant system (September 18), the electrical substation cutover at the Embarcadero junction (September 22), the parking garage foundation pour for Towers B and C (September 29), and the opening of the temporary worker access bridge that bypasses the congested surface route through Jack London (September 12). Five dates. One month. The math is not comfortable.

"We're carrying a 1.2-day float on most of these," says Lorenzo Nguyen, the Port of Oakland's senior crane operator. He has been operating heavy equipment on the waterfront for thirty-one years. "Float is what you spend when something goes wrong. We don't have room for something to go wrong."

What could go wrong is the kind of question that wakes engineers at 3 a.m.

The steel subcontractor, Horizon Bay Structural, has been behind schedule since June. Not catastrophically — six to eight days at various points — but enough that the window between delivery and installation has compressed from comfortable to tight. The Phase 2 columns and beams that should have been on-site by August 28 did not arrive until September 2. That was last week. The installation crew worked six-day weeks to compress the schedule back into alignment, and by early this week, they were approaching the September 15 deadline.

"We're going to make it," Ibarra says. "But we're making it because we're burning through contingency. The next delay doesn't have anywhere to hide."

The water main is being relocated by a crew from Consolidated Infrastructure Partners, a Sacramento-based contractor running a parallel operation on the Embarcadero side of the site. That work is, by all accounts, on schedule. The electrical cutover is controlled by PG&E, which operates on its own calendar and has its own relationship with the concept of deadline flexibility. The parking garage pour requires sequencing with the structural steel in a way that leaves almost no margin for error.

The temporary access bridge is the wild card. It's a temporary structure — designed to last eighteen months, intended to route construction traffic away from the Jack London commercial corridor while the main entry boulevard is being rebuilt. If it opens on September 12 as planned, it absorbs roughly 200 heavy vehicle movements per day that would otherwise move through the retail district. If it doesn't open, that traffic stays on Telegraph and Jack London for another two to three weeks.

Travis Green, a construction laborer with Horizon Bay, stands in the pre-fab concrete staging yard at 12th and Embarcadero. Stacked in front of him are column pour tubes for the parking structure — forty-one of them, arrayed in rows, each one a precise cylinder of reinforced concrete waiting for its position in the grid.

"I've never seen a timeline like this," Green says. "We're not making mistakes because we can't afford to. We're not making mistakes because nobody wants to be the guy who pushes the whole thing back."

The physical reality of the site is the reality of constraint. Cranes operate in a spatial choreography that leaves no room for improvisation. A single delivery truck arriving off-schedule can cascade through an entire day of hoisting operations. A welding crew that runs forty minutes late pushes the next crew's start time, which pushes the inspection window, which pushes the documentation, which pushes the permission to move to the next phase.

Farah Lindqvist, the city's primary building inspector for Baylight, has been signing off on completion certificates at a rate of roughly three per week. She is a careful inspector — the kind who will not certify a floor until she has walked every section — but speed is now a factor in her calculus.

"The city wants this done," she says. "The developer wants this done. The workers want this done. The question is whether wanting it and building it at this pace are the same activity."

The Baylight district will be beautiful when it is finished. The architectural renderings show a waterfront promenade, market halls, residential towers, and the kind of street-level activation that transforms a working waterfront into a destination. The schedules assume it will be finished.

The workers who are building it know what happens when two critical systems break at the same time — when the water main and the electrical substation both slip, or when the temporary bridge and the Phase 2 steel both run late. It doesn't cascade slowly. It accelerates. It becomes a problem that requires decisions from above, and those decisions take time, and time is the one thing September does not have.

The crane swings another piece of steel into place. The welding crew moves in behind it. The temporary access bridge remains on schedule for now. The September deliverables remain achievable. The float, compressed though it is, still exists.

But Ibarra is right about one thing: the schedule is what matters now. Not the vision. The vision can wait. The schedule cannot.

**The Shift**

*At Baylight's waterfront, construction workers stake their claim on Oakland's future — one boot, one beam, one careful hour at a time.*

By Sharon Okafor | Bay Tribune Lifestyle

I arrived at Jack London Square at 5:47 AM on a Tuesday in August. The Baylight construction site was quiet — just the cranes and the fog. The steel skeleton of the Waterfront Commons was barely visible against the gray. By 6:15, the first workers arrived.

Elijah Roberts pulled into the lot in a truck that's seen better days, boots already on his feet before the engine cut off. Forty-nine, West Oakland, construction laborer for twenty-three years. He didn't wave at anyone. He grabbed his hard hat from the passenger seat, checked his phone once — a text from his daughter, he told me later — and walked toward the site with the kind of focus that comes from knowing exactly where you need to be.

"You come out here long enough, you stop noticing the hour," he said, waiting for the safety briefing. "Your body knows when it's time to work."

I spent two mornings there, watching the same rhythm unfold: the arrival, the briefing, the dispersal into the work. Nobody rushed. Nobody lingered. They knew where they were going and they went.

Marcus Ibarra, the site foreman — fifty, Jack London, fifteen years managing construction crews — was already checking measurements by the time the crew assembled. He studied blueprints, watched the crew set beams, corrected a lift angle without raising his voice.

"We're building something that lasts," he told me. "You feel that, you work different."

By 8 AM, the site was fully alive. Howard Turner, forty-six, Piedmont Ave, was operating one of the smaller lifts — moving materials, clearing debris, the kind of invisible work that keeps the visible work possible. He'd been doing this for eighteen years. When I asked him what he loved about it, he didn't hesitate.

"It's honest," he said. "You show up, you do the work, you can see what you did when you leave. Not everybody gets that."

I watched the crew through the lunch break. Around noon, a food cart pulled up near the site entrance — run by a woman named Desiree Chen, fifty-two, Jack London, who's been feeding construction workers for four years. She has three coffee thermoses, a grill, and a gift for remembering how people take their coffee. "Elijah likes his black," she told me, already pouring. "Howard takes two sugars. Travis takes cream and honey."

Travis Green, forty-three, Jack London, a laborer who's been on-site since July, sat on a stack of pallets with his sandwich and told me about the neighborhood he's watched change. "When I was a kid, this whole area was port operations and warehouses," he said. "My uncle worked the docks. Now it's becoming something else. Something bigger. I'm part of building that. That's something."

Elena Lewis, forty-eight, longshoreman at the Port of Oakland, works near the construction site. She told me the waterfront feels different now — busier, more purposeful. "The port's always been here," she said. "But now there's this energy. Like the whole city is waking up."

I returned at 5:30 PM. The crew was breaking down for the day. Elijah was among the last to leave, picking up a piece of debris someone else might have missed, throwing it in the bin. He looked at the day's progress — a section of the interior frame had been set and secured — and nodded to himself.

"Tomorrow we do it again," he said. "And we get a little further."

The waterfront at dusk was quieter than at dawn. The cranes were still. The fog was coming back. Elijah's truck was one of the last out of the lot.

I asked him once, over coffee at Desiree's cart, whether he thinks about the finished version — the promenade, the market halls, the towers. He shook his head. "I think about tomorrow's pour," he said. "That's the job."

**Who's Actually Getting Hired?**

*The workforce agreement says Oakland. The subcontractor chain says otherwise. September 15 is three weeks away and nobody's counting.*

By Jax Caldera | Bay Tribune [OPINION]

I'm sitting at the bar at the Anchor & Rail on Embarcadero West — close enough to the Baylight site that you can smell the concrete dust when the wind shifts off the water. It's 4:45 PM on a Tuesday. The afternoon shift is ending. Men and women are walking in still wearing their site badges, hardhats hooked on belt loops, boots with waterfront mud on them.

I start counting who I recognize as Oakland faces. Fruitvale faces. West Oakland faces. The faces the workforce agreement was written for.

I get to four. Then I stop, because the rest of the room isn't from here.

Here's what the Baylight workforce agreement says, in the version that's circulating: thirty percent local hire minimum, with "local" defined as Alameda County resident. That's the language. Alameda County. Population 1.6 million. The county runs from Fremont to Albany. You can live in Dublin, forty-five minutes from this bar stool, and you're "local" under this agreement.

Translation: the word "local" in the workforce agreement does not mean Oakland.

Mateo Walker knows this. He's sixty-two, Jack London District, Trade Union Representative. He's been working these streets longer than Baylight Construction Authority has existed. I asked him what the agreement actually guarantees for Oakland residents specifically. He said: "The language is Alameda County throughout. We pushed for Oakland-specific language in the sub-tier agreements. It's not there."

Aaliyah Baker — seventy-four, also Jack London, also a union rep, and the one person who's read every version of this document — put it more plainly. "The top-tier contractors filed their local hire numbers. They look fine on paper. What they don't show you is what happens at the second and third subcontractor level, where most of the hours actually live."

There it is. The count.

Baylight's prime contractor is responsible for hitting that thirty percent threshold. But the prime contractor is not the one pouring concrete or pulling wire. The prime hires subs. The subs hire sub-subs. By the time you're three layers deep into the subcontractor chain, the workforce agreement is a document that exists in a filing cabinet in someone's office, not on the job site. Nobody down the chain signed it. Nobody down the chain is being audited against it.

Kevin Kim is fifty-two. West Oakland. Master Electrician with twenty years of commercial experience. The kind of worker the Baylight workforce agreement was written in a press release to protect. I asked him directly if he's gotten Baylight hours.

He said he put in for two subcontracts. Both went to firms based in Livermore.

Javier Harris is forty-two. Also West Oakland. Also an electrician. He heard about the Baylight electrical work from a friend, submitted his paperwork through the union hall in April. "I followed up three times," he told me. "They said the roster was full."

The roster was full. In April. Before ground even broke.

Now here's what I want you to do. I want you to go find Shawn Nguyen. He's forty-three, Fruitvale, construction laborer, and he is on the Baylight site. He got in. He's one of the four faces I counted at the bar. I asked him how he got the hours. He said his cousin works for a sub-subcontractor out of San Leandro that picked up a concrete finishing package. He drove to San Leandro to apply. He is an Oakland resident getting Oakland-adjacent wages through a San Leandro firm on a project that has an Oakland-first workforce agreement.

That is what winning looks like under this system.

Omar Allen is sixty-one. Uptown. Construction laborer. Twenty-two years on commercial sites across this city. He is not on the Baylight site. He does not know why. He applied through the standard channels. Nothing came back.

The dare: go ask the Baylight Construction Authority how many of the workers currently on site are Oakland residents — not Alameda County, Oakland — and what mechanism they have to verify that number at the sub-tier level. Ask them what happens when the September 15 workforce agreement deadline passes and the numbers don't check out.

See if they have an answer.

The agreement hits its deadline in three weeks. Five deliverables, five promises. The workforce agreement is one of them. The TIF language and the remediation bond are still unsigned. The city is about to sign off on an agreement that no one has built a compliance mechanism for, covering a subcontractor chain nobody is auditing, using a definition of "local" that includes forty-five minutes of freeway.

I'll be back at the Anchor & Rail on September 16th. I'll be counting again.

My question is: who else is?
