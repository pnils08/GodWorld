**Day 35: OARI's Dispatch Problem Has a Ten-Day Window**

*The police chief hasn't signed. Three pilot districts are waiting. The council built a clock and the city is watching it tick.*

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

**OARI Implementation Clock: Day 35 of 45. Dispatch integration unsigned. Three pilot districts named (D1 West Oakland, D3 Fruitvale, D5 Temescal) but no routing protocol signed. Montez signature deadline: Day 40 (Tran marker). Contingency threshold: Day 45 (single-district fallback).**

The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative was supposed to be running by now. It is not running. What it is doing, on Day 35 of its 45-day implementation window, is sitting in the narrow space between a program that has cleared every bureaucratic gate and a program that still cannot answer the most basic operational question: when someone in the three designated pilot districts — West Oakland, Fruitvale, Temescal — calls 911, who responds?

The dispatch integration protocol — the document that tells the Oakland Police Department's communications center how to route mental health and substance-related calls to OARI's crisis teams instead of armed officers — requires the signature of Police Chief Rafael Montez. Montez has not signed. His office has not refused. His office has issued no public timeline for review completion.

What his office has done, according to two sources inside City Hall, is request additional data on call classification error rates from the OPD communications center's existing triage system. The request is reasonable in isolation. At Day 35, nothing is in isolation.

The 45-day clock was written into the implementation framework by the council at Councilmember Leonard Tran's insistence. Tran, the District 2 independent who voted yes on OARI, built the timeline as a condition of his support — a mechanism to force the initiative from authorization into operation before bureaucratic gravity could slow it to a crawl. On Day 40, Tran's office has indicated he will formally request a status update from the Mayor's office and the Police Chief. That's five days from now. It is the first hard political checkpoint the implementation will face.

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, the OARI program director, has built what she can build without dispatch integration. Twelve crisis response workers have completed training. The program has leased space in West Oakland for its operations center. An equipment budget of $2.1 million has been allocated and partially spent. What she does not have is a signed protocol that lets her team answer calls.

"We are operationally ready for pilot deployment in a single district," Tran-Muñoz said in a statement to the Tribune this week. "What we are waiting for is the routing mechanism that connects us to the people who need us."

OPP Councilmember Janae Rivers, whose District 5 is one of the three named pilot zones for OARI's deployment, framed the delay as institutional resistance. "Every day without a signed dispatch protocol is a day where someone in crisis gets a badge instead of a counselor," Rivers said. "Chief Montez has had this document for three weeks."

CRC Councilmember Nina Chen, who voted against OARI, sees the delay differently. "This is what happens when you build a 45-day clock for a program that needs a 180-day runway," Chen said. "The dispatch question isn't bureaucratic foot-dragging. It's the hardest operational problem in the entire initiative, and rushing it creates liability."

If Day 45 arrives without a signed dispatch protocol, the contingency framework — drafted by Tran and accepted by the OPP majority — allows for a single-district pilot deployment using direct referrals from community organizations rather than 911 dispatch integration. It would be a smaller program. It would not be the program the council authorized. But it would exist.

Council President Ramon Vega, who has maintained studied neutrality on OARI's implementation pace, has scheduled a Committee of the Whole review for mid-September — roughly two weeks after the Day 45 deadline. That timing is not accidental. Vega is positioning himself to evaluate the outcome, not the process.

The city is ten days from finding out whether OARI launches as designed, launches diminished, or enters a political limbo that neither the council nor the mayor's office has planned for. The clock doesn't care which one they choose.

The city is holding two clocks this week.

One is OARI's — Day 35 of 45, the dispatch protocol unsigned, the police chief quiet, the council watching. Carmen's reporting lays it out with the precision this story deserves: the program is built but can't answer the phone. Ten days to find out what that means.

This edition holds all of it. The waiting and the living. The policy and the parish. The countdown and the feast day. Oakland has always been a city that carries more than one thing at a time. This week, we're paying attention to what it's carrying.

**The Check That Came and the Line That Didn't Move**

*Beverly Hayes got her $18,500. The 277 families behind her got a processing rate.*

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

Beverly Hayes, 58, a tenant rights advocate from West Oakland, received her Stabilization Fund disbursement this week. The check was for $18,500. She is one of twelve families — the first twelve — to receive money from the $4.2 million initial funding pool that the council authorized last spring.

The announcement from the Office of Economic and Workforce Development framed the disbursement as a milestone. It is a milestone. Twelve families who applied for help received it. That is real. What the announcement did not address, and what this beat has been tracking since the fund was established, is the 277 households still in the processing queue.

The math is not complicated. OEWD processes between 15 and 18 applications per month with its current staff of three reviewers. At that rate, the remaining 277 families are looking at a minimum of fifteen months to clear the queue — closer to eighteen if the pace doesn't accelerate. If the next round of funding authorization doesn't arrive before the initial pool is exhausted, the queue doesn't just slow. It stops.

CRC Councilmember Warren Ashford has filed a formal request for quarterly processing audits — a mechanism that would force OEWD to report its throughput publicly every ninety days. "The fund works," Ashford said. "The question is whether it works at a pace that matches the need. Right now, it doesn't."

At St. Columba Catholic Church in Fruitvale, where the Assumption of Mary drew a full house this week, parishioners navigated the fund's existence alongside their own. The faith institutions — St. Columba, the Cathedral of Christ the Light, First Presbyterian, Shiva Vishnu Temple — have been filling the gap that the fund was designed to close. They've been doing it longer and, so far, faster.

Beverly Hayes has her check. The 277 do not. The processing rate is the processing rate. The faith communities are still open on Saturday mornings.

**The Gap at the Top of Oakland's Economic Machine**

*Deputy Mayor for Economic Development Marcus Osei is recovering. His portfolio is uncovered. Nobody has said who's in charge.*

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

Deputy Mayor Marcus Osei has been on medical leave for two weeks. His office has confirmed he is recovering from what a spokesperson described as a cardiac event. The Tribune wishes him well and expects the city to do the same.

What the Tribune also expects — and has not received — is a clear answer about who is managing the economic development portfolio that Osei oversees. His responsibilities include the Office of Economic and Workforce Development, the Baylight Construction Authority liaison, the Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, and the Stabilization Fund implementation oversight. These are not minor portfolios. They are the largest active economic commitments in Oakland city government.

The Mayor's office has indicated that coordination on economic development matters will be "handled internally" during Osei's absence. That is not a formal delegation of authority. It is not an acting appointment. It is not a public announcement with a defined scope. It is a phrase offered in response to a reporter's question.

The Baylight Construction Authority has filed all five of its pre-mobilization deliverables. The remediation bond instrument is filed but not executed. The September 15 mobilization threshold approaches. These filings require city-side review and sign-off. Osei's office handled that review. Who handles it now?

The Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, announced more than a year ago with a $12.5 million commitment, has produced no public enrollment update. The pipeline is designed to feed qualified workers into Baylight's 560 local-hire construction positions. If the pipeline hasn't built its bench, the local-hire commitment becomes a number on paper. Osei's office tracked this. Who tracks it now?

This is not about one man's health. It is about whether Oakland's economic infrastructure has a single point of failure, and whether the answer to that question is yes.

**BUSINESS TICKER: The Queue Behind the Announcement**

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

Beverly Hayes has been waiting since early spring. She's 58, a tenant rights advocate from West Oakland, and she was approved for $18,500 in Stabilization Fund assistance months before the first check was cut. This week, her name is on the list. Twelve families are receiving the first disbursements from the $4.2 million initial funding pool — twelve out of 277 still in the queue at the Office of Economic and Workforce Development. The math behind the announcement is where the policy gets difficult. I've reported this before and it bears repeating: processing runs at roughly 15 to 18 approvals a month. At that pace, the remaining families are looking at close to two years before the queue clears — if it clears, if the fund holds, if the next funding authorization comes before the need outpaces the money. CRC member Warren Ashford has filed a request for quarterly audits on processing velocity, which is the right impulse translated into bureaucratic language. I've been following Darius Clark, a bakery worker in West Oakland who was handed an application form in the spring and told to wait. I don't yet know whether he's among the twelve or still among the two hundred and seventy-seven. That uncertainty is its own kind of answer about what "disbursement underway" actually means on 7th Street.

Across the water, the Baylight Construction Authority has filed every document required for the September 15 mobilization threshold — all five deliverables on schedule, including the remediation bond instrument that needs to clear the soil on the former waterfront parcel before a shovel goes in. Filed is not executed. Keisha Ramos, the Authority's director, has confirmed the filings are complete. What hasn't happened is bond execution, which is what actually triggers mobilization. The 1,400 construction jobs with a 40 percent local hire target — 560 of those positions going to Oakland residents — are the most significant labor commitment this waterfront corridor has seen in years. The Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, now twelve months from its initial $12.5 million announcement with no public update on track enrollment, is theoretically the infrastructure that feeds qualified Oakland workers into those jobs when construction begins. Whether the pipeline has built the bench it needs is a question neither the Baylight Authority nor City Hall has answered publicly. I spoke with Travis Green, 43, a construction laborer who works out of the Jack London area and has been watching the Baylight site for months. "Every week someone tells me it's almost time," he said. "Almost is a long time to hold your breath."

The one part of Oakland's economy not waiting on paperwork is Jack London after dark. Summer has pushed foot traffic along the waterfront corridor to its highest levels of the year, and the restaurants have been doing real business — Dockhouse BBQ, Green & Gold Tavern, and the Blue Lantern Bar running late covers on weekends, Harborline Grill holding steady as the corridor's dinner anchor. Ray Muñoz, 54, a longshoreman who knows this waterfront as well as anyone, told me last week that the evening crowds are different from what he's used to seeing — less local, more citywide, people who came down for summer and found a neighborhood worth staying in. "Jack London has always been good for a drink after a shift," he said. "Now it's good for dinner too." That's not a small distinction for the restaurant owners along Water Street who have been watching their Saturday reservation books fill in ways they haven't seen in two summers. Whether the Baylight construction crews — already established as a lunchtime customer base along the corridor — eventually convert into permanent neighborhood economic density is the longer bet every operator on this strip is quietly making.

**The Doors That Open in August**

*Two churches, two neighborhoods, one week of Oakland holding its faith calendar against the news*

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

St. Columba Catholic Church in Fruitvale opened its doors for community support on Wednesday. Not for Mass — though that came later — but for the thing that happens before and after Mass in a parish that knows its neighborhood is carrying weight: the tables set up in the fellowship hall, the coffee, the box of diapers someone left by the entrance, the sign-up sheet for the Saturday food pantry that fills up before Friday.

Calvin Turner, 38, a mechanic from Fruitvale who volunteers at the parish's Saturday food pantry, was stacking folding chairs in the side aisle when I arrived. He does this most weeks. The chairs are old and they stick, and he has a way of popping them open with one hand that suggests he stopped counting how many times he's done this a long time ago.

"Father Avila asked if we could do two community nights this month instead of one," Calvin said. "I said yeah. What else am I going to do — sit home and read about the Stabilization Fund?"

He wasn't being cynical. He was being precise. The $4.2 million fund approved by the council last spring has not reached Fruitvale in any form that the people inside St. Columba can point to. The faith institutions fill the space: the weekly food pantry, the seniors lunch, the informal networks that form around parish hall tables and remain long after the last plate is cleared.

Father Manuel Avila, who has led St. Columba for eleven years, was in the back office when I stopped in. He said something I wrote down: "We are not the safety net. We are the floor. The safety net is what the city promised. We were here before that."

Across town on the same day, the Cathedral of Christ the Light on Grand Avenue observed the Assumption of Mary — the high holy day marking Mary's taking up into heaven. The Cathedral draws from across the city, not from one neighborhood. On Wednesday evening, its nave held Lake Merritt families, Downtown workers who stopped in after the commute, and a few faces I recognized from Fruitvale — people who attend St. Columba on Sundays but come to the Cathedral for the Assumption because the building itself feels like the occasion demands it.

The Assumption doesn't pause for policy deadlines or dispatch disputes or implementation clocks. It arrives on the calendar the way it always has, and the churches that observe it do what they've always done: light the candles, fill the pews, say the words.

Oakland's faith institutions are running at elevated capacity this summer. St. Columba and the Cathedral are two of the reasons. They were doing this work before the policy arrived. They're doing it while the policy waits.

Calvin Turner locked up the fellowship hall around nine. The candles in the nave were still burning. Fruitvale was quiet outside — not empty, just drawn in, the way it gets in late August when the heat has been sitting on the neighborhood for weeks and everyone is managing something they haven't named yet.

"Same time next week," he said to no one in particular, and walked to his truck.

**The Other Calendar**

*Shiva Vishnu Temple observes Janmashtami as Oakland's interfaith August continues*

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

The Shiva Vishnu Temple in Montclair observed Janmashtami on Thursday evening — the birthday of Lord Krishna, one of the most joyful dates on the Hindu calendar. The celebration ran past midnight. The parking lot on Shepherd Canyon Road was full by eight.

I arrived around nine-thirty. The temple was bright and loud in the best way — families with children, the smell of prasad from the kitchen, devotional singing that carried out through the open doors into the Montclair hills. This is not a somber observance. Janmashtami celebrates the birth of a god who was, among other things, a trickster and a butter thief. The mood inside matched.

A woman near the entrance was arranging marigolds around a small Krishna figure. Her daughter, maybe six, was trying to help and mostly scattering petals. Neither seemed to mind.

Oakland's sacred calendar in August is fuller than most people realize. The Assumption at the Cathedral and St. Columba. Community support nights at First Presbyterian Downtown. And now Janmashtami in Montclair — a different tradition, a different neighborhood, the same impulse: mark the time, gather the people, hold what matters against everything else.

First Presbyterian Church downtown opened its doors for community support this week too — a quieter affair, a Wednesday evening with coffee and conversation in the fellowship hall, the kind of gathering that doesn't make the news but keeps a congregation connected. The church sits near City Hall, close enough that the people who attend can see the building where the Stabilization Fund and OARI are being debated. They come anyway. The sacred calendar doesn't wait for the civic one.

The Shiva Vishnu Temple has been in Montclair for decades, serving a Hindu community that stretches across the East Bay. Janmashtami draws families from Fremont, from Berkeley, from neighborhoods across Oakland that don't otherwise intersect. On Thursday night, the temple was one of the fullest rooms in the city.

By midnight, the celebration had reached its peak — the moment marking Krishna's birth. The singing intensified. Children who should have been asleep hours ago were wide awake and vibrating. The parking lot was still full.

Oakland held four faith observances this week across four neighborhoods. Catholic, Presbyterian, Hindu. Fruitvale, Lake Merritt, Downtown, Montclair. The sacred calendar doesn't coordinate. It doesn't need to. The city is large enough to hold all of it at once, and this week it did.

**Oakland After Dark**

*The city's late-summer nightlife surge is real, and it's not just Jack London*

By Kai Marston | Bay Tribune Arts & Entertainment

Something happened to Oakland's nights this August. They got longer. Not astronomically — the days are already shortening. But the city is staying out later, spending more, filling sidewalks and patios and bar stools that six months ago were empty by ten.

The numbers back it up. Foot traffic along the Jack London waterfront has hit its highest levels of the summer. But Jack London isn't the whole story. Temescal's OakTown Social has been running weekend covers for the first time. Downtown's Temple Lounge and Pulse District are pulling crowds that spill onto the sidewalk. The Merritt Club on Lakeshore has a line on Saturdays now — something the owner told me hasn't happened since before the pandemic.

This is what a prosperity city looks like when the weather cooperates and the calendar is clear. Sixty-three degrees and clear skies. No major civic disruptions on the weekend calendar. People with paychecks and nowhere urgent to be.

I walked the Uptown corridor on Saturday around nine. The galleries on Telegraph that feed into First Friday energy were still lit, even though First Friday was weeks ago. A couple argued about where to eat — KONO Kitchen or Midnight Bistro — and split the difference by getting drinks at Dragon Gate Lounge in Chinatown first. The arts scene in Oakland doesn't wait for a festival or a designated night. It runs on its own clock, and right now that clock says stay out.

The cultural activity across the city is elevated — not just nightlife, but public spaces, retail, the kind of ambient energy you feel when a city is using itself. Lake Merritt on a Sunday afternoon. The Saturday market at Jack London. The taqueria on International that doesn't close until the last customer leaves, which this week meant midnight.

There's a temptation to read this as escapism — the city going out because the civic news is heavy and the Stabilization Fund checks are slow and the OARI clock is ticking. I don't think that's what this is. Oakland doesn't go out to forget. Oakland goes out because August is here and the city knows how to be in August. The worry doesn't leave. It just shares the sidewalk with everything else.

By eleven on Saturday, the Jack London corridor was still going. Blue Lantern Bar had its patio full. Somewhere in Temescal, OakTown Social was playing something with horns. Somewhere in Montclair, the Janmashtami celebration at Shiva Vishnu Temple was approaching midnight and the parking lot was still packed.

The city was awake. All of it. At once.

**They Put Oakland on the List. Where Is Everybody?**

*The NBA expansion committee named Oakland a candidate city for 2042. The man who could make it happen was courtside in Chicago.*

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

I'm going to tell you something that happened this week and then I'm going to tell you why it made me angry and then I'm going to tell you why the anger turned into something else.

The NBA Board of Governors released its preliminary expansion candidate list for 2042. Oakland is on it. We're on the list. This city — this particular city, the one that lost the Warriors to San Francisco and spent years pretending it didn't sting — is being considered for a franchise.

And the man who could make it happen, the man who has more capital, more sports infrastructure, and more name recognition than anyone else in Oakland's orbit, was sitting courtside at the United Center in Chicago watching his Bulls clinch the number one seed in the East.

Mike Paulson. Owner of the A's. Executive of the Year for the Bulls. The most visible sports figure in two cities and the invisible man in this one particular conversation.

I asked Dante Nelson about it. Dante's 41, a security guard from Downtown who's been going to A's games since before the Coliseum renovations. He remembers Oakland Arena. He remembers the Warriors. He remembers what it felt like to have two professional sports teams and a city that could hold both.

"They put us on a list," Dante said. "Lists don't build arenas."

He's right. But the list exists. And the Baylight waterfront, where Paulson's construction authority is about to break ground on a $2.1 billion development that includes — in its original design documents — a flexible-use arena site, is the only plausible location for an NBA facility in Oakland.

The two-city tension that has defined Paulson's public life for the last two years is not going away. It's getting louder. He built the best team in the Eastern Conference. He's building the most ambitious waterfront project Oakland has seen in a generation. And the NBA just told Oakland that the door is open, and the only person with a key is standing in a different time zone.

I don't know what Paulson wants. Nobody does. That's the problem.

**Ramos Gets the Ball**

*The A's announce their 2041 rotation. The order tells the story.*

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

The Oakland A's released their Opening Day rotation Thursday, and the order reads like a generational handoff written in pitching assignments:

1. Eduardo Ramos — RHP
2. Marcus Rivas — LHP
3. Travis Coles — RHP
4. Jarrett Ellis — LHP
5. Benji Dillon — LHP

Ramos gets the ball first. That is the headline, and it is the right one. The 26-year-old right-hander earned this with a season that announced him as the staff's future: a 2.89 ERA, 201 strikeouts, and a consistency that made the decision feel less like a competition and more like an acknowledgment.

Rivas slots in at two, the lefty complement who has been the rotation's steadiest arm for three years now. Coles, acquired this offseason on a two-year, $46.18 million deal, takes the three spot — the veteran addition who gives this rotation its depth. Ellis, the second lefty, holds four with the mid-rotation reliability that keeps a staff from leaning too hard on its top end.

And Dillon. Benji Dillon at five.

Five-time Cy Young Award winner. The man who defined what this rotation was for the better part of a decade. Slot five is not a demotion. It is what time does to everyone, even the best. Dillon's fastball has dropped from 95 to 91. His slider still breaks, but later, and hitters have started sitting on it. He is 38 years old and he is the fifth starter on a team that has four arms younger and, this spring, better.

The season opens next week against the Angels. Ramos will take the mound first. Dillon will take it fifth. Between those two assignments is everything this franchise has been and everything it is becoming.

**The Fifth Starter**

*Benji Dillon defined a dynasty from the front of the rotation. Now he holds it together from the back.*

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

I have watched Benji Dillon pitch since his first start in Oakland. I was in the press box when he threw seven shutout innings against the Yankees in what turned out to be the opening salvo of the dynasty's first championship run. I watched the fastball that sat at 96, the slider that broke so late it looked illegal, the left-handed delivery that hitters said they could time in the cage and couldn't touch in the game.

That was a different pitcher. Not a different person — the same quiet, methodical, almost eerily composed presence who has occupied the same locker in the A's clubhouse for nine years. But a different arm. The arm that threw 96 now throws 91. The slider still breaks. It breaks later. Hitters have adjusted. Dillon has adjusted to their adjustments. But the margin is thinner. The margin has been thinning for two years.

Slot five in the 2041 rotation is not an insult. It is not a demotion. It is what time does. Ramos earned the top of this rotation with his 2.89 ERA and his 201 strikeouts and his 26-year-old arm that hasn't learned yet what it's going to lose. Rivas, Coles, and Ellis earned their spots. Dillon's slot is the one that was left, and he will take it the way he has taken every assignment — without complaint, without drama, with the quiet professionalism that made him a five-time Cy Young winner and will make him a Hall of Famer.

What I want this city to understand is what it means to have a pitcher of Dillon's caliber willing to pitch fifth. It means the rotation is deep enough to put a future Hall of Famer at the back. It means the dynasty built something that doesn't collapse when its best arm ages. It means the transition is happening the way transitions are supposed to happen — gradually, with dignity, with the old guard holding the door for the new one instead of being pushed through it.

I don't know how many more starts Dillon has in Oakland. He hasn't said. The front office hasn't said. This may be the last season, or it may not. What I know is this: when Dillon takes the mound fifth in the rotation, the four pitchers ahead of him will know who's behind them. That's not nothing. In baseball, that's almost everything.

**The Awards Don't Answer the Question**

*Bulls finish 58-24, clinch the East's top seed. Paulson wins Executive of the Year. The ownership statement still says what it says.*

By Selena Grant | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

Let me be precise about what happened at the end of the regular season: Mike Paulson built the best team in the Eastern Conference, finished 58-24, and was named the NBA's Executive of the Year. And then the ownership statement that has been hanging over this franchise all spring was still hanging there, unchanged, unretracted — "long term commitments were not made."

The Bulls enter the playoffs as the number one seed. They drew Orlando, the eighth seed, in the first round. The matchup is favorable on paper, which is the least interesting thing about where this franchise is standing right now.

Start with Hank Trepagnier, because the league already has. Trepagnier won Rookie of the Year. He earned a spot on the All-Rookie First Team. Earlier this season, he took home NBA Cup MVP. Final averages: 16.7 points, 8.2 rebounds, 5.1 assists, 1.3 steals, and 1.5 blocks per game. That last line is why the ROY conversation was brief — nobody else in the rookie class put up a defensive stat line alongside an offensive one. Trepagnier is nineteen years old and he is playing like someone who has watched the game film of everyone trying to guard him and decided to make those adjustments irrelevant.

Adash Stanley, his classmate, earned All-Rookie Second Team honors at 12.1 points, 5.1 assists, and 42 percent from three-point range. The 42 percent matters as much as anything else — Stanley has been Chicago's most reliable off-ball shooter in critical possessions, and playoff defenses will be forced to respect that. His technical foul count — six on the season, three in a four-game stretch earlier — is the one knot in an otherwise clean statistical argument. Playoff officiating is different. Stanley will need to find a way to play with the same edge and fewer flagged moments.

Then there is Josh Giddey, whose season deserves more than the bullet-point treatment it tends to receive. Career year: 19.8 points, 7.0 rebounds, 7.4 assists, 1.4 steals per game. The All-Star snub came back in January and sent Giddey to a South Side youth clinic instead of an All-Star skills competition. What followed was a sustained second half of the season that can be read as either a response to the national media's blind spots or simply the natural progression of a player who had been building toward this. Either way, the production is real. The turnover rate dropped. The shot selection matured in ways I have watched on tape — fewer pull-up threes in traffic, more early-clock attacks that made defenses choose. Giddey enters the playoffs as a genuine postseason threat. The Magic will need to account for him.

Paulson's Executive of the Year award recognizes what he built. The front office has assembled two All-Rookie caliber players, a career-year point guard, and a 58-win regular season in the architect's second year in Chicago. By that accounting, this is the best sports organization in the Eastern Conference.

And by another accounting — the one that Paulson has not addressed publicly, and that Golden State has not stopped whispering about — he is the best executive in basketball without a long-term commitment from the franchise that employs him.

The award and the ownership statement arrived in the same news week. One said Paulson is the best in the business. The other said no one made promises. The Bulls are in the playoffs. The question remains open.

CHICAGO GROUND

**The Umbrella She Didn't Bring**

*Bridgeport processes the best season in franchise history the way Bridgeport processes everything — over coffee, in the rain.*

By Talia Finch | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

The storm came in off the lake fast on Thursday morning, the way August storms do in Chicago — no negotiation, no warning, just a wall of gray moving south along Lake Shore Drive and then rain hammering everything at once. I was already at Romano's on 35th when it started. The ceramic mugs were full. The ceiling fans were off for the first time in weeks. Outside, 35th Street turned silver.

Paulette Okafor came in with her hair wet. She works the mail route two blocks off Halsted, and she'd walked from the bus stop without an umbrella — not because she forgot, she said, but because she thought she could beat it.

"I did not beat it," she said, setting her bag down and pulling up the seat across from mine.

She's been coming to Romano's most Thursday mornings for years. I've been finding her here for the last few months, ever since the ownership statement came out. She works with children all day; she thinks in terms of what you can plan for and what you can't.

The Bulls finished 58-24. Number one seed in the East. First round against Orlando. Paulson won Executive of the Year. We both knew all of this when we sat down. The television above the counter had the morning sports segment on mute, the crawl running through the award announcements.

"That's real," Paulette said, looking at the screen for a moment. "That's not something anyone can take away. The award, the record — that's documented."

I asked her what it felt like to watch it, given everything.

She thought about it. She picked up her mug. Outside, rain was sheeting down the windows and the gutters were running fast.

"It feels like watching someone win a prize at the company picnic the day before they might put in their two weeks," she said. "You're proud of them. You want to celebrate. And you just — you keep waiting for the other thing."

Raymond Polk came in twenty minutes later, also wet, carrying an umbrella he'd given up on about thirty seconds after opening it. He's retired — used to work custodial at one of the schools — but he'd been out early running errands before the rain started.

"Executive of the Year," he said, before he even sat down. "I saw it this morning."

He got his coffee. He sat across from Paulette, the two of them as familiar with each other in this booth as people who have been processing the same news cycle for months, which is exactly what they are.

"He deserves it," Raymond said. "Nobody's arguing that. I want to be clear that I'm not the person who's going to sit here and say he doesn't deserve it."

Paulette looked at him over her mug. "Nobody said you were."

"Right," he said. "It's just — you win Executive of the Year, and the ownership statement is still out there. Still says what it says. And you want to celebrate the award, and you want to believe that this is the thing that locks him in, that now the numbers are so good that it has to happen—"

He stopped. Outside the storm was getting worse, not better. The Red Line was still running but the platform at 35th would be soaked.

"Does it?" Paulette said. Not to him specifically. More to the mug.

That's where Romano's was on Thursday morning. The city holding a 58-win season like an umbrella it brought but knows won't be enough if the real weather comes. Playoff basketball starting this week. And somewhere in a conference room, the question that has shadowed this season since March still sitting on a table, unanswered.

The city went to work in the rain. The Bulls go to the playoffs. The ownership statement remains exactly what it was.

LETTERS TO THE EDITOR
############################################################

Dear Editor,

I've written to you before about the Stabilization Fund. I'm writing again because you reported twelve families got their checks this month, and I want those twelve people to know that somebody in that queue of two hundred and seventy-seven was rooting for them.

I'm somewhere in that two hundred and seventy-seven. I don't know where. I've called twice and both times the woman who answered was kind and had nothing new to tell me.

I keep reading the updates the way you read a departures board at the airport. Looking for movement. The Tribune said the processing rate is fifteen to eighteen applications a month. I did the math. That's at minimum eighteen months to clear the queue if nothing changes and nobody new applies.

I'm not saying this to be grim. The money is real, the fund is real, the twelve families who got checks this week are real. I believe that. What I need is someone to tell me eighteen months is the honest number, or give me a different honest number. I can work with a number. What I can't work with is silence.

— Carla Edmonds, 58, West Oakland

---

Dear Editor,

The Feast of the Assumption fell on a Saturday this year and St. Columba was full. Father Avila's been our pastor for eleven years now and I've never seen him look more at peace than he did that morning. The altar guild had flowers everywhere — white dahlias, marigolds. Somebody had piled more at the base of the Mary statue outside. I had to step around them to get to the door.

People in my parish are carrying things right now. The Transit Hub vote. The Stabilization Fund. Grown children who left the neighborhood because they couldn't afford to stay. We pray, and then we go home and read the Tribune and worry about the same things we were praying about.

I'm not saying that's wrong. The sacred and the practical have always lived together in Fruitvale. They have to.

What I want to say to the city is this: your faith communities are still here. Still full on Saturdays in August. Still providing what no fund can quite provide. I hope somebody in government is paying attention to that, too.

— Sister Rosario Aguilar, 67, Fruitvale

---

Dear Editor,

I sold my produce market on International Boulevard three years ago. I ran it for twenty-two years, one block from the Fruitvale BART station. I know what it looks like when the city makes transit promises to this neighborhood. I have watched the station from that block since the nineties.

The morning after the council vote I walked past. Same crack in the sidewalk at the corner of East 12th. Same trash can that the city empties every other Tuesday if you're lucky. Nothing had changed yet. Of course nothing had changed yet.

But. Seven to one. I'll admit that number sat with me. Warren Ashford crossing over. That's not nothing. Seven to one is different from five to four. It means something different about what the council actually believes.

The construction won't start for years. I know this. The community design sessions are next spring. The final vote is still a long way off. I'll be older than I want to think about. I might not see the ribbon cut.

Still. Seven to one. I'm watching.

— Miguel Orozco, 71, Fruitvale

---

Dear Editor,

I get off work at eleven. I cook at a restaurant in Jack London and I walk home through West Oakland most nights because I like it better than the bus at that hour. Better air. Quieter. Mine.

But this August is different. The tamale lady on Peralta Street — I don't know her name, I just know her cart — she's been set up past ten o'clock every single night this month. That's new. She was never out that late before. I asked her once and she said "it's busy, mijo," and went back to her work.

People are out. The parks aren't empty at nine. There are conversations happening on stoops that would have been inside a year ago. Something about this summer feels like the city exhaled.

I'm going to say something honest. Last year I thought about moving. The rent, the hours, the feeling that Oakland was always just about to tip into something bad. I didn't go. I got stubborn about it.

I'm glad. I walked home last Tuesday night and it felt like mine again.

— Jemaine Farris, 29, West Oakland
