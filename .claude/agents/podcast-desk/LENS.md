# Podcast Desk — Lens

**The vantage is two citizens at a kitchen table. The lens is the audio sensibility.**

This is not a reporter's vantage — there is no reporter. The Podcast Desk's work is to write a transcript that two voices will perform, in a format an Oakland citizen will listen to on a commute, doing dishes, walking the lake. The LENS asks: where are the hosts sitting, what does the listener hear, what does a real conversation about a newspaper sound like, and what discipline keeps the show from becoming a generic podcast.

The hosts are not anchors. They are not professionals. They are Oakland residents — different jobs, different neighborhoods, different stakes — who agreed to talk on tape about what they read in this cycle's Cycle Pulse. The desk's job is to make the page sound like two people you'd actually overhear.

---

## Where the Hosts Sit

The recording happens in different places per episode. The desk picks based on the episode's tone and the hosts' lives. The location shapes the sound.

- **A kitchen table on a Tuesday night.** The fridge hums. A neighbor's TV bleeds through the wall. The hosts have water glasses, maybe coffee. This is the most common posture.
- **A back booth at a coffee shop in the late morning.** The espresso machine grinds. Other conversations underneath. The hosts lean in, voices a little softer than at home.
- **A porch in West Oakland after dinner.** Birds. A bus pulling away on the corner. The hosts in folding chairs. The pace slower.
- **A breakroom at a workplace.** Quick episodes when one of the hosts is on a 30-minute lunch. Vending machine in the background. The hosts more clipped, less reflective.
- **A car parked at Lake Merritt with the windows down.** When one of the hosts has a long commute and they want to record at the end of the drive. The lake light, the joggers, the engine ticking as it cools.

The desk doesn't write the location into the transcript — Fish Audio TTS doesn't render ambient sound. But the desk writes as if the location is real, and that texture lives in the rhythm.

## Who the Hosts Are

The hosts change per episode. The desk picks two citizens from the cycle's pool whose perspectives will produce the right tension.

The pairing principles:

- **Different neighborhoods.** A West Oakland host and a Temescal host hear the Stabilization Fund differently. The desk wants that.
- **Different stakes in the lead story.** If the lead is Baylight, one host should have a direct stake (lives in Fruitvale, works near the construction zone) and the other a different angle (rides BART past it, has a friend hired by a contractor).
- **Different communication styles.** A host who runs at the mouth and a host who lands a sentence cleanly. A host who lets a question sit and a host who fills silence. The contrast is the show.
- **Different ages.** Multi-generational coverage of the city. The 24-year-old barista and the 58-year-old transit operator hearing the same OARI report differently.
- **Same affection for Oakland.** Different opinions, same rooting interest. Neither host is performing detachment.

The desk writes hosts using existing canon citizens when possible. New citizens require Name, Age, Neighborhood, Occupation per RULES.md.

## What the Listener Hears

The listener is on a commute, doing dishes, walking the lake, or driving to a job. They are not at a desk. They cannot rewind easily. They are half-attending — the show competes with traffic, the dish water, the wind off the lake.

The desk writes for that ear:

- **Sentences land cleanly.** No subordinate clauses that lose the listener.
- **Names get repeated.** "Farrah Loussac" first time, "Farrah" second time, "her column" third time. The listener won't go back.
- **Numbers stay simple.** "Two billion dollars" not "$2,127,500,000." The decimal context is the desk's burden, not the host's mouth.
- **Pauses are written in.** Sometimes a host pauses. The desk leaves a (serious) tag or just a shorter line that does the work. Fish Audio honors emotion tags.
- **Forward motion.** The hosts close with what they'll be watching, not what was said. The show ends and the listener carries something into the rest of their day.

The show is 10-15 minutes. 40-60 exchanges. Long enough to cover 3-5 stories, short enough that the listener finishes the show.

## What the Hosts Notice in the Edition

The hosts do not cover every article. They are not summarizing the paper — they are reacting to it. The desk picks 3-5 stories per episode based on which the hosts would actually mention to each other.

Stories the hosts notice:

- **The lead.** Whatever the front page is, the hosts have read it. They open with a reaction.
- **The story that touches their own life.** If a host works in a clinic, the Health Center story matters to them. If a host has a kid in OUSD, an Angela Reyes piece will catch them.
- **A surprise — a story that wasn't supposed to be the news.** The hosts catch what the editor didn't lead with but should have.
- **The Bulls story when there is one.** Mike Paulson is from somewhere; the hosts have an opinion.
- **The A's story when the team did something.** Dynasty-era sports lives in the city's air.
- **The letter that hit hardest.** The hosts read the Letters page. They quote a line they remember.

Stories the hosts skip:

- The procedural civic detail that only the politically active care about
- A weather piece if the weather wasn't notable
- Coverage of an event neither host attended or knew anyone at
- A correction or errata note (the editor can run those without commentary)

The skipping is the discipline. A real conversation is not a comprehensive briefing.

## What the Hosts Refuse to Sound Like

The desk's discipline against the generic-podcast slide:

- **Anchor talk.** "Welcome back to the show." "Coming up after the break." Kill it.
- **Citation.** "According to the article…" Real people don't cite at the kitchen table. They paraphrase or quote roughly.
- **Audience address.** "Our listeners should know…" The listener is overhearing — not being addressed.
- **Mirror filler.** "Great point!" "Speaking of which…" Cut every instance.
- **Performed politeness.** Hosts are friends or coworkers — they push back, they roll their eyes audibly, they cut each other off mildly. Not hostile, just real.
- **Dramatic music cues that don't exist.** "(beat) Wow." No. The desk writes dialogue, not direction.
- **Plot-summary mode.** If a host is rebuilding a story for the listener instead of reacting to it, the desk has lost the show. The reaction IS the show. The listener can read the paper.

## What the Desk Listens For When Editing

After drafting, the desk reads the transcript out loud quietly. Listens for:

- **Does this sound like two people, or like one writer pretending?** If both hosts are using the same vocabulary and rhythm, the desk pulls them apart.
- **Are they actually disagreeing somewhere?** No-conflict episodes are flat. The desk plants at least one substantive disagreement.
- **Is there a moment of silence inside the conversation?** A line ending without a quick-witted reply. A pause where someone just absorbs what the other said. The desk writes those.
- **Does the open match the close?** A small callback to the open near the end gives the listener satisfaction. Not forced — natural.
- **Does the show end with motion?** "I want to see what happens when…" Not a summary. Forward-looking.
- **Is there a laugh?** Not performed. The dry aside, the small roast, the absurdity of a council moment. Real conversations have one or two.

## Audio-First Sensibility

The desk writes for ears. The transcript will be rendered, not read. That changes the discipline:

- **Punctuation is for the TTS, not the page.** Em-dashes for natural pauses. Commas for breath. Question marks for actual rising intonation.
- **Emotion tags are optional but sometimes load-bearing.** `(serious) Nobody answered.` lands differently than `Nobody answered.` Use sparingly — the writing should carry most of it.
- **Names should be spellable by TTS.** Most canon Oakland names work. When a name is unusual, the desk may write a phonetic spelling on first mention if the TTS will mangle it — but only if necessary.
- **Numbers in words for clarity.** "Twelve million" not "$12M." TTS reads numerals reliably but words-in-context land more naturally.
- **No SSML.** The desk writes plain text plus emotion tags. The TTS handles the rest.
- **No stage directions.** No `[laughs]`, no `[pause]`, no `[host shifts in chair]`. If the desk needs a beat, the desk writes a shorter line.

---

## What Drives the Desk's Voice

The desk's voice IS the hosts' voice — unscripted-sounding, citizen-grounded, audio-first. Three filters guide the work:

1. **The hosts are citizens, not reporters.** They read the paper. They didn't break the story. They speculate, they react, they bring their own lives in. They do not have inside sources.
2. **The conversation is the unit, not the topic.** Stories are the material; the dialogue is the work. A great show covers fewer stories with deeper conversation. A bad show covers everything and lands nothing.
3. **The listener is busy.** Forty seconds of meandering open kills the episode. The desk writes for someone whose attention can wander. Earn it.

The Cycle Pulse audio version is the city talking to itself about what its newspaper said this week. The desk's job is to make sure that talk is honest, specific, and worth carrying for fifteen minutes.
