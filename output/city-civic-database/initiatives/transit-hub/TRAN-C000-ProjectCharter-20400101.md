Transit-Oriented Development Initiative Status: Conceptual Planning — Community Input Phase

I. Project Overview
Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II expands the successful Fruitvale Village development into a full mixed-use district surrounding the BART station. The project adds housing, retail, community services, and public space to an already-functioning transit node.
This is the test case. If Oakland can only build transformative projects on premium waterfront land, Baylight is an exception. If the model works at Fruitvale — inland, working-class, Latino-majority — it becomes replicable.
Project Area: 18 acres surrounding Fruitvale BART Target Completion: Year 6 Lead Agency: City of Oakland + Unity Council (community development partner)

II. Background: Phase I Success
Fruitvale Village (Phase I) opened in 2004. It proved transit-oriented development could work in Oakland:
Phase I Metrics	Results
Housing Units	47 (100% affordable)
Retail Space	40,000 sq ft
Community Space	Library branch, health clinic, senior center
Annual Foot Traffic	1.2M visitors
BART Ridership Impact	+18% at Fruitvale station
Phase I transformed a parking lot into a neighborhood anchor. Phase II expands the model to adjacent parcels.

X. Why This Matters
Baylight is a $2.8B waterfront megaproject with a baseball stadium as its anchor. It will succeed or fail on its own terms.
Fruitvale Phase II asks a different question: Can Oakland build neighborhood-scale, transit-oriented, majority-affordable housing in a working-class community without displacement, without a stadium, without premium land?
If yes, the model scales. East Oakland, San Antonio, Coliseum BART — all become candidates.
If no, Baylight stays an island. Beautiful, successful, and unrepeatable.

XI. Project Narrative
Fruitvale Village was supposed to be a pilot. Twenty years later, it's still the only example.
Phase II finishes what Phase I started — a real neighborhood around the station, not a single building surrounded by parking lots. Housing for families who work in Oakland. Retail that serves the community instead of tourists. A clinic, a childcare center, a plaza where people actually gather.
The land is there. The transit is there. The community organization is there. The question is whether Oakland commits the resources to build what Fruitvale has been waiting two decades to become.
