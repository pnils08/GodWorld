Bay Tribune Editorial — By Mags Corliss
The Weight of Continuity
Oakland, California — February 2040
The winter light over Jack London Square has a strange quality this year — neither warm nor cold, as if the city itself is waiting for permission to move forward.  In the offices of the Bay Tribune, we used to feel that same expectancy every October, knowing the A’s would still be playing when the nights turned sharp.  Now the noise has quieted.  The dynasty that carried a city for more than a decade is beginning to exhale.
Benji Dillon wants one more run, one year, nineteen million dollars, and the right to finish on his terms.  John Brever, once a sure arm, is searching for lost command.  The rotation feels like a patchwork of memory and hope, and somewhere in Double-A a young right-hander named John Ellis stretches in the cold, hearing the whispers of what’s coming.  These are ordinary baseball details, but they point to something larger — the moment when legacy stops being a story and becomes a burden.
I’ve watched this club long enough to understand that every rebuild has two fronts: the ledger and the heart.  The accountants know exactly how much the aging core costs; the fans know exactly what it meant to them.  Between those numbers and those feelings sits a franchise trying to prove it can evolve without erasing its reflection.  Oakland itself has done that dance for decades — fighting to stay whole through relocation rumors, economic shifts, and the slow bleed of modern baseball’s realities.
The question hanging over this spring isn’t whether the A’s can contend again; it’s whether they can keep meaning something while they change.  Continuity isn’t about the same faces in the same uniforms — it’s about keeping the connection between the team, the city, and the people who fill the small spaces between innings.  The ushers who remember every child’s name.  The vendors who still call out Dillon’s slider count.  The reporters who, even after all these years, find themselves writing eulogies disguised as recaps.
Baseball, at its best, teaches you how to let go without forgetting.  Maybe that’s what this offseason is really about — the grace to remember greatness while making room for whatever comes next.  The A’s have always been an experiment in resilience, and maybe the weight of continuity is simply the price of having mattered this long.
