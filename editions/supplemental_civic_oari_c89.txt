THE CYCLE PULSE 89 — SUPPLEMENTAL — CIVIC AFFAIRS
OARI Day 45: The Clock Ran Out
By Carmen Delaine and Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune

DAY 46

The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative missed its deadline.

Forty-five days. That was the implementation window written into the authorization language when the council voted 5-4 to fund a $12.5 million behavioral health pilot program. Three districts. Nine two-person teams. Trained clinicians and peer counselors responding to mental health calls instead of armed officers.

Day 45 came and went last week. No teams are operational. No calls have been diverted. The program exists on paper and nowhere else.

Here is what happened:

Twelve responders have been hired and onboarded. Four additional offers are confirmed. Six candidates remain in final interviews. The staffing pipeline is moving — slowly, but moving.

The vehicles are not ready. Nine response vehicles were ordered. Delivery was projected at four to six weeks from order confirmation. The order was confirmed on Day 22. The vehicles have not arrived.

The staging sites are not secured. District 1 (West Oakland) can use existing Department of Violence Prevention facilities. Districts 3 (Fruitvale) and 5 (Temescal) require new staging arrangements. The Planning Office was formally asked to identify sites and negotiate leases. As of Day 45, no leases have been signed.

And then there’s the dispatch integration.

-----

THE SIGNATURE THAT DIDN’T COME

Chief Rafael Montez of the Oakland Police Department was asked to sign a dispatch integration specification that would allow OARI teams to receive calls routed through OPD’s 911 system. Without that signature, behavioral health teams cannot be dispatched. They can exist. They can train. They cannot respond.

The specification was delivered to Montez’s office on Day 28. It sat there for seventeen days.

Montez did not sign.

His office released a statement on Day 44: “OPD is committed to working with the Department of Violence Prevention on a dispatch protocol that ensures officer safety and appropriate call routing. We are continuing to review the specification and expect to provide a response within the coming weeks.”

The coming weeks. The deadline was yesterday.

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, the OARI Program Director, was asked for comment. “We delivered a specification that met every technical requirement OPD identified in the planning process,” she said. “We answered every question. We revised every section they flagged. The document that’s sitting on the Chief’s desk is the fourth version. At some point, reviewing becomes stalling.”

Montez’s office did not respond to a follow-up request for comment.

-----

THE COUNCIL MEMBERS WHO VOTED YES

Janae Rivers represents District 5. Temescal was supposed to have operational OARI teams by now. It doesn’t.

“I voted for this program because my constituents need it,” Rivers said in a statement. “They’re still waiting. The implementation failures are unacceptable, and Chief Montez’s refusal to sign the dispatch specification is the single largest obstacle. I’ve requested a formal explanation from OPD and expect one before the next council session.”

Rose Delgado represents District 3. Fruitvale was also on the pilot list.

“My district has been asking for behavioral health crisis response for years,” Delgado said. “We finally got the votes. We finally got the funding. And now we’re being told the police department won’t cooperate with the program the council authorized. That’s not oversight. That’s obstruction.”

Leonard Tran gave the fifth vote. His district — D2, which includes Chinatown and parts of Downtown — was not selected for the pilot. He crossed the aisle to support the program anyway.

“I voted yes on OARI because the model is sound,” Tran said. “But Day 45 with zero teams deployed is not the pace I expected. I am formally requesting an expansion pathway that includes D2 in the second phase, and I expect the Mayor’s office to provide a revised implementation timeline before I consider any further votes on related appropriations.”

-----

THE PEOPLE WHO ARE STILL WAITING

Ruben Castillo is a high school history teacher in Fruitvale. He’s been tracking the OARI clock since the vote passed.

“Thirty-five days in, I told your reporter the program was waiting for the police chief to sign off,” Castillo said. “Now it’s Day 46 and we’re still waiting. I teach my students about how government works. This is what I’m supposed to tell them? That you can pass a law and the police department can just not do it?”

Gloria Meeks is 57 years old. She works the night shift at a parking garage in Downtown. Her district wasn’t in the pilot, but she’s been watching.

“I asked when Downtown would get considered for the second round,” Meeks said. “They told me to wait for the pilot data. There is no pilot data. There’s no pilot. So what am I waiting for?”

She paused.

“I’ll keep asking until somebody gives me a month. An actual month. Not ‘the coming weeks.’”

Carla Edmonds is 58, retired, West Oakland. She wrote a letter to this paper after the vote passed, predicting delays. She was right.

“I wanted to be wrong,” Edmonds said. “I’m not wrong. I’ve watched this city pass things and then watch them die in implementation my whole life. OARI isn’t dead yet. But it’s not breathing either.”

-----

WHAT HAPPENS NOW

The council has three options.

First: wait. Give the Mayor’s office and OPD more time to resolve the dispatch integration. Hope the vehicles arrive, the staging sites get leased, and the program eventually launches. This option requires no additional council action and produces no accountability.

Second: escalate. The council can formally request — or demand — that Chief Montez appear before a public session to explain the unsigned specification. This would create a record. It would also create a confrontation between the legislative and executive branches over police department autonomy.

Third: modify. The council can revise the OARI authorization to remove the OPD dispatch requirement, allowing the program to launch with an independent call routing system. This would require a new vote, a new implementation timeline, and likely opposition from the CRC bloc, which voted against the original measure.

Mayor Avery Santana has not made a public statement on the missed deadline. His office declined to comment on the dispatch specification or the implementation timeline.

Ramon Vega voted no on OARI. His position has not changed. But he told the Tribune this week that he is “open to reviewing the pilot data if the program ever produces any.”

Nina Chen, who also voted no, was less measured. “Zero operational teams at Day 45. This is what we warned about. The program was not ready. The timeline was not realistic. And now the council is going to blame OPD for a failure that was built into the authorization from the beginning.”

Warren Ashford declined to comment.

Elliott Crane, still recovering, issued a written statement: “Implementation timelines exist for a reason. When they are missed, someone is accountable. The council should determine who that is before authorizing any additional funding.”

-----

Eleven years of covering Oakland City Council has taught Carmen Delaine one thing about missed deadlines: the question is never whether the program survives. The question is who gets blamed and what happens next.

OARI is not dead. But Day 46 looks a lot like Day 1 — except now the clock is broken, and nobody knows who’s supposed to fix it.
