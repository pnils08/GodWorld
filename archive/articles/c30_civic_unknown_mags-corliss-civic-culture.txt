MAGS CORLISS — Civic Culture
“The Lantern Parade Silence: When Oakland Walked Without Speaking”
Cycle 30 – Downtown Lantern Parade Attendance Analysis
Four hundred people attended the Downtown Lantern Parade—double the expected turnout. They walked in silence through fog so thick you couldn’t see the person ten feet ahead.
I talked to the organizers, the participants, and the neighbors who watched from their windows. Nobody can explain why the parade felt like a funeral procession instead of a celebration.
Ryan Delgado, 52, the parade organizer, has coordinated the event for six years. He’s a delivery driver from Glenview who volunteers with the Downtown Oakland Association.
“We usually have music, kids running ahead with glow sticks, people laughing and taking photos. This year? Dead silence. I tried starting a chant—‘Light the way!’ like we always do—and maybe five people joined in, then it just… died. Everyone went back to walking quiet.”
The parade route runs from Frank Ogawa Plaza down Broadway to Jack London Square—approximately 1.2 miles. Participants carry paper lanterns, traditionally decorating them with winter wishes or personal messages.
This year, the lanterns were blank.
Carmen Vega, 41, the candle vendor who sold out of votives at the Winter Riverfront Festival, walked in the parade with her daughter.
“I asked other parents if they helped their kids decorate lanterns. Nobody did. My daughter—she’s seven—asked if we could write something on hers. I said ‘of course, what do you want to say?’ She said, ‘I don’t know what to say.’ A seven-year-old who always has something to say didn’t know what to write.”
Marcus Walker, 48, a dishwasher from Jack London, stood on the sidewalk watching the parade pass.
“I’ve lived here nine years. I watch this parade every cycle. Usually it’s loud, joyful—people celebrating winter, you know? This time it looked like people were marching to something, not from it. Like they were going somewhere they had to go, not wanted to go.”
The fog that evening registered a weather impact rating of 1.2—higher than typical, but not unprecedented. What was unprecedented: the silence.
Jalen Hill, 35, a line cook from Jack London, walked in the parade with his partner.
“We got about halfway through and I turned to him and whispered—I actually whispered, like we were in a library—‘Should we just go home?’ And he whispered back, ‘I think we’re supposed to finish.’ Not ‘I want to finish.’ Not ‘let’s see it through.’ He said ‘supposed to,’ like it was an obligation.”
Marco Brown, 42, a nurse aide from Downtown, watched from his apartment window overlooking Broadway.
“I counted lanterns for something to do while I waited for the fog to clear. Four hundred lanterns. Maybe thirty had writing on them. The rest were just… light moving through fog. Made me think of those old photos of cities during blackouts—people holding candles because the power’s out, not because they want to.”
The parade ended at Jack London Square at approximately 9:30 PM. Participants dispersed immediately. No gathering at the waterfront like previous cycles. No photos, no celebration.
Ryan Delgado, the organizer, stayed behind to collect the lantern frames for reuse next cycle.
“I asked volunteers to help, like we always do. Eight people usually stay. This year? Just me. Everyone walked away. I stood there alone with four hundred paper lanterns in the fog, and I realized—I don’t know what we just did. We had a parade. But I don’t know what it was for.”
