THE CYCLE PULSE 89 — SUPPLEMENTAL — CULTURE & COMMUNITY_Paulson
The Carpenter’s Son
By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune

OAKLAND — There is a hammer on Mike Paulson’s desk.

Not a trophy. Not a plaque. Not one of the six World Series rings or the NBA Cup hardware. A carpenter’s hammer — worn handle, steel head, the kind you’d find in a toolbox in Bridgeport or Örnsköldsvik or any place where men build things with their hands.

“It’s a symbol,” Paulson told me. “The job is never finished. The build never becomes the built. The hammer is never put away.”

I sat with Paulson for an hour in a quiet room off the main offices. No cameras. No staff. Just a conversation about the man behind the franchises — the carpenter’s son who builds dynasties the way his father built houses.

-----

THE CARPENTER’S THREAD

Mike Paulson was born in 1987 to Lars Erik Paulson and Maureen Shannon-Paulson. The pairing read like a Chicago fairy tale: a stoic Swedish carpenter who came to America at age seven, and a fiery Irish-Italian woman from the Romano-Shannon family — the clan that ran the bakery on 35th Street and held quiet stakes in properties across the South Side.

Lars was a master carpenter. Broad hands, few words, endless patience. The kind of man who could make a house stand or a child believe in their own hands. He came from Örnsköldsvik, in the Ångermanland region of Sweden, where local lore still mentions the Paulson line as master carpenters dating back to the late 1800s.

Maureen was the opposite — warmth and resolve, laughter and fire. The family motto, passed down through Lars’s line, translates roughly as: “The hands remember what the heart learns.”

I asked Paulson what silence sounded like in a house with a father who didn’t say much.

“Words are often left to interpretation,” he said. “What did that mean? I’ve experienced this firsthand. But actions, when deliberate, leave little room for interpretation. My father was a man of effort and execution. Not much for the reflection — well, at least not verbally.”

And his mother?

“Compassion. Empathy. The ability to walk in another’s stride, even for a brief moment. Feel the levity of the feeling, not the words.” He paused. “Let your heart speak.”

-----

TWO CITIES

Paulson has said from podiums that Oakland is home. That his family is here, that he’ll raise his kids here, retire here someday. But he grew up Bridgeport — Red Line to school, Romano’s bakery at seven in the morning, a neighborhood where every family name carried both a story and a secret.

I asked him how he holds two cities at once without one of them feeling like a lie.

He smiled. “To answer your question in the most basic of terms — my mortgage is in Oakland.”

The mortgage doesn’t lie. Neither do the numbers. Six championships in Oakland between 2027 and 2037. An NBA Cup in Chicago in his first full season. The Baylight District stadium deal — the biggest development Oakland has seen in decades — moving toward foundation piling on October 10.

“Oakland is home,” he said. “That’s where I live. Where I hope to marry one day.” A pause. “Let’s just hope she doesn’t live in a third city.”

He laughed. I laughed with him. Three cities might break even Mike Paulson.

-----

THE WALKOUT

In the early cycles of this year, Paulson walked out of an Oakland press conference. The health crisis in Temescal was ongoing. He made predictions he shouldn’t have made. The city watched him leave the podium and wondered if he’d come back.

He came back. Cycle 73. Faced the room. Apologized to P Slayer by name — the columnist whose question had prompted the exit.

I asked him what that moment felt like — not the lesson after, but the realization in real time that he’d done harm when he was trying to help.

“Never quite had that feeling,” he said quietly. “Hope to never have it again. The idea of thinking news is good and it’s actually not — all the while, it wasn’t even my news to share.”

He let that sit.

“It maybe better supports my ties to Oakland beyond sports and where I live.”

-----

BRIDGEPORT WATCHES

In Chicago, a woman named Paulette Okafor works school administration in Bridgeport. She told my colleague Talia Finch that she kept her phone face-down for weeks because she didn’t want to see the notification that Paulson had left for Golden State.

Paulson addressed her by name from a Chicago podium. He said he’d read what she told our paper. He said, “That’s not a fan being anxious. That’s family. I feel that.”

I asked him what he owes the people who believe in what he’s building — the ones who keep their phones face-down.

“I owe them a winner,” he said. “That was the goal. Outside of that, it’s how I carry myself as someone from Chicago who will always have ties to the city. My goal was to make a situation better and go from there. That’s the goal.”

Raymond Polk is a 58-year-old contractor from Bridgeport. He grew up three blocks from Paulson’s childhood home. When the Golden State rumors swirled, Polk sat at Calhoun’s Tap and told Talia: “He’s from here. You don’t leave twice.”

Paulson honored that. Denied the Warriors rumors directly. Said there had been no conversations.

But he was careful with his words when I asked about the future.

“It’s hard to see that as a possibility today,” he said about leaving Oakland. “My Chicago deal was one year. We have multiple projects coming to life in the Baylight District.”

Today. Not never. Not impossible. Just not today.

-----

THE PREMIER CITY

The Baylight District is Paulson’s largest project yet — a stadium, housing, commercial space, a transformation of the Jack London waterfront. The tax structure is tied to the city’s success, not the franchise’s. Variable exemptions that scale with Oakland’s prosperity.

“If Oakland is better, everyone is better,” Paulson said. “It’s really about naming Oakland the premier city.”

The premier city. That’s a big phrase for a man who came here in 2026 with a franchise that had been told it didn’t deserve nice things.

I asked him what he’s actually building toward — not the next championship, not the next roster, but the thing at the end.

“The end will write itself if I take care of the now,” he said. “Bulls are in the Eastern Conference Finals. A’s season started. Baylight District in motion. A new stadium for the A’s is something I’m very excited for. The entire Baylight District is a huge motivator.”

-----

COMMUNITY

His father’s family had a saying: “Händerna minns det hjärtat lär.” The hands remember what the heart learns.

I asked Paulson what his heart has learned that his hands are still trying to build.

“Community,” he said. “It’s the overriding theme to what I’m about. If Mike Paulson is remembered for anything, I hope it’s framed on how I brought people together. People that wouldn’t have come together otherwise.”

He paused.

“And the release of the NBA considering an expansion here tells me the work is paying off.”

An NBA expansion franchise in Oakland. A second major league team. People who wouldn’t have come together otherwise, now together.

His mother Maureen is still in Chicago. Still in Bridgeport. He’s been trying to get her to visit Oakland.

“Just leaving the city she’s lived in,” he said, smiling. “She’s a social one.”

The Shannon vein runs deep. So does the carpenter’s thread.

-----

THE HAMMER

Mike Paulson is 54 years old. He has built six championships in Oakland, an NBA Cup in Chicago, and a stadium district that will reshape this city for generations. He keeps a carpenter’s hammer on his desk because the job is never finished.

His father built houses. He builds rosters, franchises, and — if you believe him — communities. People who wouldn’t have come together otherwise.

“The build never becomes the built,” he told me. “The hammer is never put away.”

For a man who grew up watching his father work with lumber and nails, Mike Paulson has spent his life proving that the same principles apply to people. Foundation first. Precision second. And the patience to know that what you’re building might outlast the hands that built it.

The mortgage is in Oakland. The heart is divided between two cities. And the hammer stays on the desk — waiting for the next thing that needs building.
