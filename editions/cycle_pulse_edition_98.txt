### Four Years to the Floor: Apprenticeship Pipeline Passes 9-0 — and What It Binds

**Four cycles after it stalled, the youth apprenticeship pipeline cleared the council without a dissenting vote — and tied its slots, by contract, to the two biggest construction projects in a generation.**

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

Elijah Roberts has been driving nails in West Oakland for twenty-three years. He knows which crews get called back and which ones get cycled out when the framing is done. He knows what a local-hire clause looks like in a project agreement and what it looks like two years into construction, when the contractor is running behind and the foreman stops checking zip codes. "The paper says one thing," Roberts said. "The site says something else. I've seen it my whole career."

What the city put on paper this week is different, Roberts says. Not because the language changed — local-hire language has been standard for a decade — but because for the first time, the apprenticeship slots are contractually linked to the projects that will actually absorb them.

The Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, a $12.5 million initiative four cycles in the making, cleared the full nine-member council without a dissenting vote. Mayor Avery Santana committed the funding before the vote was called. The bill's binding mechanism — the part Roberts has spent two decades watching fail in practice — ties apprenticeship completions to the Fruitvale Transit Hub community benefits agreement and to Baylight's local-hire targets. Contractors on both projects will be required to draw from the pipeline as slots open.

"That's the piece," said Shawn Neighbor, who runs a crew out of Fruitvale and has watched two generations of young laborers get priced out before they can get licensed. "Every other program ends when the ribbon gets cut. This one doesn't cut the ribbon until the people are placed."

---

The 9-0 tally — Carter, Tran, Delgado, Vega, Rivers, Crane, Ashford, Chen, Mobley, all present, all yes — was unanimous in the formal sense. It was also, behind closed doors, less frictionless than the count suggests. Three CRC members who have raised concerns about Baylight's labor framework in prior cycles voted yes without recorded objection. Elliott Crane, back at the dais after health-related absences, cast the ninth vote. None of that dissipates on passage. The implementation phase is where those concerns will find their footing again.

What passed is not a program. It is a contractual architecture — an attempt to answer the question the city has been circling since the Baylight ground was broken: who gets the work, and how do you make the answer stick when the money is moving?

The pipeline itself focuses on youth, targeting workers in the eighteen-to-twenty-four range who lack existing trade certifications. The CBA linkage gives those completions a guaranteed on-ramp to the two largest construction projects currently active in the city. In theory, the sequence runs from recruitment through training through placement without requiring a young worker to navigate the informal networks that have historically determined who gets the call.

In theory.

---

Elias Varek, who has spent the better part of a decade connecting workforce pipelines to construction corridors in the Fruitvale and Jack London districts, was direct about where the mechanism depends on execution. "The contract language is correct," he said. "The question is whether someone is tracking the slots in real time or waiting until the quarterly report comes back short." His read: the linkage to the Baylight and Fruitvale CBA frameworks creates an accountability trail that previous workforce programs lacked. But a trail and a reckoning are not the same thing.

Roberts put it more plainly. He said he has watched local-hire numbers look fine on paper until a project moves into specialty phases and the crew composition quietly shifts. "You don't notice it happening until you look up and everyone on the scaffold is from Livermore," he said. "By then the contract's three-quarters done and nobody's going back."

He said he is cautiously watching this one. That the CBA link is the right approach. That the 9-0 vote means the political cover is there. He stopped short of optimism, in the way that twenty-three years on a trade tends to discipline a person away from optimism.

---

The initiative moves to implementation-active status. The city will begin drawing from existing workforce intermediaries to establish the recruitment pipeline; the first cohort timeline has not been announced. Disbursement authority sits with the Deputy Mayor's office pending staffing confirmation.

Both the Fruitvale Transit Hub CBA framework and the Baylight local-hire targets are now downstream dependencies of this program's execution. If the pipeline stalls, those commitments stall with it. If the pipeline delivers, the two largest civic construction projects in a generation will be obligated to absorb what it produces.

That is the bet the council made unanimously. Whether it is the kind of bet the city knows how to win is a different question, and not one the vote answers.

Elijah Roberts drove nails for twenty-three years before a council took it seriously enough to put the right language in the right places. He said he'll believe the rest when he sees who's on the scaffold.

### The Week the City Exhaled

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

A city can only hold its breath so long. Last cycle Oakland held it — Día de los Muertos and First Friday on the same night, the warmest mood this city has worn in cycles. This cycle it let the breath go. The streets are quieter. The tills are slower. Across seventeen neighborhoods the mood eased back toward ordinary, and Maria Keen found, in Fruitvale, that ordinary can mean rested — and, by one real measure, safer.

But the quiet is where the work gets done, and this cycle the work was a long time coming. After four cycles stalled, the youth apprenticeship pipeline cleared the council nine to nothing — and, the part that matters, its slots are now bound by contract to the Fruitvale Transit Hub and to Baylight's local-hire targets. A promise with teeth, for once.

Hold that next to Jax Caldera's accounting of the Stabilization Fund: eighteen checks out of two hundred and eighty, more than a year in. One promise the city learned to bind. One it still hasn't. Read them together. That's the cycle.

— M.C.

### The Money That Hasn't Moved: Inside the Stabilization Fund's Long Stall

By Jax Caldera | Bay Tribune Freelance

---

It's a Thursday morning and I'm at a laundromat on Wood Street, watching somebody's uniform spin while I try to figure out why a woman who applied for Stabilization Fund money over a year ago still hasn't seen a check.

She asked me not to use her name. She's not worried about the city. She's worried about paperwork. "I don't want to be the one who made noise," she said. She's been making calls, though. Quiet calls. The kind where you sit on hold and then get transferred and then sit on hold again. Eighteen months in. Still waiting.

Here's the number nobody is leading with: eighteen. As in, eighteen disbursements completed out of two hundred and eighty targeted. The $28 million West Oakland Stabilization Fund — passed, celebrated, framed as a lifeline for residents and corridor businesses that were supposed to be shielded from the development boom — has moved eighteen checks in more than a year of disbursement-active status.

Two and a half per month, roughly. That's the pace.

You don't need a degree to do that math. At that rate, the last check lands sometime well past any reasonable definition of help arriving while it was still needed.

I want to be fair here, because the numbers deserve it: the Mayor's office this week forced Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro to sign off on a dated get-well plan. Real dates. Named accountable operators. Director Marcus Webb on the line. The target is six disbursements per month starting next month, with authorizations complete and full disbursement by C106. Okoro's own words, on record: "No more steady-state framing on a fund that isn't moving at a steady state."

That's something. That's more than the city has delivered since this fund opened its disbursement window. I'll give it that.

But I'll also give you this: it took an executive directive to produce a disbursement schedule for a fund that was approved over a year ago. That's not a plan. That's a plan to have a plan, issued fourteen months late.

Warren Ashford — CRC, District 7 — started asking for an independent third-party audit cycles ago in the pages of this paper. That was a year back. That request has been deferred. Not answered, not denied, not funded. Deferred. The audit Ashford asked for hasn't happened. The pace targets Ashford was asking them to account for didn't get a timeline until this week.

One year. From "we need an audit" to "here's a schedule." That's the story.

The fund was built on a real premise: protect the people who built West Oakland from getting priced out while the cranes were still going up. That logic hasn't changed. But a fund sitting at eighteen out of two hundred and eighty isn't protecting anything. It's a policy position with bad plumbing.

The woman at the laundromat wasn't bitter about it. That was almost worse. She was tired. She's applied. She's qualified. She's waiting. She has been waiting for a year. The city spent that year not yet getting to the part where they set a timeline.

Here's what I want someone to answer: if Okoro's plan works perfectly — if Marcus Webb hits six disbursements every single month without a slip — when does the woman on Wood Street get her money?

### $420 or $2,807: OARI Resets Its Own Cost Number

By Bay Tribune Civic

---

The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative completed its second full citywide cycle this week with 2,411 crisis calls across all nine districts — and its director used the occasion to put a council benchmark on record as wrong.

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz told council staff that the $420-per-call figure cited in prior budget discussions does not reflect actual program cost. Current per-call cost: approximately $2,807, accounting for startup staffing, equipment, and administrative overhead. Tran-Muñoz projects that figure amortizes to roughly $900 by cycle 106 as call volume rises and fixed costs are absorbed.

The counterframe the director offered: system cost-avoidance — the emergency-room diversions, avoided arrests, and repeat-call reductions — runs an estimated $2.4 million to $4.1 million against the program's total expenditure to date.

On performance: District 8 became the first expansion district to hit the 6.0-minute response lock. District 7 remains the hardest case — one vehicle operational, a second requested. D7's Warren Ashford has not publicly responded to the capacity gap in his district.

The $420 number came from the council. OARI's director said it was wrong. That correction is now on the record.

### The City Catches Its Breath: The Week After the Festival

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

I'm standing on International Boulevard on a Tuesday morning, the storefronts of Fruitvale behind me still wearing last weekend's warmth like a coat they're about to shed. It's the Tuesday after Día de los Muertos and First Friday collapsed into one night — the kind of night that leaves a city humming. This week, Fruitvale is learning to exhale.

Toby Lamont, forty-three, is sorting donated cans at the West Oakland Community Center when I find him. He's been working with the Mutual Aid Network for six years, and he has the kind of face that registers a neighborhood's temperature the way a barometer reads pressure. "The streets were full," he says, setting down a box. "Saturday night, the whole corridor was moving. People were out, businesses were slammed, there was this — I don't know — this *presence*." He pauses, uses a paper towel on his hands. "This week it's quieter. Not dead. Just quiet. And I think people need that."

The mood across Fruitvale has cooled this past week, and the tills have gone quieter with it. On any other week, that would read as decline. But Toby's right — it's not decline. It's a neighborhood taking a breath.

The bright spot is real and specific. Violent crime in Fruitvale fell this past cycle, running meaningfully below the neighborhood's recent norm. Not a dramatic plunge, but the kind of quiet good news that lands differently when a neighborhood has just hosted the city's biggest celebration. The Saturday crowds, the energy, the festival lights — and then the aftermath brought safer streets.

I catch Augusta Davis, forty-one, coming out of the Fruitvale Community Clinic at midday. She's a health worker who sees the neighborhood's rhythms in who walks through the door. "The festival was beautiful," she says, shifting her bag to her other shoulder. "And yes, we're quieter this week. But quiet isn't bad. I'm seeing the same people. Same kids. Same families. They're just not running on adrenaline anymore."

The storefronts look a little less urgent than they did on Saturday — fewer people browsing, fewer voices in the corridors. Temescal, a few neighborhoods south, felt the cooldown hardest of all on the retail side — its storefronts the quietest in the city this week. But here in Fruitvale, where the dip was gentler, the quiet feels earned rather than empty.

What strikes me, walking the corridor on a Tuesday, is that the exhaustion isn't despair. The lights are still up. The cafés are still open. The people are still here. The city held its breath for one night — Día de los Muertos altars next to First Friday galleries, the whole city moving at once. Now it's letting that breath go, slowly, in a week of ordinary streets and ordinary work.

That's not a problem. That's a city resting.

Come next cycle, Fruitvale will have found its rhythm again. But this week, the neighborhood is learning to be quiet and safe at the same time — and that's a kind of balance that takes more than numbers to understand.

### After a Career on the Docks, Anna Baker Retires

By Bay Tribune Culture

---

Anna Baker, trade union representative at the Port of Oakland, has announced her retirement after a long career working the waterfront. A Jack London resident, Baker spent her working life in the midst of the port's daily operations, representing those who load and unload the city's commerce.

Her departure marks the end of a significant tenure in one of Oakland's defining institutions. The port — where the city meets the container ships and cranes, where goods move and livelihoods turn on the tides — loses someone who knew its rhythms as well as anyone.

For many working the docks, Baker's retirement signals a transition at an institution that has structured Oakland's waterfront labor for generations. It's the kind of quiet marker that registers less in headlines than in the day-to-day lives of the people who depend on the work the port provides.

### A New Chapter: Paulson Takes Basketball Operations for the Oaks

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

Mike Paulson added a second franchise to his portfolio this cycle, accepting the role of Head of Basketball Operations for the Oakland Oaks — the city's incoming NBA expansion club — while retaining his post as general manager of the Oakland A's. The dual appointment, confirmed through the Oaks' front-office announcement, places one executive at the helm of the most consequential sports build this city has undertaken in decades, and raises a question serious enough to ask plainly: whether the architecture of a championship baseball franchise and the ground-floor construction of an expansion basketball roster can coexist under the same set of hands.

The surface case for Paulson is not difficult to assemble. He has spent years demonstrating that roster construction is a discipline, not a sport-specific instinct — the A's championship runs were built on asset accumulation, contract discipline, and a willingness to move on talent before the market agreed. Those principles translate. An expansion franchise lives or dies on draft capital and the intelligence of its early selections, and Paulson has proved he understands the value of a future asset more than the comfort of a present one. Owner Elias Varek, who built Civis Systems before turning his attention to the Oaks, has made his expectations explicit: a competitive debut season, not a rebuild in disguise. Whether that promise reflects genuine optimism or the standard expansion-owner optimism that evaporates on contact with a fourteen-win first season remains to be seen.

The construction timeline is worth examining precisely because Varek's promise depends on it. The expansion draft is scheduled for C99; the NBA draft follows in C100. Those two events are the Oaks' entire foundation — the expansion draft will offer a pool of unprotected veterans, useful for culture-setting but rarely for winning, while the NBA draft represents the first real opportunity to land a player around whom a roster might eventually orbit. Head coach Wilson Shepard, who spent last season as an offensive specialist on Minnesota's staff, is regarded as a teacher of offensive system and player development. That hire is calibrated correctly for a franchise that will be young and learning. But a first-year head coach building a system alongside a basketball operations chief who is simultaneously managing an A's title run is a significant amount of institutional weight resting on relationships that have not yet been tested under pressure.

The workload question is the one Paulson's appointment does not answer cleanly. The A's are in the middle of a season that carries real stakes. The Oaks, at this stage, are a franchise without a roster — a structure waiting to be filled — and the expansion draft and NBA draft are the mechanisms by which it gets one. Front-office preparation for an expansion draft is not passive. It requires personnel evaluation, contract analysis, negotiation sequencing, and a philosophy about what kind of team the Oaks intend to be in year one versus year five. Doing that work at depth, while also managing the decisions of a contending baseball club, is either a manageable division of attention or an invitation to thinly cover both. Which of those it turns out to be will not be visible until the C99 expansion draft reveals the quality of the evaluation work done now.

What this appointment brings that no other hire would, at this particular moment, is Paulson's understanding of Oakland as a sports market — its patience, its expectations, its history with franchises that made promises and then departed. The Oaks are named for the 1960s ABA club, a deliberate gesture toward a basketball lineage that predates most of the city's current residents. That framing matters. Varek bought the name and the history; Paulson is the one who has to build something that earns it. The A's have given Oakland championships. Whether the same executive can give Oakland a basketball franchise worth believing in is the question this appointment opens without answering.

The expansion draft is one cycle away. The answers start then.

### The Home-Run Race: Quintero and Taveras Light Up SF

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

I was standing up by the third one.

I didn't plan to. I had my beer, I had my seat, I was ready to watch a blowout and feel good about it the way you feel good about a blowout when your team is 53-14 and the whole league knows you're something different this year. Clinical. Comfortable. I was going to sit back and enjoy the architecture.

Then Ernesto Quintero hit the third home run of the night and I was just — up. On my feet. In the aisle. Some stranger's hand found my shoulder and I didn't even look to see whose it was.

That's what 24 looks like when it's the most in baseball.

---

We put fourteen on San Francisco Thursday night. Fourteen to one. The Giants never had a chance, and you know what — that's not even the story. The story is what happened inside that demolition. The story is two guys who came up together, who play in the same clubhouse and probably give each other grief about the same minor-league memories, now locked in a home-run race that's making the whole season feel electric.

Ernesto Quintero: 24 home runs. League leader.

Eric Taveras: 23.

One behind. One night, two bombs from Taveras, three from Quintero, and now they're staring at each other across the clubhouse probably laughing about something none of us will ever hear. That's the thing about this — it doesn't have the mean competitive tension you'd expect. These aren't two guys trying to bury each other. They're two guys making each other better and carrying this roster along for the ride.

---

Quintero is from West Oakland. Born and raised. He's twenty-four years old and he leads the majors in home runs.

You can read that sentence a hundred times and it still doesn't settle. A kid from here, our neighborhood, our schools — standing at the top of the most visible leaderboard in the sport. The DH slot could go to anyone. Could go to a veteran, a guy brought in from outside, somebody with a longer resume. It went to a kid from the corner of Thirty-Fourth and Market, and he is rewarding that faith every single night.

I've watched this city follow this team for a long time. I've watched us love a franchise through years when loving them cost more than it gave back. So when I tell you this feels different — when I tell you that watching Quintero launch one into the right-field seats with the ease of a man who belongs exactly where he is — believe me. I know the difference between a mirage and the real thing.

This is the real thing.

---

And then there's Taveras. Twenty-three. One back.

The thing nobody's saying out loud but everybody's thinking: if Taveras catches him, if Taveras passes him — Quintero is going to go get it back. That's just how this works. That's how it works when two competitors who actually like each other are chasing the same number. They push. They answer. They push again.

Thursday night it was Quintero who answered. Three times.

Next week could be Taveras's turn to send a message. That's what makes this fun. That's what makes a dream season into a *great* dream season — not just the winning, but having something to watch inside the winning. A subplot worth caring about. A race with a pulse.

---

Some people want to talk about roster math right now. Who fits, who doesn't, who might get moved. I get it. The front office has decisions to make. But that is not what I'm here for tonight.

I'm here because I stood up in the seventh inning and a stranger's hand found my shoulder and neither of us needed to say a word.

I'm here because West Oakland is leading the majors in home runs.

I'm here because Oakland, fifty-three and fourteen, has a home-run race inside a dynasty run, and it is *magnificent*.

The chase is on. Enjoy it.

### Rivas Returns, the Streak Rolls

By Bay Tribune Sports

---

Henry Rivas walked back out to the mound this cycle and looked like he had never left.

Seven innings, no runs, seven strikeouts. The dislocated shoulder that cost him two months and handed Carmen Mesa his rotation slot was nowhere in evidence. Rivas was sharp and economical, and the Oakland A's swept the Kansas City Royals and Toronto Blue Jays in back-to-back series to push their record to 53-14.

The rotation is whole again. Mesa, who held the slot steady through Rivas's absence, has been optioned to AAA — a reward for a job well done, and a difficult one. He'll be first in line when rosters expand or if the rotation needs him again.

At 53-14, the A's are building something that demands attention on its own terms. One clean start at a time.

### Oaks Set Expansion Draft for Next Cycle

By Bay Tribune Sports

---

The Oakland Oaks have locked in their timeline: the expansion draft arrives next cycle, with the NBA draft to follow the cycle after. Owner Elias Varek has called for a competitive debut, and the front office now has a blueprint to work from.

First-year head coach Wilson Shepard, an offensive specialist who spent last season as an assistant in Minnesota, takes the helm as the organization begins building its roster from scratch. Expansion drafts favor patience over splash — existing franchises protect their core and expose their depth, which means Oakland will be assembling its opening-night lineup from a pool of veterans on their way somewhere else, role players looking for a home, and developmental talent from the draft.

Shepard's offensive pedigree suggests the Oaks will be worth watching once the pieces are in place. Whether Varek's competitive promise survives contact with an expansion roster is the question that carries into next cycle.

I don't need a mechanism. I need a check.
