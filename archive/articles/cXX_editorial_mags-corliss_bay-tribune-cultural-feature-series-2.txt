By Mags Corliss
Green and Gold, Black and Brown: The Soul of an Oakland Crowd
Oakland, California — 2040

On any given night at the Coliseum, you can map the city just by listening. The accents shift from section to section; the chants move from English to Spanish to Tagalog to something in between. The drum line under the left-field bleachers starts a rhythm that folds in salsa beats, gospel claps, and hip-hop snares. It’s Oakland’s heartbeat, painted in green and gold.

Baseball here has always been about more than baseball. The Athletics may be the team on the field, but the crowd has become the city’s living mural—a mixture of generations who built, migrated, or rebuilt their way into belonging. The great wave of Black families from the South, the Latino boom that redefined the neighborhoods, the Asian and Pacific Islander communities that came through the port and never left—all of them found something familiar in the diamond. A space where hustle looks like heritage...

Out in the parking lot before first pitch, you can still find that identity cooking on the grills. Korean short ribs next to jerk chicken, pupusas beside ribs and collard greens. There’s no fusion menu; it’s coexistence in practice. Kids run between tailgates wearing Kelley jerseys one size too big, shouting through the smoke, trading words their parents taught them in different languages. The food and the sound tell you what the city has always known—that Oakland doesn’t blend, it layers.

Inside, the stands carry their own kind of democracy. In Section 149, you’ll see a grandmother who’s been coming since Reggie’s days sitting beside a first-generation student from East Oakland who found the team through Darrin Davis highlight reels. They don’t share much in common except the ritual: the cap, the chant, the belief that the A’s are theirs because the city refuses to belong to anyone else.

When people talk about the “culture of baseball,” they usually mean nostalgia. In Oakland, culture means present tense. The team’s cross-cultural fanbase isn’t a marketing statistic; it’s a survival mechanism. In a city shaped by migration, disinvestment, and renaissance, the crowd itself became the most permanent thing. Even as ownership shifted and stadium debates dragged on, the bleachers stayed multilingual, multi-colored, multi-rhythmic.

After the game, as traffic creeps toward 880 and the lights dim, you can still hear the city echoing in the hum of radios and open car windows. Hip-hop fades into Spanish pop, reggae cuts through both, and someone yells a final “Let’s go Oakland!” into the night. That’s not just fandom—it’s identity rehearsed. In Oakland, green and gold aren’t just colors. They’re coordinates: proof that a city of every shade still knows how to move together.
