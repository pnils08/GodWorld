## SELENA GRANT — Bulls Beat

### "Vucevic and DeRozan Gone: Paulson Clears the Deck in 48 Hours"

**United Center — Chicago, IL**

The locker room is quiet. Not the quiet of focus. The quiet of absence.

Nikola Vucevic's stall: empty. DeMar DeRozan's corner: cleared out. Two franchise anchors, gone before the trade deadline even approached.

Mike Paulson didn't wait.

**The Deals (Details Unconfirmed):**

- **Nikola Vucevic** → Sacramento Kings
- **DeMar DeRozan** → Memphis Grizzlies

**Bulls receive:**
- 2026 Sacramento Kings 1st Round Pick (unprotected)
- 2026 Memphis 1st Round Pick (unprotected, unclear which — Memphis holds multiple 2026 firsts)

Full trade details expected in Cycle 44. What we know now: Chicago just shed approximately $55-60M in combined salary and acquired two lottery-range picks in what projects as a loaded 2026 draft class.

**The Math:**

With these moves, Paulson has positioned the Bulls for:
- 3 first-round picks in 2026 (own + Sacramento + Memphis)
- Nearly $80M in projected cap space
- The youngest roster in the Eastern Conference

This is not a retool. This is a demolition.

**The Vucevic Context:**

Sources close to the team confirmed tension between Vucevic and the front office dating to before Paulson's official appointment. An unnamed source reported Vucevic making "disparaging comments" about Paulson's potential hiring — comments the center publicly denied but never fully walked back.

Whether that friction accelerated his departure or was incidental to basketball decisions remains unclear. What's clear: Vucevic is no longer Paulson's problem.

**Locker Room Temperature:**

I spoke with three players on condition of anonymity. The consensus:

"We knew something was coming. You don't hire a guy who built what he built in Oakland to run it back with the same roster. But two trades in two days? That's fast. That's a message."

The message: This is Paulson's team now. Everyone else is auditioning.

**What's Next:**

With Vucevic and DeRozan gone, the Bulls' remaining core consists of:
- Coby White (26, under contract through 2027)
- Patrick Williams (23, extension-eligible)
- Young prospects and expiring contracts

Paulson has cap space, draft capital, and time. The question is whether Chicago fans have patience.

Full trade breakdowns coming Cycle 44.

**Names Index:** Nikola Vucevic (Center, traded to Sacramento); DeMar DeRozan (Forward, traded to Memphis); Mike Paulson (General Manager); Coby White (Guard); Patrick Williams (Forward)

## MARCELLO REYES — NBA Investigation / National

### "The Oakland Playbook Comes to Chicago: What Paulson's First Moves Tell Us"

**National Analysis**

In Oakland, Mike Paulson built a dynasty through three pillars: cost-controlled talent acquisition, analytical roster construction, and ruthless asset management. Six World Series titles in fifteen years. A farm system that never stopped producing.

Forty-eight hours into his Bulls tenure, we're seeing the blueprint translate.

**The Parallel:**

When Paulson took over the A's in 2025, he inherited a bloated payroll and an aging core. His first moves: trade veterans for prospects, shed salary, stockpile draft capital. The pain lasted two seasons. The dynasty lasted a decade.

Chicago just watched the same opening sequence.

Vucevic and DeRozan were productive players on a bad team. In Paulson's calculus, productive players on bad teams are the most dangerous assets — good enough to win meaningless games, not good enough to contend, and expensive enough to prevent flexibility.

He removed them immediately.

**The Cross-Sport Question:**

Basketball isn't baseball. The draft is less predictable. Star acquisition happens through free agency and trades, not farm systems. A single transcendent player can flip a franchise overnight in ways that don't exist in MLB.

Paulson knows this. His cap space strategy suggests he's planning for the 2026 free agent class as much as the draft. The $80M in projected room isn't an accident — it's a war chest.

**The Dual-Role Concern:**

Paulson remains Oakland's GM. He's now also Chicago's GM. Two franchises, two sports, two time zones.

League sources express private skepticism. One Eastern Conference executive, speaking anonymously:

"You can't be in two places at once. Either Oakland suffers, Chicago suffers, or both do. The man's brilliant, but he's not magic."

Paulson's response, through a spokesperson: "I've built systems that don't require my presence to function. Oakland runs. Chicago will learn to run the same way."

That's the theory. We'll see if it holds.

**Names Index:** Mike Paulson (General Manager, Bulls / Athletics); Nikola Vucevic (Center, traded to Sacramento); DeMar DeRozan (Forward, traded to Memphis)

## TALIA FINCH — Chicago Nightlife / Fan Energy

### "The Oakland Guy Just Blew It Up: What Chicago's Saying Tonight"

**From the Floor of Benchmark Bar, West Loop**

The trade notifications hit during the third quarter of the Bucks game. Nobody was watching the Bucks game after that.

I counted seventeen phones lighting up within thirty seconds. Then the arguments started.

**The Optimists:**

"Finally. Someone with a plan. We've been mediocre for years. Paulson's the first guy who looked at this roster and said 'this isn't good enough' out loud." — Marcus, 34, insurance broker, been a Bulls fan since the Rose years.

"Three first-rounders in 2026? Do you know how loaded that draft class is supposed to be? We're about to be the Thunder." — DeShawn, 28, graphic designer, already running mock drafts on his phone.

**The Skeptics:**

"This dude is literally still running a baseball team in California. When's he gonna be here? When's he gonna watch games? He traded Vooch and DeMar from his couch in Oakland." — Janelle, 31, teacher, wearing a faded DeRozan jersey.

"We just became a farm system for a guy who's going to leave for his real job whenever Oakland needs him." — Tony, 45, contractor, ordering his third beer.

**The Mood:**

It's not anger. It's not excitement. It's something in between — the vertigo of watching your team change identity in real time.

The Bulls have been stuck in the middle for years. Not bad enough to tank properly. Not good enough to matter in April. Vucevic and DeRozan were the faces of that stuckness — talented, professional, and ultimately insufficient.

Now they're gone, and Chicago is staring at a blank page.

**Overheard:**

"At least in Oakland he won. He won a lot. Maybe he knows something."

"Yeah, but that's baseball. This is basketball. It's different."

"Is it though? Isn't it all just... assets and math?"

Silence. Someone ordered another round.

**The Vibe Check:**

Chicago sports fans are used to dysfunction. The Bears have been breaking hearts for decades. The Cubs took 108 years to win a title. The Bulls haven't been relevant since... well.

Paulson represents something unfamiliar: competence arriving suddenly, making big moves without asking permission, treating sacred cows like trade chips.

It's jarring. It might also be necessary.

By midnight, the bar had split into two camps: "Trust the Process" and "Fire Him Already." Both camps were drinking heavily.

This is going to be a long season.

**Names Index:** Mike Paulson (General Manager); Nikola Vucevic (Center, traded to Sacramento); DeMar DeRozan (Forward, traded to Memphis)
