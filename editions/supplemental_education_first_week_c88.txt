THE FIRST WEEK
Four Oakland Schools. Four Neighborhoods. One City Getting It Right.

By Angela Reyes | Bay Tribune Education

----

The smell is the same everywhere.

Whiteboard marker and floor wax. Somewhere in the back, a pencil sharpener doing its September work. A bin of hand sanitizer by the door — refilled, full, lid cleaned. These are the sounds and smells of a school building that has been maintained over the summer, that is ready, that expects children and has made room for them.

I spent the first week of September 2041 in four Oakland classrooms. Four neighborhoods. Four different school cultures, four different sets of pressures, four teachers who have been doing this long enough to know what Day 1 feels like when it goes right, and what it feels like when it doesn't. September 2041 went right. That is the story.

In this city, in this year, that is enough.

----

**FRUITVALE BILINGUAL ACADEMY — FRUITVALE**
*3700 International Boulevard, at 37th Avenue*

Rosa Medina, 58, has been principal of Fruitvale Bilingual Academy for eleven years. She was in the parking lot at 6:45 the first morning, before any teacher, before any parent, before the kitchen staff had the milk cartons stacked. She does this every first day. She says it is the only way to catch what a school looks like when it thinks nobody is watching.

"The building tells you something before the children arrive," she said. "Whether the custodial team came back in good shape. Whether the summer repairs held. Whether the bulletin boards got done." She gestured at the main hallway, where paper sunflowers spelled out BIENVENIDOS / WELCOME in alternating gold and green, hand-cut by the office staff the week before. "This tells me something about the spirit of a building."

Fruitvale Bilingual is a K-8 bilingual school — English and Spanish, woven together from kindergarten through eighth grade, not siloed by language level but genuinely dual. The model is called "enrichment bilingual," which means both languages are the language of instruction, every day, across every subject. It is not a remedial program. It is not a bridge. The children here are becoming bilingual professionals, and Medina says it out loud, every orientation, every year.

The school runs 480 students. On Day 1, 471 came in. Medina noted this before I asked.

"Ninety-eight percent on the first day is a relationship number," she told me, standing outside her office at 8:04 AM as a third-grade parent flagged her down from the hallway. "It means the families know us. It means they came back."

The parent was Brianna Nguyen — early thirties, two kids at the school, a four-year-old strapped to her chest in a carrier. She'd brought in a thermos of coffee and left it on Medina's desk without ceremony, the way you leave something for someone you've known for years. "Same spot as last year?" she asked. Medina laughed and said yes.

----

Valentina Cruz, 34, teaches third grade at Fruitvale Bilingual and has been doing it for nine years. She is the kind of teacher who has strong feelings about classroom furniture arrangement. Her room runs in clusters of four — not rows, not U-shape, clusters — because she says third grade is the year children either begin to love working together or decide they never will, and the clusters telegraph the expectation before she says a word.

On the first morning, twenty-three students came in and stood at the door until she waved them to their assigned seats, which she had written on index cards taped to each desk corner. She does this because in a bilingual classroom, the first five minutes establish the day's tone, and a child who has to hunt for their seat is already behind.

"I count them in," she told me after the morning session, in the small faculty lounge where a coffee maker had been going since six. "I look at each face. I'm already watching. Which kid looks fed. Which kid doesn't. Which kid is looking for their friend and relaxed when they find them, and which kid is looking for their friend because they need them."

On Day 1, Cruz noted a boy in the back cluster — a nine-year-old who arrived last, sat down without making eye contact, and spent the first twenty minutes of math instruction facing the window. She said nothing to him directly. She moved closer, adjusted the trajectory of her pacing, asked a question to the cluster that he happened to be part of. By 10 AM he'd turned his chair around.

"By October, teachers are collecting data," she said. "In September, you're just watching. You're trying to understand what you're working with."

Her classroom walls held the bilingual contract on butcher paper — "En esta clase / In this classroom" — twenty-two class agreements written in both languages, one line at a time, both languages side by side. A small Oakmesh sensor sat in the upper corner of the room near the ventilation register, the indicator light a steady green. Cruz mentioned it to me only when I asked — they'd been installed in the spring, she said, and she'd stopped noticing them by June. "The air quality's good. I check the dashboard on my phone if a kid starts sneezing. That's all I use it for."

In the front office, Medina pulled up the morning attendance sheet on her laptop. The Tenth Street Digital parent app showed three logged late arrivals with reasons already submitted — a younger sibling's health appointment, a doctor visit, one unreported. The app pushed them directly to her dashboard by 8:15, which she said saved two desk hours per week compared to the phone-tag system the school used before. She didn't linger on the app. She closed the laptop and went back out into the hallway.

Tomás Aguilar, a father whose fifth-grader transferred from a school in Hayward over the summer, was waiting near the front steps when pickup came at 3:15. He'd spent the morning running logistics — where was the gate, what was the pickup lane, where did his daughter go if the bus was early. He stood off to the side, watching. I asked him how it felt.

"She came out smiling," he said. "At the old school, first day, she came out quiet. I don't know what the difference is. Something here." He shrugged. "I'll take it."

----

**ALLEN TEMPLE ACADEMY — EAST OAKLAND**
*8501 International Boulevard, at 85th Avenue*

Allen Temple Academy occupies a building that went through two full renovation cycles in the mid-2030s — new roof, new HVAC, new plumbing, seismic retrofit on the northeast wing. The brick facade is original, scrubbed clean, the entry steps relaid in grey composite that holds up better in rain. A new marquee sign out front reads, in LED letters that the principal changes every week: FIRST DAY. WELCOME BACK. WE MISSED YOU.

The school is a K-8 with 310 students, one of three OUSD schools in the deep East Oakland corridor that feed into Castlemont and Skyline for high school. The neighborhood surrounding it is the most sparsely documented in Bay Tribune coverage — this paper has named almost no residents, visited no businesses, stood on none of its corners. The absence is something I can see in my own notebooks. I'm trying to fix it.

Marcus Ellison, 41, has taught fifth grade at Allen Temple Academy for fourteen years. He grew up six blocks from where he teaches. He came back after his credential program at Cal State East Bay, accepted an offer at Allen Temple, and has declined to leave three times since, most recently two years ago when a Rockridge school approached him about an opening.

"I know these kids," he said simply, sitting at his desk before homeroom while students arranged themselves in their seats. His classroom held twenty-six students. His desk held a coffee mug, a grade book, and a clear plastic bin labeled EXTRA PENCILS — the bin was full.

Ellison runs a structured classroom. Not rigid — structured. His morning opens with ten quiet minutes called "Settle In": students write one sentence about one thing they did over the summer, then two minutes of sharing at their table. He does not post these on the wall. He collects them, reads them, and returns them the following Monday with a one-sentence response. He has done this every first week for fourteen years.

"It tells me where they are," he said. "Not academically. Emotionally. A kid who writes 'I played games' is giving me nothing. A kid who writes 'I went to my grandma's because my mom had to work double shifts' is giving me everything."

On the wall beside the whiteboard, a square sensor about the size of a thick paperback book displayed a small LED readout: AQI 41. GOOD. Ellison had mounted it himself, with a facilities work order, after the spring installation. He told me he'd turned it into a science lesson by the third day — had students look up what AQI meant, what 41 compared to, what number would trigger a concern. A bar chart now lived on the bulletin board next to the sensor: ALLEN TEMPLE AIR QUALITY — WEEK 1, hand-labeled, the bars drawn by student volunteers during lunch.

"They asked to do it," he said. He looked at the chart for a moment. "That's fifth grade. They want to feel ownership of something real."

The only parent I was able to reach at Allen Temple was Jamal Thompson, who lives in the neighborhood and whose son is in Ellison's class this year. He works a schedule that doesn't allow for morning drop-off, so I caught him at pickup — he'd rearranged something to be there on Day 1. He is quiet, precise in how he speaks.

"The school called us in June," he told me. "Not to tell us something was wrong. Just to introduce the supply list and confirm the homeroom. I was surprised. My older son's first school didn't do that." He looked at the marquee. The LED letters were cycling through a new message: FIFTH GRADE IS WHERE IT STARTS. "That sign. Marcus changes it every week. My son asks me what it says every pickup. That's not nothing."

**CLAREMONT HILLS ELEMENTARY — ROCKRIDGE/TEMESCAL**
*5959 Claremont Avenue, north of College Avenue*

Claremont Hills Elementary sits above the flatlands, at the point where Rockridge starts to feel like it's edging into the hills. The school has 445 students, a parent-teacher organization that raises roughly $85,000 per year for enrichment programming, and a complicated reputation as one of the highest-performing elementary schools in OUSD — a fact that makes some parents in the district proud and makes others uncomfortable.

I notice this tension in the parking lot at 7:45 AM on Day 1. The drop-off line is long and orderly. Several parents are in business casual attire headed to early meetings. A few are in running gear, just back from the lake path. The kids moving through the front gate are wearing new backpacks — the kind that come from stores rather than thrift shops, with ergonomic frames and pencil compartments.

This is not a sin. A prosperous school in a prosperous corridor is simply a fact of a city that has grown. What matters is what happens inside.

Diane Sato, 52, has taught fourth grade at Claremont Hills for twenty-four years. She was hired at twenty-eight, before her daughter was born, before her first sabbatical, before the school's population shifted. She has watched the neighborhood change from her classroom window. She stays because the children are still children.

"Every September, they come in and they are very convinced they know things," she said, standing at the classroom door as students filed in, each one greeted by name. She knew all twenty-four by name before the first day — she'd memorized the class list in August. "They know their multiplication tables. They know how to code. They know the Pythagorean theorem because their parents taught them last summer." She paused. "They don't know how to disagree with a friend. That's what I teach."

Sato's room felt different from the other three I visited — more curated, perhaps, the supplies more plentiful. A small library alcove ran along the back wall, the books organized by genre with hand-lettered tabs. In the far corner, a beanbag chair and a low bookshelf marked the "calm corner," a practice she'd maintained for fifteen years. Above the whiteboard, a row of student self-portraits from the previous class still lined the wall — Sato leaves them up the first two weeks of September to honor the class that was, before replacing them with the class that is.

Priya Marchetti picked up her son at 3:15 and stopped to speak with a parent she knew at the front gate. I caught her for two minutes before she was absorbed back into the school community. She's lived in Temescal for six years, works in healthcare administration, has a fourth-grader and a first-grader at Claremont Hills.

"The first grader ran out of the building," she said. "Arms open. Kid did not have a bad day." She smiled. "That's all I needed to know."

Ariana Lewis, a parent from Rockridge whose daughter is starting Claremont Hills this year, told me she'd visited two other OUSD schools before choosing this one — not because she was shopping for prestige, she was careful to say, but because she wanted dual-language programming that didn't exist here and Fruitvale was too far. "It's a good school," she said. "I know it's a good school. I just wanted them to know she'd been read to since she was six months old. I didn't want them to put her in a remedial anything."

Her daughter is a reader. She walked out of school with a chapter book open in front of her, reading while she walked down the steps.

**OAKLAND URBAN ARTS AND SCIENCES ACADEMY — DOWNTOWN/KONO**
*1520 Webster Street, between 14th and 15th*

Oakland Urban Arts and Sciences Academy — OUASA, said like "you-asa" by the students — occupies a former industrial building in the KONO corridor that was repurposed in 2034, its high ceilings and concrete floors retrofitted into open classrooms with moveable walls. The school runs K-12, 520 students, with a project-based learning model and a citywide draw. You can tell from the pickup crowd: kids arriving from Fruitvale by bus, from Rockridge by bicycle, from East Oakland by BART. The school is intentionally diverse in a way that requires maintenance.

Tobias Scott, 47, is the principal. He has a doctorate in educational leadership from Stanford and spent eight years at a school in Oakland before coming to OUASA when it opened. He is the kind of administrator who does not sit in his office. On Day 1, I found him in three different places in the first hour: the 9th grade wing, the K-2 hallway, and the lunch prep area where a catering coordinator was setting up the school's hot lunch station.

"The school has to feel like it belongs to the kids," he said, walking a hallway at a pace that required me to keep up. "Not to the adults. The adults are guests. The kids are residents." He stopped at a bulletin board outside the seventh-grade science room. A sign read: WHAT QUESTIONS WILL YOU ASK THIS YEAR? Below it, blank index cards in three colors — red, blue, yellow — with a stack of markers hanging from a hook. Some cards already had writing. A seventh-grader had written: Why does BART always smell like that? Another: Can you run a city on solar? A third: What's it like to be a journalist?

Scott read all three without comment and kept walking.

----

Kwame Asante, 38, teaches middle school math and data science at OUASA and has been there since the school's second year — eleven years total. He is the teacher who built the data wall in the seventh-grade hallway, a six-foot display he updates weekly with school attendance, community temperature surveys, and, starting this year, a student-run metrics board connecting to the after-school Gridiron Analytics program.

His classroom runs in rows of two — not because he prefers rows, but because the moveable walls mean his room has two configurations, rows for direct instruction and clusters for project work, and on Day 1 he wants rows. "First day, they need to see me," he said. "Day 3, they need to see each other."

On his wall, between the class schedule and the materials list, hung a GRIDIRON ANALYTICS poster — the company's green-and-white design, a baseball diamond inside a data grid. Below it, smaller text: AFTER-SCHOOL DATA LITERACY PROGRAM — REGISTER AT THE FRONT OFFICE. Asante pointed at it when I asked about the after-school program, with the same economy of motion he used for most things in his classroom.

"Daniel Yoon and Christine Nakamura came in last spring and showed us what they were building," he said. "They wanted feedback from a teacher. We worked on the curriculum together. Now I'm the in-school liaison." He said it without pride, as though this was a natural thing — a company from Temescal co-working space coming to talk to an OUSD teacher before finalizing a curriculum. It was a natural thing. That's the point.

Twenty-eight students in his homeroom. On Day 1, all twenty-eight came in.

Ivy Cook, a parent whose son is starting seventh grade at OUASA this year, had found the school through a friend's recommendation two years earlier and spent the intervening time on the waitlist. She made it. Standing at the Webster Street entrance at 3:15, watching her son come down the stairs in conversation with another boy she didn't recognize, she said she'd been anxious all day.

"I kept checking my phone," she said. "The school app sent a lunch notification at noon. That sounds small. It's not small. The last school didn't have that." She paused. "He's got a friend. Day one. That's it. We're done. We're staying."

Theo Banks, whose daughter is in the third grade at OUASA, had the same phone in his hand when I found him — not because he'd been checking it, but because his daughter had texted him from a school computer at lunch. The text read: dad i made a volcano for science. He showed it to me. The volcano was represented by an emoji.

----

**WHAT ANGELA NOTICES**

I have a gradebook from my last year in the classroom. I kept it. It is in a drawer in my apartment, under some other things, and I have not opened it in three years, but I know it's there. It has twenty-seven names in it, and beside each name, a code I developed over several years that tracked not grades but signals: attendance consistency, morning temperament, whether the child ate breakfast, whether they engaged or performed engagement. These two things look the same from a distance. A teacher can tell the difference from twelve feet away.

What I was watching for, in four classrooms across four neighborhoods this week, was not test scores or program spending or initiative implementation. I was watching for the signals. The teacher at the door who knows every name. The extra pencils. The bin of hand sanitizer, refilled. The marquee that changes every week. The child who comes out with a chapter book open in front of her, reading while she walks.

These are the signals that say: this school knows you. This school was waiting for you.

In a prosperous city, in a functioning system, this should be unremarkable. The fact that it is still worth writing about — that I still feel it as a relief, a small exhale, when I see it — is something I think about on the drive home.

The schools are working. The teachers are in the buildings. The children came back.

For September 2041, that is the story. It is enough of a story.

############################################################
AFTER THE BELL
Youth Life in 2041 Oakland — When the 3:15 Whistle Blows

By Angela Reyes | Bay Tribune Education
############################################################

----

The building closes but the day doesn't.

At 3:15 PM on a Wednesday in September, I stood at the corner of International Boulevard and 37th Avenue and watched what happens when 480 children leave Fruitvale Bilingual Academy at once. The bus bays fill first — four OUSD yellow buses, loading in order by route number, the drivers already in their seats, the monitors walking the line. Then the walkers, moving in clusters: siblings together, best friends together, the loners who are not sad, just private. Then the pickup cars, circling the block because the drop-off zone backs up past the taqueria on the corner.

The taqueria owner comes out at 3:20 every school day, I am told, and props the door open.

By 3:35, the block is quiet again.

The question is: where did 480 children go?

----

**THE WALKERS — FRUITVALE**

In Fruitvale, a significant portion of students walk to after-school care at the Fruitvale Recreation Center, three blocks west of International Boulevard. The center opens at 3 PM and runs a supervised program until 6:30 for working families. On the Wednesday I visited, forty-one children from Fruitvale Bilingual had signed into the program by 4 PM, ranging from kindergarten to seventh grade.

Dario Vega, 44, has directed the Fruitvale Recreation Center for six years. Before that, he worked at a center in the Dimond neighborhood, and before that, he played semi-professional soccer for six years, a fact that explains both his build and his approach to managing rooms full of children: patient, structured, comfortable with energy.

"After school is not school," he told me, standing in the main activity room while a table of second-graders worked on a puzzle and a group of fifth-graders played a card game that had its own elaborate rules. "The worst thing a rec center can do is try to be school part two. The kids have just come from six hours of sitting down and listening. They need to move. They need to choose. Then they'll settle."

The Fruitvale Rec Center program runs a loose structure: first thirty minutes is open play or outdoor time (weather permitting), then a snack break, then a homework block, then a second free period until pickup. The homework block is supervised by two education aides — not teachers, not tutors, but people who can answer a fraction question and spell "Mediterranean" and know when a kid needs encouragement versus needs to be left alone.

Ivy Lopez, a parent who works afternoon shifts at a distribution warehouse in San Leandro, relies entirely on the rec center program for her two children. She picked them up at 5:45.

"I am there every day I can be," she said, in the parking lot outside the center. "When I'm not — I know they're here. That's the whole thing. I know where they are."

Her son, who is in fifth grade, had won the card game. He showed her the score on a piece of paper. She congratulated him with the gravity it deserved.

----

**THE PROGRAMS — EAST OAKLAND**

Allen Temple Academy runs its own after-school program on-site, funded through a combination of OUSD extended learning dollars and a partnership with Allen Temple Baptist Church next door. The church provides a second facility space — the fellowship hall — for overflow when the school gym is in use.

Marcus Ellison stays until 5 PM twice a week to run a voluntary tutoring session he calls "Office Hours," a name he borrowed from his college experience. The name matters to him.

"College students go to office hours," he said. "I want fifth graders to understand that the people who teach them are available. That you can ask a question after class. That there is no after class." He keeps a sign-in sheet. Some weeks, two students come. Some weeks, twelve. "This week, Day 3, I had seven. That's a good first week."

Davion Freeman, 12, is in Ellison's class and lives four blocks from the school. He does not take the bus. He walks home with his older sister, who is in eighth grade at the same school, every day. On the Wednesday I followed him — with his mother's permission — he stopped at a corner store to buy a bag of chips, talked to a man sitting outside who turned out to be a neighbor who'd known his family for years, and arrived home at 3:41.

He sat at the kitchen table and started his homework without being asked.

"I like to get it done," he said, when I asked. He looked at the worksheet for a moment. "Then I can watch whatever I want."

His sister came home twelve minutes later and made them both a snack.

----

**THE DATA PROGRAM — KONO/TEMESCAL**

The Gridiron Analytics after-school data literacy program runs Tuesday and Thursday at OUASA, from 3:30 to 5:00 PM. It is, on the surface, a math program. But Keisha Morales, 29, who coordinates the program for Gridiron Analytics and runs the Tuesday sessions, describes it as something more specific.

"We're teaching kids to read a number and ask what it means," she said, sitting at the front of the OUASA auxiliary classroom where fourteen middle schoolers were arranged at tables, each with a tablet open to a baseball dataset. On the board: a single question in large text: DOES STRIKEOUTS PER GAME PREDICT WINS?

"We use baseball because Oakland has a team," Morales said. "And because the A's have been good for a long time, which means there's a lot of data, which means the patterns are more interesting."

Jerome Banks, 14, a ninth-grader at OUASA whose father is Theo Banks, was in the Thursday session. He'd been in the program since sixth grade — one of the original cohort when Daniel Yoon and Christine Nakamura piloted the first version at two schools. He now mentors the newer participants, a role the program formalized in its second year.

"The first year, I thought it was about baseball," he said. He was looking at a tablet, half-attending to our conversation, in the way ninth-graders attend to things they've already understood. "It's not about baseball. Baseball is just the language. We're learning how to not be fooled by a number."

He paused.

"That's what Christine said. 'Don't be fooled by a number.' First day, she wrote it on the board. I still think about it."

The program's enrollment for Fall 2041 is thirty-eight students across the two weekly sessions, the most it has had since launching. Morales said the wait list has grown to twenty-two names. "We're working on it," she said. Whether that means more sessions, more facilitators, or a partner school location is, she said carefully, "a conversation that's ongoing."

----

**THE WALK HOME — ROCKRIDGE**

Not every child goes to a program. At Claremont Hills Elementary, where a parent-run enrichment fund supports music and visual arts instruction, many students also simply go home.

Diane Sato told me she'd asked her class on Day 2 what they do after school. The answers were varied in the way children's answers always are — specific, unguarded, occasionally surprising. One child said she practices piano for forty-five minutes. One child said he plays video games. One child said he goes to his grandmother's house and helps with dinner. One child said he doesn't do anything, with the authority of someone who has thought carefully about the merits of doing nothing.

"That kid is going to be fine," Sato said.

Kevin Park, a parent from Rockridge whose daughter is in second grade at Claremont Hills, picks up every day at 3:15. He is retired, which makes him unusual in the pickup line — most of the parents who pick up are running against a clock, checking phones, loading kids into cars while giving driving directions to voice assistants. Park is not doing any of this.

"I got her a library card this summer," he said. "We go to the Rockridge branch on Tuesdays. She picks two books. I pick one — she has to read mine, I have to read hers. Then we argue about it at dinner." He smiled. "She wins most of the arguments."

His daughter came out in the middle of the crowd, found him immediately, and showed him something in her backpack. A paper, folded. She waited for him to read it. He read it, nodded seriously, folded it back the same way, and put it in his shirt pocket like a dispatch from the front.

----

**THE HOURS BETWEEN**

I've been reporting on Oakland schools for eleven years. Before that, I was teaching in one. The question I was trying to answer in the first year after I left the classroom — the question I am still trying to answer — is this: what does it take for a city's children to be all right?

Not successful. Not high-achieving. Not future-economy-ready. Just: all right. Safe. Fed. Known by at least one adult. Going somewhere at 3:15 that has been thought about.

In September 2041, in four Oakland neighborhoods, the answer is: it takes a lot of ordinary things working at once. A rec center director who understands that after school is not school. A teacher who stays until five for office hours that eight kids might come to. A baseball dataset that teaches ninth-graders not to be fooled by a number. A grandfather who argues about library books at dinner.

The ordinary things are not ordinary to provide. They require funding and staffing and decisions that were made years ago and are reflected now, this September, in the fact that 480 children left Fruitvale Bilingual Academy and most of them went somewhere, and the places they went were ready for them.

Forty years ago, that was not reliably true in this city. It is closer to true now than it has been in a long time.

I still have my gradebook. I still think about those twenty-seven names.

The ordinary things matter.

THE QUIET INVESTMENT
How Oakland's tech companies are showing up in schools. Follow the money.

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

----

When Oakmesh Systems installed air quality sensors in three East Oakland schools last spring, nobody made a press release about it. No ribbon cutting. No "partnering to improve student outcomes" statement. Marcus Tan, the company's founder, had a conversation with a principal about indoor PM2.5 levels and what happens when a classroom reaches 35 micrograms per cubic meter. One thing led to another.

The sensors are there now. They hang on classroom walls like thermostats. The data runs live to student dashboards — not as a gesture toward STEM education, but because a science teacher can use real-time air quality to teach data interpretation, and because knowing when to open windows saves money on HVAC cycling. Tan's company didn't donate the hardware. The district bought it. The installation was on a pilot grant from the Bay Area Air Quality Management District. The data ownership lives in a shared agreement nobody's written a story about.

This is what tech investment in Oakland schools actually looks like in 2041. Not as heroic gesture. As infrastructure.

Tenth Street Digital's app is the most visible thread. Jasmine Torres, 28, grew up in Fruitvale and learned to code during a late-night conversation with a stranger at a coffee shop who worked in tech. She built a civic tools suite — permit trackers, budget visualizers, and one piece that sticks: a parent communication platform that actually works on mobile. Fruitvale Elementary uses it. So do four other schools. Torres built it as a contractor. The district pays her firm for maintenance and support. Not a partnership. A service contract. The money moves. She stays in Fruitvale. The school gets a product that parents use because it's functional, not because it's funded by a tech company's PR budget.

Gridiron Analytics landed differently. Daniel Yoon and Christine Nakamura, both Stanford PhDs and both trained in predictive modeling, run a data literacy after-school program out of Downtown Oakland. They work with middle school students on baseball statistics — learning how to read raw datasets, spot patterns, calculate probabilities, understand what the numbers don't tell you. The program costs money. Who funds it? A combination: partial district funding for after-school hours, partial grant support from the Oakland Education Foundation, partial subsidy from Gridiron itself because Yoon and Nakamura believe in it. No entity is carrying the whole load. The students learn data literacy. The company gets visibility into early-stage analytical thinking. The district funds hours, not salaries.

The Mills College pipeline is the longest investment — the one that moves through years instead of quarters. Mills's computational ethics program has been feeding Anthropic and other Jack London companies since 2037. How many Oakland graduates made that jump? Fifteen in the last three years, I counted in public records. How many of them came through public school classrooms where someone mentioned the possibility? Unknown. The pipeline isn't officially tracked. But it exists as fact: an Oakland kid takes AP Computer Science at a school with decent resources, gets exposed to ethics frameworks in a college program a BART ride away, and lands a technical job in the same city three years later, making $110K, still living in Oakland because the rent hasn't killed the possibility entirely.

That's the thread nobody talks about. Not which tech company "gave back." Which tech company moved money into the system in a way that stuck.

Oakmesh Systems didn't donate sensors as corporate citizenship. They sold them to a district that chose to buy them. Torres didn't volunteer to build parent software. She formed a business. Yoon and Nakamura didn't establish a scholarship fund. They created a program that cost them, partially, because it worked for three populations at once.

The question Jordan Velez always asks: who gets paid first, and for what? In Oakland's tech-education ecosystem, the answer is: whoever can prove the work gets done. The money follows the work. The city gets schools that function because infrastructure improved, not because someone felt generous. That's not romance. That's investment. That's the kind that lasts.
