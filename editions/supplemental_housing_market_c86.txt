KEYS TO THE CITY

What it looks like to buy — and sell — a home in Oakland right now

By Sharon Okafor | Bay Tribune Lifestyle

[Photo: DJ Hartley / Bay Tribune]

The house on Chabot Road had been on the market for eleven days. Eleven days, which in Rockridge in August 2041 is not a short time — it is the full span of a conversation that a neighborhood is having with itself about what it's worth.

Priya Sandoval, 38, knew this. She pulled her car to the curb, sat for a moment, and looked at the house before she got out. Two stories. Deep porch. A Japanese maple in the front yard going copper at the edges. She had her phone out with the listing pulled up, but she wasn't looking at her phone.

"I always sit in the car first," she told me later, over coffee at the open house staging table. "Before I see the inside, I want to know what I feel in my chest when I look at it."

Sandoval works in biotech supply chain logistics, lives currently in Temescal, and has been looking to buy in Rockridge or Piedmont Avenue for eight months. She said it with the easy fluency of someone who has rehearsed the sentence many times. Eight months of Saturdays. Eight months of sitting in the car first.

She is not unusual. I spent three weekends tracking the Oakland housing market — open houses, listing meetings, two closings, one pre-close walkthrough — and the picture that emerged is of a city that people are actively, specifically, deliberately choosing to live in. Not because they have to. Because they want to.

---

The agent showing the Chabot Road property was Desiree Achebe, 44, of Harborview Residential, a boutique real estate firm she founded three years ago with offices in Uptown. Achebe grew up in East Oakland, bought her first home in Fruitvale at 31, and now specializes in what she calls "legacy blocks" — neighborhoods where people stay, where the ownership goes deep, where a sale is an event that people on the street notice.

"In Rockridge, you know when a house goes on the market," she said. "The neighbors know before the sign goes up. That's not gossip — that's a neighborhood paying attention."

Achebe walks a showing the way some people walk a museum. Slowly, with pauses. She let Sandoval lead the tour of the Chabot house, staying half a room behind, only speaking when asked. When Sandoval stopped in the kitchen and put both hands on the counter, Achebe stopped too. She said nothing.

"That's the moment," Achebe told me afterward. "When their hands go down on a surface. They're testing if it holds them."

The kitchen held. Sandoval lingered for four minutes.

---

Harborview Residential is one of the smaller agencies working the Oakland market, but in a city where buyers increasingly want someone who knows the block rather than the algorithm, Achebe has built a following. Her associate, Marcus Thibodeau, 36, runs the firm's waterfront portfolio — Jack London condos, the new Baylight-adjacent listings, the properties where the bay is visible from the bedroom window.

"Jack London is different from the rest of Oakland," Thibodeau told me when I visited him at a listing off Embarcadero West on a Thursday afternoon. "People who buy here are buying the water. They want to wake up and know it's out there."

The listing was a two-bedroom condominium on the fourth floor of a building that had been retrofitted with smart-home integration — lights, climate, security, appliance monitoring all run from a single panel by the front door or a phone. Thibodeau walked me through the panel the way you'd explain a new car to someone: enthusiastic, technical, clearly expecting to do this twelve more times this week.

"The buyers I get here, they want this," he said, tapping the panel. "They're not intimidated by it. They grew up with this. The question they ask isn't 'how does it work.' It's 'can I add to it.'"

I asked him what the difference was between showing a smart home and showing a traditional property.

He thought about it. "With a traditional house, you're selling what's there. With a smart house, you're selling what's possible. It's a different psychology. You have to show them the platform, not just the room."

---

Two miles away in Temescal, Kevin Nelson, 55, a tattoo artist who has owned his home on 49th Street for about five years, was not thinking about selling. He was thinking about his neighbor's for-sale sign.

"Third house on the block this year," he said. We were standing on his front steps. He had a mug of coffee and the relaxed authority of a man who has no plans to go anywhere. "Doesn't worry me. People sell. That's how it works."

Nelson bought when a realtor drove him to see a corner unit and told him to look at the afternoon light. He looked. He bought. Now, he watches the open houses on weekends with a particular local calm — not hostile to the newcomers, genuinely curious about who'll end up next door.

"The neighborhood changed," he said. "It changed and it's still the neighborhood. Temescal has that. Some places change and they lose something. This place kept the thing it had." He paused. "I don't know what to call the thing it had. It's just the thing."

He was describing, without the vocabulary, what behavioral researchers call place attachment — the emotional bond between a person and a physical location. I spoke with Dr. Anita Lourdes, an environmental psychologist at UC Berkeley, who said the Oakland market, in a prosperity period, creates an unusual dynamic: high attachment in existing owners, high aspiration in new buyers, and very little of the ambient anxiety that characterizes markets where people feel pushed rather than pulled.

"When a market is working well — when people are buying because they want to, not because they have to, and selling because they're ready, not because they're forced — the emotional register is completely different," Dr. Lourdes said. "You see pride from sellers. You see aspiration from buyers. The energy in an open house is fundamentally different when everyone in the room chose to be there."

---

I watched that energy at an open house on Benvenue Avenue in Rockridge on a Saturday afternoon. Twelve people came through in the two hours I was there. Kevin Ochoa, 76, an AI safety researcher who has lived in Rockridge for over a decade, walked through with the proprietary confidence of a man who knows this neighborhood block by block. He wasn't buying — a neighbor had told him the house was open, and he'd stopped in.

"I like to see what's happening to the inventory," he said with a small smile that made clear he understood exactly how that sounded. "When I moved here, there were three houses on this block for sale in one summer. Now there's one every few months. That tells me something."

What it tells him, he said, is that people are staying. That the cycle of turnover has slowed because the people who bought into Rockridge have decided, mostly, that they're not leaving.

"That's the best thing you can say about a neighborhood," he said. "People came. Then they stayed."

---

Priya Sandoval put in an offer on the Chabot Road house the following Monday.

I know because Desiree Achebe texted me — not to share the news, she texted to tell me Sandoval had asked if the Japanese maple would convey with the property. It would. It does. In the paperwork, someone had thought to specify it.

I think about that when I think about what the Oakland housing market looks like right now. Not the interest rates, not the square footage, not the listing strategy. The maple. Someone making sure the tree comes with the house. Someone deciding, in writing, that the thing that made them sit in the car and feel something in their chest was worth naming and keeping.

That's what buying a home in Oakland looks like in August 2041. People choosing specifically. People staying deliberately. A city confident enough in itself that the maple is worth the paperwork.

THE BLOCKS

What makes an Oakland neighborhood? Walk the streets with us.

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

I have lived in seven Oakland neighborhoods over forty years, and I still can't tell you which one I love best. I've tried. I made a list once — Fruitvale, Temescal, West Oakland, the Laurel, Rockridge — and I got as far as writing down the names before I gave up, because ranking them felt like ranking languages. Each one says something the others can't.

What I can tell you is what they feel like on a Tuesday afternoon when you've got nowhere to be, and what they feel like on a Saturday when the city turns itself loose. That's what the market actually runs on, I think. Not square footage. Not school ratings. Not proximity to BART, though everyone mentions BART. It's the feeling you carry home with you.

---

ROCKRIDGE

Drive up College Avenue on a Saturday morning and the first thing you notice is the trees. Oakland has trees everywhere — it's not a barren city — but Rockridge has old trees, the kind that shade the street in thick, cathedral arches. The Craftsman bungalows sit back behind their lawns, their wide front porches catching the light in that particular late-summer way that looks like a photograph from 1928.

I had coffee last week with Kevin Ochoa, 76, who has lived on the same Rockridge block for nearly two decades. He came here for the work — he's an AI safety researcher at Anthropic, one of the East Bay's big employers — but he stayed for the porches. "I can see three neighbors' front porches from my own," he told me, nursing his coffee. "That's rarer than it sounds."

He's right. Rockridge is a neighborhood where people still use their front porches, still wave across the street, still know the names of each other's dogs. The houses here are substantial — two-story Craftsmans, smart home upgrades wired into century-old frames, renovated kitchens behind leaded-glass windows. They look like they've earned their place, which is a feeling a house can give you if it's stood long enough.

Linda Chow, 51, also an AI safety researcher, moved to Rockridge with her husband eight years ago. She grew up in the Sunset District in San Francisco and was initially skeptical about crossing the Bay. "I didn't understand what Oakland was," she said, laughing a little. "Then I walked College Avenue once on a fall afternoon and I understood immediately."

Rockridge works because it doesn't feel like it's trying to impress you. The restaurants don't have velvet ropes. The bookstore has cats sleeping in the windows. The Saturday farmers market fills a parking lot with stone fruit and fresh pasta and the particular smell of someone charring peppers over an open flame. You can live here at a high level of comfort without ever feeling like you're performing comfort for an audience.

---

TEMESCAL

Temescal is Rockridge's younger, louder cousin, and it knows it. Telegraph Avenue in Temescal on a Saturday afternoon is something else — food truck smoke mixing with somebody's Bluetooth speaker two blocks away, a line out the door of a ramen place that's been fully booked since before you woke up, the occasional smell of fresh ink from a tattoo shop with its door propped open.

The housing stock in Temescal is different from Rockridge. More Victorians, some of them freshly painted in the deep jewel tones that the Bay Area does better than anywhere else — plum, forest green, ochre. More apartments in converted Edwardians with high ceilings and bay windows looking out onto Telegraph. More live-work spaces, because Temescal has always attracted makers and artists who need room for both a studio and a bed.

Tyrone Baker, 55, is a ceramicist who has his studio in the neighborhood and lives four blocks away. He's been in Oakland most of his adult life and moved to Temescal when he found a unit with north light, which is what ceramicists look for when they're looking for an apartment. "The light here in the morning," he said, gesturing at nothing in particular, "is very particular. It doesn't come in and shout. It just fills up."

That's Temescal. It fills up. By noon on a weekend, every coffee shop is full, every sidewalk table is taken, and the vibe is genuinely warm — people staying too long, ordering another round, not rushing. The neighborhood has that quality of making time slow down a little, which in a dynasty-era city moving at this pace is worth something real.

---

JACK LONDON

The Jack London waterfront is newer in feel even when it isn't new in fact. Standing at the edge of the estuary on a clear evening, with the lights of the Bay Bridge reflecting off the water and the A's stadium lit up across the way, you understand why people pay what they pay to live here. The view is doing a lot of work.

But Jamal Brown, 69, a harbor tugboat captain who has lived here for years, would tell you the view is the least of it. "You live near the water, the water gets into your habits," he said one morning, standing at the railing of his building's common deck. "I'm up before the sun because the shipping lane gets busy before the sun. I know the sound of every different kind of hull moving through that water."

There are converted warehouse lofts with eighteen-foot ceilings and industrial windows. There are newer high-rise condos with smart home systems and roof decks and doormen. There's the old warehouse district where artists claimed space two decades ago and some of them are still there, surrounded now by the Baylight development rising along the former port edge. The neighborhood is in conversation with itself about what it wants to be, and the conversation is a good one — energetic, not anxious.

What Jack London offers that nowhere else does: the sense of being at the edge of something. Not the edge of the city, not the literal edge, but the edge of where Oakland touches the larger world. The container ships move past. The tugboats go out. The cranes stand in the evening light like prehistoric creatures. It makes you feel like you're part of a city with real consequence.

---

FRUITVALE

I grew up in Fruitvale, which means I'm incapable of describing it without bias, so I'll just describe it.

International Boulevard on a Saturday afternoon sounds like six languages at once. The smell of carnitas comes off the taqueria on the corner before you can see the restaurant — it hits you half a block away. The street art on the shuttered storefront has been there three years but someone has painted a frame around it, like a curator acknowledging what's good.

The houses in Fruitvale are different from the Craftsmans of Rockridge or the Victorians of Temescal. Many of them are bungalows that have been with the same families for generations — painted in colors that announce themselves, with gardens that spill over the sidewalk, with statues of La Virgen visible through front windows. Some of the newest buildings in the neighborhood are mixed-use developments with retail below and units above, and they've been designed to fit the street instead of replace it.

What Fruitvale has that nowhere else does: the experience of a neighborhood that knows itself. It has been through things. It has also been celebrated, studied, written about, visited by people who wanted to understand Oakland. It receives all of that attention and then closes its door at night and has dinner, and the dinner is exceptional, and the family is loud, and the walls are covered in photographs, and nobody needs a stranger's validation to know what they have.

I walked International Boulevard last Sunday, late afternoon. A woman was selling tamales from a folding table at the corner of 35th, a hand-lettered sign propped against the cooler. She was doing about twelve conversations at once — a neighbor asking after her sister, a kid asking for extra salsa, a man in a truck rolling down his window to call out an order. She handled all of it with the efficiency of someone who has been doing exactly this for exactly this long, which is to say: expertly.

That's Fruitvale. That's the block, and the whole neighborhood is the block.

---

I have moved seven times in this city and I have never once moved to a neighborhood that disappointed me, which I think is Oakland's deepest secret: every part of it is good, and each part is good in a different way.

The market understands this. People aren't just buying square footage. They're buying a version of the city. And in August 2041, there are a lot of versions to want.

THE CLOSER

Patrice Lemon has sold more Oakland homes than she can count. She hasn't run out of things to say about any of them.

By Mason Ortega | Bay Tribune Food & Hospitality

I spent a Thursday afternoon following Patrice Lemon through three properties she was showing in a single afternoon. A two-bedroom loft in Jack London. A Craftsman bungalow in Piedmont Avenue. A Victorian in Temescal that she'd already shown six times. By five o'clock she had a signed offer on the Victorian and was back at the office at Lemon & Root Realty, on Telegraph near 40th, updating her board.

She didn't break a sweat. I've worked brunch service in kitchens that moved slower than this woman's Thursday afternoon. Three showings, a close, a board update — she ran it like a line cook running tickets: next plate, next plate, never stop moving, never look rushed.

"This is a good market," she said, uncapping a green marker and drawing a thick line through a listing she'd posted three weeks ago. "Not good like easy. Good like honest. People who want to be here know what they're looking at. I don't have to sell Oakland. Oakland sells itself."

Patrice Lemon, 52, has been selling homes in this city for twenty-one years. She grew up in Temescal — her mother's house is three blocks from the office she now owns — and she's watched the neighborhood shift around her the way a person watches a slow tide. She founded Lemon & Root in 2027 with her business partner, Diane Root, who handles the financial side while Patrice handles everything that requires being in a room with another human being. The agency specializes in what Patrice calls "character property" — homes that have a history, a stance, a reason to exist. Victorians, Craftsmans, converted warehouses, lofts above loading docks. Not the cookie-cutter stuff.

"A house is a decision," she said. "When I put a buyer in front of a property, I'm not showing them square footage. I'm showing them a life. Who do you want to be when you wake up in the morning? What do you want to hear through the window?"

The Victorian on Shattuck — the one she closed Thursday — has original fir floors, a clawfoot tub, and a kitchen that was renovated twice: once badly in the 1980s and once correctly in 2031. She's sold it twice. The previous owners, a couple who'd bought it as their starter home when they were thirty-two, outgrew it and moved to Rockridge when their third kid arrived. They cried at the closing. Patrice had a box of tissues in her bag.

"People think real estate is transactional," she said. "It's not. You're handling the most expensive thing most people will ever own, and it's not a car — you can't return it. Every closing is a transfer of someone's whole life situation. I take that seriously."

She shows this seriousness in unexpected ways. She knows the commute time from every property she lists to the main transit hubs — not from an app, from memory. She knows which streets flood when it rains hard and which don't. She's walked every block in the neighborhoods she works, not for research, but because she lives there and always has. When Kevin Nelson, 55, a tattoo artist who's been in Temescal for years, mentioned he was thinking about buying instead of renting, it was Patrice he called.

"She showed me three places," Nelson told me, standing in front of his shop on Telegraph. "But the one she really wanted me to see — she drove me over there herself and said, 'Just look at the light in the afternoon.' It was a corner unit. The light was incredible. That's the one I bought."

That was about five years ago. Nelson's still in the unit.

The Jack London loft I trailed her through Thursday morning — twelve-foot ceilings, a view of the estuary that on a clear day shows the whole span of the Bay Bridge — was listed by Lemon & Root two weeks ago and had already seen multiple showings. The buyers she brought through were a couple in their late thirties, both working in the tech corridor that's grown up around the Baylight District. They stood at the window for a long time without saying anything.

Patrice stood in the kitchen and let them look. She didn't talk. This, she explained later, is most of the job.

"The home either speaks to you or it doesn't. My job in that moment is to shut up and let it talk."

She's been doing this long enough to know when a buyer's going to close and when they're not. The couple at the Jack London loft — she gave them even odds. They asked about parking and then asked again. That's a tell, she says. "When buyers start asking about the same thing twice, they're trying to talk themselves out of something they already want." She said she'd follow up Friday.

After the Victorian closing, she stood in the Lemon & Root office for a moment, marker still in hand, looking at the board. Twenty-two listings. Six pending. Four closed this month.

"I always wanted to do something that felt like it mattered every day," she said. "I thought maybe medicine, maybe law. But I grew up in these streets. I know what it means when a family gets into a house they love. It's everything."

She capped the marker. Her phone buzzed. She answered it before the second ring.

"This is Patrice."
