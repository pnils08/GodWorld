**Day 45: Oakland's Alternative Response Initiative Reaches Its Own Deadline**
*OARI's 45-day implementation clock expires this week — dispatch integration still unconfirmed*
By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

The Oakland Alternative Response Initiative passed the city council on a 5-4 vote seven weeks ago. The ordinance included a 45-day implementation window — the kind of deadline that sounds firm until it isn't.

This week, the clock runs out.

As of Labor Day weekend, the question the initiative's sponsors have declined to answer in plain terms is whether a single co-responder team has taken a single call in Districts 1, 3, or 5. The Mayor's Office confirmed Friday that four responders are finalized and four remain under offer. What the office did not confirm — and what the Tribune has asked three times since August — is whether OPD's dispatch integration protocol has been formally approved.

That protocol is the program's technical spine. Without it, co-responders cannot be routed to behavioral health calls through the existing dispatch system. Without routing, the teams exist on paper.

Councilmember Janae Rivers, whose District 5 is one of the three pilot areas, said Monday that her office had received "verbal assurances" from Chief Montez that OPD was prepared to proceed. Whether verbal assurance constitutes the written integration spec the ordinance requires is a distinction the administration has not resolved publicly.

"I believed in this program when I voted for it," Rivers said. "I still believe in it. But 45 days means 45 days. If we need more time, we say we need more time — we don't just let the calendar pass."

Councilmember Leonard Tran, who provided the decisive fifth vote in June, offered a colder read. He said he would "recalibrate — not cancel" if the pilot districts had not seen active deployments by the deadline. He did not use the word "cancel." He did not rule it out either.

The three CRC members — Crane, Ashford, and Chen — voted against the initiative and have not publicly reopened that position. Elliott Crane, who is recovering and participated in recent council sessions remotely, declined through a spokesman to comment on implementation status. Nina Chen said only that the council would evaluate performance data when it became available.

Calvin Turner, a mechanic in Fruitvale who has watched city programs move through his neighborhood for two decades, sat outside a shop on East 14th Street Tuesday and said the program's situation sounded familiar to him. His district — D5 under Janae Rivers — was one of the named pilots.

"They vote on something, they say it's happening, and then you wait," Turner said. "I'm not saying it's broken. I'm saying I've heard 'the program is in place' before the program was actually in place."

Turner said he had not heard of any co-responder deployments in his neighborhood. He said he would notice. "That's not a program you'd miss if it was running."

Gloria Meeks, who works nights at a parking garage on Broadway in Downtown, said she was watching the OARI situation for different reasons. Downtown was not included in the pilot districts — a gap she flagged when the initiative passed.

"I work until two in the morning," Meeks said. "I know what the streets look like at two in the morning. If this program works in D1, D3, and D5, maybe they expand it. That's what I'm waiting to see."

The administration has until end of business Friday — the technical conclusion of the 45-day window — to confirm written dispatch integration approval. If that deadline passes without documentation, the Mayor's Office will face a harder question: not whether OARI works, but whether it has started.

**The Clock and the Calendar**
*By Mags Corliss | Editor-in-Chief*

September arrived the way it does in Oakland — wearing summer's clothes but carrying fall's weight.

The OARI clock runs out this week. Carmen Delaine has been counting the days since July, and the number is now the same as the number of days the ordinance promised. Forty-five. Whether a single co-responder has taken a single call in the pilot districts is a question the administration has not answered plainly, and the Tribune has asked it three times.

**Fruitvale Residents Report Water Pressure Drop; Utility Investigating**
By Trevor Shimizu | Bay Tribune Transit & Infrastructure

Residents and business owners along International Boulevard in Fruitvale reported inconsistent water pressure beginning Thursday morning, with some households describing flow reductions sufficient to interrupt showers and slow commercial food prep during a holiday weekend. East Bay Municipal Utility District confirmed Friday it had dispatched crews to assess the corridor between 35th and 42nd Avenues.

EBMUD did not identify a cause by press time. A district spokesperson said the issue appeared "localized" and that pressure readings at the primary distribution node had not triggered the threshold for a formal advisory. No boil-water notice has been issued.

The timing is inconvenient in multiple directions. Labor Day weekend pushed the disruption into the highest residential-use period of the week. Several small restaurants on International Boulevard reported partial service interruptions during their lunch service — a problem that compounds during a holiday stretch when foot traffic is higher than normal.

Rafael Phillips, 26, who servers at a restaurant near Fruitvale BART, said he came in Friday morning to prep for the holiday weekend and found the kitchen sink running at about half its normal pressure. His manager called EBMUD and was told to monitor the situation.

"It came back up by noon," Phillips said. "But if it happens again on a Saturday — that's a real problem. That's the middle of service."

The infrastructure question behind the immediate inconvenience is not new to Fruitvale. The neighborhood is carrying old pipe stock through a period of increasing development pressure, and the $230 million Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II project — authorized in August — will bring construction activity to the corridor that utility engineers typically flag as a risk factor for pressure variance.

Elena Soria Dominguez, the Planning Lead on the Transit Hub project, said in August that the design consultant RFP would be issued next month. Until a contractor is selected and a mobilization timeline is published, the utility disruption question sits in a planning gap: the project is real, the construction is coming, but the coordination protocols between the Transit Hub builder and EBMUD have not been publicly established.

Lucia Polito, 64, who has operated a small business in Fruitvale for more than twenty years, said Thursday's pressure drop was the third utility irregularity she could recall in the past eighteen months — not counting a sinkhole on a side street near her block two years ago.

"Every time something happens, they fix it and move on," Polito said. "Nobody asks why it keeps happening."

EBMUD said it expects a preliminary assessment by mid-week.

**The Waterfront Is Moving. Some Questions Aren't Answered Yet.**
By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

A commercial drone clipped a light standard near the intersection of Broadway and Embarcadero Friday afternoon, scattering pedestrians and drawing a brief OPD response. No injuries were reported. The drone's operator was not immediately identified.

The incident lasted about four minutes. The light standard sustained minor damage. OPD issued a standard statement and cleared the scene.

What makes it worth noting is what it interrupted. The blocks around the Jack London waterfront are in a different state than they were two months ago. Heavy equipment has been staged near the Baylight development footprint. Remediation crews have been observed on two parcels along the estuary edge. The noise is different. The fencing is different. The neighborhood feels like it is waiting for something large to happen.

The council passed the Baylight District bond 6-3 in June, with all three CRC members voting no. Mayor Santana said at the time that construction would begin September 15. That date is two weeks away.

What the city has not said publicly — as of Friday — is who holds the anchor tenant lease. The Baylight Authority disclosed in July that two unnamed tier-one technology companies were in active discussion for the lead office space. The Q3 deadline for anchor tenant disclosure is the same deadline as fiscal close on the TIF bond instrument.

The Tribune requested comment from the Baylight Authority on Tuesday. The authority said in a brief written statement that negotiations were "proceeding on schedule." It did not name the tenant or confirm that the TIF had been finalized.

Derek Simmons, 38, who works in the Jack London corridor and has watched the Baylight preparations unfold from his office windows, said the physical activity near the waterfront had accelerated noticeably in the past two weeks.

"Something is moving," Simmons said. "Whether it's moving toward September 15 or past September 15, I can't tell you. But something is moving."

Warren Ashford, who voted no on Baylight and has been the CRC's most consistent voice on the TIF's thirty-year fiscal exposure, declined to comment on the timeline. His office said he was reviewing implementation documents.

When an office says it is "reviewing documents" two weeks before a stated groundbreaking, that is either due diligence or it is distance. Luis Navarro has seen both, and they don't always look different from the outside.

**BUSINESS TICKER**
By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

The two-week review window for Oakland's Stabilization Fund applications is now live. Mayor Santana's deadline expires in thirteen days. Two hundred seventy-seven applications are in the queue — approved families waiting for disbursement authorization, borderline applications waiting for final processing, and new submissions that have arrived this week. The four accountability conditions attached to the $387,000 authorization last month are now operational: monthly Finance Committee reporting, quarterly audits, the two-week review window itself, and Community Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro's direct oversight. What makes this period different from the last is that the benchmarks are now public and measurable. The CRC's demand for an independent third-party audit has added another layer of pressure — OEWD Director Marcus Webb knows the city is watching the process, not just the outcomes. In Fruitvale, the Transit Hub has moved from council vote to design phase. The architects are now incorporating final resident feedback before the project enters permitting. Groundbreaking remains eighteen months out. That timeline matters because it overlaps with Baylight's mobilization window. Both projects will compete for construction labor, equipment vendors, and apprenticeship pipeline slots within roughly the same twelve-month window. I asked Ray Muñoz, 54, a longshoreman at the Port of Oakland and a regular at the Saturday market, whether he was seeing the construction crews shift from project offices to actual job sites. "They're eating," he said. "Three days ago I saw them in clusters at Harborline Grill, talking about equipment deliveries. That's different from the sales talk. That's mobilization." I spoke with Jalen Hill, 35, a line cook at a Jack London restaurant who has been watching the corridor fill up through the summer. "The evening crowds are real," he said. "But they're not stable yet. One bad weekend and we'll know whether this was summer or whether this is the new baseline."

The distinction Jalen is making is crucial for every commercial corridor in Oakland right now. The surge in Jack London this summer — elevated evening activity, secondary spillover into Uptown, the Saturday market running above capacity — has generated real payroll increases for retail and food service workers. But economic surges are not the same as economic shifts. A surge is a weather pattern. A shift is a climate change. Jalen's point is that nobody knows which one this is yet. The first Friday of September should tell part of that story. If the crowds hold, the conversation moves to hiring and wages. If they collapse, it's a data point. For the Stabilization Fund families waiting in the queue, a two-week window means decisions land in the coming weeks. For the labor pipeline question, the Baylight + Transit Hub overlap is no longer a future scenario — the timeline is now.

**Where September Comes In Through the Front Door**
By Kai Marston | Bay Tribune Arts & Entertainment

By nine o'clock Friday night, the stretch of Telegraph between 19th and 23rd was doing what it does the first Friday of every month — filling in from the edges. People came through gallery doors that stayed open late, past tables of hand-screen-printed tote bags, past a kid playing something between jazz and something that doesn't have a name yet on a secondhand keyboard propped against a parking meter.

But this was the first Friday of September. Labor Day weekend. The summer's last official exhale sitting right up against fall's first inhale.

I was in the KONO stretch by eight, watching the dinner crowd spill out of KONO Kitchen and merge with the gallery walkers coming north from Uptown. These two populations usually operate on slightly different clocks, and on an ordinary First Friday they barely overlap — the restaurant crowd peaks at seven-thirty, the art crowd peaks closer to ten. Labor Day weekend compressed the timing. People had nowhere to be Saturday morning, so they stayed.

Marin Tao, whose large-format mixed-media pieces currently occupy the main wall of a gallery space on 23rd near Broadway, stood just inside the open door talking to two people who had clearly not planned to stop but had. Tao's work this month is dark blues and rust oranges — colors that sit right at the season's turning. "I painted these in July thinking about October," Tao said to no one in particular, or maybe to everyone. "Everybody's surprised they look like fall."

Avery Peña, 20, came up from Lake Merritt on the bus with a friend. She had the slightly deliberate look of someone trying to make an evening out of a Friday on a budget. "We do this when it's the first Friday," she said, outside the gallery on 23rd, eating a paleta she had picked up from a cart near 20th. "It's free. The art is free. You just have to show up."

That's the thing about First Friday that resists easy description: it is simultaneously one of Oakland's most high-profile cultural events and one of its most genuinely accessible ones. No cover. No reservation. No dress code that anyone enforces. Rico Valez, whose studio is in the Uptown corridor and who has been a fixture of the August and September gatherings for years, put it plainly when I caught him outside a group show on 21st: "Labor Day used to feel like the city closing a book. Now it feels like the city turning a page."

By ten-thirty, the crowd had thinned to the people who were really staying — the ones who had found a conversation and weren't ready to finish it, the ones who had found a painting and weren't ready to leave it. The 62-degree air off the bay was the kind of September cold that reminds you something has shifted.

The summer was over. Nobody seemed upset about it.

**A Door That Stays Open**
By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Cultural Liaison

The fellowship hall at Lake Merritt United Methodist Church, on the Adams Point side of the lake, opened for community support this week — tables set up, sign-in sheet near the entrance, coffee going by eight in the morning. This is not a new program. But it is a new season, and congregations here mark the turning of seasons by what they add.

The September opening is what members call "expanded hours." The hall, which has functioned as a community space for walk-in needs throughout the year, formally widens its availability at the start of fall — food staples stocked, referral sheets updated, chairs arranged in the kind of informal clusters that tell people they are welcome to sit without purpose.

Marco Johnson, 38, a nurse aide who lives in Adams Point, came in Tuesday morning. He was not there for himself. He was there, he said, to put a flyer on the board for a neighbor who had lost work. "She doesn't come to places like this on her own," Johnson said. "But if I put it somewhere she'll see it mentioned, maybe she comes." He taped the flyer next to the coffee station and left.

Elijah Campbell, 52, drives a taxi and has known about the church's open-door policy for several years. He has sent people here before. He described the hall's function with the plainness of someone who has seen a lot of referral systems: "It's not a clinic. It's not a shelter. It's a room where you can figure out what you need and nobody asks you to explain why."

That distinction matters in a city with enough service organizations to fill a directory. The Methodist congregation is not trying to replicate what the social safety net provides. They are filling in around its edges — the Monday someone gets bad news, the Wednesday a family hasn't eaten, the Friday someone needs to sit somewhere warm without a form to fill out.

Devon Walker, 27, a server who lives in the neighborhood, stopped in on his way to a Thursday opening shift. He picked up a utility assistance referral sheet — not for himself, he clarified, but for his landlord's other tenant, an older woman who had mentioned to him she was having trouble. He folded it into his jacket pocket. "You just pick it up in case," he said.

The congregation is not posting announcements this week about capacity or numbers served or outcomes. Asked about that, a church volunteer said only that they had been doing this long enough to know that the people who needed to know would find out.

**The Year Starts Here**
By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Cultural Liaison

Steve Conrad, 76, has lived in Rockridge for over thirty years. He is not Jewish. He will tell you that himself, unprompted, in the way of someone who has explained it enough times to be at peace with the explanation. But he has walked past Temple Sinai on Rockridge's commercial strip every September for most of those three decades, and this year, the first time I happened to catch him outside the synagogue on the evening of Rosh Hashanah, he stopped on the sidewalk and watched the families going in.

"I never learned what the prayers say," he said. "But I know what September looks like here. It looks like this."

Rosh Hashanah — the Jewish new year, marking this year the beginning of 5802 — fell in the first week of September, and both Temple Sinai in Rockridge and Beth Jacob Congregation over on Piedmont Avenue held their observances within the same span of days. The two congregations draw from overlapping neighborhoods but different traditions, and the coincidence of the calendar brought two distinct versions of the same turning into the East Bay in the same week.

I've covered this stretch of September for years. The Jewish new year sits inside the same week that schools settle into their second week of classes, that the last of the summer produce is winding down at the Temescal farmers market, that the morning air starts carrying the particular cool that means fall has actually arrived and isn't just threatening to. Rosh Hashanah belongs to September the way the month belongs to it — not as an afterthought but as an anchor.

Lucia Polito, 64, who I've known from her years of volunteering at St. Columba in Fruitvale, was not at Temple Sinai. She texted me this week about something else entirely, and when I mentioned I was heading to Rockridge for the observance she wrote back: "Every tradition has the same idea. Once a year you stop and you take stock." I think she's right. The forms change. The impulse doesn't.

Outside Temple Sinai by eight o'clock, the families were dressed up. Not extravagantly — nice shoes, good jackets, the particular effort of an occasion that mattered. A few older members greeted each other in the way people greet each other when they see each other mostly at services — the warmth of irregular frequency, the warmth of shared ritual. Children moved through the clusters of adults with the impatience of children who don't yet understand that standing still is also a form of marking time.

Steve Conrad was still on the sidewalk when I passed him on my way in. He had his hands in his jacket pockets and was watching the door.

"New year," he said, by way of nothing in particular.

"New year," I said back.

The door closed behind me, and through it I could hear the sound of a congregation settling — chairs, voices, the particular silence before something begins.

**Opening Day: Ramos Shuts Down Angels, Richards Steals the Show in 8-1 Statement Win**
By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

The Oakland Athletics opened the 2041 regular season with a performance that left little ambiguous about what this team intends. In front of a full house at the Coliseum on Tuesday afternoon, Arturo Ramos threw seven innings of three-hit baseball, striking out ten Angels hitters in a methodical, authoritative display that served as the first chapter of what the franchise is calling its season of finales and first chapters at once. The final score was eight to one. It was not close.

Ramos, who enters this season as the unambiguous ace and Opening Day assignment carries weight in this rotation, held the Angels to a single run across seven innings, dispatching the lineup with the kind of efficiency that makes scouts reach for new adjectives. His fastball sat at ninety-five and touched ninety-seven in the fifth inning. The slider had twelve-to-six bite. The ten strikeouts came against a Los Angeles lineup that entered the game with a .271 team average last September. When manager Deacon Seymour pulled him after ninety-two pitches in the seventh, the Coliseum stood for him. Ramos jogged to the dugout, tipped his cap once, and sat down. That is who he is.

But if Ramos was the engine, Martin Richards was the story.

The third baseman went three-for-four with two home runs and two runs batted in, producing the kind of afternoon that Richards has quietly been capable of producing for years without receiving the commensurate recognition. Both home runs were to left field, both came against left-handed relief pitching, both cleared the fence with the kind of unhurried authority that belongs to a man who has spent seven seasons learning exactly what the Coliseum's winds ask of a right-handed pull hitter. Richards is in a contract year. He knows it. The numbers from Tuesday suggest he intends to make that conversation loud.

"He's been doing this for years," Seymour said after the game in the visiting manager's corridor. "People keep being surprised. I'm not surprised anymore."

Eric Taveras, the second baseman who arrived in the offseason as part of the roster reconstruction project, hit his first home run as an Athletic in the fourth inning on a first-pitch fastball that he turned on with the kind of quick hands the club's analytics operation identified when they brought him in. It is one swing in one game on the first day of April, and context demands the appropriate caution — but first swings in first games tell you something about whether a player is ready, and Taveras looked ready. Center fielder Danny Horn added a solo shot in the sixth to extend the lead.

The 8-1 victory puts the A's at one and zero heading into the second game of the homestand against Los Angeles. It is the earliest possible record to have, but the manner of it matters. Ramos at his best, Richards doing his work, Taveras announcing his presence — these are the storylines Seymour sketched on a whiteboard in Mesa last March when he was learning how to write lineup cards as a manager for the first time. Tuesday was the first page of the version that counts.

**Busch Trade Closes a Chapter, Opens a Question About Minor League Depth**
By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

Hours before Arturo Ramos threw his first pitch on Opening Day, the Oakland front office quietly sent Peter Busch, outfielder and unlikely October hero, to Cleveland along with two minor league arms — right-handed starter Jose Baez and reliever Francisco Davila — in exchange for Liam Wade, a twenty-two-year-old infielder from the Guardians' Double-A affiliate who projects as a shortstop and second baseman at the major league level.

The transaction closes a complicated chapter. Busch, who hit the home run in the 2040 playoffs that gave Oakland the lead in what became a series-sealing win, entered this spring in a roster construction bind. The A's outfield, with Darrin Davis healthy after two seasons of ACL rehabilitation and Danny Horn entrenched in center, left Busch without a clear path to at-bats. He is twenty-eight, a right-handed bat with above-average range and the kind of October legend status that makes fans want to see him in the lineup. The front office's calculation was that sentiment and playing time were not going to align, and that the honest answer to Busch's situation was a trade to an organization that could offer him a starting role.

Busch was, by multiple accounts within the organization, frustrated by the decision. That frustration is understandable. It is also, in the context of how rosters are built for championship runs, beside the point. The A's have a more complete outfield without him than they did when he was here. That is the difficult arithmetic of winning.

Wade is the other half of the question. The Guardians' system has ranked him as a top-five organizational prospect in each of the past two seasons, with evaluators citing plus range at shortstop, a solid hit tool, and enough patience at the plate to project an on-base percentage above .360 at maturity. He has not played above Double-A. He will not be in Oakland this season, and possibly not next season. The club shipped Baez — who was the organization's fourth-ranked pitching prospect as recently as last cycle — and Davila alongside Busch to acquire him, which tells you something about how much they believe in Wade's ceiling.

The larger question the trade raises is what it reveals about minor league construction. The A's have added Liam Wade to address what the front office has identified as a shortage of shortstop depth below the major league level. That the shortage required trading two pitching prospects to correct suggests a system that has been leaning forward into the big league rotation at the cost of depth farther down. With Benji Dillon in his final season and the back half of the rotation still developing, that calculation bears watching as the system moves through the next two or three years.

For now: Busch is a Guardian. Wade is in the pipeline. Opening Day still went eight to one.

**The Coliseum Woke Up Today and I Was Not Prepared for How That Would Feel**
By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

I have been to eleven Opening Days. I know what they are. I know the smell — the fresh cut grass mixing with the garlic fries and the remnants of last night's fog still burning off the third-base side. I know the particular quality of the crowd noise around one o'clock when the lineup cards come out and someone in section one-oh-six has already had too much optimism since ten in the morning. I know all of it.

I was still not prepared for what happened inside me when Arturo Ramos walked out of the tunnel to start Tuesday's game.

This is not a rational response, I want to be clear about that. Ramos has been the ace for two seasons. He has started in October. He has been to All-Star Games. He is not a surprise. Nothing that happened on Tuesday should have been a surprise. And yet when he took the mound and the crowd came up and the first pitch went for a called strike at ninety-five miles per hour, I had this feeling I can only describe as: this is actually real. This is actually happening. The season has actually started.

Seven innings. Three hits. Ten strikeouts. I won't pretend I was counting strikeouts in the moment — I was counting them later, after, the way you go back and verify a dream because you want the details to hold up. They held up. Ramos was as good as you need your best pitcher to be on the most important first day of a very particular season. I want to say that again. He was as good as you need him to be. When I say that about an ace on Opening Day I mean it as the highest possible compliment, because what you need is perfection and what you get, usually, is something considerably less.

And then Martin Richards hit two home runs.

Martin Richards. The man who has been our quiet answer at third base for seven years. The man who hits the ball hard to left field, fields everything, shows up for every game, and has somehow contrived to be the least famous excellent player on every roster he's ever been on. He had three hits and two home runs and two runs batted in and I'm going to be honest with you: I had to check his name in the box score not because I didn't know it was him but because my brain had some trouble processing that the unsung guy was the one carrying the afternoon.

He's in a contract year. I want to say that out loud.

Now: here is the part where I acknowledge what some of you are going to type into the comments. It's one game. The Angels were not the World Series roster. The opener is a beautiful lie that the season tells before the truth of April and May comes crashing in. I know all of that. Believe me, I have earned the scars that produce that knowledge.

But here is what I also know: we have Isley Kelley and Vinnie Keane and Benji Dillon in their final season, and we have Arturo Ramos dazzling the stadium on Opening Day, and we have Eric Taveras hitting a home run in his first game as an Athletic, and Danny Horn adding one in the sixth, and the scoreboard reading eight to one when the whole thing was over, and I am telling you — the Coliseum today had a specific electricity that is not just hope. Hope is what we felt in April before the years that ended badly. This felt like the beginning of something that knows what it is.

One game. One and zero. I've never been happier about an entirely insufficient data point in my life.

Oakland. Welcome back to the regular season. I'd been saving this feeling.

[OPINION]
**On the Distant Noise of a Basketball Franchise That Might, Someday, Belong to Us**
*A Richmond Archive Essay*
By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

There is a particular kind of news that arrives in September and refuses to sit still. It is not the kind that breaks — it is the kind that accumulates, the way fog accumulates at the top of the Caldecott Tunnel and you cannot tell from the valley floor how thick it has become until you are already inside it.

The NBA has confirmed that Oakland is among the final group of cities under consideration for expansion. This is the news, and it is real, and I want to spend some time with it before it becomes arguments.

I have been covering Oakland sports since nineteen seventy-seven. I covered the Raiders when they played in the Coliseum the first time, before the move and the return and the second departure and the silence that followed. I was at the Arena when the Warriors won in two thousand fifteen, and I sat with the city afterward in the particular way you sit with a city that has just been given something magnificent and is not certain it will get to keep it. The Warriors went to San Francisco. The city absorbed it the way Oakland absorbs things — with a mix of rage and pragmatism and a long institutional memory for exits.

What strikes me about the NBA expansion news is not the basketball. The basketball is, at this point in the story, hypothetical. What strikes me is the phrase I keep encountering in the early reporting: a young technology executive, locally connected, is rumored to be part of a potential ownership consortium. That phrase contains several layers worth examining.

The first layer is geography. Locally connected. Oakland has been trying to keep sports locally connected for fifty years, with uneven results. The A's are still here because a ownership group understood that Oakland is not a market to exploit but a city to build inside. The question of whether an NBA ownership group would arrive with the same orientation — or whether it would arrive with the Chase Center model in mind, the gleaming arena in the water, the team that plays in San Francisco because San Francisco has more corporate sponsorship to offer — is not a small question. It is, in fact, the only question.

The second layer is the building question. Baylight is under development. The clearance is done. The pressure that clearance adds to the Paulson two-city situation is something I will let the beat reporters untangle — they are closer to those conversations than I am and they are better at the specific measurements of franchise economics. What I will say from forty-four years of watching Oakland navigate these arrangements is that the city's relationship to arena development is not a clean one. There is a long record here of promises made in the abstract that became specific when the land was cleared and the renderings arrived.

I am not predicting anything. I am noting that the questions which attend this rumor are the same questions Oakland has been asking about its sports franchises since I was a young man covering Al Davis. They are questions about permanence. They are questions about whose benefit the team is built for. They are questions that deserve to be asked in September, before the fog closes in.

For now, the A's are one and zero. Arturo Ramos threw seven innings and ten strikeouts in his first start of the season and the Coliseum was as full and as loud as it has been since October. That is today's story, and it is a good one. The NBA rumor is next year's story, or the year after that, and it will become whatever Oakland is willing to fight for it to become.

I have covered this city for forty-four years. It fights for what matters. The question is always whether it knows what matters before it's too late.

That is the question that the fog at the top of the Caldecott eventually answers for everyone who drives through it. You just have to wait until you're inside to see clearly.

**The Man They Didn't Vote For**
By Selena Grant | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

Here is the case for Josh Giddey's place in the postseason conversation: he put up 39 points, nine rebounds, and four assists in Game 5 against Orlando — a 134-111 blowout on the road that turned a 2-2 series into a 3-2 Chicago lead and ultimately finished a 0-2 comeback. His series average was 25 points, seven rebounds, and six assists. The All-Star ballot from January is a footnote now.

Let me be clear about what that 0-2 start looked like from the outside. The narrative wrote itself: the number one seed dropping back-to-back home games to Orlando, a franchise that spent the entire spring watching its general manager decline to discuss his contract future and its ownership decline to clarify it. There were people — I'll be honest, including people in this press corps — who were prepared to frame a first-round exit as confirmation that the awards were premature. That the 58-win season was a house of cards.

Then Giddey happened.

It wasn't just the 39 points in Game 5 — it was the six-game body of work. Twenty-five points per game. Seven rebounds. Six assists. He attacked switching defenses early in the shot clock rather than settling for the pull-up threes in traffic that plagued him earlier in the season. His turnovers, which sat around 3.1 during stretches of the regular season, were manageable in this series. His field goal selection matured in ways that held up under playoff officiating and physical defense from a Magic team that was not here to concede.

Hank Trepagnier averaged 20 points and 8 rebounds through the series. His assists average — 5.3 per game — means he ran offense out of the post and made secondary reads even when double-teamed. For a nineteen-year-old in his first playoff run, that number doesn't happen by accident. Adash Stanley contributed 14 points and six assists. On a team that needed four wins in a row to advance, those numbers held up in the games that mattered.

The Bulls are through. They won four straight after falling into a hole that most top seeds don't climb out of. They head to the second round having answered the only question that mattered about the first one.

A source familiar with the front office's thinking noted this week that Paulson has remained characteristically quiet about the team's second-round preparation and, separately, about his contract status — the same posture he has held since March. The franchise stability indicator in league circles has shifted from "uncertain" to "stable" with the series win. That may reflect the basketball. It does not yet reflect the office.

The Giddey game was real. The comeback was earned. What the second round tells us about both of those things is the next question.

**The Morning After the Morning After**
By Talia Finch | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

Labor Day in Bridgeport smells like charcoal by ten in the morning. Somebody on the block behind Romano's had a grill going before the coffee finished brewing, and the smoke drifted through the propped-open door of the restaurant and mixed with the dark roast in a way that was not unpleasant. The ceiling fans were running again. The week had turned cool and dry, the way early September sometimes delivers in Chicago — a gift after August, before October comes to collect it.

Paulette Okafor was already there when I arrived. She'd beaten me by fifteen minutes, which she noted.

"I had nowhere to be," she said. "The school's still closed for the holiday."

She had her coffee in front of her, the ceramic mug she always gets, and she was not looking at her phone. That was new. For most of the spring and summer, her phone had been either face-up watching for alerts or face-down keeping them away. This morning it was in her bag.

The Bulls closed out Orlando in six games. They were down 0-2 before they won four in a row. The last one was 134-111. Giddey had put up 39 points in Game 5 and the city had spent the rest of the week finding ways to say his name correctly — the emphasis question had become a neighborhood pastime.

"I watched Game 5 with my sister," Paulette said. "She came over. We hadn't done that since the regular season."

I asked her what it felt like in the room when Giddey started going.

"Loud," she said. "Very loud. My sister knocked over a bag of pretzels when he hit that third-quarter run and nobody picked them up until halftime."

Raymond Polk came in around ten-fifteen, which is later than usual. He works contractor jobs and normally has to be somewhere early, but Labor Day had cleared his schedule the way holidays do for people who work in their bodies.

"Morning," he said, dropping into the booth across from Paulette without needing to be invited. He'd been here enough times that the booth was already his.

He ordered coffee. He looked out at 35th Street, where a few kids were riding bikes in the still-sparse Monday traffic.

"We're in the second round," he said eventually. Not to either of us specifically.

"We are," Paulette said.

Raymond turned his mug in his hands. He's been doing that the whole time I've been coming here — a slow quarter-turn, back and forth, like he's working something out.

"I keep waiting to feel only the good thing," he said. "And I almost got there on Friday night after Game 6. For about two hours I almost just felt the win."

Paulette looked at him. "Almost."

"Almost." He smiled — not a sad smile, just an honest one. "Then I woke up Saturday and the contract was still whatever it is."

That's the arithmetic Bridgeport has been doing all year. A win is a win. The series is over. The team is alive. And the question they have been carrying since March is still the same question, unchanged, sitting in the back of the booth like a fourth person who didn't order anything.

Outside, the charcoal smell was getting stronger. The morning was building toward noon and the holiday was doing what Chicago holidays do — gathering weight and heat and noise, the alley traffic and the kids and the distant sound of someone's sound system finding its footing. Romano's was filling up.

"You know what I want?" Paulette said, picking up her mug. "I want to get to a second-round game and not think about it until it's over."

Raymond nodded. "That's fair."

"Just watch basketball," she said. "Like a person who just watches basketball."

She picked up her phone — out of the bag, finally — and opened it to the schedule. Second round. The Bulls were still alive. She set it face-up on the table, and for once the face-up meant something uncomplicated.

**"The Hands That Hold the City"**

Dear Editor,

This Labor Day, I watched the Port come alive at dawn. The fog hadn't burned off yet, and the tugs were pulling a container ship in slow and steady. I've been doing this work for forty-three years — since my thirties — and I've never taken September 5th for granted. It's the day the city remembers that we move things. We carry things. We make the machine work.

But this year, something felt different. It wasn't a celebration. It was a notice.

I read about Deputy Mayor Osei in the hospital. Elliott Crane still recovering. Marcus pulled a muscle keeping the city balanced. That's what happens when you're carrying too much weight. The machine doesn't care if you're tired. It wants its work done.

Labor Day used to mean we got a day off. Now it means we're reminded we don't have one. The port runs every day. The ships don't wait. And neither does this city.

I'm grateful the city remembers us. But I'd be more grateful if we could remember each other on days that weren't holidays.

Elena Lopez
Harbor Tugboat Captain, Jack London

------------------------------------------------------------

**"First Friday and the Quiet Work"**

Dear Editor,

I've been running the Temescal theater for nine years. Every month, First Friday comes around and the whole corridor comes alive. Telegraph Avenue fills up, the galleries open their doors, people walk in expecting to be moved by something — a painting, a play, a conversation. They want the city to surprise them.

But this month, walking through the crowds, I noticed something I hadn't quite seen before. People are tired. Not angry. Not cynical. Just tired.

First Friday is supposed to celebrate creativity. But creativity is labor too. The people who make this city beautiful — the artists, the performers, the people who stay late to fix the lights — we're the workers nobody counts in the reports.

This month, I want the city to know: we're watching you try. We see Elliott Crane coming back. We're glad Marcus Osei is healing. And we're keeping the lights on while you figure out how to carry what you're carrying.

The show goes on. That's what we do.

Sofia Lucero
Theater Director, Temescal

------------------------------------------------------------

**"Healing as the Real Work"**

Dear Editor,

I'm a medical social worker at Highland Hospital. I spend my days helping people navigate systems that weren't built to help them navigate. Insurance, housing after discharge, medication access, finding shelter. The work is real. It's necessary. But it's also invisible.

This Labor Day, Deputy Mayor Osei was in our hospital. Not as a politician looking at metrics, but as a human being who got hurt and needed to heal. I thought about that a lot — what it means for the person making decisions about the city to experience the city the way ordinary people do.

It is. We're doing it. Thousands of us in hospitals and clinics and social service offices are doing the work every single day, whether the leadership is there or not. We show up. We figure it out. We help the next person.

When the Deputy Mayor comes back to work, I hope he remembers that he was cared for by a thousand invisible hands. The rest of us count on that same care. We're still here. We're still working.

Carlos Jackson
Medical Social Worker, Lake Merritt

------------------------------------------------------------

**"Work That Lasts"**

Dear Editor,

I make things that last. That's what I do. I'm sixty-four years old, and I've been making things — readings, oils, herbs, guidance — for most of my life. People come to Fruitvale from all over Oakland because they know my work is real. Because I listen. Because I care.

Watching the city this summer, I noticed something: the people who carry the city are getting tired. The Deputy Mayor is healing. The Council members are struggling under the weight of the decisions they have to make. And the rest of us — working, making things, showing up — we're holding the city up while they figure it out.

First Friday is coming to Fruitvale. The galleries will open. The streets will fill. And I'll be here in my shop, doing the work that people need — not because it makes me rich, but because it makes people feel less alone.

This September, I want to say: thank you to everyone carrying the city. And I want to say: it's okay to rest. The work will wait for you. The people will wait. The city will keep turning.

Lucia Polito
Self-Employed, Fruitvale
