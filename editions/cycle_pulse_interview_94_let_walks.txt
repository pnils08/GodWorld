# After A Parade

**A conversation with Mike Paulson about the season the table got reset, the phone call that hasn't come, and the route the floats will take when this thing finally lands.**

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

---

When you've been writing about the same franchise for almost four decades, you learn the difference between the rooms where the news is made and the rooms where the news is corrected. The first room is louder. The second room is the one that matters.

I sat in the second room this past cycle, across from Mike Paulson. The coffee had been on a warmer too long. Paulson was in shirtsleeves. He had read the paper. He had read my piece on Isley Kelley going seven for nine against Baltimore in the same cycle the television feature aired. He had read P Slayer's column on Martin Richards — the player, in Slayer's reading, we forgot to count. He had also read, on the front page, Jordan Velez's transcription of five words Elias Varek had said into a microphone at a tech-expo panel about hiring Paulson to run the Oakland Oaks. *"I will absolutely be making that phone call."*

He sat down. He thanked me for the Kelley feature. And then he reset the table.

---

I will tell you what he said, because the city deserves to hear it the way he said it, and because the writing this paper did last cycle deserves to be measured against it.

The only confirmed farewell is Vinnie Keane. We are running the campaign with him. The city will get the cycles to say goodbye, the way it has been getting them. That much, the reading was right.

What the reading was wrong about — and Paulson said it plain — is the rest. Mark Aitken's contract runs through two thousand forty-three, which is on paper and immovable. Isley Kelley, Martin Richards, and Benji Dillon are in different rooms than the one Slayer and I built last cycle.

"Preliminary discussions took place on contracts," Paulson said, "and we have decided to wait until after the season. We respect the right to do so."

That is a sentence with two halves and you have to read both. The first half says talks have happened. The second half says both sides chose the silence. The men in question have agreed, alongside the front office, to leave the question open until baseball settles it. Paulson took, with a clarity I am going to honor here, some issue with the framing that the doors had already closed. As he put it, dryly: "I'd think I'd know first, as the person deciding."

He's right. He would. And the paper — this paper, including me — wrote the posture as if the deciding had already happened in a room nobody had been invited to. The posture had shape. We described the shape. But shape is not signature. I am going to keep that distinction in my notebook for the rest of this season.

---

The thing Paulson said next was the thing I had not heard before, and it is the sentence I came out of that room carrying.

"Benji Dillon came back on a one-year deal to make one last run with Vinnie."

I want you to read that the way I heard it.

It is not a roster note. It is not a depth-chart adjustment. It is two pitchers — and I keep needing to correct myself, because Dillon is the pitcher and Keane is the bat — two men who built a clubhouse together making a particular decision about how they leave one. One year. Deliberate. To ride together to whatever this season ends as. Benji could have walked already. He chose not to. He chose to come back for the cycles he and Vinnie have left.

There is a long history of these decisions inside Oakland baseball and most of them never get written. The catcher who restructures so the starter can stay one more year. The veteran who takes a bench role because the rookie at his position needed somebody to model how to lose a game and come back the next day. They happen. They mostly happen quietly. Dillon's one-year is in that lineage and I would not have known if Paulson had not put it in this room.

I will say what I think it means, since Paulson would not. It means the dynasty's last act has architecture. The men inside it have already arranged the chairs.

---

About the season itself, Paulson is not pretending it is over.

"I appreciate the conclusion one can make thirty games into a season," he said, "but it's not a hot-take decision on my end as GM. The outcome of the season plays a role. The goal is to win the championship — what are the factors of it happening or not happening."

And then the line I am going to put in the lede of every column I write about this front office for the rest of this campaign.

*"And will be ready, hopefully after a parade."*

The last parade was two thousand thirty-five. I stood on Broadway that afternoon and watched confetti get caught in the streetcar wires. The city smelled like beer and asphalt and history. We did not know it then, but that was the last true crescendo. Paulson sat in the office where I just sat with him and told me, in this cycle, on the record, that the work of this season is to give Oakland another one. He did not say it in a press release. He said it across a table to a beat writer he has known for years.

I am going to take him at his word, the way you take the man at his word when he is the one signing the checks and the one who has not done this lightly.

If the parade comes, the men who walk it are the men this paper has spent a cycle preparing to say goodbye to. If it does not, contract talks resume in November and the let-walks question reopens with the leverage rearranged by what happens between now and October. Either way, the city has been told, by the person who would know first, that the answer to the future of this roster is downstream of what happens on the field over the next four months. Not upstream.

---

I asked him about the phone call.

He spoke about Elias Varek the way you would expect a sitting general manager to speak about a man whose company has just put its name on the franchise's new ballpark. Warmly. Without hedge. "What a great man Elias is," he said, "and what a wonderful thing it is that Oakland is where he chose for his legacy." He credited Civis Systems with being one of the reasons Oakland is the destination it is now. That is — and I want to mark it for the record — a sitting A's GM endorsing the civic project of the man publicly courting him to leave the A's. Both things can be true. In this room they were.

Then the answer.

"I have not received that call."

There were five words from Varek on the front page of this paper last cycle. There were six from Paulson in this one. The call has not been made. The bid has not landed. The recruitment, as of this conversation, exists only in the air around a panel microphone.

Paulson did not close the door. He said, instead, what he learned from the one year he ran the Chicago Bulls — that the front-office system he has built can be, in his phrase, "somewhat autonomous." That he could run the Bulls last season and the A's would still, in his estimation, be the best team in baseball. The Bulls year, in his telling, proved that the system travels.

And then the line that is going to be the spine of the next sports piece I write, and probably the one after that.

"The system travels," Paulson said, "and the culture adapts."

He went on. Vinnie Keane is, in his words, "a one of one." The core of these dynasty A's has been mostly positional — really, he said, only Dillon is the pitcher inside that core — which means the franchise has spent two decades settling on the bats and adapting the staff around them. "As you adapt," he said, "you lock on to the culture core."

This is the language of a man who has thought about handoffs. It is also the language of a man who is telling you something true about why expansion is harder than takeover. You can hand somebody a system. You cannot hand them Vinnie running an academy on Mandela Parkway. You cannot hand them Mark sitting in a city meeting because he is the player rep and the community liaison. You cannot hand them Isley letting a film crew into the basement footage room for eight months while he keeps hitting. Those things grew here. Paulson knows the difference between the thing that travels and the thing that does not.

---

I asked him, at the end, what the parade looks like, if it comes. The men on the floats are men this paper has been writing toward farewell. The new bats — Reyna, Coles, Mesa, Quintero — will be on the floats too. What route do the floats take.

His answer was the closing image of our conversation, and I am going to leave it in his words.

"The parade is through the new paved streets of the Baylight District. Through Civis Field — The Town."

Read that, and read what it gathers. Baylight is rising. The district has been under construction since before this season opened. Civis Systems has its name on the ballpark and its software on the city. The parade route Paulson described in his closing breath is one that runs through both halves of the story this paper put in print last cycle — through the players the city is preparing to say goodbye to, and through the infrastructure that is being built by the man who wants to call Paulson next.

The dynasty's last act ends, by the GM's own description, on streets that did not exist when the dynasty began.

That is what I came out of the room with. And it is the only image worth keeping.

I will be writing the rest as it comes.

— H.R.
