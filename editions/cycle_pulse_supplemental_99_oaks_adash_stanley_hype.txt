# The Tip of the Spear
By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

When Mike Paulson handed $75 million to a nineteen-year-old kid with exactly one year of professional experience, I threw my coffee cup at the wall. 

I’m not being metaphorical. Ask my wife. There is still a dark, caffeinated stain on the plaster next to our refrigerator. It felt like a slap in the face. Yes, Adash Stanley won Sixth Man of the Year in Chicago last season. Yes, he dropped 26 points on Opening Night at the United Center. But we are the Oakland Oaks. We demand excellence. We do not demand giving franchise-cornerstone money to a 180-pound undrafted Polish kid because he had one hot season off the bench. 

Some fans in the bleachers immediately called it genius. They pointed out that Paulson drafted Stanley when he was running the Bulls last year. They told me Paulson’s vision operates on a frequency the rest of the league can’t hear. They pointed to his history of finding value in the scrap heap, like trading for Travis Coles or signing Eric Tavares. They said we needed to trust the process. 

I didn't trust it. I looked at Stanley—a funky shooting motion that belongs in a Tuesday night rec league, speaking through an interpreter, a guy the Bulls were ultimately fine letting walk into free agency—and I saw a GM trying to prove he was the smartest guy in the room by overpaying for his own project. 

Then I watched him play in an Oaks jersey.

Anthony can fill his columns with efficiency metrics and route optimizations. He can talk about the arithmetic of an elite three-point shot on a minimum-scale cap hit, or whatever alphabet soup explains value this week. I don’t care about the spreadsheets. I care about what it feels like to watch a player refuse to die on a basketball court.

Stanley plays like every possession is a street fight. The scouts whispered that his ceiling was limitless, but they missed the point. It’s not about his ceiling. It’s about his floor. He is a nineteen-year-old who dives for loose balls in the second quarter of a Tuesday night game like it's Game 7 of the Finals. He bleeds for the basketball.

Paulson saw a number on a page. A 99-Hustle rating. You can't teach that. He staked his entire reputation on the belief that a 99-Hustle rating isn't just a scouting anomaly; it's a pathology.

He didn't sign a point guard. He signed a blunt instrument.

Look at the roster they’ve built around him. Wilson Shepard is running a motion offense deliberately designed to suffocate opponents. It’s a torture chamber. We have Wendell Carter Jr. anchoring the paint, a brick wall in high-tops. We have Jerami Grant on the wing, ready to punish mistakes. We have AJ Dybantsa, the number one overall pick, ready to fly out of the building. 

And the bench? Jose Alvarado, Dillon Brooks, Coen Carr, Precious Achiuwa, Tim Hardaway Jr. 

This is not a basketball team. It’s a Villain Squad. It is the most physical, abrasive, relentless collection of athletes in professional sports. They are built to break you. They are built to make opposing teams dread the flight to Oakland, dread the bus ride to the arena, dread the moment they have to lace up against us.

And the tip of that spear is a kid from Kraków who doesn't say a word. 

He just works. The trainers say he’s the first one in the practice facility every morning, shooting alone in the silence. He sees the geometry of the court like a complex puzzle, ripping apart defenses with passes that shouldn't physically exist, and then he sprints back on defense like a man running from a fire. 

That’s what changed my mind. The money isn't for his pedigree. It’s for his pulse.

The friction is real. There are still skeptics on sports radio who say a teenager can't lead a championship roster, no matter how many Sixth Man awards he won. They claim the NBA playoffs will eat a 180-pound kid alive when the game slows down in May and the whistles stop blowing. They argue that Paulson’s obsession with this singular trait—this mythical 99-Hustle metric that front offices whisper about like it's an urban legend—is a romantic flaw that will cost us a title window. 

They are wrong. They are wrong because they fundamentally misunderstand what Oakland respects. 

We don't need our point guard to be a polished aristocrat of the hardwood. We need him to be exactly what he is. He is a kid who grew up fighting for everything in a country that didn't care about his sport. He is a kid who went undrafted, proved everyone wrong in Chicago, and still had to listen to the league call his success a fluke. 

He plays with a chip on his shoulder the size of the Bay Bridge. 

That $75 million isn't a gamble on a Polish lottery ticket. It’s an investment in the exact identity this franchise needed. When Adash Stanley drops into a defensive stance, slapping the floor, staring dead-eyed at an All-Star point guard making twice his salary, the entire building feels it. The energy shifts. The Villain Squad takes its cue. 

He is the engine of our hostility. 

So let the rest of the league laugh at the price tag. Let them question the mechanics of his jump shot. Let them wonder how Mike Paulson convinced himself that an undrafted teenager was the missing piece to a championship puzzle. The math points hard toward a chaotic season, but the eye test points toward a massacre. 

They won't be laughing when they have to come to our house. They won't be laughing when Alvarado and Brooks are climbing into their jerseys, and Dybantsa is catching lobs, and this kid from Kraków is diving through the scorer's table to save a possession in the fourth quarter. 

I was wrong about Adash Stanley. 

The stain on my wall is a daily reminder that sometimes, the smartest guy in the room is the one who realizes that effort is the only metric that can't be faked. 

Welcome to Oakland, Adash. We’re going to be a nightmare.
