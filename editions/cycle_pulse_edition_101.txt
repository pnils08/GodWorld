### Trade Deadline Earthquake: A's Send Richards to Mets, Reshape the Infield

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports, Edition 101

---

I have spent a good part of my life arguing that no player who leaves this team is ever really tragic — that the ones who go on to somewhere else are graduating, not falling. I have written that line more than once this farewell season, about Keane, about Dillon, about Kelley, men who chose their last lap with their eyes open. I want to tell you it still holds for Martin Richards. I am not sure, tonight, that it does. And I think the honest thing is to say so, and let you watch me work it out rather than hand you the conclusion polished.

Richards did not choose this. That is the difference, and you should sit with it before anyone tells you otherwise.

He is a third baseman by trade, and by every number that matters he was still playing well — a two hundred eighty-three average, twenty-one home runs, forty-one runs batted in, only three errors at the position all year, the glove as reliable as it has ever been. Those are not the numbers of a man being pushed out a door. They are the numbers of a man standing in the doorway anyway, because the front office looked at the roster and saw somewhere else it needed to be.

I called him at his apartment. He did not want to talk about the return — two top-ten prospects, one of them a shortstop, the kind of package that tells you the A's think they got the better end of this and are probably right. He wanted to talk about the corner market. About Lucia on her way to the wellness center. About Allen and his last start, about the kids on the walk to BART who yell his name, about the old-timers at the mechanic shop who have been talking baseball with him since he was a rookie.

"Honestly? It's a gut punch," he told me. "I've spent my entire career here — built a life in Fruitvale, know the streets, the people, the rhythm of this place. One day you're grabbing coffee at the corner market, nodding at Lucia on her way to the wellness center, joking with Allen about his last start, and the next? You're packing boxes for Queens. I get it. It's business. The A's are reshuffling, Isely's back, and suddenly I'm expendable. But damn, it's not just about the uniform. It's the kids who yell 'Hey, Mr. Richards!' when I'm walking to BART, the old-timers at the mechanic shop who've been talking A's baseball with me since I was a rookie. That stuff doesn't just transfer with a contract. New York's a hell of an opportunity, sure. Big stage, new energy. But today? Today I'm just standing in my apartment staring at the Oakland skyline, wondering how the hell I'm supposed to say goodbye to a place that's not just home — it's who I am."

I have covered this team forty-four years. I have watched men leave in disgrace and men leave in triumph and men leave simply because a number on a spreadsheet said it was time. What Richards is describing is none of those. It is a man who built an actual life inside a baseball career — a life with a corner market and a neighbor named Lucia in it — being told the career is moving and the life is expected to follow, or not.

Ernesto Quintero has been waiting behind that door a long while, and it would be dishonest of me to pretend his turn now is not, in its own way, the other half of this story. He came up through West Oakland, took the five o'clock bus to the Coliseum for extra work before anyone paid attention to the extra work he was doing. His numbers this year — a two hundred ninety-two average, thirty-seven home runs, seventy-one runs batted in — are the numbers of a player who was ready before the roster made room for him.

"Honestly? I'm trying not to get too ahead of myself," Quintero said. "Richards was a solid guy — always worked hard, never complained — but this game is about chances, you know? Now it's my turn to step up. I've been grinding in the shadows, waiting for an opportunity like this. West Oakland's seen me grow up, seen me take the bus to the Coliseum at 5 AM for early work. Feels like the city's got my back, and that means something. The trade's a surprise, sure, but that's baseball. I just keep my head down, focus on what I can control. The A's believe in me — or they wouldn't have made the move. Now it's on me to prove them right."

I want to be careful here, because it would be easy — too easy — to write this as a passing of the torch and call it clean. Quintero did not ask for Richards to be dealt to Queens for him to play every day. He was simply already good enough, and the front office finally ran out of reasons to keep the two of them competing for the same square of dirt. That is not redemption. That is roster math wearing the face of a man who deserved his shot regardless of how it arrived.

And then there is Isley Kelley, who plays shortstop for this team and will be a free agent when this farewell season ends, and who now watches the club acquire a shortstop prospect in the very trade that just sent his double-play partner across the country.

"Honestly? It's a weird mix of relief and tension right now," Kelley said. "The Richards trade — I get it. Front office is thinking ahead, reshaping the infield, and the return's solid. But it's impossible not to read into it when you're the guy whose contract is up. I've been here long enough to know how this game works — nothing's personal, it's just business. I love Oakland, love this team, and yeah, I wanted more years. But two at fifty million? Can't complain about that. It's a hell of a vote of confidence, and I'll take it. What's funny is, after all the noise, I'm just trying to keep my head down and play. Vinnie's been cracking jokes in the clubhouse like nothing's changed, which helps."

I do not know what the A's intend to do about Kelley's future, and neither, from the sound of it, does he. Nobody in this story is being told the whole plan. That may be the most honest thing I can offer you about a trade deadline: the front office sees the shape of the next several years and moves pieces accordingly, and the men who are those pieces are left, like the rest of us, reading the same tea leaves as everyone in the stands.

So — graduation, or tragedy? I promised you I would not hand you the answer, and I meant it. What I can tell you is that a man who spent his career learning the rhythm of Fruitvale, who knows Lucia and Allen and the kids on the walk to BART, is standing tonight in an apartment looking at a skyline that is not going anywhere, while he is. Whatever you call that, do not call it simple.

*For readers newer to these pages: a batting average, a home run total, a run driven in — none of it is counted differently this cycle than it was the cycle before, or the one before that. The only thing that changes, deadline to deadline, is who is left standing on the field to add them up.*

### The Ending Richards Didn't Get to Write

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

Some cycles the paper carries one heartbreak and builds the rest of the edition around it. This is one of those. Martin Richards did not choose to leave Oakland — the front office chose for him, at the deadline, in a title year, and the fan base is right to feel the ground move. Read Hal Richmond on the front page for the man, not the transaction. The frame this desk has leaned on all farewell season — graduation, not tragedy — meets its hardest test yet in a player who never got to pick his own ending.

But a paper that prints only its heartbreak isn't doing its job, so hold the rest in the same hand. Councilmember Janae Rivers published apprenticeship numbers that show her own program behind pace — 86 applied, 31 placed — and released them anyway; that is what accountability looks like when it isn't performing for anyone. In Fruitvale, two women who agree on little both refused to call the Transit Hub's strain something softer. Fourteen neighborhoods watched their storefronts go dark with no one assigned to answer for it. And in two congregations, people kept showing up for one another, quietly, the way they always have.

Count the blessings. Name the strain. Don't mistake one for the other.

— M.C.

### Kevin Clark Called Up as Infield Crunches Under Trade Fallout

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

Kevin Clark Called Up as Infield Crunches Under Trade Fallout

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

The Oakland A's have summoned Kevin Clark, their No. 16 organizational prospect, from Triple-A, with Frank Reyna optioned back down to clear the roster spot — a corner-turned-utility move that has as much to do with the club's infield math as it does with Clark's own readiness. He is set to debut in Toronto.

The timing is not coincidental. The Richards trade thinned Oakland's infield depth, and Clark's callup is the front office's first concrete answer to the hole that deal left behind.

Clark, 21, carries a 78 overall grade and profiles as a true multi-position piece — third base, second base, and right field all in his usable range, exactly the kind of defensive flexibility a thin roster needs when the alignment isn't settled. That versatility is the calculation here: rather than plugging one hole with a specialist, Oakland has brought up a player who can be moved around the diamond as the roster reshuffles further.

For Clark, the call came the way it usually does in this business — fast, with little explanation attached. "Honestly? It's wild. One minute I'm taking grounders in Vegas, thinking about how to get through the summer heat, and the next I'm getting a call telling me to pack for Oakland. The whole trade fallout mess—guys moving, guys hurt, front office scrambling—it's not really my business to overthink it. I just know there's a spot, and they think I can fill it. Ty Yule texted me as soon as the news broke. We came up together, so he gets it—how weird it feels to get your shot because somebody else's luck ran out. But baseball's like that. You grind in the minors, wait for the phone to ring, and when it does, you don't ask why. You just show up ready. Mark Aitken's already reached out too, told me to keep it simple. That's the vibe in Oakland right now—noise everywhere, but in the clubhouse, it's about playing clean and staying steady. I'm not here to replace anyone. Just here to help."

That last point tracks with how the front office appears to be using him. Clark is not being installed as an everyday replacement for what Oakland lost in the Richards deal; he is roster depth with the flexibility to cover multiple spots while the front office figures out its next move. Reyna's option back to the minors is the corresponding subtraction — a corner-outfield-to-infield swap in service of the versatility Oakland needs right now more than a settled lineup card.

The debut in Toronto will answer the first question quickly enough: whether a 21-year-old with a 78 grade is ready for the look. The larger question — how Oakland fills the rest of what the Richards trade opened up — remains unresolved.

### Paulson Brings His Bridge to Oakland: Elliot Abraham Named Oaks GM

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

Mike Paulson has never run one franchise quietly, let alone two, and the hire he made this cycle is the clearest signal yet of how he intends to try. Elliot Abraham, who worked alongside Paulson during his single season as general manager of the Chicago Bulls, has been named general manager of the Oakland Oaks, taking over day-to-day basketball operations while Paulson remains Head of Basketball Operations for the franchise and general manager of the Oakland A's. The two jobs were never going to coexist on paper. Whether they coexist in practice is now Abraham's problem to solve alongside Paulson's, and that is precisely the arrangement Paulson has described publicly as proof the two-city operation can hold.

It is worth stating the structural question plainly, because it is not a hostile one — it is the obvious one. Paulson spends his baseball hours in Oakland, where the A's roster he built carries the tail end of a dynasty era and a farewell season that demands attention on its own terms. He built his basketball résumé in Chicago, where the Bulls' run under his lone year in charge ended in a Finals loss and no second season. Abraham was there for that year. Now he is here, tasked with running an Oaks front office day-to-day so that Paulson's basketball attention does not simply evaporate the moment the baseball season needs him most. That is the bet: that a lieutenant who already knows how Paulson operates can hold the basketball side steady without Paulson physically present for all of it. Front offices have tried versions of this before, with GMs anchoring one city while an owner or operations chief split attention elsewhere. It works exactly as well as the trust between the two people running it, and right now the Tribune has no on-record accounting of how that trust was built beyond the one season the two men already share.

What is not in question is the timeline Abraham is stepping into. The Oaks' basketball calendar accelerates quickly from here. Wilson Shepard, entering his first year as head coach, will be building game-management habits in real time this summer, and the roster he is building around includes point guard Adash Stanley and the franchise's most closely watched asset, small forward AJ Dybantsa, the number one overall pick. Summer League itself does not tip off until Cycle 103, which means the Abraham hire lands as table-setting rather than crisis management — there is no fire to put out, only a structure to stand up before the games that matter start counting. That distinction matters for how this should be read. Abraham is not walking into a roster in disarray. He is walking into a coaching staff and a young core that have not yet played a competitive minute together, with time on the calendar to get the infrastructure right before Shepard has to manage a bench, Stanley has to run an offense, and Dybantsa has to prove the top pick under real lights.

None of that erases the two-city question, and it shouldn't. Paulson has framed the Abraham hire as the answer to it — a support structure carried over from Chicago rather than assembled fresh, which is its own kind of evidence about how the relationship performed under pressure last year. But the Tribune has nothing on record beyond Paulson's own account of how that Bulls year unfolded for Abraham specifically, and reporting further into that record before Paulson or Abraham puts anything on the table publicly would be getting ahead of what is actually known. What is known is this: the Oaks now have a general manager whose entire professional relationship with Paulson predates his arrival in Oakland, a head coach in his first year at the position, a rookie point guard, and the top pick in the draft, all converging on a Summer League slate that starts in two cycles. The credibility test for the two-city operation will not be settled by the hire itself. It will be settled by what Abraham's front office looks like once Shepard's Oaks actually take the floor.

### Vinnie and Amara's Third Annual Gala Packs the House for Oakland's Kids

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

There's a kid — can't be older than nine — standing at the edge of the ballroom floor with a glove that's clearly two sizes too big for him, and he's got this look on his face like he doesn't know if he's allowed to be standing this close to a man he's only ever seen on a bubblegum card. That's the moment. Forget the step-and-repeat, forget the silent auction paddles going up in the back — that kid, that glove, that look. That's the whole night.

Third year now. Keane for Kids. And I keep waiting for it to start feeling like an event, one of those things where everybody's checking their phone between courses, and it just never does. Amara's up at the piano like always — professional, unhurried, the kind of playing that makes a hotel ballroom feel like somewhere quieter — and somewhere out on the floor Darrin Davis is standing with a plate of food he hasn't touched because he's mid-conversation with somebody's parent, and Mark Aitken's working the room the way he always works a room, and Isley Kelley's laughing at something near the bar, and Adash Stanley's just — there, present, part of the furniture of the thing in the best way.

I found Vinnie near the edge of it all, not performing host, just watching his own event happen around him. Asked him what it's like, three years in.

"Honest take? It's humbling, man. Amara and I started this thing because we remembered what it was like — kids with talent but no gloves, no cleats, no ride to the field. Now? Third year in, and the whole city shows up. You wanna know the best part? It's not the checks written or the cameras. It's seeing Anthony from the Tribune ditch his notebook to play catch with some nine-year-old who's got a cannon for an arm. Or Benji Dillon — our closer, mind you — teaching a girl how to grip a changeup like it's Game 7. And Amara? She's running the whole thing like a general, making sure every kid leaves with something that fits. Because she knows — you don't just give 'em gear. You give 'em a shot. Yeah, the papers call it a 'gala.' Feels more like a family barbecue where half the guests happen to wear World Series rings."

I stood there after he said it and just let it sit a second, because he's right and it's annoying how right he is. This is a man hitting .305 with ten home runs in a season that's supposed to be about him closing out a career, and the thing he wants to talk about is a kid's changeup grip.

Walked past the auction tables on my way out. Somebody's kid was asleep on a folding chair, glove still on his hand. Nobody moved him. Just let him sleep it off, the whole party humming on around him like that's exactly where he belonged.

That's the gala. That's the third one. Nobody's counting the money on the way home — they're counting the kids who got a ride to the field this year.

### 86 Applied, 31 Placed: The Apprenticeship Pipeline's First Real Numbers

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Civic

---

86 Applied, 31 Placed: The Apprenticeship Pipeline's First Real Numbers

By Luis Navarro

Twenty-eight cycles ago, this desk asked a question and got a press release instead of an answer.

C73: the Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline launches as part of the Baylight supplemental. Three entry tracks. Eight trades. A target of 400-plus direct Baylight placements by Year 3. The column that ran alongside that story asked whether the outreach behind it would actually reach East Oakland youth, or just the kids already standing near the door.

This cycle, Councilmember Janae Rivers (D5) published the first cohort fill report. It is the first time anyone has put real numbers next to that promise.

86 applied. 31 placed. 40 still in intake. 15 screened out.

Do the math: 31 placements toward a 400-by-Year-3 goal is under eight percent of the target — behind the pace that goal implies, this far from Year 3. Not catastrophic. Not hidden, either — Rivers didn't have to release a number that shows her own initiative short of schedule, and she did it anyway.

The first attempt at this report didn't go that cleanly. OPP's initial draft shipped with zero actual placement figures — a report about a numbers program that had no numbers in it. It had to be pulled and rerun before Rivers' office produced the count that's on the table now. Worth noting. Not the story.

The story is where the applicants are coming from. The cycle's own numbers show two of the program's three target neighborhoods produced no applicants at all — meaning the pipeline is still drawing from the same networked pockets of the city it started with, twenty-eight cycles after launch.

That's the gap Elijah Roberts flagged back in C98, when he raised doubts about the program's binding mechanism — whether a placement pipeline without real enforcement teeth would actually reach past its own front door. This cohort report doesn't resolve that question. It documents it in numbers for the first time.

Rivers sees it too.

"Honestly? Seeing those numbers — 86 applied, 31 placed — it's progress, but it's not enough," Rivers said. "Not when we've got young folks in East Oakland, West Oakland, desperate for pathways out of the grind. I stood at that ribbon-cutting for the apprenticeship hub last year thinking, This could change everything. And it is changing things — just slower than I'd like."

"I ran into Freddie Silecki at the bakery yesterday — you remember him, coached my nephew in MUBA back in the day — and he said, 'Janae, you got kids who don't even know these programs exist.' He's right. We're hitting the usual suspects: the kids already plugged in, already networked. But what about the ones slipping through? The ones who don't have a Rabbi Yael or a Coach Freddie nudging them forward? Thirty-one placements? Let's make it 300. Let's make it so every kid who wants a shot can see a door open."

Thirty-one placements against a goal of 400 by Year 3, with the clock already twenty-eight cycles in. That is the number that matters here, and Rivers put it on paper herself.

Her approval rating climbed five points this cycle, 75 to 80 — a rise that lands on the same week she published a report showing her own program behind pace. Oakland, apparently, would rather have the real count than another ribbon-cutting.

### Fruitvale's Bargain Comes Due

By Trevor Shimizu | Bay Tribune Civic

---

Four cycles ago, Councilmember Rose Delgado stood at the groundbreaking for the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II and drew a line in wet concrete. The Community Benefits Agreement terms, she said, are "not subject to value-engineering... if they slip, the project has slipped." It was the kind of promise that sounds easy to make on a stage with a shovel in your hand. Four cycles later is exactly when a promise like that gets checked against what actually got built.

What actually got built, so far, is a project still sitting in construction-planning. No phase advance this cycle. None the cycle before that, or the one before that. Four cycles, no movement — and Transit Hub Planning Lead Eloise Soria-Dominguez, who has certified this project at every stage since the CBA and the groundbreaking, published a compliance report this cycle confirming what residents have been saying at bus stops for weeks: the project is contributing to Fruitvale's inflow strain through three separate channels.

That word — strain — is doing real work in Fruitvale right now, and Delgado is refusing to let city hall smooth it into something easier to say. Asked directly whether the neighborhood's growth numbers and its capacity problems were really two sides of the same story, she didn't take the bait toward a tidy narrative. "Fruitvale is not in trouble," she has said on the record — but she won't fold that reassurance into the strain data just to keep the story clean. Both things are true at once, in her accounting: the neighborhood is growing, and the neighborhood is straining under that growth, and neither cancels the other out.

Soria-Dominguez's report gives that tension a paper trail. Her office has signed off on this project at every checkpoint — the CBA certification, the groundbreaking, and now the compliance filing — and each time the oversight question has gotten sharper, not softer. A planning lead who has publicly staked her name to a project's benefits language doesn't file a report naming three channels of strain lightly. That she did it anyway is the story underneath the headline: the accountability structure Delgado promised at groundbreaking is still active, still watching, four cycles in.

Rose Delgado, who represents Fruitvale on the council, put the district's own read on the word "bargain" bluntly. "Honestly? The Tribune calling Fruitvale a 'bargain' feels like someone admiring the paint job while ignoring the cracks in the foundation," she said. "Yeah, my block's got the best pupusas this side of the bay and Lucia's aura readings at the wellness spot somehow always hit right — but try telling Ms. Gonzalez that her rent's a 'steal' when she's sweating the 20% hike come lease renewal. Fruitvale's heart's always been its people — not some 'hidden gem' narrative. Fix the potholes, keep the libraries open late, and let us stay in the homes we've poured love into. That's the due I care about."

Soria-Dominguez, who has watched the Transit Hub's promises and its slippage from inside the planning office, described the same bargain from the ground level. "Honestly? It's complicated," she said. "Fruitvale's always been a place where people hustle to make ends meet, and this so-called 'bargain' feels like just another layer of that. Yeah, the transit hub's brought jobs and attention, but it's also pushing rents up — people who've been here generations are getting squeezed out. I see it at the taquerias, at the bus stops, in the way neighbors eye each other like, Who's next? I love this place — the energy, the grit — but if we're not careful, the 'bargain' will be our community paying the price while someone else cashes in. I'm tired of holding my breath. We need real solutions, not just shiny promises."

Two women, two vantage points on the same stalled project — one holding the council seat, one holding the compliance file — and neither one is willing to call it fine. Neither is willing to call it a disaster, either. Delgado's own standing with the district has moved upward this cycle even as she says this out loud, which is its own kind of data point: residents don't appear to be punishing her for refusing to paper over the strain. If anything, saying "not in trouble" and "still straining" in the same breath seems to be what they wanted to hear.

The CBA language Delgado set at groundbreaking was never a marketing line — it was a test she wrote for herself, four cycles in advance, knowing exactly when it would come due. It has come due. The compliance report says the project is contributing to strain through three channels. It does not yet say the apprenticeship placements, the wage floors, and the rest of the benefits language have failed to materialize — only that the accounting exists, that someone is still watching, and that the answer to whether Fruitvale's bargain holds is going to be decided in the next few cycles of construction-planning, not in this one's press release.

### Two Doors Open: Faith Communities Answer a Hard Cycle

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

Two people from Maya Cordova's job-training workshop came back to Telegraph Presbyterian this cycle. One brought a friend.

That's the story I've been watching at Telegraph for several cycles now. Not rescue, not salvation—just consistency. The congregation started this workshop two cycles ago, and this cycle it was still running, still drawing back the people who'd been through it before, still opening a door to something that felt like an actual skill, an actual future. Two cycles is nothing. But for the people who needed it, it was everything.

I wanted to see what that looked like in other congregations this cycle. Oakland's faith communities reported five events with serious tags—events that didn't make the Tribune last cycle, but that I should have been there for. So I went to B'nai Tikvah on Piedmont Avenue on a Thursday, and I found Yael Bauer in the kitchen.

She'd just come out of the grief circle. The people were still in the fellowship hall, not quite ready to leave. She was moving between rooms—checking the pantry table, refilling coffee, doing the small work that holds a gathering together. When I asked her what the congregation had been doing with the hard cycle, she didn't shift into a spokesperson voice. She just told me:

"As the Rabbi at B'nai Tikvah Synagogue, I've seen firsthand how our faith communities have stepped up during what's been a hard cycle for so many. The needs are deep and varied—food insecurity, mental health struggles, isolation. It's not just about opening our doors; it's about opening our hearts and truly being present for one another. At B'nai Tikvah, we've expanded our food pantry, hosted healing circles, and created spaces for people to grieve, whether it's the loss of a loved one or the loss of a sense of normalcy. Faith, for me, has always been about connection—to each other, to something greater, and to hope. That said, it's not easy. There's a heaviness in the air, and sometimes it feels like we're barely keeping up. I've had moments where I've had to pause and remind myself that it's okay to feel overwhelmed. We're not meant to carry it all alone."

That word—*heaviness*—is the cycle I've been seeing. Not one crisis, not one thing, but the accumulation. And the fact that places like B'nai Tikvah and Telegraph Presbyterian are still showing up to it, still opening doors, still making spaces where people don't have to carry it alone.

Deputy Mayor Okoro and Councilmember Tran both confirmed they were aware of the grief gathering and the workshop continuation. Neither one was overstating the city's role in them. These weren't initiatives launched from City Hall. They were congregations doing what they've always done—making room for the people in front of them.

That's the two doors. Not salvation. Not sanctuary in the metaphorical sense. Just infrastructure. Just the steady, unglamorous work of showing up when a cycle gets heavy, and trusting that consistency matters more than the crisis.

### Fourteen Neighborhoods, One Pattern: Oakland's Retail Slide Has No Owner

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

---

Joaquín Nakamura got promoted this cycle. The Uptown kombucha brewer, who has spent years building the kind of neighborhood anchor that keeps foot traffic alive on commercial corridors, earned a senior position — a small, real win in a city that doesn't generate many of them lately. The thing is, if you stand outside his shop in Uptown and look at the storefronts around him, the promotion feels less like progress and more like rearranging furniture on a tilting floor.

Uptown's retail landscape took a sharp hit this cycle. So did Rockridge, Grand Lake, Eastlake, Downtown, Jack London, Glenview, Dimond, San Antonio, KONO, Temescal, West Oakland — the better part of fourteen commercial districts across Oakland. Some are down slightly. Others are hemorrhaging: San Antonio and Uptown took the sharpest hits of the cycle, storefronts going dark faster than anywhere else in the city, and the mood on those blocks sank right along with the ledgers. The pattern cuts across neighborhoods that have little in common except location and a common problem: they are contracting, and nobody is answering for it.

"Honestly? It's frustrating as hell," Nakamura said. "I see it every day—storefronts sitting empty, the same 'For Lease' signs gathering dust. Uptown used to have this energy, you know? Now it feels like we're all holding our breath, waiting for someone to step in and fix it. But who? The city's stretched thin, landlords are playing the long game, and small businesses like mine are just trying to stay afloat. Henry and I were talking about it last week—how even the A's staying doesn't feel like enough to turn the tide. It's not just about big anchors; it's the little spots, the cafés, the bookstores, the places that give a neighborhood its pulse. And yeah, I got promoted this cycle, but what's the point if the streets outside my shop keep thinning out? I don't have answers, just the same question everyone else does: When do we stop sliding and start building again?"

The retail slide is systemic, not scattered. It spans commercial districts that serve different populations and draw different consumer bases. Yet there is no coordinated city response. The neighborhoods don't share a common initiative, don't point to a single office accountable for reversing the trend, don't report alignment on strategy. Some neighborhoods — Temescal and West Oakland — have active business-support initiatives in place, but those weren't enough to offset the cycle's decay. Most have nothing.

The absence isn't neutral. When a neighborhood's retail layer thins, it drains more than square footage. It shrinks the tax base that funds schools and streets. It kills the casual foot traffic that neighborhood restaurants, bars, and services depend on to stay solvent. It turns commercial corridors into commute corridors. It signals to families that the neighborhood isn't a place to build a life in, just pass through.

What makes this cycle different from the dips that neighborhoods have survived in the past is scale and absence of a visible owner. In earlier cycles, a single neighborhood's retail decline could be traced to a specific event — a recession, a major anchor closing, a temporary business pause. This time, better than a dozen neighborhoods are moving in the same direction simultaneously. And when pressed, the city has no coordinated answer.

Nakamura's promotion is real. The kombucha shop is still there. But the question he asked — when do we stop sliding and start building again — hangs over every commercial street in Oakland. It's not being answered at the neighborhood level, and it's not being answered at the city level either. That's the story: not why the slide happened, but that it's happening everywhere and nobody is charged with stopping it.

### Four Cycles In, I'm Checking on the Work

I wrote in these pages not long ago that the work has to stay here — that if Fruitvale was going to host this Transit Hub, it had to be the kind of work that holds the neighborhood, not erases it. Four cycles into the groundbreaking, I'm checking in.

The site's still moving. Not fast, but moving. The CBA came due this cycle and the strain is real. Neighbors asking me when their street's going to be passable again, when the scaffolding comes down. I've done enough construction to know: you break things before you fix them. But what I'm watching for is whether the people running this thing remember what the neighborhood needed in the first place — apprenticeships for local kids, local hires on the job, the work staying here.

From where I stand, the council and the builders are still at the table. That's the thing that matters most.

— Reggie Soto, Fruitvale


### Thirty-One Placed Is a Floor, Not a Ceiling

I've had my doubts about the apprenticeship program. I said it last cycle when they came around asking questions — the numbers needed to prove it works, not just the talking points. Now we've got the numbers. Eighty-six applied. Thirty-one placed.

I've been doing this work twenty-three years. A thirty-six percent placement rate is what you call a floor, not a ceiling. It means two-thirds of people who bet on the pipeline walked away empty. That's not binding them to anything — that's setting them up for the second disappointment.

What I want to see is why the gap is what it is. Is it the training? Is it the sites? Is it that apprenticeship wages don't hold people through the learning curve? Because right now it reads like the program built the capacity to try and called that success. The real work is figuring out why the ones who didn't make it didn't make it, and whether the pipeline adjusts next cycle. That's what "binding mechanism" actually means.

— Elijah Roberts, West Oakland


### Thirty Years of Translation, and What Gets Lost

After thirty years providing translation services in Chinatown and across the city, I'm stepping away from the work. This cycle feels like the right moment — a pause where the city is asking itself hard questions, and I've spent my whole life helping people say what they need to say across languages and sometimes across walls.

I've watched this neighborhood hold steady through a lot. I read about the faith communities answering a difficult cycle, and I think about the conversations I've had in small rooms — the parents explaining their children's futures, the elders talking about what the city used to be, the young people trying to figure out where they fit. Translation isn't just words. It's witness.

The retail is struggling everywhere. Chinatown held longer than some neighborhoods, but the strain is there now too. I think about the young people I worked with, the ones trying to keep small businesses alive, and I hope the city remembers what happens when neighborhoods lose the places where people gather, where they know the owner, where they can tell their story and be understood.

Thirty years is a long run. I'm grateful for it.

— Illy Jile, Chinatown
