By Mags Corliss
The Quiet Kind of Glory
Oakland, California — June 2040

Oakland shines best when no one’s looking.  The days have grown long and slow, the kind where the fog doesn’t roll in until after dark and the games blend softly into evening.  You can hear the city breathe with the rhythm of the ballpark again — radios tuned low, corner cafés half‑empty while the ninth inning plays out, the steady hum of people trusting the team to do what it has learned to do: endure.

It’s not the champagne years anymore, and maybe that’s the beauty of it.  This team doesn’t play to prove anything; it plays to keep something alive.  Henry Rivas commands innings with the stillness of an old clock.  Benji Dillon, who was supposed to fade, has turned experience into a weapon sharper than velocity.  John Ellis, new to the noise, works with the calm of someone who doesn’t need applause.  Around them, Aitken and Kelley move with quiet precision, their gloves and bats speaking a language everyone in Oakland understands — work done well.

Fans here don’t measure greatness by trophies; they measure it by continuity.  The crowd no longer waits for miracles.  They wait for the small things — a groundout turned with ease, a rookie tipping his cap to the upper deck, a veteran lingering for one more high‑five down the line.  That’s what glory looks like here now — not loud, not fleeting, but lasting.

Every win feels earned twice — once on the field, once in the faith of those who keep coming back.  The dynasty might have dimmed, but the light never left.  It just learned how to burn slower, steadier.  The quiet kind of glory isn’t about dominance; it’s about devotion.  And Oakland, once again, wears that kind of light well.

— End of Column —
