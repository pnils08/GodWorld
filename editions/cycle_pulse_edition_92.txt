The Day She Named Dates
Patricia Nolan gave up asking about the Temescal corner twelve cycles ago. This cycle, the Mayor named three dates — and then did it four more times.
By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

The lot at Temescal Avenue and 47th Street has been 2.3 acres of cleared dirt and chain-link fence for as long as Patricia Nolan has been walking this block in the mornings. She is 55, a retired teacher, and she has been doing the Claremont corridor walk long enough to know what the corner looks like in every light: the gravel pale at dawn, the fence throwing its thin shadow east, the sign wired to the chain-link that references a project she stopped believing in sometime around Cycle 80.

She kept walking past it anyway, the way you keep walking past something you've learned not to read.

"Patience is turning into being ignored," she said at a public comment session during that period, and then went home, and the lot stayed fenced, and twelve cycles passed.

This cycle, three dates arrived.

Architect contract: Cycle 93. Site acquisition finalized: Cycle 94. HCAI permit filing: Cycle 95. Mayor Avery Santana named them in a statement eleven words long: "Bobby Chen-Ramirez clears the choke points by Cycle 95. That's the deadline." Project Director Chen-Ramirez accepted the schedule on the record. Perkins&Will Oakland — the $4.5 million fixed-fee architect firm attached to the project through the C80 council vote that authorized the health center 6 to 2 — will execute the design contract beginning in Cycle 93. Alameda Health System remains the named operator. The dates are public, attached to specific deliverables, and the project director has acknowledged them.

That sentence alone would have been the news. It was not the news.

---

Mayor Santana's approval rating moved from 73 to 78 in the same cycle she issued five statements that each absorbed a political cost the city had been deferring for months. The statements came in a single working session. Every council seat except District 1 registered a one-point drop. D1's Denise Carter, whose West Oakland base has waited on Temescal funding since the original vote, was the sole elected exception, moving two points up.

The pattern is worth reading before the decisions themselves: a mayor who spent the better part of a year distributing political exposure across deputy offices and study timelines absorbed all five pressure points in one day. There are several ways to interpret that kind of consolidation. The approval data reflects one.

The second statement named Marcus Osei. Osei has held the Deputy Mayor for Economic Development title since before the Stabilization Fund vote. He has been hospitalized since Cycle 72, recovering from injuries sustained in a traffic collision, and the Economic Development portfolio has operated on a series of temporary arrangements that stretched from a matter of weeks into more than a year of cycles. "Marcus Osei is not returning to the Economic Development portfolio," the Mayor said. "His recovery is ongoing, and Brenda Okoro leads this department forward." No timeline for Osei's return was offered. The statement did not describe the arrangement as temporary.

Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro — Community Affairs, now also Economic Development — received the consolidation with a defined expiration. The third statement set it: "Brenda Okoro consolidates three portfolios until the backlog clears. Then we reset. Council oversight checkpoint at 90 days." The 90-day window expires at Cycle 95, the same cycle the Temescal Health Center is due to file its HCAI permit. Quarterly economic development briefings to the full council are now mandated under that structure. The Mayor's language — "then we reset" — implies a reassessment but not a guaranteed rollback. Council oversight means council time on a consolidated portfolio that has grown considerably. Okoro's office did not offer comment before deadline.

The fourth statement turned to Baylight. Phase II of the Baylight redevelopment has been framed across multiple cycles as a November briefing item, a forward horizon, a coming scope discussion. "Baylight Phase II arena feasibility is not a November briefing — it is the Phase II briefing," the Mayor said. "The scope is comprehensive, the timeline is now." The next action is set for Cycle 94. Keisha Ramos, Director of the Baylight Redevelopment Authority, received the Phase II directive and will carry it forward. What Phase II scope means in dollar terms and decision terms will be the Cycle 94 question.

The fifth statement named Oakland's NBA path. "An Oakland team means Oakland jobs, Oakland ownership, and an Oakland story. We build it that way or we don't build it." Elias Varek, founder of Civis Systems and the named lead of the Oakland ownership group, has been the public face of the ownership framework discussions. The Mayor's framing — "we build it that way or we don't build it" — is the first time she has attached a conditional structure to the ownership question in public. What the conditional does to the timeline is not yet clear.

---

Santana's approval number is a data point. It is not an explanation for five decisions issued in a single cycle after a year of deferred timelines. The more useful frame may be the shape of what was deferred, and why each of these five things came due in the same session.

Osei's absence created a management limbo that neither the council nor the Mayor's office formally resolved for twenty cycles. Okoro's authority grew informally over that period. The Baylight November briefing was rescheduled more than once. The NBA ownership conversation had been framed as ongoing. The Temescal Health Center had three separate delivery phases — architect contract, site acquisition, regulatory filing — none of which had a public deadline attached.

What changed this cycle was not the urgency of any single item. What changed was that the Mayor put names on each one. Names and, in three cases, numbered cycles.

---

Patricia Nolan walked the Claremont corridor again. The corner at Temescal Avenue and 47th is the same 2.3 acres it was last cycle and the cycle before. The fence is still there. The sign is still wired to the chain-link, referencing a project that now has a project director on the record and three deadlines by which specific things are supposed to happen.

She was asked whether she believed the date.

"I believe the date," she said. She said it the way someone says something they want to be true and are not yet ready to say out loud that they don't know.

The Cycle 93 architect contract is the first check. After twelve cycles, it will arrive before she has to revise the answer.

The Day the City Moved
By Mags Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

Some cycles the city holds still. Most cycles, really. One initiative edges a half step, one decision defers another cycle, the construction goes up but the paperwork behind it does not. That is governance — the long work of almost.

This cycle was different. The Mayor moved on five things in one day. A health center that has sat in design phase since the 6-2 vote at Cycle 80 now has three deadlines with names attached to them. Fruitvale's transit rebuild — carried on a shelf through 88 cycles of almost — is ready for a vote. Baylight's arena integration has a briefing date and a scope. The Economic Development portfolio has a permanent leader, time-limited oversight, and a council checkpoint. An NBA team has a community-benefit framework before it has a name.

Not everything that moved was unambiguous. Our accountability columnist asks a question no one answered: if the Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline was built to feed Baylight construction eighteen months before shovels hit dirt, where are the apprentices? That is journalism doing the thing the city needs.

What sits on the desk is an eleven-piece edition about a city that chose movement. It will not stay this way. But it is this way this cycle.

— Mags

Temescal Health Center Finally Has a Clock
After years of design-phase delay, the Health Center finally has a clock with three names on it.
By Dr. Lila Mezran, Bay Tribune Health Desk

The corner of Temescal Avenue and 47th Street looks like a lot right now. In twelve cycles, if three deadlines hold, it will look like a clinic.

On Tuesday, Mayor Avery Santana directed Bobby Chen-Ramirez, director of the Temescal Community Health Center project, to execute the architect contract by next cycle, lock site acquisition by the cycle after that, and file the state licensing application with the Healthcare Access and Information division by C95. Chen-Ramirez accepted all three.

"Ninety cycles of design phase ends now," Chen-Ramirez said. "I have the Mayor's authority, the architect firm locked, and a site address. Architect contract is done next cycle. We're moving."

The Mayor's language was shorter and less qualified than any statement this desk has printed in twelve cycles of covering this project.

"The Temescal Health Center gets built," Santana said. "Bobby Chen-Ramirez clears the choke points by Cycle 95. That's the deadline."

---

This Health Desk has a specific memory of Temescal that the project file does not fully capture.

Between Cycles 34 and 39, a citywide cognitive cluster spread across six Oakland neighborhoods in thirteen days — Laurel, Uptown, Fruitvale, Jack London, Eastlake, Rockridge. Forty-three documented cases. Transient cognitive disruption, brief time discontinuity, no environmental or infectious cause identified. The CDC Epidemic Intelligence Service came in on C37. FEMA Region 9 was on standby by C39. This desk covered every cycle of it alongside Dr. Patricia Nwosu at Alameda County Health and Dr. James Okonkwo at Highland Hospital. No cause was ever named.

Six cycles later, the spike came to Temescal specifically. Level 2. Three elementary schools and one middle school. A six-block radius of children presenting with symptoms no pathogen could explain. Parents kept kids home. Shock-flag data continued to register through C48, C49, C50. The kids recovered. No cause was named.

By C63, a different symptom profile — cognitive fog, brief disorientation, three unrelated adults across three neighborhoods within 48 hours. The crisis arc reactivated. Stabilized by C67.

Three distinct health emergencies in thirty cycles. None connected. None explained.

Through all of it, Temescal had no neighborhood clinic.

---

The council voted 6-2 to authorize the $45 million Temescal Community Health Center at C80. Vega and Tran crossed the aisle to join the full OPP bloc. Ashford and Chen voted no. Crane was absent.

Then the project slowed.

Five months passed before the Mayor issued the priority designation — issued C85, after Council Member Janae Rivers of District 5 called the delay "an indictment of process." The RFP went out C86. The operator — Alameda Health System — finalized its service menu at C90. The site Phase I environmental clearance and geotechnical assessment were completed C91, returning results the project team describes as manageable: moderate seismic retrofit scope, on-site parking feasible.

Patricia Nolan, 55, a retired teacher who has lived in Temescal through all three health waves, attended the C86 community meeting where 47 residents showed up — 70 percent of them for the first time. She has been patient about the project for longer than most.

"Patience is turning into being ignored," she said in C85.

She has not reversed that position publicly. But there is a C93 deadline now, and a site address, and an architect.

---

The architect is Perkins&Will Oakland. The firm carries twelve completed healthcare projects and OSHPD-3 experience — the state classification that covers outpatient and clinic facilities. Contract negotiation concluded this cycle at a fixed fee of $4.5 million. Execution is scheduled next cycle.

The path Chen-Ramirez described runs as follows: architect contract signed and schematic design at 30 percent by C93; site intent declaration filed, HCAI pre-application meeting completed, and schematic design at 80 percent by C94; by C95, 100 percent construction documents submitted to HCAI, structural certification and OSHPD seismic approval in hand, medical gas design complete, site acquisition closed, and cost estimate locked to within 5 percent of the $33 million construction budget.

After C95, HCAI plan review takes between six and twelve months. The state controls that clock, not the city. If review runs nominal, construction preparation begins by C97.

The services planned: primary care, behavioral health, dental, pharmacy, pediatric care, evening and weekend hours.

That last item — evening and weekend hours — was the top practical ask from the working residents who came to the C86 meeting. Dental was third on the list. Behavioral health was second.

---

Marcus Carter, 54, has cut hair in Temescal for long enough to have seen the neighborhood through everything the health data records. He runs his shop four blocks from the corner of Temescal and 47th.

He watched the pediatric spike in real time — watched parents move pickup routines, watched the school schedules go sideways, watched people he'd seen every week start driving to external clinics for routine care.

"I'll say it this way," Carter said. "People in this neighborhood have heard timelines before. The ones who still believe are the ones who haven't been burned too many times. The ones who've been burned are going to need to see dirt move on that corner before they breathe."

He paused.

"The Mayor gave a date. That's different than what we've had. I'm not dismissing that. But C95 is the clock, and the clock starts now."

---

Twelve cycles is a long time for a neighborhood with Temescal's health history to go without local clinical infrastructure. The C45 pediatric spike happened to kids whose parents were driving them across zip codes for follow-up care. The C63 adults who reported symptoms had a three-month wait at the nearest behavioral health provider.

The response to that gap is now at a corner address: Temescal Avenue and 47th Street, 2.3 acres, cleared for construction on the environmental side, with a signed operator, a named architect, a Mayor's deadline, and a project director who said — publicly, on the record — that the design phase is over.

Mayor Santana, asked whether she was confident in the C95 filing date, said: "Three years of architectural review is enough. The city has what it needs. Bobby moves."

Whether the health center gets built is the watch this desk will keep.

---

Fruitvale Transit Hub Clears the Bar — Now Comes the Amendment Fight
First phase advance in 88 cycles — now the cost-cap fight between Vega and Ashford heads to the floor.
By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

Elena Soria Dominguez grew up near the Fruitvale BART station. She knows the station the way people know a building that shapes their commute, their neighborhood, and the distance between where they live and where the city's resources stop arriving. This cycle she returned to that geography with a clipboard and a certification checklist, and she cleared it.

Seven of eight Community Benefits Agreement requirements for the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II are verified met. The eighth — final seating of the Oversight Committee — closes before the C93 council vote. After 88 cycles without a single phase advance across any of the city's active initiatives, the Transit Hub moved from vote-scheduled to vote-ready.

"All eight community benefit requirements are verified met or committed to close before the C93 vote," Soria Dominguez said. "The CBA framework is operationally ready for council approval."

The $230 million project — a comprehensive rebuild of the station area at E. 12th and 34th — now goes to the council with one council fight still in front of it.

---

## What Got Certified

The CBA Soria Dominguez certified is specific. A Community Land Trust deed restriction locking 35% of residential units as affordable for 30 years or more. An $18 million small business stabilization fund contracted through the Community Development Department in partnership with the Unity Council. Commercial space preservation covering 20% of development with a 25-year rent lock and right of first refusal for existing tenants. A hiring floor requiring 30% apprentice labor and 50% of permanent jobs drawn from Fruitvale residents, at a wage floor of $25 per hour. A 15,000 square-foot community plaza at the station entrance with $250,000 in annual programming commitments. And binding arbitration with $500,000 minimum liquidated damages if terms are breached.

The eighth item — Oversight Committee seating — represents the enforcement layer. Without it, the rest of the CBA is a contract without a monitor. Soria Dominguez has committed to closing that gap before the vote.

Council District 3's Rose Delgado, whose district includes the Transit Hub site, has tracked the certification process throughout. Fruitvale has had a representative on council watching this move through committee for years. The vote will be the first time the project clears a council chamber.

---

## Vega's Calendar and His Answer to Ashford

Council President Ramon Vega calendared the vote and announced his position in the same statement.

"The Transit Hub vote is scheduled. That vote happens. Fruitvale has waited long enough," Vega said.

He also addressed the amendment Warren Ashford has signaled — a $230 million hard cost cap, a 5% contingency ceiling, a provision requiring council re-review if costs exceed that ceiling, and quarterly external audits of CBA compliance. For Vega, the conditions add nothing the existing CBA doesn't already provide.

"A cost-cap amendment treating the council as though those protections don't exist adds no value — it adds procedure," he said.

That is not a hedge. It is a published rejection. Vega has 11 years of council familiarity between himself and this reporter, and he does not issue published rejections as opening negotiating positions.

---

## Ashford's Conditions

Warren Ashford, 78, representing D7 from Rockridge — the CRC voice who pressed the Stabilization Fund independent audit the council finally authorized and who opposed the Baylight bond on fiscal grounds — is not speaking in generalities. His conditions are written: $230 million hard cap, 5% contingency maximum, any overrun triggers mandatory council re-review, quarterly external audits throughout implementation.

"We support the Transit vote if cost is capped, consequences are written, and compliance audits are in place," Ashford said.

The math on the main motion does not require CRC's support. Four OPP members — Carter, Delgado, Rivers, Mobley — plus Vega constitute five votes. The amendment, if brought to the floor, would need to pass before the main vote proceeds. If it fails, CRC faces a choice: accept the CBA as certified or register a protest vote that changes nothing for Fruitvale but goes in the record.

What the amendment costs, if it gets traction it mathematically shouldn't have, is time. The pre-vote certification window is not unlimited. If Ashford forces procedural review that delays the calendar, the Oversight Committee seating deadline runs against a moving target.

---

## Fruitvale Has Waited

Delia Fuentes, 44, drives a school bus through Fruitvale every morning. In earlier Tribune coverage she raised the question that has not gone away through the long wait on this project — rents moving before construction starts, the promise of redevelopment running ahead of the protections meant to come with it. The CBA Soria Dominguez certified answers that question in specific terms: a Community Land Trust deed restriction, an $18 million stabilization fund, a 25-year rent lock on 20% of new commercial space, a wage floor, binding arbitration if terms slip. Whether those specifics hold when construction begins is the part of the record Fuentes and her neighbors have said they are watching.

The civic inflow pressure on Fruitvale has been building. The neighborhood sits in a high-severity strain zone — residents moving through an area where service investment hasn't kept pace with density. The Transit Hub rebuild was designed as a phase-one answer. Its CBA was designed to make sure the rebuild served the people already there.

Soria Dominguez certified seven of eight. The eighth closes before the vote. The vote is scheduled for C93.

What the council does with Ashford's amendment is the only question the data can't yet answer.

---

The Motion Isn't Filed Yet. The Votes Are Already Moving.
Rivers files the D2 motion this cycle. Tran's vote depends on whether the motion names an implementation cycle.
By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

Here's what we know.

Councilmember Janae Rivers (D5, OPP) is filing a District 2 implementation motion this cycle. The motion would extend the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative — now running in D1, D3, and D5 — to Downtown and Chinatown, the neighborhoods Councilmember Leonard Tran (D2, IND) has represented for nearly a decade. Rivers has the OPP bloc behind her: four seats. Tran is the fifth vote she needs for a majority.

Here's what we don't know.

We don't know what the motion says yet. And that's the whole question.

"The evidence is in," Rivers said this week. "D2 deserves what D1 and D5 already have. We're filing the motion this cycle."

Tran has not said he'll vote no. He has said what it will take to vote yes.

"A framework with thresholds isn't enough," Tran said. "I need a committed implementation cycle for my vote on expansion."

Six words carry the weight: a committed implementation cycle. Not a framework. Not thresholds. A cycle number in the motion text. Without it, Tran says, he is still the blocker. With it, he says — and this is the part Rivers is betting on — they move forward together.

---

THE FRAMEWORK SAYS C95. RIVERS SAYS THAT'S SLOW-WALK.

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, OARI's program director, published her expansion framework this cycle. It is meticulous. It names C95 as the D2 decision point. It sets renewal thresholds: at least 75% non-police resolution across all four outcome categories — behavioral health, welfare, substance-use, homelessness — sustained across two consecutive quarters, with community complaint volume stable or declining. Expansion adds three more gates: District Attorney Dane's legal framework cleared, Chief Montez's OPD dispatch performance confirmed sustained, and community advisory certification in D2. A C94 quarterly data release feeds the C95 comprehensive vote.

Tran-Muñoz's framework is designed to survive a legal challenge. Rivers reads it as a slow-walk. Tran reads it as sufficient — if the motion mirrors it.

That's the gap. If Rivers writes the motion with C95 as the named cycle, Tran has said he's in. If she writes C94, or writes no named cycle at all, the coalition fractures before the vote is called.

"If Rivers files a D2 implementation motion without that cycle commitment, my vote isn't there — I'm still the blocker," Tran said. "But if she writes the motion with a named implementation cycle that aligns with Tran-Muñoz's thresholds, we move forward together."

Rivers's read on Tran: "Leonard Tran made a bet three cycles ago that this program would work. It did. Now he has a choice: let the bureaucracy move at its pace, or deliver on the promise he made to his district. This is not pressure — it's asking him to keep his word."

They are describing the same vote in opposite terms. Tran calls it a threshold question. Rivers calls it an accountability question. The motion text will resolve it — or it won't.

---

DANE'S MOVE: PARTNER, NOT OBSTACLE

The most significant coalition shift this cycle did not come from council. It came from the fourth floor of the Rene C. Davidson Courthouse.

District Attorney Clarissa Dane has been silent on OARI expansion for two cycles. This week she stopped being silent.

Dane's office endorses OARI's diversion model. She named three specific legal-framework items her office requires before expansion proceeds: scene-safety authority (what triggers OARI responders to call for OPD backup, who documents the safety assessment, and how that documentation binds future charging decisions); evidentiary protocols (chain-of-custody when a call begins as OARI and escalates to arrest, initial-response documentation, and statement admissibility); and escalation triggers (where OARI diverts, where DA discretion is preserved, and how cases involving repeat offenders, domestic violence incidents, or suspected firearm presence are handled).

"OARI's diversion model aligns with prosecutorial standards," Dane said. "Three legal-framework items need settlement before expansion."

Dane went further. She proposed a quarterly prosecutorial audit: OARI-to-DA case data published alongside Tran-Muñoz's evaluation data, on the same cycle. Her office as institutional partner, not institutional obstacle.

That proposal is Dane's own. It does not appear in Tran-Muñoz's framework. It does not appear in Montez's operational report. Dane is positioning the DA's office to absorb one of the loudest objections to OARI — that diversion leaves prosecutorial gaps — by volunteering to publish the receipts.

The question this raises: if Dane's three items become a floor amendment — a condition appended to Rivers's motion — does that give Ashford and Chen (CRC) a legal-sufficiency argument to justify a yes vote, or a procedural argument to delay?

Neither Ashford nor Chen has commented on Dane's statement.

---

MONTEZ: THE BLOCKER THAT ISN'T

Six cycles ago, OPD's posture on OARI was measured support with specific concerns about backup protocols and call-load distribution. Chief Rafael Montez spent the pilot period documenting those concerns in real time.

The documentation is now done.

"Six weeks of operational data confirms the pilot works," Montez said. "The protocol refinements we identified are now in place and holding."

The numbers behind that statement: seven OPD backup requests across D1, D3, and D5 in the first six weeks of the pilot, all handled without incident. Call-load reduction in pilot districts has translated to faster response times on serious crime calls. Montez did not call for caution. He confirmed performance.

That shift matters for the coalition math. OPD was the implicit backstop for CRC's expansion skepticism — if the chief had concerns, expansion opponents had institutional cover. Montez has removed that cover. Whether CRC votes with the data or against it is now an explicitly political question, not an operational one.

---

THE MATH

Here's what the coalition looks like as Rivers prepares to file.

OPP holds four seats. Tran is the fifth, available under the condition he has stated publicly. That's five votes — a majority on a nine-member council — if the motion says what Tran has said he needs it to say.

The CRC bloc (Ashford, Chen, Crane) has three votes and a pattern: they opposed OARI's original authorization on a 5-4 vote. Mobley (OPP, D9) voted yes on original authorization. Vega (IND, D4) voted no.

The original motion passed 5-4. Rivers doesn't need CRC. She needs Tran.

What the motion says when it's filed is not a detail. It is the vote. If Rivers writes C95, Tran has said he's there. If she writes something else — or nothing named — he has said, on the record, that he isn't.

The motion is this cycle. The vote follows.

---

Where Are the Apprentices?
The Cycle 73 doc is a spec, not an announcement. Where are the apprentices the pipeline was built to feed?
By Jax Caldera | Bay Tribune Accountability

# Where Are the Apprentices?

The city paid for a program. Built the spec. Named the schools. Signed the checks. Now the cranes are up — so where's the pipeline?

---

I was sitting at a table outside the Parkway on Tuesday when I pulled the document on my phone. Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, version 1.0, last updated C73. Workforce Development Board logo at the top. Five high schools named. Eight trade locals named. Six funding sources listed out to the dollar.

It is not an announcement. It is a spec.

So when Mayor Avery Santana said at the C92 council session that the Youth Apprenticeship Initiative "has been announced for three years," I had a question. And when Councilmember Janae Rivers followed her by saying OPP will write "the law that makes it real," I had a second one. The document I read was not a press release. It was 73 cycles of planning, budget allocation, and signed commitments. There is a version number. Version 1.0. Programs that are just announced don't get version numbers.

Here is what the spec actually says, because the city has apparently stopped saying it.

The Oakland Workforce Development Board is the lead agency. OUSD is a partner. So is Peralta CCD. The Building Trades Council. Five high schools are named outright: Oakland Technical, Skyline, Fremont, Castlemont, McClymonds. Eight union locals are named with specific trade pathways — NorCal Carpenters, IBEW Local 595, UA Local 342, Ironworkers Local 378, Laborers Local 304, OE Local 3, SMART Local 104, Cement Masons Local 300. The financing table totals $12.5 million: $3.2M from the city Workforce Development Fund, $2.8M from the state's Career Technical Education grant, $1.5M Peralta in-kind, $1.2M Building Trades Training Trust, $1.8M Federal Apprenticeship Grant. And $2.0M from the A's Ownership Group — not Varek's NBA group — the baseball ownership, tied directly to the Baylight CBA formalized at C83.

The city budget allocation hit at C80. The A's commitment was locked at C83. The program's target launch was Year 3, Quarter 1 — specifically designed to be eighteen months before Baylight broke ground, so Oakland workers would be trained and placed when construction started.

Baylight piling is 28% complete. The project is four weeks ahead of schedule.

Director Keisha Ramos told the council this session that Baylight has 436 local hires year-to-date. That's 41% of the workforce — exceeding the 40% target in the CBA. That number is real. I'm not saying it isn't. I'm asking where the workers came from. Because the spec's projected contribution starting Year 3 was 400 to 600 direct Baylight placements annually — and neither Ramos, nor the Mayor, nor Rivers mentioned the Pipeline when they reported the hiring numbers.

Ramos flagged that women-in-trades are sitting at 18% against a 20% target. The spec had a 25% stretch goal and a plan to get there: Ironworkers Local 378 and Laborers Local 304 running women's pre-apprenticeship intensives through the high school pathway. If that pathway is live, the variance has an explanation. If it isn't, the Building Trades is doing the remediation alone, without the structure the spec built around it.

Councilmember Rivers said OPP "will write the law that makes it real." The approval pathway in the v1.0 doc ran through C74 to C83 — Workforce Development Board design, OUSD MOU, Peralta agreement, state grant application, city budget, A's commitment. That is not an announcement. That is a nine-step program build with money behind it. If Rivers is now drafting legislation, what is in her bill that the approved spec doesn't have? Which step didn't close? Which partner didn't sign?

The Mayor said the NBA team becomes Youth Apprenticeship's "direct pipeline." Maybe it does. But the Baylight pipeline was supposed to start eighteen months before the cranes showed up — not after the NBA arena announcement. The question isn't about vision. The question is about sequence.

I went looking for enrollment numbers. How many students from Oakland Technical are in the HS Pathway? How many Young Adults from West Oakland are drawing the $600 a week stipend the spec authorized? How many of the 139 registered apprentices Ramos counted at Baylight came through the Pipeline versus through direct trade-local referrals?

Nobody has published those numbers.

The document is version 1.0. The schools are named. The money landed. The cranes are running. If the Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline launched on schedule, the Workforce Development Board owes Oakland a progress report. If it didn't, somebody owes Oakland an explanation for why $12.5 million in committed funds and a fully-built spec are being described this cycle like they're a new idea.

Which is it?

---

— Jax Caldera

Baylight Phase I Sprints Ahead; Phase II Opens Competitive Procurement
Piling 28 percent complete and four weeks ahead; the Phase II RFP opens next cycle.
By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

# Baylight Phase I Sprints Ahead; Phase II Opens Competitive Procurement

Baylight Redevelopment Authority Director Keisha Ramos released the second operational dashboard this week, and it tells two stories: the construction sprint is real, and the governance framework is holding.

Piling commenced 4 weeks ahead of baseline schedule. Of 1,247 total piles required for Phase I, crews have driven 349 — 28% complete. The acceleration has not come at the cost of crew safety or labor discipline. Turner Construction, Webcor, and Bay Bridge Constructors have awarded $690 million in contracts year-to-date; $78 million of this cycle's new awards went to SBE subcontractors, representing 42% of the fresh procurement. The Authority's commitment to local hiring shows in the numbers: 436 workers on site to date, 41% Oakland residents against a 40% contractual commitment.

"Piling is running 28% ahead of schedule," Ramos said in her statement to the council. "We're absorbing the acceleration without crew overruns or safety compromise." The discipline is visible in the worker composition: 240 journey-level tradespeople, 139 apprentices, 57 non-union local workers. That split — 55% journey, 32% apprentice, 13% non-union — maps directly to the Building Trades' training pipeline. The variance that flags on the dashboard is women-in-trades at 18% against a 20% target. Ramos flagged it publicly rather than burying it in footnotes.

"We're working with our Building Trades partners to close that gap by next-year alignment," Ramos said. That's the credibility signal. The Authority didn't apologize; it named the gap and the pathway.

Lorenzo Nguyen, 58, operates cranes at the Port of Oakland and lives in Jack London. He watches the piling rigs from his apartment. "Four weeks ahead means the neighborhood is about to feel construction pressure longer," he said. "But the jobs matter. I know three guys on those crews now. This is real work, not a promise." The Port's own contracts are layered into Baylight's supply chain — concrete mixes, structural steel, equipment staging. The acceleration compresses that load into the same district, the same weeks. The infrastructure is keeping pace so far.

Mayor Avery Santana reframed the Phase II briefing as a comprehensive feasibility study rather than a ceremonial announcement. "Baylight Phase II arena feasibility is not a November briefing — it is the Phase II briefing," she said. "The scope is comprehensive, the timeline is now." The Authority announced the Phase II Infrastructure and Arena Design Feasibility RFP opening next cycle as open-competitive procurement. The scope covers master planning, structural engineering, site-support infrastructure, and arena configuration scenarios — three design variants for a 20,000-seat indoor venue with mixed-use plaza integration.

Elias Varek, founder of Civis Systems and part of the ownership group exploring NBA expansion, has had preliminary conversations with the Authority about Phase II planning. No executed contract exists. When the RFP opens next cycle, Civis Systems will be eligible alongside all qualified architecture and engineering firms. CBA negotiation — the labor agreement tied to franchise establishment — will be a separate OEWD-led track under Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro's oversight, beginning when the franchise formally constitutes.

The Authority's structural independence is the point. "Phase II planning procurement is open," Ramos said. "Civis Systems has had preliminary conversations; no executed contract exists. We're announcing the formal RFP now." The Varek connection is real, but it does not predetermine the procurement outcome.

Warren Ashford, Council Member District 7 and CRC caucus chair, accepts the dashboard delivery and escalates the governance demand. "The public can now see what the project promised," he said. "The council needs external verification of what the project delivered." He's calling for rolling independent audits on a schedule: quarterly reviews of cost and schedule status, annual reviews of financial projections. The audit reports route to the council, not to the Authority. The TIF financing runs 30 years; the only mechanism that survives political change is audited data.

Ashford's demand reframes dashboard transparency as an opening move, not a closure. The Authority showed the work. The council is establishing the oversight that lets it trust the work without re-verifying every number. It's what governance looks like when both sides are serious.

Stabilization Fund: Signatures Are the New Bottleneck
Processing is solved. The 191 approved applications waiting on a signature are not.
By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

# Stabilization Fund: Signatures Are the New Bottleneck

The Stabilization Fund's processing machine is humming. Marcus Webb, the OEWD Program Director managing the fund, has reviewed 285 of 295 applications in two months. He has approved 221 of them—$3.87 million committed to households across West Oakland, Fruitvale, and Jack London. Ninety-four families have checks in hand. The approval rate is running clean: 8% denials, 12% appeal rate on those denials, and a sustainable processing pace of fifty applications per week.

The bottleneck has shifted. It is no longer application review. It is executive authorization signatures.

Webb has 191 approved applications queued for sign-off. The paperwork is ready. The eligibility is confirmed. The money is allocated. What's holding the release is the signature authority above Webb's desk—the chain that routes through Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro and up to the Mayor's office.

"The consolidation of three portfolios under Brenda Okoro has not altered the Stabilization Fund's processing schedule or authorization authority chain," Webb said. Okoro oversees economic development, housing, and workforce initiatives under a 90-day consolidation cap. Webb briefs her weekly. The authorization path is the same as it was under her predecessor: application review and eligibility determination at Webb's level, then a request up through Okoro to the Mayor's office.

The math is straightforward. If signatures hold the current pace—roughly keeping up with the backlog as Webb batch-readies paperwork weekly—the authorization queue clears by the C95 checkpoint. That's when the Mayor's consolidation cap expires and the council conducts its oversight review. By then, 221 families will have their money. The second wave will move to C97-C98 disbursal, dependent on how fast the executive side signs.

But Webb flagged a question for the council to consider at that review: one consolidated director managing three major portfolios can deliver on schedule this cycle. But authorization approval cycles may become a capacity ceiling if portfolio work grows. That's structural risk, not operational failure—yet. Okoro is holding pace. The question is whether one person signing off on three simultaneous portfolio tracks stays sustainable when the next initiative launches or when another crisis requires emergency allocation capacity.

The council will have that answer by the C95 review. For now, 127 households are waiting on a signature. Fifty more applications come in every week. The queue clears only if the authorization pace holds.

The Darrin Davis Game
Four hits, three home runs, nine RBIs — and the A's at eighteen and one.
By Anthony | Bay Tribune Beat Reporter

The final line reads four hits, three home runs, nine runs batted in. Oakland eleven, Chicago three. The sports feed logged it before the stadium lights finished cycling down, already naming it what it is: the Darrin Davis Game. Fifteen consecutive wins. A record of eighteen and one through nineteen games. The numbers are correct. What the numbers don't fully capture is the architecture behind the night — what had to be built, and who had to move, for Davis to be standing in left field when the Cubs came to town.

Start with the position change, because everything else flows from it. Darrin Davis is thirty-one years old, a two-time Most Valuable Player, born and raised in Ohio, now a Laurel resident playing left field for the Oakland A's. He came back from ACL rehabilitation last season and the organization had a decision to make. Ernesto Quintero — twenty-four years old, from West Oakland, a designated hitter with the kind of contact authority that shows up in exit velocity readings before it shows up in the box score — needed the DH slot to develop. General manager Mike Paulson and Deacon Seymour's coaching staff chose to move Davis to left field to create that opening. The tradeoff is real and documented: Davis carries a sixty-three defensive metric in left field, which ranks at the bottom of the starting outfield tier across the league. Oakland's front office made the deal anyway.

Tuesday night was the argument for that decision.

Davis went to work in the first inning and did not stop. His first home run came off the Cubs' starter with a runner on first — a pull-side drive that left the bat at the kind of exit velocity that renders stadium geometry irrelevant. The second came in the fourth, a two-run shot to the opposite gap that showed the full horizontal range of his power. By the time he launched the third, a three-run drive in the seventh that cleared the visiting bullpen, the question had stopped being whether Davis was locked in and started being whether the Cubs had any mechanism to retire him. Nine runs batted in. The kind of night that gets its own name.

What makes it worth examining further is what the night cost, which is nothing. Davis's defensive metrics are a carrying cost — they appear every game, registered in positioning adjustments and coverage angles that Seymour's staff manages through shifting and outfield alignment. On a night when Davis hits three home runs and drives in nine, the cost disappears into the accounting. What remains is a twenty-eight-point-four-million-dollar-a-year left fielder putting up a line that belongs in a franchise record book.

The quieter story is John Ellis.

Ellis is twenty-six years old, drafted in the second round in 2033, slotted fourth in a rotation that begins with Arturo Ramos and runs through Henry Rivas — currently sidelined with a dislocated shoulder — and Travis Coles. When Rivas went down, the rotation's depth became the question mark. Ellis answered Tuesday. He allowed ten hits and three earned runs across his start, struck out two, and left the game with his second win of the season. That is not a dominant line. It is also not a line that required the offense to carry the bullpen on a high-leverage night. Ellis gave Oakland what it needed from a fourth starter: innings, contact management, a win. The contract the organization is paying him is a fraction of what the front-office principals spent on the top of the rotation, which is exactly what you need from the bottom.

The streak sits at fifteen games. The record sits at eighteen and one. Through game nineteen, Oakland has been hitting, pitching, and, when necessary, being saved by one player deciding a particular evening belongs entirely to him. Ramos has the ace line. Coles, at twenty-two years old, has been nearly untouchable. Dillon, in what everyone in the organization understands to be his final season, has provided stability at the back end that no one had a right to expect. But the rotation lost Rivas, and the offense has had to absorb that cost.

Tuesday night, it absorbed it entirely. Darrin Davis — Laurel resident, thirty-one years old, wearing the wrong position by any defensive measure — went four for four against a Cubs staff that had no answer for him.

The front office built the team this way on purpose: position sacrifice, depth pitching, lineup construction designed to sustain nights when the starters struggle and the bullpen is thin. Nineteen games in, the design is holding. Tuesday night it held spectacularly.

They'll call it the Darrin Davis Game for a long time.

Taveras Bursts Through
I was wrong. The signing I hated is leading Major League Baseball in home runs.
By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

# Taveras Bursts Through

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

I was wrong.

I don't say that often. Ask anyone who's read this column for more than a week — I say a lot of things loudly and I walk back maybe ten percent of them. But I have to say it here, in print, where the archive lives and Mara can quote it back to me when she wants to win an argument: I was wrong about Eric Taveras.

I hated this signing. Seven years. Two hundred and twenty-five million dollars. For a career .244 hitter who'd never finished a full season as an everyday second baseman. I wrote those words. I stood behind them. I'll own them now the same way I'm owning the reversal — completely, without hedge.

Here is what nineteen games of baseball has delivered: a .333 average, sixteen runs batted in, a .768 slugging percentage, and nine home runs that lead Major League Baseball. Nine. Lead. MLB.

You don't have to be a numbers person to feel that. You just have to have a pulse.

---

I know what the pushback sounds like. I've been having it in my own head for two weeks. Small sample. Hot streak. The league hasn't figured him out yet. The pitchers will adjust, the launch-angle gurus will find the zone, the whole thing craters in July and we're stuck with that contract until 2047. Some version of that argument has been living in my DMs since his third home run.

Those people aren't wrong to be careful. Baseball is a sport that humiliates certainty. I've watched too many April darlings become August cautionary tales to celebrate a nineteen-game line without looking both ways.

But here's what I keep coming back to: a dead-red fastball hunter doesn't hit .333 by accident. That profile — wait on the heater, punish it, make them throw the pitch they don't want to throw — it's a real thing. It's a plate approach, not a hot week. Scouts saw it. The front office saw it. The number was two hundred and twenty-five million dollars because somebody believed the box score was hiding something the film was showing.

I told you the ceiling was fantasy. The box score is showing you the ceiling right now.

---

There's a version of this column where I dissect the mechanics, walk you through the swing path, explain why the 2039 Home Run Derby wasn't a fluke. Anthony can write that column. He probably already has a draft. That's not what I do.

What I do is tell you how this feels.

It feels like the front office went all-in on a bet I thought was reckless and they're cashing. It feels like Deacon Seymour put Taveras in the two hole on opening day — right there in front of God and the whole first-base side of the Coliseum — and said: this is who we are now. It feels like a 25-year-old from the Dominican Republic walked into a roster full of legends and said, without a word, that he belongs.

That's not nothing. That is, in fact, the whole thing.

---

You know Vinnie Keane is still here. He'll finish this season with us and I hope he finishes it proud. That piece belongs to Hal — I'm not writing the farewell, Hal will do it right and he'll do it slow and it will be worth waiting for. But I'll say this much: the organization made a choice. You can feel it in the lineup card every night. Keane rides off slow into the sunset. Taveras bursts through the door.

That's not tragedy and it's not cruelty. It's how a dynasty survives. You have to have someone ready to burst through the door. Paulson found him. Seymour handed him the at-bats. And Taveras — this career .244 hitter on a contract I spent two columns calling a catastrophe — has nine home runs in nineteen games.

I was wrong. He's real.

Oakland, the next seven years just got a lot more interesting.

---

reporter: Hal Richmond
Three fifty-three in seventeen at-bats. A farewell season refusing the script.
By Hal Richmond

There is a number that keeps catching me this cycle when I look at the box scores, and it is not a number you would expect from a man in his last season. Three fifty-three. That is not a retirement statistic. That is not the line of a player drifting gracefully toward the exit. That is the line of a man who is still inside the game — inside the specific, unreplicable pleasure of the game — in a way that most men stop being at thirty, let alone thirty-eight.

Vinnie Keane is thirty-eight years old. He has four hundred and thirty-six home runs. He has six World Series rings, the last of which he won when he sent a baseball into the October air in two thousand thirty-one and nobody in the Coliseum sat down for nine minutes afterward. He has a two thousand thirty-one MVP award. He has the Vinnie Keane West Oakland Baseball Academy, housed in a rehabbed firehouse on Mandela Parkway, where children learn to play ball for free because he decided that was what you do when the city has given you what this city gave him. He has, in short, everything a man could accumulate in a career like his.

And still he is hitting three fifty-three.

I have written about Keane before — in other cycles, when the season was building and the dynasty felt like something that might go on forever — and I wrote then that he is joy. That sentence has stayed with me because I believe it is still the most precise thing I know how to say about him. He has always played with a quality that communicated pleasure in the act itself — not the result, not the celebration, but the swing, the moment of contact. Most players in a farewell season are playing for the record of it. They are filing the paperwork of departure — showing up so the fans can show up, going through the motions of conclusion. Keane is not doing that. When he walks to the plate in a platoon DH role, with seventeen at-bats spread across the calendar of a very long season, he is doing the thing itself. He is swinging.

That is a distinction I want to press on, because it matters. There is a version of the farewell season that is ceremonial — the long goodbye, the tributes, the curtain call that goes on until everybody in the building has had their moment of recognition. It is a thing that happens to the great ones, and it happens because the great ones have earned it, and I do not begrudge any player that kind of send-off. But Keane has never been interested in the ceremony of himself. He is interested in the game. He is interested in the particular small problem of a pitcher's arm angle and a ball arriving at a certain height and what his hands need to do in the quarter-second available to him. Two home runs in seventeen at-bats is not ceremonial. Two home runs in seventeen at-bats is a man doing the work.

I have been covering Oakland baseball for forty-four years. I have watched players go out gracefully and players go out badly and players who simply disappeared between one season and the next, their departure registered only in the roster footnotes. The ones I remember — the ones whose names still carry weight when I say them in a room full of people who love this game — are the ones who stayed in it until the very end. Not stayed on the roster. Stayed in it. Stayed present in the actual physical reality of facing a pitcher and doing something useful with what he threw. Keane is doing that. He is refusing, in the most specific and literal sense, to show decline.

The firehouse on Mandela Parkway is part of this. I think about it often when I think about Keane — the way that after the rings and the home runs and the nine minutes of standing ovation, he went back to the neighborhood and turned a building into a place where children learn that this game belongs to them too. That decision tells you something about the man. It tells you that the joy he has always communicated from the batter's box was never only about himself. Joy that large tends to want somewhere to go. The Academy is where his went.

This cycle I have watched him take his plate appearances with the same focus he brought to October baseball when the whole world was paying attention. He does not appear to notice the discrepancy between his status in the city's imagination and his status on the lineup card. He has seventeen at-bats. He is hitting three fifty-three. He is, by every available measure, still here.

The farewell season belongs to the fans and the organization and the long memory of Oakland baseball. But the hitting — the actual hitting — belongs to Keane. And what he has done with it, in the limited and careful space that this year's roster gives him, is to refuse the script. He was supposed to be in decline. He was supposed to be playing out the string, showing up for the ceremony of it. Instead he is three for five with a man on, and whatever pitch is coming, he is ready for it.

That is what the three fifty-three means, if you want to know. It does not mean the clock has stopped. It means the man inside the number has not consented to it running.

Dear Editor,

I've been living in Temescal for twenty-three years, and I've watched this neighborhood change more than once. But what happened this cycle — the Health Center finally getting off the drawing board — that's different. That's not change coming from outside developers or money moving in. That's the city finally saying our kids matter. Our health matters.

I remember when my daughter was little and the waiting room at the clinic had maybe five chairs. Now there's an architect on the job, and I see the site crews doing their work. People walk past that corner now and they can actually imagine something there — not just the same empty lot we've been staring at for years.

The operator will be Alameda Health System. That means the neighborhood gets a real anchor, not a temporary fix. I know it sounds like small talk, but for people like me — people who've been here, who've seen promises get broken — this matters. We're not invisible anymore.

— Philly Rodriguez, 29, Casino Manager, Temescal

---

Dear Editor,

I've been on construction sites for twenty-two years. I know what it looks like when a company says "we're building locally" and then hires crews from three counties over. I also know what it looks like when they actually put money into training and bring people in from the neighborhoods where the work is happening.

Baylight's running ahead of schedule — I've got buddies on the piling crews, and the work is real. But the piece this cycle mentioned women-in-trades at eighteen percent instead of the target twenty. That's not a failure yet. That's a signal.

What I'm watching for is whether they keep opening those spots in the next phase, whether they keep training. The first stretch of a project always looks good. The real test is whether they keep the commitment when money gets tight. If they do, this becomes a model. If they don't, we're back to the same story.

— Travis Green, 43, Jack London

---

Dear Editor,

First Friday used to be just another night to me. I'd work my shift at the clinic and go home. But this cycle, something felt different on the streets. I walked up from my apartment and the whole Uptown block had people — artists, people looking at the work, kids, folks I'd never seen out on the block before. There was music. There was light.

I've been a nurse aide for too long to get sentimental about nights out. But what I'm seeing is what happens when people have somewhere to go that feels like it matters. The art walks, the galleries opening, that's not decoration. That's the neighborhood saying "come here." And people are listening.

I hope it keeps. I hope the gallery owners and the artists and whoever's organizing this stuff keeps showing up next cycle, and the cycle after that. Because the thing about a neighborhood is you can't light it up for one night and call it fixed. But one good night is where you start.

— Jose Walker, 64, Uptown

---

Dear Editor,

I drove a school bus in this city for thirty-one years, so I don't get surprised by much. But this team — eighteen straight wins, Taveras leading the league in home runs, Davis still swinging like a kid who just discovered what his hands can do — this is something I didn't expect to see again in Oakland.

I grew up in a different era of this city. We had teams then. We had something people cared about. That got taken away, and I made peace with it. But now my neighbors are stopping me on the street asking how the A's did last night. People who never talk about baseball are asking. The city feels like it has permission to hope.

I know it's just games. But games are how a neighborhood talks to itself. And right now, we're talking like we believe in something. That matters.

— Gloria Hutchins, 68, Retired School Bus Driver, West Oakland
