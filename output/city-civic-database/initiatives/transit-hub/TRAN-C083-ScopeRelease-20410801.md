# PROJECT SCOPE RELEASE — Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II

**CITY OF OAKLAND**
**OFFICE OF CITY PLANNING**
250 Frank H. Ogawa Plaza, Oakland, California 94612

Transit-Oriented Mixed-Use Development
August 2041 | Mid-Cycle Release

---

## I. Purpose of This Release

This document provides the full project scope for the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II development, which is now entering its developer selection phase. Community visioning sessions conducted over the past several months have concluded. The City Planning Commission approved the conceptual plan. The BART Joint Development MOU has been negotiated. The project is ready for the next stage of the approval pathway.

## II. Project Background

Fruitvale Village, the original Phase I development, opened in 2004. It transformed a BART station parking lot into a functioning neighborhood anchor with 47 affordable housing units, 40,000 square feet of retail space, a library branch, health clinic, and senior center. Annual foot traffic exceeds 1.2 million visitors. BART ridership at the Fruitvale station increased 18 percent following completion.

Phase I was intended as a pilot. Nearly four decades later, it remains the only example of successful transit-oriented development at this scale in Oakland. Phase II expands the model to adjacent parcels, building the full mixed-use district that the original development anticipated.

The question this project answers is straightforward: can Oakland build neighborhood-scale, transit-oriented, majority-affordable housing in a working-class community without displacement, without a stadium anchor, and without premium waterfront land?

## III. Development Program

### Housing

Total units: 420

- Affordable units (at or below 60% AMI): 280 units (67%)
- Deed restrictions: minimum 55 years
- Current Fruitvale residents receive priority for 30% of new affordable units

Building distribution:
- East Parcel: 180 units, 6 stories, family-sized priority
- West Parcel: 140 units, 5 stories, senior housing 55+
- Infill sites: 100 units, 3–4 stories, mixed workforce and market rate

### Retail and Commercial

Total commercial space: 85,000 square feet

- Neighborhood retail: 35,000 sq ft
- Restaurants and food service: 22,000 sq ft
- Small business incubator with below-market micro-suites: 15,000 sq ft
- Community-serving office space for nonprofits and social services: 13,000 sq ft

50% of retail leases reserved for Fruitvale-based or Latino-owned businesses.

### Community Facilities

- Expanded health clinic: 8,000 sq ft (partnership with La Clínica de La Raza)
- Childcare center: 80 subsidized slots
- Workforce development center: training rooms and job placement services
- Flexible community event space: 4,000 sq ft

### Public Space

- Central plaza expansion to 1.2 acres (doubling existing plaza)
- Pedestrian-priority paseo network connecting parcels
- 0.8 acres of pocket parks and green space
- $800,000 allocated for commissioned public art

## IV. Financing Structure

Estimated total investment: $230 million (mixed public, private, and nonprofit financing model)

The $22 million City Housing Trust Fund commitment requires council appropriation as part of the final approval vote.

## V. Transit Integration

- Three new pedestrian entries to fare gates
- Relocated AC Transit hub with covered waiting
- 200 secure bike parking spaces with bike share station
- Dedicated ride-share zone
- Car-light parking ratio: 0.5 (210 spaces for 420 units)

Target: 25% increase in Fruitvale BART station ridership by Year 7.

## VI. Workforce and Economic Impact

Phase II construction feeds into the Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline, using the same trade pathways established for larger infrastructure projects currently in the city's development pipeline.

## VII. Approval Pathway and Timeline

The project has completed community visioning sessions and conceptual plan approval. Current phase: Developer RFP and Selection.

The council vote requires a simple majority (5–4). The $22 million Housing Trust Fund commitment is included in the appropriation measure.

## VIII. Infrastructure Context

Fruitvale Phase II adds a second major development node to Oakland's regional infrastructure network. The city's infrastructure capital budget currently stands at $140 million against an identified maintenance need of approximately $200 million. The project's transit-first design and car-light parking ratio are intended to minimize additional load on road infrastructure.

The Baylight District development, which received council approval this week, will draw on the same regional transit and utility systems. Sequencing between the two projects' construction phases will require coordination to avoid resource conflicts and cumulative infrastructure strain.

## IX. Public Comment

The developer RFP process will include a public comment period. Community organizations, residents, and interested parties are encouraged to participate. The Unity Council will host information sessions in Fruitvale in the coming weeks.

Written comments may be submitted to the Office of City Planning.
