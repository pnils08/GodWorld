Heat for the Long Days
Oakland, California — July 2040

The heat sticks to everything this time of year.  The ballpark, the air, the people.  Even the wins feel heavier, stretched out across long afternoons where the fans clap slower, the outfield grass glows too bright, and the city hums like it’s half-asleep but still watching.  Oakland’s been here before — on top, confident, almost bored — and that’s exactly when it has to remember what made it dangerous in the first place.

The scoreboard says easy, but the grind never is.  Henry Rivas and Arturo Ramos keep the rotation calm, clockwork in motion.  Benji Dillon, The Golden Arm, carries himself like a craftsman who knows that brilliance isn’t speed anymore — it’s control.  Darrin Davis, The Ohio Outlaw of Oakland, brings the fire back, sprinting out doubles like the game still owes him something.  The city feels that, feeds off it. The A’s play like a team trying to remember what hunger feels like.  Ellis is still learning how to breathe through pressure, Horn still playing as if every run might decide October.  Mark Aitken and Isley Kelley steady it all — one swing, one glove, one heartbeat at a time.  Even now, when they lead by miles, no one smiles for long.  They know how fast dominance turns to dust when you stop sweating for it.

Out in the parking lot, the night crowd doesn’t leave.  Radios buzz from open trunks, churro smoke twists into the sky, and someone yells that the A’s are too good to be this quiet.  Maybe they’re right.  Maybe the city’s supposed to roar again.  Because Oakland doesn’t wear success well when it’s clean — it likes it scuffed, loud, and earned.

Let the summer drag, let the games blur.  The heat’s the point.  It burns the easy out of everything, leaving only what can stand it.  Out here, that’s how you stay great — by never letting the fire fade, even when you’re winning.
