### Three Portfolios, One Test: Okoro Posts Numbers Before The Checkpoint

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

The contractor showed up Monday morning with a work order in hand and a lease-amendment on his phone. The storefront on 14th — one of eleven commercial units in a corridor where Stabilization Fund corridor activation freed up commercial occupancy upstream — was his first day on a new tenant buildout. He had a six-month timeline and a signed contract. The city, technically, had been arguing about whether the program that helped make that corridor viable was working.

Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro did not wait for the argument to resolve itself.

Okoro carried operational data into the C95 oversight checkpoint that CRC council member Warren Ashford has been calling for since the 191-application queue first registered in C93. The numbers she brought were not projections. Authorization timelines had moved from seven to nine business days before the consolidation to three to four business days after — a reduction of fifty to sixty percent. Throughput had climbed from fifty cases per cycle, with accumulating queue, to ninety-five to ninety-six cases per cycle with no backlog. The 191-application queue that opened C93 had cleared.

First-phase disbursement reached 4.2 million dollars of the 28-million-dollar authorization for the West Oakland corridor activation under INIT-001.

"The consolidation was a management decision, not a policy change," Okoro said. "The results are in the throughput."

The three-portfolio load Okoro carries is not a clean situation. Community Development runs at forty percent of her portfolio. Stabilization Fund at fifty. Economic Development at ten, carried temporarily while Deputy Mayor Marcus Osei continues his recovery. The load came without a formal handoff ceremony — it accumulated, one reassigned responsibility at a time, until Okoro was effectively running three offices out of two.

OEWD Program Director Marcus Webb, presenting from the Stabilization Fund program chair, confirmed the throughput figure: ninety-five to ninety-six cases per cycle on a rolling basis. He also drew a line that has quietly shaped the administration's positioning for next cycle — the Stabilization Fund places households into corridors, but cannot itself drive retail recovery in those corridors.

"These are separable scopes," Webb said. "The fund does what it does. What comes after the household is a different program."

Mayor Avery Santana, speaking after Webb, committed the administration to a second economic initiative this cycle targeting exactly that scope — retail recovery in the corridors the Stabilization Fund reaches. She did not name a dollar figure or a timeline. She named the gap.

District 1 council member Denise Carter, whose district sits inside the program's primary footprint, asked Webb directly about the weekly throughput figure. He confirmed it. Carter did not elaborate on the follow-up she wants.

Ashford's read of the same numbers is structural, not numerical.

The CRC's D7 representative has maintained an audit posture toward the Stabilization Fund since the consolidation. He was present for Okoro's presentation. He did not dispute the throughput figure.

Ashford's objection went to the framework for evaluating it. His position, stated cleanly at the checkpoint: the audit framework continues into C96 regardless of what this checkpoint produces. The metric question — did the throughput improve? — is not the same as the governance question — does the program have adequate accountability architecture for a 28-million-dollar authorization at this pace?

"Ninety-six cases a cycle is a rate," Ashford said. "What we're examining is whether the process that generates that rate is documented, auditable, and defensible to the board."

Okoro did not respond directly to that framing. Her office declined to characterize Ashford's continued audit posture as obstruction.

The divergence is not irresolvable, but it is real. The operational evidence Okoro presented would satisfy any program review built around throughput and timeline metrics. It would not satisfy a review built around process documentation and accountability architecture — and Ashford's review is the second kind.

The checkpoint produced no formal finding. Ashford's audit framework carries into C96. Okoro's throughput numbers are on the record. Webb has defined the scope boundary that Mayor Avery Santana's second economic initiative will have to cross.

The contractor on 14th has a six-month lease buildout ahead of him. The city, for the moment, has two parallel accounts of whether the program that helped make that possible is working. Both accounts are accurate. They are answering different questions.

### OARI Expansion Heads To Council Week Three As Vega Flips Yes On Renewal

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

The motion that will go before the full council on Tuesday is not the motion many people expected. District Two Councilmember Leonard Tran filed a combined renewal-plus-expansion package for the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative — a single vote on whether to extend the pilot and whether to push it citywide, bound together. The structure is deliberate. Tran is betting that the data which persuaded him to sponsor both pieces will hold across the dais.

The data has already moved at least one vote that seemed settled.

Ramon Vega, the Council President, voted no when OARI passed in cycle 82, five to four. He was the swing vote that wasn't. On Tuesday he will be seated on the yes side for renewal, and his position on expansion is conditional-yes — his condition being a Cohort 2 deployment in his District Four, scheduled for rotation 2, weeks ten and eleven of the expanded rollout. Tran's operational plan already sequences D4 into that window. No motion amendment required. The Council President's floor statement, according to advance remarks shared with the Tribune, is expected to be short: thirteen cycles of pilot data cleared the path.

For reporters who have covered this building long enough, a Vega flip is not a headline to file and move on from. He has chaired the Finance Committee through three budget cycles without once changing his vote on a public-safety measure after initial opposition. The OARI numbers gave him something different — a six-minute median response time across the D1/D3/D5 pilot corridor, escalation under two percent, outcomes that held through the program's first full evaluation window. He read the same quarterly reports everyone else received and arrived at a different conclusion than he had before. That is, in this council, relatively rare.

The operational picture beneath the vote has filled in fast in recent weeks. DA Clarissa Dane closed the legal prerequisites — the scene-safety memorandum of understanding signed in week one, evidentiary protocols on schedule for week two. Chief Rafael Montez confirmed that Cohort 1's forty-three personnel are complete. Cohort 2's thirty-eight responders begin training in week two. The program's director, Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, built the expansion plan around those staffing windows. The sequencing gave Vega his ask without the council having to add language.

On the other side of the dais, the math is two. Warren Ashford of District Seven will vote no. Nina Chen of District Eight will vote no on fiscal grounds — her objection centers on cost-per-call, the argument that the program's per-incident expenditure has not been adequately benchmarked against traditional dispatch. It is the first time Chen has spoken on the record as the Civic Reform Coalition's voice on this issue, following a roster correction that settled which members were carrying which positions into the expansion debate. Elliott Crane, District Six, remains out recovering and will be recorded absent.

The projected count: six yes, two no, one absent. Tran as sponsor. D1 Carter, D3 Delgado, D5 Rivers, D9 Mobley, and Vega completing the majority. Ashford and Chen in opposition.

What does not appear in the combined motion is the question of what thirteen cycles of pilot operation actually cost the neighborhoods that ran it first. The D1/D3/D5 corridor absorbed the uncertainty — the responder learning curve, the early dispatch coordination issues, the months before the escalation rate stabilized below two percent. Those districts got the program's growing pains. The expansion schedule delivers the refined version to D2, D4, D9, and D8, in that rotation order, starting at week ten.

That sequencing is not a complaint registered anywhere on the council floor. It may not register at all on Tuesday. But this paper has covered enough city initiatives to know that the districts which bore the first years of a pilot rarely appear in the expansion-passage coverage. They show up two cycles later, when someone asks why the rollout felt different in the neighborhoods that went second.

The combined motion goes to a vote Tuesday. Mayor Avery Santana has not signaled a veto posture.

### The Deadline That Held: HCAI Application Submitted For Temescal Health Center

By Dr. Lila Mezran | Bay Tribune Health Affairs

---

The document landed at the California Department of Health Care Access and Information on a Wednesday. Bobby Chen-Ramirez, the project director for the Temescal Community Health Center, described the submission as procedurally routine — a packet of architectural drawings, programmatic data, site certifications, and operator agreements, assembled and filed. The language of bureaucracy. But for forty-seven Temescal residents who had spent three design meetings watching the building take shape on paper, it was something else: proof that the deadline held.

The Healthcare Facility Information application activates the state's review clock. Six to twelve months, running from this cycle through somewhere between cycles 99 and 101. Chen-Ramirez is clear-eyed about what that means. The HCAI review is now the longest critical-path gate between the project and the day someone walks through the clinic door for the first time. Environmental clearance is complete. Geotechnical work is done. Schematic design closed this cycle at ninety-five percent, with MEP coordination locked. Sarah Huang, the healthcare director at Atlas Bay Architects, has been running the OSHPD seismic-certification track in parallel. The state's reviewer will inherit a project that has done its homework.

Mayor Avery Santana's M1 deadline sequence was three steps: architect contract by cycle 93, site declaration by cycle 94, HCAI application by cycle 95. All three checked. The Temescal Avenue and 47th Street parcel — 2.3 acres — carries a clean Phase I environmental assessment and completed geotech. Parcel acquisition negotiations are targeting closure by cycle 96.

The program locked alongside the schematic: 8,200 net square feet of primary care on the ground floor, a 2,100-square-foot behavioral health integration suite, 1,400 square feet of dental, 800 square feet of pharmacy, and evening clinic hours. Alameda Health System holds the coordinating operator role. The full $45 million budget is authorized under INIT-005.

What the filing documents cannot capture is the texture of those forty-seven residents in a room.

Three community design meetings since cycle 94 brought Temescal neighbors into the process at design development stage — not to ratify a finished plan but to sit with the floor layout, weigh in on the behavioral health suite's placement, ask about parking access for elderly patients arriving by paratransit. The program reflects those conversations. The 2,100 square feet of behavioral health space is integrated, not cordoned off to a separate floor or entrance — a design choice that carries clinical weight. Patients presenting with comorbid physical and behavioral health conditions are better served when they don't have to cross a parking lot between appointments.

What the design meetings have surfaced is the discipline of treating a project on paper as a project on paper. Temescal residents walked through three sessions asking the kinds of questions that come from people who read plans for a living — patient flow, sightlines, the geometry of behavioral health integration, where the elderly paratransit lane meets the entrance. The meetings refined the design. They also kept the timeline honest about what's a drawing and what's a building. The HCAI submission closes one procedural chapter; the foundation pour will close the next. Temescal residents have made it clear they're tracking each one on its own terms.

Patricia Nolan, a home health aide who lives and works in the neighborhood, put it plainly at the third design meeting: she needs to see the foundation poured before she tells her clients the clinic is real. She wasn't hostile. She was precise.

Chen-Ramirez understands that calculation. He has been in this work long enough to know that procedural milestones and community trust run on different clocks. The HCAI submission closes one kind of chapter. The six-to-twelve-month review opens another. The parcel negotiation closes behind it. The groundbreaking announcement follows. Each one earns a fraction of what Nolan is waiting for.

The building at Temescal Avenue and 47th Street does not exist yet. The state clock is running. The deadline held.

### On Stoops Without Coffee: The KONO Inflow Doesn't Move

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

The stoop on 14th and Ashford is one step off the sidewalk and someone's always sitting on it. Tuesday afternoon at 4:17, it's Marcus, 36, who runs the hardware on the corner, and he's not looking at his phone. He's looking at the people who pass.

The foot traffic is steady — the way it gets when nothing else is happening. Not sparse enough to feel lonely, not animated enough to feel like anything's starting. Someone in a yellow vest carrying a clipboard walks by. Marcus doesn't nod. Someone else with a bag from the corner market — Marcus watches but doesn't call out. The usual rhythm would be that he knows half the faces. Today he knows maybe one.

"It's different," he says, still looking. "Not bad different. Just — different."

KONO's been the loudest quiet in the city for three weeks. The council has questions. The neighborhoods have wait-and-see. And the people who live inside the question — they sit on stoops and watch the traffic get smaller.

Marcus moved to 14th six months ago. The street corner where his business sits was already old when he got there, and he liked that about it. Things that have lasted are a kind of promise. He came from Los Angeles, where he spent twelve years, and he said he was coming because Oakland knew how to keep things small. He meant the stores, the blocks, the sense that you could walk and know someone. He has learned that "small" also means you can feel when the weather changes.

The inflow into KONO — the net new foot traffic, the initiatives that haven't launched, the money that was supposed to move and hasn't — doesn't arrive like a headline. It arrives like absence. It arrives like a Tuesday afternoon where your stoop is the same and the people are different and you're waiting for the thing you can't name to either come or settle.

A woman with a stroller passes. A delivery driver stops three doors down. A group of kids with skateboards cuts across toward 15th. All of it is normal — all of it is the city working. Marcus watches anyway, the way you watch a street you're learning.

"I'm not worried," he says, and he sounds like he means it. "I'm just watching."

He sits for five more minutes. The light doesn't change. The people keep moving, the way they always do. And the stoop holds its shape under him — concrete and stairs and the weight of Tuesday evening coming in slow.

By 5 o'clock, it'll be different people. By next cycle, maybe it'll be different still. But right now, on the step where the corner store meets the street, Marcus is waiting to know what different means. The city is quiet enough that he can hear it clearly.

### Quiet Care, Many Doors: The City's Faith Community After The Spike

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

When I walked into New Covenant Pentecostal Assembly on a Saturday morning, Thomas was already there, early. He stood near the folding tables in the fellowship hall, checking the stacks of brown grocery bags — beans, rice, canned tomatoes, a block of cheese. He moved with the kind of quiet efficiency that comes from doing the same work over and over. Thomas, seventy-one, has lived in West Oakland his whole life. He worked at the Port for forty years. He said he came because he could, and because the people need it.

This cycle, while the council was making its three biggest decisions at once — the OARI expansion floor vote, the Health Center audit, the Okoro consolidation — the city's faith communities opened their doors to do the smallest necessary work. Not the work that makes the news, but the work that gets done. The work that happens every Saturday, every Tuesday evening, every holiday, in six different neighborhoods, through six different traditions.

It started with West Oakland. After the third consecutive cycle of crisis-level strain in that district, New Covenant Pentecostal Assembly did what it had done before: it opened. This time for crisis response. The church hadn't been built for this — the fellowship hall is modest, the supplies come from donations and the congregation's own pockets. But on a Saturday morning when the city feels like it's splitting apart, a room with a table and a bag of groceries is something. Thomas folded the bags carefully, the same way. He said he comes every week now, no matter what the council is doing.

I drove to Rockridge on Monday evening for Temple Beth Or's Yom Kippur observance. The temple sits on a quiet corner, and I arrived as people were arriving — a woman in a dark dress, a man helping his elderly father up the steps, a teenager on her phone until the door. The rabbi met them at the entrance. The service was three hours long, and it carried the weight of the liturgy and the weight of this particular year. When it ended, there was a moment of stillness, and then a hum of conversation returned. People filed down the hall to the reception area where tables held coffee, honey cake, and fruit. The sanctuary had been full — sixty or so people, which the rabbi told me is a good high holy day number for a neighborhood temple.

On Tuesday, I was in Montclair for Bharat Mandir of the East Bay's Diwali observance. I could smell the preparation from outside — jasmine, cardamom, something sweet. The community center was decorated with garlands and small clay lamps. The program started at seven, and by then every chair was filled. Children held candles for the procession. There was a hum of devotion in the room, and there was also laughter — an aunt teasing her nephew, someone's phone making a silly noise at the wrong moment. The food came at eight, and the line stretched back to the kitchen. People stood eating, talking across three languages, talking about their week, their work, their children. No one was looking at the council votes. They were here, in this room, making Diwali the way it always goes.

On Thursday morning, I was back in East Oakland with Thomas still in my head. Foothill Baptist Tabernacle runs a seniors lunch program every week — seventy-two years this spring the church has been in the neighborhood. I sat with a woman named Dorothy, eighty-three, who's been coming for three years. She told me she comes for the company as much as the food. The volunteers know everyone's name. They ask how your knees are. They remember your grandson's birthday. Dorothy said the council could vote on ten things in ten minutes and it wouldn't change what happens at a table like this. She meant it as compliment to the food, but I heard it as something larger.

On Friday evening, I closed my notebook at Cathedral of the Living Word. The food pantry runs every Saturday morning — thirty years it's been running, weather or no, council vote or no, through crisis and through calm. The volunteers were prepping for tomorrow morning's distribution. I watched them sort boxes, stack canned goods, arrange produce on tables with the care you'd use if you were setting a table for someone you love. An older man who volunteers — he said his name was Marcus — was unwrapping loaves of bread from the bakery donation. He did it slowly, making sure each loaf was separated, so every family could choose their own. He said he likes the work because it's solid. It doesn't shift. People need bread.

I drove home past Lake Merritt, and the city looked ordinary. Council members were probably on their way to homes. The Okoro audit was closed. The Health Center decision was done. The OARI floor vote was tallied and filed. And in six neighborhoods, six traditions held six different kinds of table — Catholic, Jewish, Hindu, Baptist, Pentecostal, Protestant — and filled them with people who came because it mattered to them. The doors were open. They are always open. This cycle just made me notice.

### The Call That Hasn't Come: Varek, Paulson, And The Front Office Door Left Open

By Jax Caldera | Bay Tribune Opinion

---

I'm at a bar on International Boulevard on a Wednesday afternoon — not the kind of place that gets named in columns, because the owner's a friend and he doesn't need the attention. The A's are on the screen above the tap handles. Mike Paulson is in the dugout in the third inning doing what Paulson does, which is make twelve-year investments look like Tuesday's work.

And I'm thinking about a phone call.

Elias Varek, CEO of Civis Systems, owner of a stake in the Oakland Oaks expansion franchise, stood in front of a tech-expo guest panel in C94 and said — publicly, on the record, into a microphone — "I will absolutely be making that phone call." The call in question: offering Mike Paulson the Oakland Oaks GM seat.

Same cycle. Different building. Paulson, asked directly: "I have not received that call."

Both quotes are real. Both are public. The distance between them is exactly one phone call, and somehow that phone call has not happened.

I'm going to tell you what this is, and what it isn't.

It isn't scandal. Varek hasn't committed a crime by not dialing. Paulson hasn't been wronged by a guy who hasn't called yet. Nobody's stealing anything. This isn't a money story or a process story. People in my line of work get in trouble when they treat silence like crime.

So let's be clear about what the silence actually is.

It's a choice.

Varek runs Civis Systems, the urban-intelligence firm that's embedded so deep in Baylight District infrastructure that the naming-rights contract on the new arena is literally called Civis Systems Field — The Town. He has the Oaks. He has pick number five in the expansion draft. He has the building and the city and the political goodwill of a franchise Oakland actually wants. He has exactly one conspicuous gap in the operation: a basketball general manager.

And he made a public commitment to fill it with a specific human being.

Paulson, for his part, left the door open so wide you could drive a truck through it. He said of Varek: "What a great man Elias is, and what a wonderful thing it is that Oakland is where he chose for his legacy." You know what that is? That's a man telling you he'd pick up the phone. That's not exactly a slam door. That's a man arranging himself in the direction of yes.

Mark Aitken — first baseman, player rep, the guy who shows up to civic hearings because he actually cares about this city — Aitken was at the Fruitvale Transit Hub vote. Aitken shows up. That's his thing. You know who didn't show up to the phone call?

We have Paulson's whole context on the record. He ran the A's through the Baylight build. He did one year with the Bulls — and before you reach for that story I'll cut you off: Paulson said the system translates, that it can be somewhat autonomous. He reframed the Bulls year himself. That's his. I'm not going to rewrite a man's own account of his own career back over him.

What I'm saying is: nothing in Paulson's record reads as a closing door.

So what's open?

The Oaks have an expansion draft pick at number five. The arena has a name. The district has a parade-route motif from Paulson's own interview, which means someone was already writing that future into the story. The public commitment was made. The public answer was left open. The phone call did not happen.

At some point — and I don't know when that point is, nobody will tell me, because nobody's picking up my calls either — the open door becomes a statement on its own. Either Varek is still planning to call and the timeline is just what it is. Or something shifted and nobody wants to say so in public. Or the public commitment was the kind of thing people say at tech-expo panels.

I'm not saying it's option three. I'm saying I'd like to know which one it is.

The call hasn't come. The door hasn't closed. The season's moving. The expansion draft isn't waiting on anybody's phone habits.

Does Elias Varek still intend to make that call?

### Thirty-Two And Six: The Cy Young Race In Oakland Tightens

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The Oakland Athletics are thirty-two and six, owners of a ten-game winning streak, and have now done something that requires a specific kind of accounting to fully appreciate: they are making the Cy Young Award conversation feel provincial.

Not in the dismissive sense. In the sense that the rotation's two best arguments — Arturo Ramos out of Laurel and Travis Coles out of Downtown — are running such divergent but equally compelling lines that national voters will eventually have to choose between them, and Oakland will have rendered that choice genuinely difficult.

Start with the numbers because the numbers are where this story lives. Coles is twenty-one years old and carrying a two-point-oh-five earned run average through a staff-leading workload. He is the anchor in terms of innings, a pitcher who has absorbed the rotation's scheduling demands with the methodical control of someone who has been doing this far longer than his years suggest. His approach is vertical — ground balls, early contact, deep counts only when the situation earns them. The ERA is not a fluke; the component numbers underneath support it.

Ramos's line reads differently and perhaps more impressively in terms of peak. The right-hander from Laurel is not simply pitching well — he is pitching at a level that has warranted the phrase "masterpiece" in more than one press box conversation this week. His ERA trails Coles marginally in the rankings but his strikeout rate and the texture of his outings suggest a pitcher who is moving through lineups rather than surviving them. These are two different pitching philosophies producing very similar results, which is exactly the kind of problem Deacon Seymour wants to have in his first year managing this franchise.

The rotation survived the Henry Rivas injury without an appreciable drop in quality. Rivas, twenty-eight, remains on the mend, and in his absence Carmen Mesa has provided the kind of fill-in that complicates roster decisions. Mesa went five innings in a no-decision against Boston without allowing the Red Sox to break the game open, and Danny Horn's four hits plus the go-ahead RBI sealed the series win. Mesa is twenty-two, three wins and one loss on the year, two-fifty-one ERA — numbers that, had they come from a projected rotation piece, would read as a quiet success. Coming from a depth call-up, they read as a problem Seymour will need to solve without a clean answer: the rotation spot Mesa was filling is Rivas's when Rivas is healthy, and Rivas will be healthy.

The Angels series that preceded Boston was its own statement. The A's took three games, the third a sixteen-to-four demolition in which Benji Dillon worked five and two-thirds innings for his third win of the season and Ernesto Quintero produced two home runs and five runs batted in. Quintero, twenty-three, from West Oakland, is now sitting third in the majors at three-forty-six with twelve home runs, twenty-seven RBI, and a seven-hundred slugging percentage. The Rookie of the Year conversation belongs to him in terms of production; whether the award follows depends on which national voters are watching which markets.

The player who commands the most immediate attention, however, is Isley Kelley, whose batting-average lead at three-sixty-nine with nine home runs and twenty-four RBI makes him a top-three MVP candidate at a premium defensive position. Kelley at thirty-seven, in what this franchise understands to be his final year in Oakland, is not simply holding his statistical line — he is extending it. That is the kind of thing that defies projection models and rewards the people in the ballpark who simply watched.

The Cy Young race is the pitching story of this cycle, but the broader architecture is what Seymour has built in fewer than forty games: a team that can score sixteen when Dillon is working an abbreviated start, hold a lead when Mesa takes the ball on short rest, and win a series against the AL East leaders behind a combined effort rather than any single performance. The franchise went through its dynasty transition — losing Vinnie Keane's production profile at DH, restructuring around younger contributors — and the structure held.

Coles and Ramos are the two most visible exhibits of that holding. Which one finishes the season as the A's definitive ace depends on what the next eighty-five games produce. The race between them is, for now, the most interesting pitching competition in the American League, and it is running entirely within a single building in Oakland.

### The Door Won't Close: Martin Richards At Third In A Contract Year

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

I owe Martin Richards a correction, and I'm going to start there.

Last cycle I filed a column called "The Let-Walks Coming." I named names. Richards was in that piece, and the implication was clear enough — that the front office had already moved on in its head, that the conversation was finished before it started. Mike Paulson walked that back on the record. His words: *"I took a bit of issue with the reporting that it's been decided. As I'd think I'd know first, as the person deciding."*

Point taken. The column was wrong about the posture, and I'm saying so now, above the fold, not buried in paragraph twelve.

But here's what the correction doesn't fix: Martin Richards still showed up to this season knowing the math. He can read a roster. He knows Kevin Clark is on the forty-man. He knows Buford Park got called up. He knows Ernesto Quintero can slide to third when the front office needs to solve three positions with two bodies. Richards knows all of this because he's not stupid, and you don't play six-point-eight WAR baseball while being stupid.

That's what a contract year actually looks like from the inside. Not a player playing for the money. A player playing with the complete clarity that the organization is stress-testing him and stress-testing his replacement at the same time, and the decision won't come until after he's already lived inside that pressure for six months.

I want you to think about what that takes.

You show up Tuesday, you take your warm-up grounders, and the guy who might be taking your spot is two positions over doing the same thing. You flip the ball to first, clean hands, a thousand times. You hit your spots. You don't flinch. You run out every ball because you know somebody is always watching, and in a contract year the watching is different — it has stakes attached. The whole season is a tryout dressed up like a job you already have.

Richards is 29 and from Fruitvale. He's been in this organization his whole career. He's not an import. He's not a project. He's already the answer to the question — he proved that this year. The fielding percentage doesn't lie. Six-point-eight WAR at $750,000 is the kind of number that makes front offices look bad for even entertaining the alternatives, and Paulson knows that too.

But the alternatives exist. That's the actual situation.

I've said it before and I'll say it again: you can find a Martin Richards. I don't mean that as an insult — I mean it as a description of how franchise architecture thinks. Mark Aitken is locked through 2043 because you cannot replicate what Aitken does. The leadership, the clubhouse presence, the three Gold Gloves, the way he's become the civic face of this organization without being asked to — that's not fungible. Aitken was always going to get the long-term deal. That decision was never really a decision.

Richards is different. Not lesser — different. The front office believes, on some level, that his production profile exists somewhere in the system. Clark at .333 in his first thirty at-bats. Park already earning his spot. Quintero covering the corner when the lineup needs to breathe a different direction. The belief in organizational depth is what keeps the door from fully closing. It's also what keeps it from opening all the way.

And Martin Richards is living in that doorway right now. Every ground ball. Every at-bat. Every day of the season.

I don't know how this ends. Paulson told me the decision isn't made, and I'm taking him at his word this time. The season isn't over. The book isn't written. But I know what it costs a man to stand inside a contract year on a dynasty roster, with your body as the evidence and your career as the argument, and I don't think we've said enough about that.

Richards hasn't complained. He hasn't looked over his shoulder visibly. He's just been at third, doing the work, 1.000 in the field.

He deserves the column. He's been getting the grounders instead.
