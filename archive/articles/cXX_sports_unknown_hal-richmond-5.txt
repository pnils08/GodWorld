Hal Richmond
Part VI — Danny Horn and the Modern Center Field Crown

Center field in Oakland has always belonged to players who made the game feel thinner, as if the ballpark shrank when they started running. Coco Crisp did it with anticipation. Ramon Laureano did it with fearlessness. Darrin Davis did it with power bleeding into the gaps. But none of them ever blended every version of the position the way Danny Horn has.

It did not happen immediately. Horn’s early career in Minnesota was a collection of possibilities — 24 home runs in 2035, 31 in 2037, flashes of speed, flashes of arm, flashes of glove, none of it fully aligned. By the time he arrived in Oakland, people knew what he could be, but nobody yet knew if he could hold the pieces together long enough to matter.

Then came the stretch that defined him.

2037 was the first sign of the structure underneath the noise:
159 games, 36 doubles, 8 triples, 31 home runs, 83 RBI, 28 stolen bases.
The average wasn’t there yet (.246), but the shape was unmistakable. Power plus speed at that level in center field is not a fluke. It’s a blueprint.

2038 added discipline.
2039 delivered the masterpiece.

One hundred fifty-four games.
One hundred ninety-eight hits.
Forty-four doubles.
Thirteen triples.
Forty-three home runs.
One hundred three RBI.
A .322 average.
A .598 slugging percentage.
And an All-Star season that felt less like a peak and more like a revelation.

There have been great center fielders in Oakland, but never one with this blend of violence and precision. Crisp disrupted momentum. Laureano erased extra bases. Davis brought middle-order fear. Horn brings all three, wrapped in the statistical profile of a player who looks like he was engineered in a hitting lab and raised in a track lane.

He doesn’t steal bases recklessly — he steals them with calculation.
He doesn’t hit home runs in streaks — he hits them as a normal part of his calendar.
He doesn’t chase highlight-reel routes — he takes the direct one, the perfect one, the one that looks boring because it prevents the drama in the first place.

The 2039 season is the kind that becomes a franchise compass point.
Forty-three home runs from a center fielder is rare.
Thirteen triples from a power hitter is even rarer.
Doing both in the same year, while hitting .322, while anchoring the most important defensive position on the field, is the kind of anomaly that forces history to redraw its comparisons.

Oakland’s center field lineage has always reflected the city around it — fast, loud, unpredictable. Horn brings something new: inevitability. You feel it in innings, in series, in months where pitchers keep trying to solve him and walk off the field wondering why they’re still failing.

He isn’t the next Crisp, or Laureano, or Davis.
He is the first Horn.
And for the first time in decades, center field in Oakland belongs to a player who makes it feel like a position of dominion, not reaction.
