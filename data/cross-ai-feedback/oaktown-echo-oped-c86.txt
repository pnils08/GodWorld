Cycle 86 | August 2041
PHASE II GOT THE VOTE — FRUITVALE GOT THE SAME OLD PROMISES
Kwame Ellis
Calvin Turner has driven East 12th past Fruitvale BART for eleven years. He knows the choke point at International, the way cyclists thread between trucks like they're dodging fate, the corner where people still stand on concrete that hasn't moved since the 80s. He fixes machines. He knows what happens when infrastructure is built for yesterday and forced to carry tomorrow.
"Phase I was supposed to fix it," Turner said outside his shop this week. "Phase II better actually do it."
Last week the council voted to authorize Phase II — $230 million, the biggest infrastructure check Fruitvale has ever seen. OPP pushed hard, Ramon Vega said yes, and the planning bureau will drop a design RFP next month. Community sessions start next spring. Three scenarios on the table, all supposedly locked to six "non-negotiables":
  20% commercial space reserved for existing businesses at old rents for 25 years
  35% minimum affordable housing across 420 units (67% overall target)
  $15 million business stabilization fund
  30% construction jobs for local 18-30-year-olds
  15,000-square-foot community plaza
  Binding community benefits agreement signed before any permit
That list reads like equity on paper.
Paper is where Phase I equity went to die.
Phase I promised local hiring. Promised a stabilization fund. Promised affordability that lasted. The fund came in short. The hiring numbers were never verified. The affordable units had no long-term lock. Turner was a teenager then. He watched the promises arrive in print and then quietly vanish in practice. He's not the only one who remembers.
Rose Delgado (D3) sat through both visioning sessions. She told the council: "This neighborhood has been patient. Phase I was promised. Phase II needs to deliver."
Eighty-nine residents showed up — 73 from the neighborhood. Not one called for cancellation. That's not apathy. That's trust on its last legs.
Planning Lead Elena Soria Dominguez (who grew up three blocks away) said the input was "unusually clear": build it, but build it with us, not on top of us. The six commitments are supposed to be non-negotiable. The community benefits agreement is supposed to be binding — signed before any permit is issued.
"Supposed to be" is doing a lot of heavy lifting.
The council did not vote to break ground. It voted to authorize the budget, lock in the visioning framework as "binding criteria," and kick off design. Final scenario selection is years away. Construction permits are even farther. Between now and then, developers will lobby. Budgets will tighten. "Non-negotiable" will quietly become "negotiable." Just like Phase I.
Soria Dominguez says if a developer can't meet the requirements, the project goes back to design. That sounds like accountability. It also sounds like delay, cost overruns, and another round of community meetings where the same people show up and say the same things — and the same promises get made again.
Fruitvale isn't asking for miracles. It's asking for follow-through. It's asking for the city to stop treating "community input" like a checkbox and start treating it like a contract with teeth.
Calvin Turner is still driving East 12th. He's still waiting for the fix Phase I promised. He's still watching buses stack up and pedestrians wait on the same old corner. He's still wondering if Phase II will be different — or if it will just be Phase I with better branding and a bigger budget.
The council authorized the project.
Fruitvale is still waiting for the city to authorize itself.
— Kwame Ellis
