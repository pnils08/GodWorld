# What the GM Said in This Room

*The Oakland A's table has been reset. Paulson put it on the record. Here is what he said.*

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

---

I keep a notebook when I conduct interviews. I have kept one for forty years of covering baseball in this city, the same black paper-bound kind they sold at the drugstore on Broadway before the drugstore became a coffee place and the coffee place became a tech office and the tech office became whatever it is now. The notebook is mostly habit. The pages fill and then sit. What I actually use is what I can still hear in my head on the walk back to the Tribune from wherever the interview took place.

On the walk back from the Tribune offices this cycle — same building, different room, Paulson across a table with cold coffee — I was hearing the phrase *hopefully after a parade.*

That is a sentence with weight. It is a sentence that does not get said in these rooms by careful men unless they mean it. The last time a general manager of the Oakland Athletics said something close to it in my presence was the winter before the two thousand thirty-five season. I am going to tell you what Mike Paulson said this cycle that made me write those words in the notebook I do not open during interviews. I am going to tell you what it means for the franchise this city has been carrying in its chest for the last several years. And I am going to tell you, plainly, that the table has been reset.

---

The feature on Isley Kelley aired this cycle. You may have seen it. The producers — working for eight months in the basement of the stadium, pulling footage from boxes the franchise had not opened since the championship runs — assembled something I would not have trusted myself to write. The thirty-two MVP acceptance. The October of thirty-four, when he carried a lineup that had no business being in October. The thirty-five curtain call, the crowd standing because they needed one more look. Teammates trying not to cry on camera. The feature aired as a tribute. A farewell document. The same cycle Kelley went seven for nine against Baltimore.

You cannot plan that. You can only watch it and try to understand what it means.

I went into that interview believing I already understood. Three cycles of reading the front office. The money committed forward — Coles, Reyna, the next thing. The quiet around the veterans whose contracts are expiring. When a franchise builds its television tribute while the man is still playing, you read the posture. Slayer read it. I read it. We wrote from the reading.

Paulson reset the table.

---

Here is what he put on the record, in this room, this cycle.

It is true, he said, that the franchise is running a farewell campaign for Vinnie Keane. Keane is set to retire. That is confirmed. That was already in print. What had not been in print — what I want to make sure lands here — is the architecture underneath it.

*Benji Dillon came back on a one-year deal to make one last run with Vinnie.*

That sentence has not appeared in this paper before this piece. Read it again. Dillon is back not as an afterthought, not as a rotation anchor who happens to be aging in the same direction as his teammates, but on a deliberate one-year deal — a particular decision, made in particular knowledge — to ride out a season alongside the designated hitter who has been his teammate since they were young enough that the championships they would win together were not yet imaginable. Two men making a specific choice about how they leave a clubhouse. That is not roster construction. That is something else. I asked Paulson about it in those terms and he did not correct me.

Dillon is left-handed. He has been a starting pitcher for this franchise through five Cy Young awards, through three thousand two hundred and nineteen strikeouts, through the full arc of a career that will be argued about by Oakland baseball people for decades after he is done. He came back on a one-year deal to make one last run with Vinnie. That is now on the record in the Bay Tribune.

---

For everyone else — Benji Dillon going forward, Mark Aitken, Martin Richards, Isley Kelley — Paulson said the same thing, with precision: no statements, no contract talks during the season. Preliminary discussions took place. Both sides agreed to wait. The door is not closed. The door is not open.

I had been reading the posture as something close to settled. Slayer had been reading it the same way. We were reading what was there to read — money committed forward, youth pushed up, a television production crew given eight months of access without anyone asking them to slow down. Shape, Paulson said, is real. But shape is not signature. And the man who signs the papers said in this room that they are not yet signed.

He was direct about that. He said: *I took a bit of issue with the reporting that it's been decided. As I'd think I'd know first, as the person deciding.*

He is right. And this piece is my response to that.

---

I want to stay for a moment with what the reset means for the men being reset about.

Mark Aitken has been the Player Representative and Community Liaison for this franchise while also playing first base at a level that has earned him three Gold Gloves and a career average that would have guaranteed him a different kind of press coverage in a different kind of era. He sits in city meetings. He has been doing the civic work alongside the baseball work for long enough that the two have become indistinguishable parts of the same identity. His contract runs through two thousand forty-three. The door, per Paulson, is not open and not closed.

Isley Kelley let a film crew into the basement footage room for eight months. That is not the act of a man who is indifferent to what this franchise means. His shortstop seasons — the three MVP awards, the four hundred and forty-seven home runs, the two thousand three hundred and ninety-eight hits — sit in the archive the way a long poem sits in a library. They have been there long enough that people forget they are still being written.

Martin Richards has been standing at third base for six and a half years without asking anyone to write about him. Slayer wrote about him this cycle, beautifully and with appropriate guilt, and was the first Tribune byline to do it with any particular weight. Richards arrived, played, and let the franchise build its narrative elsewhere. His WAR in the most recent completed season was the kind of number that gets passed around by the analysts Anthony talks to. He has never popped up in a moment that made this city want to throw something. He has just been there. The door is not open, and the door is not closed, and Martin Richards will continue to stand at third base and wait for it the way he has stood at third base and waited for everything — with a patience that looks, from the press box, indistinguishable from belief.

---

And then there is the other piece of this cycle's record.

At a tech-expo panel, Elias Varek — the Civis Systems founder whose naming rights now grace the franchise's new ballpark — was asked, in front of a room, whether his ownership group would recruit Paulson to run the Oaks. He answered in five words: *I will absolutely be making that phone call.* Velez ran it on the front page. I watched the clip in the same week.

I asked Paulson about it, in this room, directly. His answer:

*I have not received that call.*

That is the full statement. The call has not come. When I asked him to sit with the question — whether, when it does come, he is the man who answers it or the man who sends it to after the season the same way he just told the veterans to wait — he talked about the Bulls, about what his partial attention cost and what it proved. He ran a franchise in another city last season in his first attempt at it, he said, and it showed the system translates. The system can be somewhat autonomous. The A's are the best team in baseball even with his time split. But an expansion franchise, he said, is a different animal.

He did not say whether he would answer the call. That question is open. I am printing it open because that is how it sits.

---

Near the end of the interview, I asked him the last thing I had come in to ask. It is the question I have been living with since I covered the two thousand thirty-five parade and stood on Broadway watching confetti stick to the streetcar rails and did not yet know it was the last one.

I asked about the culture. I asked whether the system translates and whether the *culture* translates — whether you can give someone Civis Systems' data and a stadium and a brand and still give them the thing that made Vinnie Keane open an academy on Mandela Parkway. The thing that made Mark Aitken go to city meetings. The thing that made Kelley walk into the basement footage room. The thing that made Martin Richards show up at third base, without ceremony, every morning, for six and a half years.

Paulson said: *The system travels and the culture adapts.*

Then he said: *Vinnie Keane is a one of one.*

Over the years with the A's, he said, the core that comes to mind is mainly positional — really only Dillon is the pitcher. On one hand, they settled nicely on position players. Pitching they had to adapt. As you adapt, he said, you lock on to the culture core.

I wrote that down. I have been sitting with it since.

---

Before I asked him the culture question, I let something sit in the room between us. He had said — clearly, on the record, in a sentence I told him I would take to print — that preliminary discussions on contracts took place, that both sides had decided to wait until after the season, and that they would be ready, *hopefully after a parade.*

I told him that was a sentence with weight. I told him it had been a long time since a GM said that to me in this room. The last parade was two thousand thirty-five. The men we were talking about — Vinnie, Isley, Mark, Benji, Martin — were the ones who walked the floats. Whatever else the conversation was about, he had just put on the record that the goal of this season is to give them one more.

I meant what I said. I am taking it to print.

The parade route, as Paulson described it in the final minute of the interview, runs through the new paved streets of the Baylight District. Through Civis Field. Through The Town.

I have been covering Oakland baseball since the nineteen-seventies. I have walked behind more victory parades than any writer at this newspaper, and before me, and after me, probably. I know what those streets look like with confetti on them. I know what the city sounds like when it is deciding it is allowed to be happy.

When I ask myself whether the culture translates — whether you can hand someone everything Paulson and these men have built and preserve the part that cannot be handed — I keep coming back to the academy on Mandela Parkway. Vinnie opened it. It will outlive his playing career. It is already outliving this season. That is not a system. That is a man who decided the place he was given should be better than he found it, and then made it so.

That is what will be walking next to Reyna and Coles and Mesa on the day the floats roll.

The door is not closed.

---

# I Read It Wrong

*Paulson walked into the Tribune office and said the thing out loud. I owe you the correction.*

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

---

I was at the bar when the transcript came across. DeShawn — DJ Hartley, the Tribune's photographer, guy who's been drinking in the same corner of Jack London Square since before I started doing this — he slid his phone across the table without saying anything. That's how you know something is actually news. You don't announce it. You slide it across.

I read it twice. Then I sat with my beer going warm and thought about the column I put in the C94 paper.

"The let-walks are coming." I wrote that. I wrote that Isley Kelley and Martin Richards are almost certainly playing their last season in green and gold. I wrote that you could read the decision in how the front office was operating — the youth push, the money committed forward, the quiet. I wrote "quiet" like quiet means the same thing as done.

Mike Paulson walked into the Tribune offices this cycle and told Hal Richmond something different. And Hal — who is writing his own piece in this same supplemental, the full reset — gave me the line I needed to hear.

"I took a bit of issue with the reporting that it's been decided. As I'd think I'd know first, as the person deciding."

That's not pointed at the press. That's pointed at me. I'm the one who wrote it's been decided.

---

Here's what I got right: the posture was real. Nobody is disputing the posture. Paulson said it himself in that room — the youth is there, the depth is real, the money is going forward to Coles and Reyna and the next thing. I can read a front-office posture and I read the shape of this one correctly. Quiet farewell. Youth campaign. Let the veterans walk with grace.

But shape is not signature. And Mike Paulson said he has not signed anything, and that the only confirmed departure is Vinnie Keane.

Vinnie is set to retire. That's the only sentence in this conversation that is finished. Everything else — Isley, Mark, Martin — the door isn't closed. The door isn't open. That is not the room I was writing from in S2.

I owe you that. Not "the press" owed you that. I did. We did. This column did.

---

There's something else in the transcript I want to put in front of you because nobody's printed it yet.

Benji Dillon came back on a one-year deal to make one last run with Vinnie.

Read that again. That's not a roster move. That's not asset management. That's a left-handed pitcher who has won five Cy Youngs and thrown three thousand two hundred and nineteen strikeouts making a deliberate decision — a conscious, knowing decision — to spend one more year in the same clubhouse as a man who is retiring. Hal called it "two pitchers and a designated hitter making a particular decision together about how they leave." I don't have a better way to put it than that.

I spent my whole S2 column writing about Martin Richards and what we owe him and what it costs to lose him quietly. I didn't say a word about the other side of the room — the guys who already made their choice about how this ends.

---

So let me ask the questions I'm allowed to ask in a fan column, because I don't get a seat in that Tribune office and Hal is the one who earns the answers. I just get to write what it feels like out here in the city, reading along.

What do I tell the kid who spent eight months going to games thinking every Isley Kelley at-bat was a goodbye? The one who watched seven for nine against Baltimore through the lens of farewell — not because we lied to him, but because we handed him the frame and said "this is how you watch this season"?

If Dillon came back on a one-year deal to ride with Vinnie, who's coming back for the next guy in that clubhouse? What's the door — closed, open, or something in between that we don't have a word for yet?

And Martin Richards — if the door isn't closed, if the let-walks haven't been let — who writes the piece we still haven't written? Who says it out loud while he's standing right there, not after the number runs out?

Those are fan questions. Paulson doesn't owe me answers in print. The point is the city is asking them, and the city was asking them partly because of what I wrote, and I wrote it from a posture instead of from the record.

---

Here's the thing about "hopefully after a parade."

Paulson said it in that room. Hal was sitting across the table, cold coffee, notebook he didn't open, and the GM of the Oakland A's said on the record that the plan is to wait until after the season and be ready, hopefully after a parade.

The last parade was two thousand thirty-five. Vinnie was on those floats. Isley was on those floats. Mark Aitken, Martin Richards, Benji Dillon — they walked the route. This franchise grew up around those men and those men grew up around this franchise and for three cycles now the fan section has been writing in a minor key about how it ends.

Paulson said it might end differently. Might end with them on floats again.

I don't know how to tell you what that does to a section that has been writing goodbyes. I've got Hal for the history version — he'll do it the way he does everything, spelling out years, connecting the present guys to the ghosts. I just know what it does at the bar. DeShawn slid that phone across without saying anything because he knew.

You don't announce it. You let it sit there.

---

I read the posture wrong. Paulson told us. Hal put it in the room and I'm putting it in this column. The door isn't closed, isn't open. Preliminary discussions, waiting until after the season, hopefully after a parade. That's the record.

We trust the GM on the record more than we trust ourselves reading the silence.

The let-walks might not be coming yet.

---

*P Slayer has been covering the A's since before most of the current roster was born. He lives in his feelings about it.*
