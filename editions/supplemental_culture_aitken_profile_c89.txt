THE CYCLE PULSE 89 — SUPPLEMENTAL —  CULTURE & COMMUNITY
The Equation After Baseball
By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune

OAKLAND — Mark Aitken showed up to a City Council meeting last spring wearing a gray hoodie and sitting in the third row. No staff. No announcement. Just a thirty-three-year-old first baseman with a mechanical engineering degree, a notepad, and a question about the Baylight workforce agreement.

He stayed for three hours.

“I wanted to understand the sub-tier language,” Aitken said when I asked him about it later. “The forty percent local hire target — what does ‘local’ actually mean? Who enforces it? What happens when the second subcontractor hires from Livermore instead of Oakland?”

These are not typical questions from a man who hit .289 last season with 22 home runs. They are the questions of someone who has been paying attention.

-----

THE MEETINGS

Aitken has attended more than four council meetings since the Baylight bond vote in June. He doesn’t sit in the front. He doesn’t speak during public comment. He takes notes in the same kind of notebook he used to chart his swing mechanics in Double-A.

“I’m not trying to make a statement,” he said. “I’m trying to learn. This city is building something that’s going to outlast my career. I want to understand how it works.”

He’s not the only A’s player engaged with Oakland beyond the diamond. Vinnie Keane opened a youth baseball academy on Mandela Parkway three weeks ago. Benji Dillon has funded scholarships through Oakland Unified for a decade. But Aitken’s involvement is different in kind — less philanthropy, more process.

“Vinnie builds programs,” Aitken said. “Benji writes checks. I read zoning documents. We all do what makes sense to us.”

-----

THE UPBRINGING

Understanding Aitken requires knowing where he came from.

San Luis Obispo. Son of Mayor Richard Aitken. Mother Carol Kent-Aitken, heir to the Chuck E. Cheese fortune. Uncle Andre Agassi, the tennis legend. The boy won the National Spelling Bee in eighth grade and nearly joined a British boy band called Nova Sound before baseball claimed him.

He studied mechanical engineering at Cal Poly. His scouting report read: “Leadership bet with a Gold Glove floor.” Coaches in Double-A called him “The Engineer” because he stayed after games to break down his swing frame by frame, his locker more lab bench than clubhouse stall.

When Oakland drafted him in the fourth round in 2026, they weren’t buying power. They were buying precision. The power came later — 195 career home runs, two Silver Sluggers, Rookie of the Year in 2032, Playoff MVP in 2037. But the foundation was always the same: discipline, data, and an engineer’s belief that problems can be solved if you study them long enough.

“Baseball’s like spelling,” he told a campus reporter once. “You study long enough, you stop guessing.”

-----

THE LITTLE LEAGUE

Aitken’s youth program enters its sixth year this fall. It operates out of two Oakland parks — Bushrod in North Oakland and Concordia in Fruitvale — and serves about 140 kids annually. He funds it personally, coaches when the season allows, and shows up to registration days in the same gray hoodie he wore to City Council.

“The program isn’t complicated,” he said. “Kids need reps. They need adults who show up. They need somebody to tell them the work matters.”

Howard Young is 71 years old and has volunteered with the program since its second year. He watched Aitken spend forty-five minutes last spring working with a nine-year-old who couldn’t make contact.

“The kid kept swinging and missing,” Young said. “Mark didn’t get frustrated. He just kept adjusting — grip, stance, timing. By the end, the kid was hitting line drives. Mark told him, ‘You didn’t get better because I’m a coach. You got better because you didn’t quit.’”

Young paused.

“That’s not a baseball lesson. That’s a life lesson. And he teaches it like he means it.”

-----

THE QUESTION

Aitken is 33 years old. He is not on a farewell tour like Keane, Dillon, and Kelley. His contract runs through 2043. He could play another five years if his body holds.

But he’s already thinking about what comes after.

“I grew up watching my father run a city,” Aitken said. “I saw what it took — the hours, the compromises, the days when nobody’s happy with you. And I saw what it built. San Luis Obispo is a better place because he served.”

He didn’t say he was running for anything. He didn’t announce a campaign. But when I asked him directly — “Do you want to hold office someday?” — he didn’t deflect.

“I want to be useful,” he said. “Right now, I’m useful on a baseball field. Someday I won’t be. When that day comes, I want to have done the homework. I want to understand the systems well enough to help fix them.”

He gestured toward the window, toward the waterfront where foundation piling begins October 10.

“Baylight is going to change this city. The stadium, the housing, the jobs — it’s all connected. And the people who understand how it’s connected are the ones who get to shape what it becomes.”

-----

THE IRON CORNER

His teammates call him “The Iron Corner.” The nickname came from his years at first base — three Gold Gloves, a reaction time that scouts called “near mechanical” — but it describes more than his glove.

Aitken is the player teammates look to when things fray. Not loud like Keane. Not poetic like Dillon. Not transcendent like Kelley. Just steady. Consistent. The equation that holds everything together.

“Baseball isn’t a game,” he said once. “It’s a study in staying human.”

For a man who could have been a rock star, a spelling champion, or a mayor like his father, Mark Aitken chose something harder — a life spent chasing perfect knowing he’d never reach it.

The question now is what happens when he stops chasing. And whether Oakland is ready for what he’s already studying.
