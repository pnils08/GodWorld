**Baylight District Project**

Cycle 81 – Preliminary Circulation Draft

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The proposed Baylight District Project involves redevelopment of approximately 65 acres of underutilized waterfront land into a mixed-use district anchored by a 35,000-seat open-air stadium, residential towers, retail and commercial space, marina expansion, and public open space.

This Draft Environmental Impact Report (DEIR) evaluates potential environmental effects associated with construction and operation of the project. Key impact categories include:

- Air Quality
- Greenhouse Gas Emissions
- Traffic & Transportation
- Noise & Vibration
- Biological Resources
- Hydrology & Water Quality
- Sea-Level Rise Vulnerability
- Cultural & Historic Resources
- Socioeconomic Displacement

The analysis identifies several significant impacts requiring mitigation, particularly related to traffic congestion during event days, shoreline resilience under sea-level rise scenarios, and construction-phase emissions.

---

## 2. PROJECT LOCATION & EXISTING CONDITIONS

The project site consists of former harbor and industrial parcels along Oakland's central waterfront. Current conditions include:

- Paved industrial yards
- Underutilized warehouse structures
- Limited shoreline access
- Disturbed soils with known contamination zones
- Existing marine habitat along bulkhead edges

The shoreline currently lacks continuous public access and is characterized by hardened infrastructure.

---

## 3. AIR QUALITY & GREENHOUSE GAS ANALYSIS

### Construction Phase

Estimated duration: 30 months
Peak workforce: ~8,500 jobs

Projected emissions include:

- Diesel particulate matter from heavy equipment
- Fugitive dust
- Construction-related GHG output

Mitigation Measures:

- Tier 4 Final construction equipment requirement
- Electrified staging zones where feasible
- Mandatory dust suppression protocols
- On-site emissions monitoring during peak grading

### Operational Phase

Primary emission sources:

- Event-day vehicle trips
- Commercial energy demand
- Marine vessel activity

Mitigation Measures:

- Integrated microgrid with minimum 40% renewable generation
- Rooftop solar across residential towers
- Transit-first event access strategy
- Ferry service expansion

Net GHG output projected to remain below regional threshold significance if mitigation measures are implemented.

---

## 4. TRAFFIC & TRANSPORTATION

### Event Day Conditions

Stadium capacity: 35,000
Projected peak vehicle trips: 12,000–15,000 per event

Identified Impacts:

- Increased congestion at I-880 interchanges
- Queue spillback onto arterial roads
- Increased transit demand at Lake Merritt BART

Mitigation Measures:

- Dedicated pedestrian bridge to BART
- Ferry terminal with expanded routes
- AC Transit priority lanes
- 600 secure bike spaces
- Dynamic traffic signal coordination

Residual Impact: Significant but mitigable with aggressive transit adoption targets.

---

## 5. NOISE & VIBRATION

Construction:

- Pile driving and grading expected to exceed 85 dBA at perimeter
- Temporary vibration risk to adjacent parcels

Operation:

- Stadium events may exceed evening noise thresholds

Mitigation Measures:

- Restricted pile-driving hours
- Sound attenuation panels
- Curfew for amplified concerts
- Real-time noise monitoring during major events

---

## 6. BIOLOGICAL RESOURCES

Potential impacts include:

- Disturbance of shoreline avian species
- Temporary marine habitat disruption during marina expansion

Mitigation:

- Pre-construction nesting bird surveys
- In-water work windows restricted seasonally
- Living shoreline integration
- Habitat enhancement through native plant restoration

Net long-term biological condition projected to improve relative to current industrial baseline.

---

## 7. HYDROLOGY & WATER QUALITY

Stormwater:

- 12-acre open space system includes bioswales
- Zero typical runoff discharge commitment

Flood Risk:

- Project elevation above current FEMA base flood elevation
- Sea-level rise modeling evaluated through 2100

Required Condition:

- Shoreline resilience plan for +3.5 ft rise scenario
- Floodable plaza design integration

---

## 8. SOILS & HAZARDOUS MATERIALS

Historic industrial use has resulted in localized contamination.

Required Remediation:

- Soil excavation and removal
- Groundwater monitoring wells
- Environmental remediation bonding (developer-funded)

No public liability exposure identified under current agreement structure.

---

## 9. SOCIOECONOMIC & HOUSING IMPACTS

The project includes 3,200 residential units, 1,800 of which are deed-restricted affordable units (56%).

Potential Risks:

- Nearby rent escalation
- Retail displacement pressure

Mitigation:

- Small business lease priority (30%)
- Community Benefits Oversight Board
- Annual $2.5M community fund

---

## 10. CUMULATIVE IMPACT ANALYSIS

When combined with:

- Regional waterfront development
- Port modernization activities
- Anticipated transit expansions

The Baylight District contributes to cumulative traffic and construction emissions but also aligns with long-term densification and transit-oriented development goals.

---

## 11. ALTERNATIVES ANALYZED

1. No Project Alternative
2. Reduced Stadium Capacity (28,000 seats)
3. Commercial-Only Waterfront Redevelopment
4. Relocation Alternative (Non-waterfront site)

The proposed project provides the highest economic yield but also the highest event-day traffic impact.

---

## 12. CONCLUSION

The Baylight District Project represents a significant transformation of Oakland's waterfront. Environmental impacts are real but largely mitigable with aggressive enforcement of sustainability commitments and transit integration.

The most critical environmental risks include:

- Traffic congestion during peak events
- Shoreline resilience under long-term sea-level rise
- Construction-phase emissions

With required mitigation measures implemented, the project may proceed within acceptable environmental thresholds under CEQA standards.
