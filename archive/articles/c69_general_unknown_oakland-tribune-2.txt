"The Fourth Wave: What We Know, What We Don't"
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The health-crisis arc reactivated in Temescal this cycle. Arc strength: 3.77. The highest we've measured.

I've been tracking the numbers. City-wide illness rate hit 9% — the peak of our entire simulation window. That's not a spike. That's a statement. Something is different now.

But here's what struck me most in this cycle's data: the citizens are watching.

Joel Lewis, a resident in the population ledger, made a life event entry this cycle: "prioritized their health after a scare." That's not a diagnosis. That's not a hospitalization. That's a citizen adjusting behavior based on signals they're receiving.

I don't know what scared Joel Lewis. The ledger doesn't say. But I know what I would have done if I lived in Temescal right now and read the news about elevated illness indicators. I would have prioritized my health too.

The symptom profile for Wave 4 remains undisclosed. We don't know if it's respiratory like Wave 1. We don't know if it's pediatric like Wave 2. We don't know if it's cognitive like Wave 3.

What we know:
— Arc strength 3.77 (significant)
— Localized to Temescal
— Early phase designation
— 9% city-wide illness rate
— No pathogen identified

What we don't know:
— Symptom presentation
— Transmission pattern

The clinics are "watching closely." That's county health language for uncertainty. They see patterns they can't explain.

I've seen the citizen life histories. Multiple residents logged "felt subtle shift in mood due to recent events" and "sought more information due to recent city events" in the hours surrounding this cycle's data collection. The city is paying attention. The city knows something is happening.

When I first reported on the Downtown respiratory wave back in Cycle 34, I asked a question: why does Oakland keep getting sick in ways nobody can explain?

Four waves later, I'm still asking.

Migration drift hit -37 this cycle. Seven more souls than the -30 baseline we've been tracking for months.

I could frame this as a crisis. I could write the headline: "Oakland Hemorrhaging Population." But that's not what's happening. What's happening is slower. Quieter. More painful.

Darius Williams, 54, warehouse worker in Adams Point, told me his cousin left for Sacramento. "Rent went up, hours went down."

That's the equation. That's the story playing out across every storefront on 7th Street with a "For Lease" sign. Every coffee shop with a handwritten note about renovations that never happen. Every apartment that flips to a new tenant at a higher price.

Marcus Wright logged "felt slightly more confident in their role." Andre Ward noticed "minor shifts in workplace expectations." Mina Scott experienced "a quieter workload than usual."

SECTION 4: SPORTS — OAKLAND
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Anthony Reyes
"The Leak, The Admission, The Silence"
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Three things happened this cycle regarding the Oakland Athletics:

First, someone leaked lineup configurations for early exhibition games. The information made it outside the building before Deacon Seymour filled out a real lineup card. Spring training is days away. Trust is the most important currency a first-time manager can build. Someone spent some of that currency for reasons nobody will explain.

Second, Seymour made his first significant public acknowledgment: he confirmed there was an incident at the practice facility. A strength and conditioning coach was let go.

We've been tracking that story since Cycle 57. The "A's practice disruption" that showed up in the world events ledger. The "staff rumor" that followed. Nobody would confirm anything.

Now we know: something happened, and someone lost their job.

Third, Paulson was informed about the leak. Sources say he didn't say much.

That silence is becoming a pattern. Paulson built the Bulls into an NBA Cup semifinalist. Paulson assembled the most talented roster the A's have had in years. Paulson walked out of a press conference in Oakland and never explained himself.

Spring training forces a reckoning. The GM has to be present. The GM has to answer questions. The GM has to face the city he left holding his words.

That reckoning is days away.
