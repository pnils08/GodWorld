# Product Vision — What GodWorld Is

**Created:** Session 119 (2026-03-26)
**Status:** Active. This is what we're building toward. Everything that contradicts this is the old direction.

---

## The Game

You play a baseball game. Vinnie Keane goes 3-for-4. Then you open the newspaper and the city reacted. Keane is spotted at a Jack London restaurant. A fan writes a letter about seeing him at the farmers market. A business story mentions the bar he was at. A culture piece about nightlife in the waterfront has his name in it. The civic story about the stadium district mentions residents watching construction from the same block where Keane eats dinner.

The newspaper is the world reacting to your characters as people. Not game recaps. Not civic policy journals. A living city where 675 people and 91 athletes share the same streets.

---

## The City Is One City

The engine already does this. 675 ENGINE citizens + 91 GAME citizens + 46 CIVIC officials + 29 MEDIA staff live in the same simulation. Vinnie Keane lives in the same Oakland as Carmen Delaine.

**The media layer broke it into pieces.** Civic desk gets civic data. Sports desk gets sports data. Culture desk gets culture data. The desk prompts and packets created walls the engine never had.

**The fix is porosity.** Desks get the whole city. A culture story can mention a ballplayer. A civic story can quote a restaurant owner who watches construction from his patio. A letter to the editor can reference last night's game AND the new transit hub. The sections bleed into each other like a real city does.

## Civic Programs Deploy — They Don't File Paperwork

When the council votes to pass something, it happens. Like SimCity — you zone it, it builds.

- **Vote passes** -> set a deploy cycle -> program affects the engine
- **Stabilization Fund deploys** -> families stay, neighborhood stabilizes, displacement metrics change
- **OARI deploys** -> different response to behavioral health calls, safety outcomes shift
- **Stadium authorized** -> construction starts, jobs appear, Jack London nightlife changes
- **Health Center authorized** -> Temescal gets a clinic, health outcomes improve

All the filings, documents, approvals, TIF instruments, dispatch protocols — **assumed**. The bureaucracy happens offscreen. The city feels the effects.

### Where Drama Comes From

A voice agent CAN create drama. The Mayor can stall something. A council member can demand an audit. The Police Chief can refuse to sign. But this is the **exception**, not the default. The default is forward motion.

Drama is one decision, not five documents. "The Mayor froze the fund" is a story. "The TIF bond instrument is in final signature phase for December financial close" is paperwork nobody reads.

### What City Hall Does

City hall is lighter than what was built. It:

1. Checks which programs are active and how they're affecting the city
2. Gives voices one chance to cause drama (optional — default is progress)
3. Produces statements the newspaper can quote
4. Moves on

It does NOT:
- Track 5+ documents per initiative
- Require signatures on dispatch protocols
- Generate decision queues with blocker maps
- Force every initiative onto every agenda every cycle

## Vinnie Keane Is the Most Famous Person in Oakland

Six championships. Dynasty era. He's not a sports section character — he's an Oakland character. In a real city with a six-time champion:

- He shows up in culture pieces (spotted at restaurants, nightlife, community events)
- Business stories mention him (endorsement deals, restaurant appearances, economic impact)
- Letters reference him (fans writing about what the dynasty means)
- Civic stories mention him in passing (Baylight is HIS stadium, residents talk about it)
- He exists in the fabric of the city, not locked behind a section header

The A's ARE the Baylight anchor tenant. The stadium is for them. The district exists because Oakland has a baseball dynasty that needed a new home. The newsroom should never write about "unnamed tier-one technology companies" when the answer is on the mound throwing 95.

## What the Player Gets

Mike plays a game. The engine runs. The newspaper comes out. He reads about:

- **His players** living in the city — not just box scores, but where they eat, who they talk to, what residents think of them
- **His city** reacting to what happened — nightlife after a big win, construction changing neighborhoods, new programs affecting people's lives
- **His citizens** as people — 425 have never appeared in an edition. Fresh faces, real stories, not the same 139 names recycled
- **Drama when it's earned** — a council fight, a mayor's decision, a journalist digging into something wrong. Not bureaucratic process for its own sake.

The part for the player is: this is YOUR city. These are YOUR people. The newspaper tells you what happened to them.

## What Changes

| Old Direction | New Direction |
|--------------|---------------|
| 5 initiative agents tracking milestones | Programs deploy on a cycle date, engine feels the effects |
| Decision queues with blocker maps | Voices optionally create drama, default is forward |
| Desk agents siloed by section | Desks get the whole city, sections are porous |
| Sports locked in sports section | Athletes live in Oakland, show up everywhere |
| 27-step edition pipeline | Simplified — city hall is lighter, media covers like journalists |
| Civic policy journal | A newspaper about people living in a city |
| "Unnamed anchor tenant" | The A's. Obviously. |

## The Happy Medium (TBD)

City hall still needs to exist. The question is how much. We'll find the balance between:
- **Too much**: every initiative generates 5 documents and 5 voice decisions per cycle
- **Too little**: city hall is cosmetic and the civic section has no teeth
- **Right**: city hall produces 1-2 real stories per cycle (a fight, a decision, a consequence) and the rest is the city living its life
