Vinnie Keane West Oakland Baseball Academy Opening
By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture & Community

THE FIREHOUSE OPENS

The lights came on at 4:15 in the afternoon.

Not stadium lights. Not the kind Vinnie Keane has played under for seventeen seasons, six championships, four hundred and thirty-six home runs. These were the fluorescents in a converted firehouse on Mandela Parkway — a building that had been dark for eleven years before Keane bought it, gutted it, and turned it into a free youth baseball academy for West Oakland kids.

Dante Nelson, 41, stood near the back wall with his nephew. Nelson works security downtown. He grew up three blocks from here.

“I used to ride my bike past this firehouse,” Nelson said. “We’d try to see inside. The doors were always closed. I never thought I’d see the lights come on.”

His nephew is nine. He was holding a glove that still had the tags on it.

-----

The Vinnie Keane West Oakland Baseball Academy doesn’t look like a stadium project. There’s no ribbon-cutting platform, no mayor at the podium, no corporate logo on the wall. There’s a batting cage, a pitching tunnel, a room full of used gloves and bats sorted by size, and a hand-painted mural of the Oakland skyline that wraps around the back entrance.

Keane was there, in jeans and an A’s hoodie, running a soft-toss station with a bucket of scuffed baseballs. He didn’t give a speech. He threw batting practice.

“I don’t need to talk about it,” Keane said during a break. “I just need it to be here. I needed it to exist before I was done.”

He’s 37 years old. This is his final season. He has six World Series rings and a bench role. The dynasty is ending, and the man who anchored it for a decade and a half is spending his last year opening a firehouse in West Oakland.

-----

The academy is free. No registration fee, no equipment cost, no tryouts. Kids sign up at the door. They get a glove, a bat if they need one, and access to coaching three days a week after school. The program is funded through Keane’s foundation, with matching support from the A’s Community Fund and a grant from the West Oakland Neighbors Association.

Beverly Hayes, who runs the Neighbors Association office on Peralta Street, was at the opening. She’s been processing Stabilization Fund applications for months. This was a different kind of paperwork.

“Vinnie came to the office last spring,” Hayes said. “He asked what the neighborhood needed. I told him: something that stays. Something that isn’t a promise. He came back three months later with keys to a building.”

The building is the old Firehouse 29 — decommissioned in 2030, vacant since. Keane’s foundation purchased it at auction and spent eight months on renovation. The truck bays are now indoor batting cages. The sleeping quarters are equipment storage. The kitchen is still a kitchen — kids get a snack before practice.

-----

Curtis Boone, 47, lives on 32nd Street. He brought his daughter, who’s eleven.

“She doesn’t care about baseball,” Boone said. “But she saw Vinnie on TV and asked if we could come. I told her, ‘That man has six rings and he’s teaching kids in a firehouse.’ She said, ‘What’s a ring?’ I said, ‘Come and find out.’”

By 5:30, there were forty-two kids in the building. Some were in the cage. Some were playing catch in the back lot. Some were sitting on the floor near the mural, eating orange slices and watching Keane adjust a kid’s grip on a bat.

Darius Clark, 40, was standing near the entrance. He works at a bakery in West Oakland. He has season tickets.

“I’ve watched this man hit walk-offs,” Clark said. “I’ve watched him win championships. And now I’m watching him hand a glove to a kid who doesn’t know who he is yet. That’s the part that gets you.”

-----

Keane didn’t talk to the press until the last kid left. When he did, he kept it short.

“I grew up in a neighborhood that didn’t have this,” he said. “I got lucky. I had a coach who found me. Not everybody gets found. So I’m trying to make it easier to be found.”

He looked at the building — the lights still on, the bats still lined up, the mural glowing under the fluorescents.

“This is the part that stays,” he said. “The rings are in a case. This is in the neighborhood.”

Dante Nelson’s nephew was one of the last to leave. He still had the glove. The tags were gone.

“He asked me if he could come back Thursday,” Nelson said. “I told him yes. I told him this is the kind of place you come back to.”

-----

The Vinnie Keane West Oakland Baseball Academy is open Tuesdays, Thursdays, and Saturdays, 4 PM to 7 PM. Walk-ins are welcome. Equipment is provided.
