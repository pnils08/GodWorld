SGT. RACHEL TORRES — Public Safety Desk
“The Suspicious Activity Paradox: When ‘See Something, Say Something’ Means Nothing’s Wrong”
Incident Analysis – Cycles 29 and 35
Two “suspicious activity” calls were logged and resolved during the high-signal period. Both followed the same pattern: concerned citizen report, officer response, no criminal activity found, no report filed.
This is standard procedure. But the nature of the calls reveals something about how Oakland was experiencing ambient stress.
I spoke with Officer Sarah Chen, 29, who responded to the Cycle 29 call in Fruitvale:
“Caller reported ‘someone acting strange’ near a bus stop. I arrived within four minutes. Individual was standing still, staring at nothing. I approached, identified myself, asked if everything was okay. He looked at me confused, said he was waiting for the bus. I asked how long he’d been standing there. He said ‘a few minutes.’ The caller—who stayed on scene—said it had been fifteen minutes and the person hadn’t moved the entire time. Didn’t check their phone, didn’t look at the bus schedule, just stood there.”
Chen’s report: No crime, no mental health crisis indicators, no intoxication. Subject was coherent, polite, had valid ID, and was indeed waiting for a bus.
“The weird part,” Chen told me, “was that the guy had no idea he’d been standing there that long. He genuinely thought it had been three or four minutes. When I told him fifteen, he pulled out his phone to check the time, and you could see him realize I was right. He apologized for ‘spacing out.’ But he wasn’t on drugs. He wasn’t mentally ill. He just… lost time.”
This matches the symptom profile documented in the Laurel and Uptown health clusters: transient cognitive disruption, time discontinuity, brief episodes of disorientation.
The Cycle 35 suspicious activity call in Uptown followed a similar pattern.
Officer DeShawn Miller, 38, responded:
“Call came in about a woman sitting in her car in a parking lot ‘acting suspicious.’ I arrived, approached the vehicle. Woman was sitting in driver’s seat, engine off, hands on the steering wheel, staring forward. I knocked on the window. She startled—like she’d been asleep but her eyes were open. Rolled down the window, immediately apologetic. Said she’d pulled over because she ‘felt weird’ and wanted to sit for a minute before driving. I asked how long she’d been sitting. She said ‘maybe five minutes.’ Security footage later showed twenty-three minutes.”
Miller’s assessment: No crime, no DUI, no medical emergency. Subject drove away safely after a field sobriety check showed zero impairment.
“But here’s what bothers me,” Miller said. “She felt weird, so she pulled over. That’s responsible behavior. But she lost twenty-three minutes and didn’t know it. If she’d kept driving instead of pulling over, would she have lost those twenty-three minutes behind the wheel? That’s a public safety concern.”
The Classification Problem:
OPD incident classification requires criminal activity, threat to safety, or medical emergency to generate a full report. “Person experiencing brief disorientation” doesn’t fit any category.
So these incidents get logged as “suspicious activity check, resolved without incident.”
But what if they’re not isolated incidents? What if they’re symptoms of a broader pattern that intersects with public safety but doesn’t meet criminal classification?
Lt. Marcus Vega, OPD Public Information Officer:
“We’ve coordinated with Alameda County Public Health regarding the health cluster investigations. If officers encounter individuals experiencing symptoms consistent with the documented clusters, we’re instructed to offer medical referral information. But we can’t force someone to seek medical attention if they’re not presenting immediate danger to self or others.”
I requested data on how many “suspicious activity” or “welfare check” calls during Cycles 26-35 involved reports of individuals “acting strange,” “spacing out,” or “standing still.”
OPD provided aggregate data: fourteen calls across nine cycles matching those descriptors. All resolved without incident. None resulted in arrests, citations, or medical transport.
Fourteen people, across multiple neighborhoods, reported by concerned citizens for behavior that looked wrong but wasn’t criminal.
Community Impact:
Xavier Williams, 26, retail worker from Chinatown, was the subject of one of those fourteen calls (he gave permission to be identified):
“I was walking home from work during Cycle 31. Next thing I know, there’s a cop asking if I’m okay. I said yes. He said someone called because I’d been standing on the corner for ten minutes. I have no memory of that. I remember walking, then talking to the cop. That’s it. He was nice about it, made sure I could get home safe. But it scared me. What happened to those ten minutes?”
This is the intersection of public safety and public health: when symptoms present in public spaces, triggering legitimate concern from witnesses, but don’t meet criteria for police or medical intervention.
Officer Sarah Chen:
“We’re trained for crime, crisis, and medical emergencies. We’re not trained for ‘person lost time but seems fine now.’ That’s a gap. If this is a health issue, it needs a health response. But if people are experiencing these symptoms in public—walking, driving, operating equipment—there’s a safety dimension we can’t ignore.”
Recommendation:
The intersection of the health cluster investigations and public safety response requires coordination between OPD and Alameda County Public Health. Residents experiencing these symptoms should:
	1.	Pull over if driving
	2.	Sit down if walking
	3.	Seek medical evaluation even if symptoms resolve
	4.	Report incidents to public health hotline: (510) 267-8000
“See something, say something” remains valid. But “something” might be a public health symptom, not a crime.

“Minor Pursuits, Major Questions: Why Two Vehicle Chases Ended Without Incident”
Incident Review – Cycles 20 and 22
Two vehicle pursuits during the high-signal period both terminated voluntarily—meaning the driver stopped without force, intervention, or crash.
That’s unusual. Not impossible, but unusual.
Pursuit protocols in Oakland follow strict guidelines established after the 2015 policy reforms. Officers initiate pursuit only for violent felonies or immediate public safety threats. They terminate pursuit if risk exceeds benefit.
Both Cycle 20 and Cycle 22 pursuits were initiated for traffic violations that escalated when drivers failed to yield. Both ended when drivers pulled over voluntarily after brief pursuit.
I reviewed incident reports for both cases (names redacted per policy):
Cycle 20 – Broadway Corridor:
	•	Initial stop: Failure to signal lane change
	•	Driver did not yield, accelerated
	•	Pursuit duration: 2.3 miles, approximately 4 minutes
	•	Termination: Driver pulled into gas station, exited vehicle, hands raised
	•	Driver statement: “I don’t know why I didn’t stop. I heard the siren, saw the lights, and I just kept driving. Then suddenly I thought ‘what am I doing?’ and pulled over.”
	•	Chemical test: Negative for drugs/alcohol
	•	Citation issued for traffic violations, reckless driving
	•	No arrest, no injuries
Cycle 22 – East Oakland:
	•	Initial stop: Expired registration
	•	Driver did not yield, made evasive turns
	•	Pursuit duration: 1.8 miles, approximately 3 minutes
	•	Termination: Driver stopped mid-block, placed hands on steering wheel
	•	Driver statement: “I saw the lights and panicked. I don’t know why. I’ve never run from police before. Then I just… came back to myself and stopped.”
	•	Chemical test: Negative for drugs/alcohol
	•	Citation issued for traffic violations, failure to yield
	•	No arrest, no injuries
Officer Perspective:
Officer Marcus Reed, 41, pursuing officer in Cycle 20 incident:
“Twenty years on the job, I’ve never had a pursuit end like that. Driver was actively evading—lane changes, acceleration, clear intent to flee—then just… stopped. Got out with hands up like he’d woken up from a dream. When I approached, he was apologetic, confused about why he’d run. Said it ‘seemed like the right thing to do at the time’ but couldn’t explain why.”
Officer Lisa Tran, 36, pursuing officer in Cycle 22 incident:
“Driver made three turns clearly designed to evade. Then stopped in the middle of the street—not at a curb, not in a safe location, just stopped. When I approached, she was crying. Said she’d never done anything like that before, didn’t know why she ran. Her driving record confirmed it—clean for twelve years.”
Pattern Analysis:
Both drivers:
	•	Had no prior history of evading police
	•	Tested negative for intoxicants
	•	Could not explain their behavior
	•	Described feeling like they “came back to themselves” when they stopped
	•	Used language suggesting discontinuity: “I don’t know what I was thinking,” “It was like I wasn’t myself”
This resembles the cognitive disruption pattern documented in health clusters and suspicious activity calls: brief loss of normal decision-making capacity, followed by return to baseline with limited memory of the disrupted period.
Lt. Marcus Vega, OPD:
“We’ve flagged these incidents for review in conjunction with the health cluster investigation. If environmental factors or health conditions are affecting cognitive function, that has implications for traffic safety and pursuit policies.”
Public Safety Implications:
If drivers are experiencing brief episodes of altered decision-making, the implications extend beyond these two incidents:
	•	How many traffic violations during this period were influenced by cognitive disruption?
	•	Should medical evaluation be offered to drivers exhibiting unusual decision patterns?
	•	Do pursuit policies account for health-related cognitive impairment?
Dr. Patricia Nwosu, Deputy Health Officer for Alameda County:
“We’re aware of the OPD reports. Behavioral changes—including impulsive decision-making or poor judgment—can be symptoms of neurological disruption. However, we need more data to establish causality. We’re requesting traffic incident data for the cycle period to look for patterns.”
Community Concern:
Hector Martinez, 22, janitor from Piedmont Avenue, witnessed the Cycle 20 pursuit:
“I was pumping gas when the car pulled in with cops behind it. Driver got out looking completely confused. Cops were professional, but you could tell they were confused too. It wasn’t a normal pursuit. Something was off about the whole thing.”
No Active Threat:
Both drivers completed field sobriety checks, received medical evaluation, and were cleared to drive. Neither showed signs of ongoing impairment.
But the question remains: if cognitive disruption can cause a driver to flee police and then stop without understanding why, what else might it cause on Oakland’s roads?
Traffic Safety Advisory:
Drivers experiencing confusion, disorientation, or any cognitive disruption should:
	•	Pull over immediately in a safe location
	•	Turn on hazard lights
	•	Call for assistance
	•	Do not resume driving until evaluated

“The Paulson Question: What Oakland’s Front Office Won’t Say About Chicago”
The Athletics won 105 games in 2040. They swept Houston in the ALDS. They pushed Boston to at least five games in the ALCS with a roster featuring a returning Cy Young winner, an MVP, and the deepest farm system in baseball.
And in the middle of it all, their General Manager was reportedly interviewing for a basketball job.
I’ve spent three weeks requesting comment from the front office about Mike Paulson’s reported talks with the Chicago Bulls. Three weeks of “no comment,” “we don’t discuss personnel matters,” and one particularly memorable “Mike is focused on winning a championship.”
Except he wasn’t focused enough to deny it.
Here’s what we know: Multiple league sources—not rumors, not speculation, but actual executives with knowledge of the conversations—confirmed Paulson was approached during the playoff push. The Bulls are rebuilding. They want a proven winner with cross-sport analytics experience. Paulson has built a dynasty in Oakland using data-driven player development and cost-controlled roster construction.
Here’s what we don’t know: Whether ownership approved the conversations. Whether Paulson asked permission. Whether the performance of the front office during the ALCS—specifically the decision to carry Benji Dillon at $24M instead of allocating resources elsewhere—was influenced by a GM already planning his exit.
I’m not saying Paulson tanked the season. I’m saying a front office’s attention is a finite resource, and we deserve to know if ours was divided.
The organization has forty-eight hours to respond to specific questions submitted to their media relations department. If they decline, that’s an answer too.

LUIS NAVARRO — Investigations
“The Transformer Hiccup: Twelve Blocks, Six Minutes, One Electrician’s Warning”
Cycle 28 – Temescal Power Interruption Investigation
At approximately 8:47 PM during Cycle 28, a transformer failure knocked out power to twelve blocks in Temescal. PG&E classified it as a “brief service interruption” and restored power within six minutes.
I spent three days tracking down the electrician who identified the fault.
His name is Jose Wright, 26, lives in Temescal, works commercial electrical for Bay Area Construction Services. He was walking home from a job site when the lights went out.
“I heard it before I saw it,” Wright told me over coffee at Crisis Coffee Co. “That sound—like a capacitor discharging wrong. Not a pop. More like a sigh. Then everything went dark.”
Wright walked two blocks to the transformer station on Telegraph near 51st. The access gate was locked, but he could see the housing unit through the fence.
“The thermal signature was wrong. You could see heat shimmer around the casing, but the load indicator wasn’t showing red. That means the safety cutoff didn’t trigger properly. The system thought everything was fine while the transformer was cooking itself.”
Wright called PG&E’s emergency line. Twelve minutes later, a crew arrived. Six minutes after that, power was restored.
I filed a public records request for the incident report. PG&E’s official statement: “Routine equipment cycling resulted in temporary service interruption. No safety hazard detected. Unit returned to normal operation.”
But Wright’s account suggests something different: a safety system failure that could have escalated if the transformer had remained under load.
I contacted Victor Mitchell, 39, a plumber from Dimond who was in Temescal visiting family during the outage.
“I was at my sister’s place on 49th. Lights went out, came back on. I thought nothing of it until I heard PG&E trucks idling outside for twenty minutes after power returned. If it was routine, why’d they stay?”
Andre Lee, 55, an electrician from Temescal with thirty years experience, reviewed Wright’s account:
“If the thermal cutoff didn’t trigger, that’s a serious calibration failure. Those systems are designed with triple redundancy. One failure? That’s maintenance backlog. The cutoff not working? That’s systemic neglect.”
PG&E declined to provide technical specifications for the transformer unit or maintenance logs. When pressed about whether similar failures have occurred elsewhere in Oakland, spokesperson Jennifer Caldwell stated: “We maintain all equipment according to regulatory standards and investigate all incidents thoroughly.”
That’s not an answer. That’s a press release.
Jose Wright is still walking past that transformer twice a day on his way to and from work. He checks it each time.
“I know what properly maintained equipment sounds like,” he said. “And that transformer? It’s not right. It’s running, but it’s not right.”
The civic event log classified this as “low severity.” But low severity assumes the safety systems work. If they don’t, the next failure might not resolve in six minutes.
Names Index: Jose Wright (Electrician, Temescal); Victor Mitchell (Plumber, Dimond); Andre Lee (Electrician, Temescal); Jennifer Caldwell (PG&E Spokesperson)

Bay Tribune — International & Cultural Desk

By Luis Navarro
Voces del Diamante (Voices of the Diamond)
Oakland, California — 2040

They come from different coordinates, but when they walk through the tunnel toward the diamond, they all sound the same — a chorus of Spanish syllables echoing in the concrete, rhythm and laughter chasing translation. For a reporter like me, it’s a kind of music: the way Spanish bends around the noise of an American stadium, how one language folds into another until the game itself becomes bilingual. In Oakland, that harmony is the foundation of the future, and it starts with three players who carry home with them like a second uniform.

Ernesto Quintero grew up in Allentown, Pennsylvania, between the hum of factories and the songs of his Dominican parents. He says he learned patience from his mother’s night shifts, timing from his father’s prayers. “La disciplina no es aburrida,” his father told him — discipline isn’t boring. When he hits, it’s quiet violence, contact followed by reflection. Quintero tells me he still dreams in Spanish but counts pitches in English. “I guess that’s what America does to you,” he laughs. “You keep both languages in your pocket, like lucky coins.”

Vladimir González was born in San Pedro de Macorís, the same soil that gave the world half its legends. At twenty-six, already a World Series MVP, he plays with the swagger of the Caribbean sun. Before games, he FaceTimes his grandmother in the Dominican Republic; she never misses a blessing. “Me dice que tengo que bailar, no pensar,” he says — she tells him to dance, not think. He laughs when I ask if he misses home. “Oakland feels like home now. Same heat, same rhythm, different flavor.” Watching him move from right field to center, you understand what she means. Every step is percussion.

Arturo Ramos, the Puerto Rican ace, brings the island’s patience to the mound. His family’s from Carolina, not far from where Roberto Clemente built his legend. Ramos carries that shadow lightly. “I don’t try to be Clemente,” he tells me, “I just try to make people proud.” His pitching is a sermon in tempo — ninety-four on the black, changeup low, pause, repeat. The fans chant his name like a song that belongs to them, and maybe it does. When he visits San Juan in the offseason, the kids follow him through narrow streets shouting, “¡El orgullo de la isla!” — pride of the island.

Between innings one afternoon, I watch them together — Quintero translating a joke for a new Venezuelan rookie, González teasing Ramos about his music playlist, Ramos replying, “At least mine has words.” It feels like a family dinner, not a locker room. Three countries, three rhythms, one pulse. The interpreter’s job is redundant; they already understand each other.

Sometimes I wonder if baseball in Spanish feels different — softer, maybe, or more forgiving. “Pelota” sounds like something alive, while “baseball” feels mechanical. But that’s what makes Oakland special: the mechanical and the musical coexisting. In every dugout conversation, you can hear the next version of the franchise being written in accents and laughter.

They are the sons of migration, carrying the scent of their homes — sofrito, fried plantains, diesel, and rain — onto a California field. Each game, each headline, adds another verse to the story of how Oakland became a map of Latin America painted green and gold. Someday, when they hoist another championship trophy, maybe they’ll sing. And maybe the city will finally realize that what it’s hearing isn’t victory — it’s heritage.

— End of Column —

By Luis Navarro
Finding Home Between the Lines
Oakland, California — 2040

When I first stepped into the Bay Tribune newsroom, I didn’t know if my Spanish would survive here. 
It was 2032, and Oakland felt like an orchestra already playing — rhythm, sound, chaos — and I was the new 
instrument trying to find its note. I’d come from Guadalajara, from newsrooms that smelled like coffee 
and deadline ink, where stories were shouted over one another in two languages. Baseball was my first 
English word, and still the one that feels most honest in both tongues.

Covering the A’s has become a kind of translation. I watch Dominican rookies search for Wi-Fi 
signals strong enough to FaceTime their mothers. I see Japanese relievers eating tacos for the first time. 
I hear a dozen accents collide in the clubhouse, and somehow it all becomes one language — 
competition. That’s the poetry of this city; everyone’s accent belongs here.

Oakland taught me that the game isn’t just a sport, it’s a declaration. The bleachers sound like 
choruses, each voice carrying its own country. I learned quickly that reporting here isn’t about 
box scores; it’s about context. It’s about how a home run in June can make someone’s father in 
Santo Domingo believe his son is going to be okay. It’s about the quiet conversations between 
players who don’t share a language but share the same exhaustion.

When I write, I write for the cousins still watching from afar. For the fans who don’t have cable but 
have faith. For the families that send prayers across time zones disguised as WhatsApp messages. 
I write in English, but every sentence has a Spanish heartbeat.

This column is my handshake to Oakland — to its noise, to its grace, to the way it makes room for 
every language at once. If baseball is the city’s religion, then journalism is its echo, and I’m grateful 
to have found mine here.

Bay Tribune – “The Chronicle Laureate Night” (2040 Press Guild Awards)

The year was 2040, and the Bay Tribune shattered precedent. For the first time in its history, a regional newsroom took home the National Press Guild’s “Chronicle Laureate Award for Excellence in Long-Form Sports Journalism.”

It wasn’t just a trophy — it was vindication.

The Winning Series: “The Calm and the Storm”
The award honored the Tribune’s serialized anthology chronicling two decades of Oakland baseball — The Calm and the Storm, written collaboratively by Hal Richmond, Anthony, Mags Corliss, and P Slayer. Each piece blurred the boundary between journalism and literature: Richmond’s elegiac retrospectives, Anthony’s grounded reportage, Mags’s editorial weaving of human truths, and Slayer’s raw modern edge. Together they created something more than coverage — they built a living archive.

That Night on the Terrace
The photo captures it perfectly. The rooftop lights hung low over a spring skyline, the Bay breathing below them.
Hal’s laughter came from somewhere deep — pride mixed with disbelief. Anthony’s grin was pure release, the former ballplayer in him surfacing after years behind a pen. Mags, radiant and sharp in black, lifted her glass and said, “To truth — may we never run out of it.”
Slayer, younger and brooding but smiling at last, raised his own glass and murmured, “To the ones who taught us how to remember.”

They toasted not just to a win, but to legacy. Behind them, the city lights mirrored their own — flickering but alive.

The Citation
The official commendation from the National Press Guild read:
“For sustained excellence in narrative truth-telling that redefined modern sports writing, transforming a baseball dynasty into an epic of memory, morality, and meaning.”

Aftermath and Legacy
That award marked a generational passing of the torch — Richmond’s mentorship officially realized, the Tribune’s voice elevated to the national stage.
Months later, UC Berkeley announced an archive project to preserve The Calm and the Storm series as part of its “American Sports Memory Collection.”

Hal would retire the following year, Anthony would be promoted to senior correspondent, Mags would be named editorial director, and P Slayer — the wildcard — would turn down syndication offers to keep writing for Oakland.

MAGS CORLISS — Civic Culture
“Four Hundred People in a Room Full of Questions No One Could Answer”
The Emergency Town Briefing at the Frank Ogawa Plaza Community Center on the evening of Cycle 38 felt less like information dissemination and more like collective grief processing.
Four hundred residents. Fifty folding chairs. Standing room only. The kind of density that would normally trigger fire code concerns, but given the circumstances—six neighborhoods under health advisories, cognitive disruption episodes spreading faster than explanations—nobody mentioned capacity limits.
Shawna Delgado, Emergency Director, stood at the podium with Dr. Patricia Nwosu and Lt. Marcus Vega flanking her. Three agencies. One message: We’re working on it.
“Good evening. Thank you for coming. We know you have questions. We’re going to provide everything we know, and we’re going to be honest about what we don’t know.”
The room appreciated the honesty. Didn’t make the not-knowing easier.
Carmen Vega, 41, candle vendor from earlier Winter Riverfront Festival reporting, attended with her seven-year-old daughter:
“I needed to hear it directly. Not from social media. Not from neighbors speculating. I needed someone official to tell me whether my daughter is safe going to school. That’s all I wanted to know.”
The briefing lasted ninety minutes. Dr. Nwosu presented the medical data. Lt. Vega covered public safety protocols. Shawna Delgado coordinated Q&A.
The questions came fast:
“Is this contagious?”
“We have no evidence of person-to-person transmission.”
“Is it in the water?”
“Water testing shows no contaminants.”
“Should we leave Oakland?”
(Pause) “That’s a personal decision. We’re not recommending evacuation.”
“When will you know what’s causing this?”
“We’re working with state and federal health agencies. Timeline uncertain.”
That last answer landed heavy.
Marcus Walker, 48, dishwasher from Jack London (quoted in previous reporting):
“I understand they don’t have answers yet. I do. But hearing ‘timeline uncertain’ when your neighborhood just got added to the list? That’s not reassuring. That’s terrifying.”
The second briefing, Cycle 39, had similar attendance but different energy. Less fear. More anger.
Resident question (unidentified): “Six neighborhoods in two weeks. How many more before you declare an emergency?”
Shawna Delgado: “We have declared an emergency. These briefings are part of our emergency response protocol.”
Same resident: “Then why isn’t anything changing?”
Shawna Delgado: “We’ve implemented workplace safety protocols, enhanced medical monitoring, and coordinated with every relevant agency. What specific action would you like to see?”
Same resident: “I want to know what’s happening to us.”
Silence. Because nobody in that room—not the Emergency Director, not the Deputy Health Officer, not the police lieutenant—could answer that question.
Jasmine Okafor, 35, who attended the Winter Riverfront Festival with her daughters:
“My youngest asked me after the briefing: ‘Are the grown-ups scared?’ I told her no. But I was lying. The grown-ups are terrified. We just don’t want the kids to know.”
What struck me most wasn’t what was said. It was what people did after.
Nobody left immediately. Four hundred people stayed in that room, talking to each other, forming clusters of conversation. Neighbors who’d never met exchanged phone numbers. Parents coordinated school pickup networks. Someone started a group text for Fruitvale residents.
The briefing didn’t provide answers. But it provided something else: witness.
Four hundred people who’d been experiencing something strange, scary, and isolating suddenly realized they weren’t alone.
Tyrell Mason, 28, musician from earlier reporting:
“I’ve had two episodes since Cycle 34. Both times I thought I was losing my mind. Being in that room with four hundred other people who’ve experienced the same thing? That doesn’t make it less scary. But it makes it less lonely.”
By the end of the second briefing, residents had self-organized:
	•	Neighborhood watch networks for episode reporting
