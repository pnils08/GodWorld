Hal Richmond
Part IV — Mark Aitken and Eric Chavez: The Iron Corner and the Golden Standard

Oakland has never lacked for third basemen with flair, personality, or presence. But when the conversation shifts to greatness — real, durable greatness — it always funnels toward two names separated by decades and nearly everything else: Eric Chavez and Mark Aitken.

Chavez was the prototype.
A defender whose glove looked scripted, a hitter with enough left-handed force to tilt entire series, and a franchise cornerstone who carried the weight of expectation on rosters that rarely matched his ambition. He won six Gold Gloves, a Silver Slugger, and was the face of the Coliseum in the early 2000s.

Aitken is the successor he never met, but the one who finally matched his standard.

Look at the numbers from Aitken’s prime. The years 2034 through 2039 form a stretch as complete as any third baseman Oakland has ever developed:

2034: 184 hits, 39 HR, 100 RBI, .289/.355/.521
2036: 182 hits, 35 HR, 124 RBI, .296/.374/.535
2038: 160 hits, 30 HR, .284/.373/.475
2039: 165 hits, 20 HR, Gold Glove defense at 32 years old

This wasn’t a player who survived on power spikes or short bursts. Aitken blended high-contact seasons with double-digit walk rates, played nearly every inning of every year, and delivered postseason moments that defined the dynasty. His Playoff MVP in 2037 cemented what fans already suspected — he wasn’t just part of the machine, he was one of the gears that kept it from seizing.

Defensively, the comparison becomes tighter. Chavez was smooth, artistic, reactive. Aitken is engineered, precise, consistent. Their styles differ, but their impact does not. Chavez’s reputation was built on plays that made broadcasters raise their voices. Aitken’s was built on removing hits before they had a chance to become anything memorable. His 2038 and 2039 Gold Gloves didn’t celebrate flair. They celebrated suffocation.

Where Aitken separates himself is longevity inside success. Chavez carried good teams. Aitken carried great ones. He didn’t have to be the lone star. He had to be the star that never blinked when pressure climbed and the dynasty threatened to wobble. His 2034 Silver Slugger came during a season when opposing teams game-planned specifically around avoiding his bat with runners on base. His All-Star MVP in 2036 wasn’t a showcase moment — it was confirmation that the league saw what Oakland had known for years.

Chavez played like he was fighting the league.
Aitken played like he had already beaten it.

Different eras.
Same position.
Two sides of a legacy that now feels complete.
