Slayer Syndicate – Minor League Deep Dive (AA Level)
By P Slayer
Climbing the Hill: The RockHounds Who Refuse to Wait
Midland, Texas — 2040

There’s no neon in Midland, no casinos, no crowds counting days until the call. The RockHounds live in a quieter kind of pressure — the sound of gloves popping under sunset skies, the murmurs of fans who know the next great Athletic might be on this field tonight. If Las Vegas is the last stop before the empire, Midland is the proving ground, the forge where talent turns to toughness.

The Next in Line
Julio Valencia headlines this group, a twenty‑two‑year‑old left‑handed closer with an A potential and the demeanor of a man twice his age. His fastball has climbed to ninety‑four, his sinker to eighty‑nine, and his slider drops off a table like a trick of gravity. The numbers back it up — command up four points, walk rate down three — but it’s the poise that sells him. When scouts describe composure as a tool, they’re talking about him. The only thing keeping Valencia from a promotion to Las Vegas is space, and space is a currency the Athletics spend reluctantly.

Across the diamond stands Frank Reyna, a switch‑hitting outfielder out of Pennsylvania whose bat speed makes pitchers uncomfortable. He’s twenty years old and already showing big‑league polish — contact in the seventies, power trending up, and a discipline rating that puts him among the top prospects in the system. His arm strength (eighty) and reaction time (sixty‑nine) give him versatility in the corners, and he’s stealing bases on instinct. Scouts call him “the quiet five‑tool.” In any other organization he’d be headed to Triple‑A by August, but Oakland is overloaded with outfield talent: Horn, González, Busch, Morton. The ladder’s crowded, and Reyna’s climbing anyway.

Pitchers on the Rise
Richie Haynes and Carmen Mesa could start for most Double‑A clubs; together they anchor Midland’s rotation. Haynes, twenty‑one, leans on a heavy sinker and a hard‑biting slider that plays above his velocity. Mesa, also twenty‑one, mixes changeups and cutters with precision beyond his age. Both project as mid‑rotation arms in a league that demands adaptability. The catch? Cy Newell, John Ellis, and Allen Lopez are already waiting at higher levels. There’s no shortage of arms in the Oakland machine — which means one of these two could eventually become trade collateral when the big club hunts for lineup power.

And then there’s Richie Ramiro, a twenty‑one‑year‑old first baseman with a Stopper trait — the kind of player who hits better when the team’s behind. He doesn’t talk much, doesn’t celebrate, just hits. Coaches say he’s built like a middleweight fighter: compact, powerful, efficient. His contact is developing, his raw power flashes late, and he’s learning the patience to let the game come to him. He’s the definition of quiet danger, the type of player who can turn a 3‑1 deficit into a 4‑3 win with one swing.

Midland’s Momentum
The RockHounds clubhouse doesn’t feel like Double‑A anymore. Valencia leads bullpen sessions like a teacher, Haynes and Mesa trade grips like scientists, and Reyna carries himself like he already belongs in the big‑league dugout. The coaching staff sees it too — the mix of readiness and restraint that defines Oakland’s farm system. They call it “the hum.” It’s the sound of players who know their names are about to matter.

But the challenge is the same story told a level up: no vacancies. The Las Vegas roster is stacked, and the majors are locked behind experience and contracts. Every day these players walk into the park knowing performance alone might not be enough. In Oakland’s empire, timing is the real opponent.

The Road Ahead
When the front office eventually shakes up the roster, Midland’s names will be the first to move. Valencia could replace Peña in the bullpen within a year. Reyna’s bat might force an outfield reshuffle. Haynes or Mesa could headline a trade package if the Athletics chase another playoff run. Even Ramiro, the unassuming first baseman, could find himself in green and gold by twenty‑forty‑two.

Until then, the RockHounds play on under the hot Texas sun, their shadows stretching long across the infield dirt. They’re not waiting — they’re sharpening, same as their Triple‑A brothers. Every inning in Midland is a rehearsal for history. And when the call finally comes, the echo will sound all the way to Oakland.
