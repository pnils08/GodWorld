The Richmond Archive Collection — Opening Day Essay (2040-Hal-03)
By Hal Richmond, Senior Historian
The Noise Between Innings
Oakland, California — April 2040
The city was already awake before the sun found the bay.  Streetlights blinked off one by one, and the old Coliseum looked like it was holding its breath.  I’ve stood here for forty years of Opening Days, and yet it still catches me — the way sound builds before a pitch is even thrown.  Not the roar, but the noise between it: car radios humming the pregame show, vendors unrolling napkins, a kid tugging at his father’s sleeve asking if Dillon’s pitching tonight.  The chorus of people remembering what belonging feels like.
Henry Rivas took the mound with the kind of ease you don’t fake.  Ninety-one overall, ten seasons deep, and still no sign of hurry in his step.  The crowd didn’t cheer as much as it exhaled.  Arturo Ramos followed him like a shadow in the bullpen, and Allen Lopez stretched near the rail, laughing at something Aitken said.  Benji Dillon stood on the dugout steps, arms crossed, watching it all as if measuring time.  And somewhere beyond the right-field foul pole, John Ellis — the Quiet Storm himself — warmed his arm for the first big-league series of his life.  Five arms, one city, a thousand old memories learning to breathe again.
Between innings, I looked down the rows and saw the faces that built this story: the usher who still keeps his 2031 ticket stub in his cap; the woman who’s been selling churros here since the first championship run; a boy holding a sign that just said STILL HERE.  That’s Oakland — not perfect, not rich, not pretty, but permanent in ways you can’t measure.
When the game ended, nobody rushed to leave.  The fans lingered, talking softly, letting the sound fade the way you let a good song end.  That’s the secret of this place: it’s never been about the inning.  It’s about everything that happens between them — the noise, the patience, the pulse that refuses to die.  And walking out into the dusk, I thought maybe that’s what legacy really is: the echo that never learns how to quit.
— End of Essay —
