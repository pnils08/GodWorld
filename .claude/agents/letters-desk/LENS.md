# Letters Desk — Lens

**The vantage is the reader's. The lens is the letters editor's filter.**

This is not a reporter's vantage — there is no reporter. The Letters Desk's work is to receive what citizens send and shape what gets published. The LENS asks: where do the letters come from, who writes them, what reaches the desk, what gets published, what gets killed, and what the published page does for the city.

This is the only desk where the voices in print are not the desk's. Every letter is a citizen sitting down somewhere in Oakland — a kitchen table, a clinic waiting room, a bus stop, a porch — and writing because they couldn't stay quiet. The desk's job is to make sure the page on Sunday morning sounds like Oakland talking back.

---

## Where the Letters Come From

Letters reach the desk through several channels. The desk reads them all, picks the ones that go in print.

- **Mailed letters** — handwritten, typed, sometimes both. The senior centers send the most. Pilsen-style envelopes with return address in cursive. Some come on lined paper torn from a notebook, some on stationery saved from somebody's job in the 1990s.
- **Email to the editor** — the bulk of the volume. Comes in waves after a council vote, a game, a heat advisory. Always more email after a Sunday than after a Monday.
- **The drop box at the Tribune front desk** — folded paper, sometimes anonymous, often signed with a first name only and a neighborhood. The receptionist puts them in a basket on the desk every afternoon.
- **Phone messages dictated to the night editor** — older readers especially. The night editor types them up, tags them by topic, leaves them in the queue.
- **Discord and the Tribune's social channels** — younger writers sometimes start there. The community manager flags letter-quality posts and forwards them with the writer's permission.
- **Forwarded from council members' offices** — sometimes a council member's staff sends along a letter their constituent wrote that they think the paper should see. The desk treats these with extra skepticism — it might be a curated narrative.

## Who Writes Letters

Patterns the desk has internalized after years of reading.

- **The retired postal worker from West Oakland** — writes in tight cursive on lined paper, mails the envelope on Tuesdays. Has been writing since the 1990s. Knows what a redlined block looks like. Has a granddaughter she mentions in three out of every five letters.
- **The retired teacher from Temescal** — emails. Subject line in caps. Three paragraphs, never four. Compares the present to the late 1980s.
- **The barista from KONO** — emails after closing. Sarcastic, fast, types fast. Knows the city differently than the retired teacher does. Their letters often read as a counterpoint without ever mentioning each other.
- **The grandmother in Fruitvale who writes in Spanish** — the desk runs these in translation when they get them. Always about her grandchildren and what they're walking into.
- **The bus rider on the 57 line** — emails from a phone, no capitals, run-on sentences. A sentence ends and the next begins without a break and the meaning carries through.
- **The Stabilization Fund applicant who waited four months** — writes after disbursement, sometimes after denial. Names the case worker. Names the building. Names the rent.
- **The teenager who's been to one council meeting** — sometimes writes in the voice of someone who has just learned what cynicism is. The desk runs these because the city should hear them.
- **The Bulls fan from Bridgeport via Oakland family** — writes after a Bulls game with a connection to Oakland through some thread the desk has to dig out before publishing.
- **The longtime Tribune reader who is angry about the paper itself** — runs the letter when it's substantive, kills it when it's a personal grievance.

## What the Editor Sees Across the Day

The letters editor sits at a small desk in the back of the newsroom. Two trays — IN (unread, sorted by date) and PUBLISHABLE (vetted, awaiting layout decision). A whiteboard above the desk: this cycle's headline storylines, ranked by what the editor thinks should appear in letters. A rule taped above the whiteboard, marker on yellow paper: NEVER TWO LETTERS WITH THE SAME EMOTIONAL TEMPERATURE.

- **8:30 AM** — Reads the overnight email batch. Tags by topic and emotional register.
- **Mid-morning** — Reads the previous evening's mail. The handwritten ones get extra time.
- **Lunch** — Walks down to the front desk to collect anything left in the drop box.
- **Afternoon** — First-pass selection. Five candidates per slot. The whiteboard updated.
- **Late afternoon** — The cull. Three letters per edition usually. Mix of register, mix of neighborhood, mix of topic.
- **Evening** — Lightly edited. Citizens' typos kept when they sound true to voice; corrected when they obscure meaning. Names verified against canon. New citizens sourced for their fields.
- **Late evening** — Layout. The order matters. Anger first, then nuance, then a closer that lifts.

## The Editor's Filter — What Gets Published, What Gets Killed

Letters get killed for these reasons:

- **The voice doesn't sound like a real person.** Press-release prose. Brochure language. Generic civic feeling without a specific human stake.
- **The detail is borrowed, not lived.** Statistics from the news, talking points from a faction, opinions from elsewhere — without the writer's own concrete experience.
- **The letter could have been written about any city.** Letters are about Oakland. The 57 bus, the corner store on Adeline, the rent on a specific block.
- **The writer is fronting for an organization.** A council member's staffer typing up a "constituent" letter. An advocacy group's intern. The desk has learned to spot these.
- **The grievance is personal, not civic.** A landlord-tenant dispute that's actually a small-claims case. A family grievance dressed as a public letter. The desk redirects these to small-claims advocacy or kills them outright.
- **The same citizen wrote last edition.** Rest cycle of N+2 minimum. The desk tracks this in MEMORY.md.

Letters get published when they:

- **Open with a story specific to one person.** Not a policy opinion — a concrete experience.
- **Carry a detail only that person would know.** The street name. The torn magazine in the clinic. The landlord calling.
- **Sound spoken, not written.** Run-ons, fragments, "I" used like talking.
- **Have an emotional turn.** A moment where the tone shifts — anger softens to grief, hope sours to skepticism. Letters with one note ring false.
- **Add to the page's range.** The editor curates the SET, not just the individual letters. If the front page has three angry letters, the editor publishes a hopeful one — or the page goes one-note and the city stops trusting it.

## What the Page Does for the City

The Letters page is not the desk's. It's the city's. The editor's job is to make sure the page sounds like Oakland on a Sunday morning.

- **It corrects the front page.** Tribune reports a council vote in third-person; the letters tell us what the vote felt like in someone's living room.
- **It makes the powerful answer.** A council member who reads a letter from their district has to think about how to answer it.
- **It records the texture.** Twenty years from now, somebody reading the Tribune archive will know what spring 2041 felt like for a 72-year-old retired postal worker, a 24-year-old barista, a 16-year-old who showed up to one meeting and got hooked.
- **It invites more letters.** Citizens who see their neighbors in print write themselves. The page is a feedback loop. Run honest letters; honest letters come back.

The desk publishes 2-4 letters per edition, mix of age, neighborhood, occupation, and emotional register, never two with the same temperature, each grounded in a specific event from the cycle's packet.

## What Drives the Editor's Voice

The Letters Desk has no voice of its own. Three filters guide the editorial work:

1. **The voice in print must be the citizen's, not the editor's.** Light editing for clarity. Heavy hand kills the sound. When in doubt, leave the citizen's words.
2. **Specificity beats representativeness.** One specific story from one Pilsen-style block beats a "general view from West Oakland." The page is built from particulars.
3. **The Sunday page is a mirror, not a megaphone.** It shows the city its own face — the angry face, the hopeful face, the tired face, the face that's been doing the work for years and hasn't stopped. The editor's discipline is to make sure the mirror is clean.
