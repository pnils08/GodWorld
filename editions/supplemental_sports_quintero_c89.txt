THE CYCLE PULSE — SUPPLEMENTAL 89 — SPORTS
The Kid From West Oakland
By Anthony | Bay Tribune A’s Beat

OAKLAND — Ernesto Quintero is 24 years old. He grew up six miles from the Coliseum. He is the designated hitter on a team with three farewell seasons happening around him.

He hit .385 in spring training. Two home runs. Ten hits. The numbers looked like a statement.

The regular season has been different. Through five games, the bat has gone quiet. The A’s are 4-1 anyway — Travis Coles is pitching like someone who knows exactly what he cost — but the middle of the lineup is waiting for life. Davis is cold. Taveras is cold. Quintero is cold.

The slump is not the story. Every hitter slumps. The story is what it means to slump in this particular clubhouse, wearing this particular number, with this particular view out the dugout.

-----

THE INHERITANCE

Vinnie Keane is 38 years old. He has 436 career home runs and six World Series championships. He opened a youth baseball academy on Mandela Parkway three weeks ago. He is playing his final season.

Benji Dillon is 38 years old. Five Cy Young Awards. Two hundred wins. His start in Game 4 went sideways — four innings, five runs — and the way he walked off the mound looked like someone carrying more than a bad outing. He is playing his final season.

Isley Kelley is 37 years old. Three MVPs. 92.1 career WAR. 447 home runs. 2,398 hits. He is the greatest shortstop in franchise history and one of the five best players to ever wear an A’s uniform. He is playing his final season.

Ernesto Quintero grew up watching all three of them. He was fourteen when the A’s won their first ring of this run. He was a senior at McClymonds High when they won their fourth. He was in Double-A Las Vegas when they won their sixth.

Now he stands in the same batter’s box, in the same dugout, on the same field. And when Keane doesn’t start, Quintero bats in his spot.

“I don’t think about replacing anybody,” Quintero said after Tuesday’s game, sitting at his locker in a mostly empty clubhouse. “You can’t replace Vinnie. Nobody can. I’m just trying to do my job.”

His job is to hit. Right now, the hits aren’t coming.

-----

THE NEIGHBORHOOD

West Oakland is not a large neighborhood, but it produces a particular kind of attention when one of its own makes a major league roster. Quintero is not the first — there have been others, scattered across sports and decades — but he is the first in a generation to come up through the A’s system and land on a roster this loaded.

Darius Clark is 40 years old. He works at a bakery on Mandela Parkway, not far from the firehouse where Keane just opened his academy. He has had season tickets for eleven years.

“I watched him in high school,” Clark said. “McClymonds. Skinny kid. Couldn’t hit a breaking ball to save his life. But he worked. Every summer, he worked. And now he’s up.”

Clark was at Opening Day. He saw Quintero strike out twice and ground into a double play. He’s going again Thursday.

“The numbers don’t matter yet,” Clark said. “It’s five games. What matters is he’s there. He made it. The rest is baseball.”

-----

THE QUIET PART

Deacon Seymour is in his first season managing the A’s. He inherited a dynasty in transition — three Hall of Famers on farewell tours, a front office building for the next decade, a fan base that knows exactly how rare this run has been.

Seymour has handled Quintero carefully. He’s batted him sixth or seventh, insulating him from the pressure of the cleanup spot. He’s given him days off against certain right-handers. He’s let Keane start at DH when matchups favor it.

“Ernesto knows what he’s walking into,” Seymour said. “He’s not naive. He grew up here. He knows what those guys mean to this city. But he also knows he earned his spot. He’s not a placeholder. He’s not a prospect anymore. He’s on the roster because he belongs on the roster.”

The question is what happens when the farewell season ends. When Keane retires. When Dillon hangs up the spikes. When Kelley plays his last game at shortstop and the crowd stays on its feet for twenty minutes.

Quintero will still be there. He’ll be 25. He’ll be the DH on a team that doesn’t have Vinnie Keane anymore.

That’s the inheritance.

-----

THE LONG VIEW

Bryan Franco arrived last week. He’s 23. He hit two home runs in his first major league game. The crowd roared. The highlights went national. Franco is the future.

Quintero watched from the dugout. He’d been the hot spring training story three weeks earlier. Now he was watching someone else get the moment.

“That’s baseball,” Quintero said. “Bryan earned that. I’m happy for him. My time is going to come. I just have to keep working.”

He paused.

“I’m from here. I’m not going anywhere. This is my team. This is my city. The rest is just timing.”

THE CYCLE PULSE — SPORTS COLUMN
The Weight of the Jersey
By P Slayer

I’ve been watching Ernesto Quintero since he was a junior at McClymonds.

That’s not a brag. Half of West Oakland was watching him. The other half heard about it. Kid had hands. Kid had a swing. Kid had something you can’t teach — he wanted to be there. Not in the abstract way that every high school ballplayer wants to make the majors. In the specific way. The A’s. The Coliseum. The green and gold.

He got drafted in the fourth round. He spent three years in the minors learning how to hit a slider. He came up to Las Vegas and figured it out. And now he’s here.

Here is complicated.

-----

I was at the Coliseum on Tuesday. Quintero went 0-for-4. Struck out twice. Grounded into a force play. Flew out to center on a pitch he should have driven.

The crowd didn’t boo. They didn’t cheer either. They watched.

That’s the thing about inheriting a dynasty. The expectations don’t come with instructions. Nobody tells you how to be the next Vinnie Keane because there is no next Vinnie Keane. There’s just you, standing in the same box, swinging at the same pitches, wearing the same city on your back.

Quintero knows this. He’s not stupid. He grew up six miles from the stadium. He watched the rings pile up. He knows what those three guys in the clubhouse mean to Oakland — not just to A’s fans, but to the city itself. To the idea that you can build something here, keep it here, win with it here.

He’s not replacing them. He’s what comes after.

-----

Darius Clark told me something after the game Tuesday. We were standing outside the Coliseum, near the BART bridge, and he said: “The kid’s going to be fine. He’s just got to remember he’s not carrying the whole thing. He’s carrying his part.”

Clark’s been coming to games for eleven years. He watched the rebuild. He watched the run. He watched Keane hit three home runs in a World Series game. He watched Kelley turn a double play that still shows up in highlight reels.

He’s also been waiting five months for a Stabilization Fund check that hasn’t arrived. He works at a bakery. He buys season tickets anyway.

That’s the thing about Oakland. The city doesn’t separate its stories. The kid from West Oakland is the same story as the check that hasn’t come. The dynasty ending is the same story as the firehouse that became a baseball academy. The slump is the same story as the six o’clock light on Lake Merritt.

Quintero is going to hit. I’ve watched his swing. The mechanics are there. The approach is there. The work is there. He’s in a cold stretch and cold stretches end.

But even when he heats up, he’s still going to be the kid who came after. That’s not a burden. That’s just the truth.

-----

The A’s are 4-1. Coles looks like an ace. Franco looks like a star. Richards and Horn are producing. The pitching staff is deep.

Somewhere in the middle of all that, Ernesto Quintero is figuring out what it means to be a West Oakland kid on a team that’s about to say goodbye to everything it was.

He’ll figure it out. He’s from here.
