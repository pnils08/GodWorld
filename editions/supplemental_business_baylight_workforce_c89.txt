THE CYCLE PULSE 89 — SUPPLEMENTAL — BUSINESS
The Count
By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business & Economics

THE BAR COUNT

Jax Caldera sits at the Anchor & Rail most Friday evenings. It’s a construction bar — hard hats on the hooks, boots on the rail, work talk drowning out the jukebox. Caldera has been coming here for twelve years. He knows who works what site by the dirt on their pants.

Last Friday, he counted.

“Four,” Caldera said. “Four Oakland residents. I asked around — where do you live, where’s your crew from. Four out of maybe thirty guys in here wearing Baylight badges.”

Four out of thirty is thirteen percent. The workforce agreement promises forty.

-----

THE PROMISE

The Baylight District development passed the Oakland City Council on a 6-3 vote in September 2041. It is the largest public financing commitment in the city’s history — $2.1 billion for a 35,000-seat stadium, 3,200 residential units, and sixty-five acres of waterfront redevelopment.

The workforce agreement attached to the authorization includes binding local hiring targets: forty percent of construction jobs for local residents. Thirty-five percent journey-level. Twenty-five percent apprentice. The strongest anti-displacement language built into a development agreement in years.

That forty percent translates to approximately 1,400 jobs over the project’s twelve-year timeline, with 560 designated for Oakland residents. Stadium foundation piling begins October 10.

Here is the problem: the workforce agreement defines “local” as Alameda County resident. Not Oakland. Not the neighborhoods adjacent to the construction site. Alameda County — which includes Livermore, Fremont, Hayward, and every city between here and the Altamont Pass.

An Oakland electrician competing for Baylight work is, under the agreement, no more local than a contractor commuting from Pleasanton.

-----

THE ELECTRICIANS

Kevin Kim is a master electrician. He’s lived in West Oakland for twenty-three years. He runs a small firm — four employees, all Oakland residents, all licensed.

He applied for two Baylight electrical subcontracts this spring. Both went to firms based in Livermore.

“I meet every requirement,” Kim said. “Bonded, insured, certified, local. And I lost to companies that drive an hour to get here. They’re ‘local’ too, under the agreement. That’s the loophole.”

Javier Harris is also an electrician. Also West Oakland. He submitted his paperwork through the union hall in April, applying for Baylight electrical work.

“They told me the roster was full,” Harris said. “Full. In April. Before they broke ground. I’ve been doing electrical work in this city for fifteen years. I’m on every list. And I can’t get on that site.”

-----

THE LABORER

Omar Allen is a construction laborer. He lives in Uptown — eight blocks from the Baylight footprint. He’s worked sites across Oakland for a decade. He is not on the Baylight site.

“I don’t know why,” Allen said. “I applied. I followed up. Nobody called me back. I see guys out there every day on my commute. I don’t recognize any of them.”

Allen asked around. He talked to guys he’d worked with on other jobs. A few had gotten calls. Most hadn’t.

“The ones who got in — they all came through out-of-town subs,” he said. “San Leandro. Hayward. The firms that got the packages aren’t from here.”

-----

THE ONE WHO GOT IN

Shawn Nguyen is 43 years old. He’s a construction laborer from Fruitvale. He drives Telegraph Avenue every day, past the Baylight site.

He got hours on the project. Not through an Oakland firm — through a San Leandro company that picked up a concrete finishing package.

“I’m on the site,” Nguyen said. “But I got there through the back door. My buddy knew a foreman at a sub that’s based in San Leandro. He put my name in. That’s how it works.”

Nguyen is one of the Oakland residents Jax Caldera might have counted at the Anchor & Rail. He’s also proof that the pathway to Baylight jobs for Oakland workers runs through other cities.

“The agreement says local hire,” Nguyen said. “It doesn’t say Oakland hire. And the subs that got the contracts — they’re not from Oakland. So they hire who they know. And who they know isn’t us.”

-----

THE MATH

Aaliyah Baker is a workforce development researcher who has studied local hire agreements across California. She reviewed the Baylight workforce framework for a labor advocacy group earlier this year.

“The top-line numbers look good,” Baker said. “Forty percent is a strong target. But the numbers don’t reflect what happens at the second and third subcontractor levels. The prime contractor hires a sub. The sub hires a sub. By the time you get to the workers actually swinging hammers, the local hire language has diluted down to nothing.”

Mateo Walker is an electrician and trade union representative at the Port of Oakland. He’s reviewed the Baylight sub-tier agreements.

“There’s no Oakland-specific language below the first tier,” Walker said. “The workforce agreement binds the prime. It doesn’t bind the subs. And the subs are where the jobs are.”

The Baylight Redevelopment Authority, led by Director Keisha Ramos, has confirmed that all five September 15 deliverables — including the workforce development agreement with binding local hiring targets — have been filed. Ramos did not respond to a request for comment on the definition of “local” or the sub-tier enforcement mechanism.

-----

THE COUNT, AGAIN

Jax Caldera plans to go back to the Anchor & Rail this Friday. He’s going to count again.

“Four out of thirty,” he said. “Maybe it’s different this week. Maybe more Oakland guys got on. But I’ve been watching construction in this city for a long time. I know what a local crew looks like. That’s not what I’m seeing.”

Stadium foundation piling begins October 10. The twelve-year timeline starts. The forty percent promise will either hold or it won’t.

The people counting aren’t waiting for quarterly reports. They’re sitting at the bar, asking where you live.
