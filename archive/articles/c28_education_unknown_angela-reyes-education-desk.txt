ANGELA REYES — Education Desk
“The Teacher Who Called It First: When Classroom Behavior Signals Community Stress”
Cycle 28 – Oakland Unified School District Pattern Recognition
Lily Tran, 27, teaches fourth grade at Laurel Elementary. She noticed the behavioral shift three cycles before the health advisory was issued.
“Cycle 26, Monday morning. Twenty-two students in my classroom. Eighteen of them couldn’t sit still. Not hyperactive—that’s different. This was restless. Like they were listening for something.”
Lily has been teaching for four years. She knows her students’ baseline behaviors. What she saw during Cycle 26 didn’t fit any pattern she’d encountered.
“I’d give instructions—simple ones, like ‘take out your math workbook’—and three or four kids would just stare at me. Not defiant. Not distracted. Just… blank. Then ten seconds later they’d snap back and ask ‘what did you say?’ Like they’d been gone somewhere.”
Lily mentioned it to the school counselor, David Okonkwo (no relation to Dr. James Okonkwo at Highland Hospital), 43, who works across three elementary schools in the Laurel and Fruitvale neighborhoods.
“Lily wasn’t the only teacher reporting it. By Cycle 28, I had reports from seven teachers across two schools. Same pattern: students losing focus mid-task, restlessness, difficulty following multi-step instructions. Initially we thought it was post-holiday syndrome—kids adjusting back to routine after time off. But this was different.”
The school board dispute during Cycle 30—the one that extended a meeting by two hours due to procedural conflict—was partially triggered by a budget amendment proposal to increase mental health resources in elementary schools.
Board member Patricia Ruiz, 51, who lives in Temescal, told me:
“Teachers were reporting that students seemed ‘off.’ Not sick, not misbehaving, just subtly different. We proposed redirecting funds to add counseling hours. The amendment got misfiled—someone’s error, not intentional—and when we tried to table it for emergency review, procedural rules prevented it. That’s what turned a routine vote into a two-hour fight.”
The “teacher highlight” event logged during Cycle 28 was actually a commendation for Sarah Chen, 34, a second-grade teacher at an Uptown elementary school who created a “calm corner” in her classroom during Cycle 27—before the health advisories were issued.
I spoke with Sarah, who lives in Piedmont Avenue:
“I noticed kids were anxious. Not about anything specific. Just anxious. So I set up a corner with pillows, quiet books, and a sand timer. If a student felt overwhelmed, they could go sit for five minutes. By Cycle 30, I had a waiting list. Kids were using the calm corner ten, twelve times a day. That’s not normal.”
Marcus Kim (mentioned earlier as owner of Warm Hearth Kitchen) has a daughter in third grade at a Temescal school. He noticed the change at home:
“She started asking weird questions during Cycles 28-30. ‘Dad, can you forget things that just happened?’ ‘Dad, does time ever skip?’ I thought she was being philosophical. Then her teacher called and said six kids in the class had reported ‘time feeling weird.’ That’s when I realized it wasn’t just my daughter.”
The health cluster advisories documented seven cases in Laurel and six in Uptown—all adults. But how many children experienced similar symptoms and didn’t report them?
David Okonkwo, the school counselor:
“Kids process environmental stress differently than adults. They might not describe it as ‘dizziness’ or ‘disorientation.’ They’ll say ‘I forgot what I was doing’ or ‘everything went fuzzy for a second.’ If we’re only counting formal reports to medical providers, we’re missing the pediatric presentation.”
Lily Tran has been documenting her classroom observations since Cycle 26. She shared her notes with me—specific incidents, timestamps, student behaviors:
	•	Cycle 26: Student froze mid-sentence during reading circle, resumed 10 seconds later with no memory of pause
	•	Cycle 28: Three students reported “feeling dizzy” during recess, no medical cause found
	•	Cycle 30: Entire class went silent simultaneously for approximately 8 seconds during math lesson, then resumed as if nothing happened
	•	Cycle 32: Student asked “did we already do this?” about a lesson happening in real-time
“I reported every incident to administration,” Lily told me. “I was told I was being overly cautious. Then the health advisories came out and suddenly everyone wanted my notes.”
Alameda County Public Health has since requested documentation from all schools in Laurel and Uptown neighborhoods. The pediatric assessment begins next cycle.
Angela Martinez (no relation to Elena Martinez, the climate researcher), 45, a teacher at a Chinatown elementary school, reached out after reading the health advisory:
“We saw it too. Not as severe, but the same pattern. Kids losing focus, reporting dizzy spells, asking if ‘time was acting weird.’ But we’re not in Laurel or Uptown, so we weren’t included in the cluster zone. Should we have been?”
That’s the question facing the investigation now: How large is the affected area? And if children are experiencing milder symptoms than adults, how many cases have gone unreported?
Lily Tran is back in her classroom, still documenting. The calm corner still has a waiting list.
“I don’t know what this is,” she told me. “But teachers see things first. We’re with kids six hours a day. We notice when something’s wrong before parents do, before doctors do. And something was wrong. Maybe still is.”
