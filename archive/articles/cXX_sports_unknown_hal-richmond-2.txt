Hal Richmond
Part III — Isley Kelley and Rickey Henderson: Two Engines, One Franchise

Oakland has produced power hitters, aces, glove-first defenders, and everything in between. But only twice in its long history has it produced a player who functioned as the engine of the entire team — the player who makes the rest of the lineup make sense.

The first was Rickey Henderson.
The second is Isley Kelley.

The comparison is not about style.
Rickey was a leadoff force — speed, disruption, on-base chaos.
Kelley is a middle-order terror — power, discipline, and gap-to-gap violence.

But when you strip away the differences in body type, position, and era?
The heart of the comparison becomes impossible to ignore:

Both players drove winning by the sheer volume of opportunities they created.

Look at Kelley’s peak stretch from 2031 through 2037 — seven straight seasons with 180+ hits, five seasons above 190, and a career-high 206 hits in 2032. Every year of that run, he posted an OPS in the .530–.590 slugging range, nearly identical OBP, and RBI totals that pushed or passed one hundred with room to spare.

Numbers like:

2032: 206 hits, 44 HR, 128 RBI, .326/.396/.585
2035: 194 hits, 43 HR, 124 RBI, .305/.373/.582
2037: 195 hits, 40 HR, 122 RBI, .310/.375/.591

Those aren’t ordinary All-Star seasons.
They are seasons teams build around.

Now consider Rickey’s peaks — the 1980s seasons where he led the league in runs scored, times on base, and stolen bases like he was inventing the categories in real time. Henderson changed the shape of baseball games. He controlled every inning he entered.

Kelley changes the pace of games.
Different toolset — same weight on the outcome.

Rickey’s version of pressure was psychological. Pitchers sped up, infields stepped in, the entire battery panicked before the pitch. Kelley’s pressure is physical. His contact is loud, his misses are dangerous, and his presence in the three-hole forces pitchers into the stretch even when the bases are empty.

They both came with durability that bordered on myth.
Rickey played forever.
Kelley has played 14 straight seasons with no decline, and his numbers in 2039 — 187 hits, 41 doubles, 28 HR — suggest age is something that happens to other players.

The franchise has always needed an engine.
Rickey was the one who pulled Oakland out of anonymity.
Kelley is the one who stabilized an empire.

It is not about who was better — the eras are too different, the sport too changed.
It is about how rare it is for a team to find a player who gives it identity every single night.

Oakland has done it twice.
And the echoes between them are louder than the box scores admit.
