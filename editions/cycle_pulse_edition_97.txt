### Two Came Due at Once: A Festival City Answers Its Own Calls

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

A woman on 34th Avenue has been keeping the same altar for eleven years. Same table, same marigolds, same photograph of her mother centered behind the candles. What changes each cycle is how many neighbors stop to stand with her — and this year, by her account, the line ran down to the corner.

"People are coming back," she said Friday evening, candle smoke cutting through the cold air. "Or they're coming for the first time. Either way, they're here."

The numbers behind the feeling are real. Ten neighborhoods climbed this cycle on their own, without a program driving it. Fruitvale came up hardest — the gathering corridors packed for Día de los Muertos, First Friday galleries stretching past their usual foot traffic, congregations moving between services the way people do when a neighborhood feels worth crossing for. Migration into the city has accelerated three cycles running. This is not a recovery story. Oakland does not need recovering. This is what the city looks like when it is simply working.

Into that city, on the same cycle, two things arrived.

---

The first: OARI — the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative — answered its first calls across all nine council districts. Not a pilot. Not a district-by-district rollout waiting on one more vote. Citywide, this cycle, for the first time, a co-responder team picked up calls that did not require a badge and a gun. The program had been running in three districts since its launch. Mayor Avery Santana moved it to every corner of the city this week.

Santana has been deliberate about the pace. When the program launched, she gave it room to run — enough calls to generate real data, enough time for the co-responder teams to find their rhythm inside a system that had not been built for them. The expansion is not an act of faith in the model. It is a conclusion drawn from what the model produced.

What it means for residents is simpler than the policy language: if a call comes in the night that is about crisis rather than crime, the person who shows up now may not be a uniformed officer. Across all nine districts. Starting now.

The operational shape of the expansion — how the dispatch routing works, where the co-responders are stationed, what calls they are and are not picking up — belongs to a separate account. What the front page carries is the fact of it: a program the city argued over for years is, as of this cycle, answering Oakland.

---

The second: a groundbreaking in Fruitvale.

The Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II has been a promise with a price tag for long enough that the community sessions around it became their own civic institution. People showed up not because they believed it would happen but because they had learned to document the cycle. What gets said. What gets committed. What slips.

This cycle, a shovel went into the ground.

The project — $230 million, designed to connect rail and bus service across the district's main corridors — broke ground on a day when Fruitvale was already the most alive corner of the city. The festival context was not incidental. Fruitvale has carried the argument for the Hub precisely because it is a neighborhood that knows what it looks like when people choose to be somewhere. The Hub is a bet that more of them will.

What gets built from here, and how the community benefit agreements attach to the construction timeline, belongs to the infrastructure coverage. What the front page records is that the ground moved.

---

Carmen Delaine has covered City Hall for eleven years. She has watched more than one promising program arrive, get named, get funded, and then get quiet. OARI citywide and a transit hub breaking ground in the same cycle, inside a city moving in the direction it is moving — that is not a coincidence. That is what it looks like when decisions accumulate into something.

The woman at the altar did not know either program by name when asked. She knew the altar had more company this year. She knew the neighborhood had felt different lately — not safer exactly, just less braced.

"Something shifted," she said. "I don't know when. But it shifted."

The city's record of the cycle will say two things happened. The streets knew it before the paperwork did.

---

### Citywide Means Citywide: OARI Begins Answering Every District

By Sgt. Rachel Torres | Bay Tribune Public Safety

---

When a behavioral-health crisis call comes in now, dispatch doesn't ask which district. It dispatches OARI.

That shift — quiet in administrative language, significant in operational practice — took effect this cycle as the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative moved from pilot evaluation to live citywide coverage. For the first time since the program's authorization in Cycle 82, every district in the city sits inside the response footprint.

The arrangement Chief Rafael Montez has formalized is a division of labor, not a competition. OARI responders — trained behavioral-health professionals, not sworn officers — take the lead on crisis calls where the presenting issue is mental health, substance, or acute distress without an active threat component. OPD stages nearby and steps in only when a call presents danger that a clinician cannot safely manage. Two categories. Two lanes. One dispatch.

"The classification decision happens at intake," Montez has described the framework. "We're not improvising at the curb."

---

**First Cycle Numbers**

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, OARI's director, reported the program's first full citywide cycle: response times held near six minutes, escalation to sworn-officer involvement remained rare. She has not characterized the numbers as a verdict — one cycle is a baseline, not a trend — but the holding pattern matches what the pilot districts produced in previous cycles.

West Oakland and Fruitvale carried that pilot experience into this cycle. Temescal ran alongside them. The balance of the city's districts received OARI coverage for the first time.

D8 Councilmember Nina Chen, whose district now runs inside the program's coverage area, has kept a specific eye on costs since the original 5-4 authorization vote. The program's $12.5 million behavioral-health budget was a point of contention then; it remains a measuring stick now. Her district's early numbers will be part of the accounting she's watching.

D7 Councilmember Warren Ashford has held a parallel position — program cost against program output. The first citywide cycle gives both members their first data from a scaled operation rather than a selective one.

---

**What the Reporting Standard Requires**

Mayor Avery Santana has bound her administration to a public-reporting standard for OARI: call volume, resolution type, response time, and community feedback — published every cycle. That commitment went into effect alongside the citywide expansion.

D2 Councilmember Leonard Tran pressed specifically for the community-voice component, representing the concerns of Chinatown and downtown residents who want their own assessment of the program's performance built into the official record — not just aggregate incident data, but direct resident input. The reporting framework now includes that channel.

The practical effect: the program cannot drift from its stated mandate without the drift showing up in the numbers. Every cycle's data sits against the previous one.

---

**Vega's Position Settles**

D4 Councilmember Ramon Vega voted against OARI authorization in Cycle 82. He held that reservation through the pilot phase. This cycle, with his district now inside the coverage area, he has lifted it.

No floor statement, no press announcement. The district's place in the rollout resolved the question he'd carried. That's a notable closing of the record for a program that passed by a single vote.

---

**The Division of Labor in Practice**

The structural question for the OPD/OARI seam has always been: who decides, at what moment, which responder leads? The Montez framework answers that at dispatch rather than at the scene. Calls categorized as behavioral-health crisis route to OARI first. Officers don't stand down — they stage — but the default presence at a behavioral-health call is now a clinician, not a badge.

What the first citywide cycle tests is whether that intake classification holds under volume. A six-minute response across nine districts, with escalations rare, is one data point. The public-reporting standard Mayor Santana committed to means that data point will have company next cycle, and the one after that.

The division of labor is operational now. The numbers will say whether it holds.

---

*OARI operates under INIT-002, authorized Cycle 82, 5-4. Citywide dispatch effective this cycle. Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, OARI Director. Chief Rafael Montez, OPD.*

### Ground Breaks in Fruitvale: The Transit Hub Enters Construction

By Trevor Shimizu | Bay Tribune Transit & Infrastructure

---

On a Tuesday morning in Fruitvale, the machines arrived.

Not the ribbon-cutting kind of arrival — a crane offloading to a cleared lot, survey stakes already driven, a site trailer positioned where the east pedestrian plaza will eventually stand. Elena Soria Dominguez, the planning lead for the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II, walked the perimeter before the ceremony started and pointed to the utility corridor markings in orange spray paint. The underground work begins first. It always does.

The $230 million project has been in design and environmental review long enough that the groundbreaking felt, to some in attendance, less like a beginning than a confirmation. What it confirmed: the Community Benefits Agreement — still in final draft but binding before any construction permit issues — will govern what happens on this site from the first footing to the last facade panel.

The CBA carries three structural commitments. Local hiring at negotiated thresholds. Mam-language interpretation, already operational in the planning process and continuing through construction outreach. And apprenticeship slots tied directly to the city's Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline. Soria Dominguez confirmed all three are embedded in permit conditions, not aspirational language. The regional authority submission is filed and awaiting its hearing date.

Rose Delgado, who represents the Third District and has carried the Hub through council, stood at the groundbreaking and did not soften the terms when asked about them afterward. The CBA language, she said, is not subject to value-engineering. The community benefits are the project. If they slip, the project has slipped.

The Mam-language commitment is worth holding separately from the other two. Professional interpretation running through a major infrastructure build — before construction, not after community complaints — is an operational decision that does not happen by default. Soria Dominguez reported it as already functioning in planning meetings. The question for the construction phase is whether it persists through subcontractor coordination, not just public outreach events.

Fruitvale itself arrived at this groundbreaking from a different position than it occupied when the Hub was first designed. The neighborhood's median income sits above $73,000. Sentiment is running higher this cycle than anywhere else in the city. Crime is effectively flat at baseline. The displacement pressure the Hub's critics feared during earlier design phases is, by current measure, absent. That doesn't make the CBA less necessary — infrastructure at this scale shapes a neighborhood's trajectory across decades, not a single cycle. But it does mean the project breaks ground on rising ground, not contested ground.

The apprenticeship integration slots the Hub into a city-wide training architecture that connects to the youth pipeline authorized earlier this cycle. Fruitvale residents already working trades — mechanics, electricians, the construction workforce that lives close enough to walk the job — are the intended pipeline population. Whether those slots fill from the neighborhood depends on outreach mechanics that the CBA mandates but doesn't automate.

The underground utility work sets the sequence. Foundation piling follows. The timeline that Soria Dominguez described runs through multiple construction seasons, with the regional authority hearing still pending. What broke ground Tuesday is the build's infrastructure layer — the part that doesn't photograph well but determines everything that comes after.

The CBA final draft is expected to be executed concurrent with permit issuance. Until that signature, the commitments exist in the space between binding and official. Soria Dominguez said that gap is measured in days, not cycles. Delgado, standing near the survey stakes before the ceremony dispersed, said she would hold the timeline to that.

The machines were still running when the officials left.

### West Oakland Gets Safer as the Apprenticeship Bill It's Waiting On Heads to a Vote

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

West Oakland's violent crime numbers are at their lowest point in cycles. That is not a qualifier. That is not a "but." It is the first fact.

The second fact: the bill designed to build an economic life alongside that safety — the Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline — sat untouched at legislation-filed for four consecutive cycles before this one. Filed, announced, praised at a press conference. Then nothing.

Four cycles is a long time to wait on a promise.

---

Mayor Santana moved on it this cycle. She directed Councilmember Janae Rivers (D5) to bring INIT-007 to the council floor, and Rivers took it as the Oakland Progressive Party's own. That is a meaningful shift. A bill sitting in committee is a proposal. A bill on the floor is a reckoning.

"West Oakland is getting safer," said Councilmember Denise Carter (D1), who represents the district and has watched both developments in parallel. "The work people are doing on the ground is real. Now let's give them something to come home to."

Carter has said versions of this before. The difference is that this cycle, Rivers has a calendar date to stand behind.

---

The pipeline — $12.5 million when it was announced — is designed to move young Oaklanders into paid trade apprenticeships with certified employers. The theory is straightforward: lower barriers to skilled-trade entry, widen the pool, reduce the cycle of underemployment that tracks alongside neighborhood instability.

West Oakland is the test case nobody authorized. Its safety numbers improved without the pipeline. Without the economic infrastructure the pipeline would fund. Without the living-wage placements the bill is supposed to generate.

That is either evidence that public safety investments work on their own timeline, or it is a question about what happens when the economic half doesn't follow.

---

Enzo Walker knows something about both halves.

Walker, a West Oakland tradesman, was recognized this cycle with new responsibilities in his field — the kind of advancement the pipeline is designed to make more common, not exceptional. He got there through a path that was harder to find than it needed to be.

He is one person. The pipeline is trying to make one person a recurring story.

---

The vote has not happened. Rivers will carry it to the floor. The council will then decide.

What can be tallied now is the gap: four cycles between announcement and floor motion, in a neighborhood that did not stop improving while it waited.

The accountability question is not whether West Oakland deserves this bill. The evidence on that is in the crime figures. The question is why a bill with this district's numbers behind it needed a mayoral directive to move at all.

---

*Luis Navarro covers investigations and accountability for the Bay Tribune.*

### A Modest Uptick Downtown — Watched, Not Feared

By Sgt. Rachel Torres | Bay Tribune Public Safety

---

Downtown recorded a small rise in violent incidents this cycle — a two-point move against a baseline that has been nearly flat for several cycles running. Chief Montez acknowledged it at Tuesday's department briefing without ceremony: something to monitor, not something to sound an alarm over.

That restraint is worth noting. A department that's had a rough relationship with its own credibility has learned, slowly, that inflating routine variance into crisis is its own kind of damage. This cycle's number is above the recent trend. It is not a spike. It is not a pattern. It is a data point, and the department is treating it like one.

OARI's co-responder teams now answer across every district, downtown included. No downtown incident in this cycle's count has been linked to a gap in that coverage. Montez said his office will have a fuller picture next cycle.

A calm city can hold an uptick without flinching. For now, this one is.

### Nearly One in Ten: Fall Illness Holds Steady

By Dr. Lila Mezran | Bay Tribune Health Desk

---

As the city moves into fall, roughly one in ten residents is dealing with some form of illness — a figure that has been sitting at that level long enough to be a season's fact rather than a warning.

Clinics across Oakland, including the Temescal Health Center, are watching the number the same way a nurse watches a patient's breathing: steadily, without alarm. This isn't a spike. It's the undertone the season carries, and the distinction matters. A spike demands a response. A plateau demands attention — which is different.

What bears watching is whether it holds. Fall historically softens these numbers before winter tightens them again. If the city's clinics stay staffed and the Temescal expansion continues on pace, the infrastructure is there to absorb a modest seasonal rise without the waiting-room backup that turns a manageable illness count into something harder to clear.

For now, the trend is steady. Nearly one in ten — present, noted, monitored.

### First Friday Meets the Day of the Dead: An Art Walk Through a City at Its Warmest

By Kai Marston | Bay Tribune Culture

---

The evening splits the difference between two traditions, and the city shows up for both.

Telegraph between 19th and 23rd fills first—the galleries opening their doors all at once, the sidewalk turning into an extension of the wall space, people moving between frames and conversation with the same easy rhythm. At Art Walk Cafe, the line for coffee wraps around the corner by 7:30. The air holds the smell of espresso and marigolds—those thick, peppery blooms shipped in by the armload, showing up in window arrangements across Fruitvale, in handmade arrangements at storefronts and home altars, orange and gold against white walls and painted wood.

It's not a collision between the two nights so much as a recognition. First Friday's been happening for eleven years—galleries on rotation, the same routes worn into habit. Día de los Muertos arrives every year like clockwork, with its own altars and its own pacing, remembrance moving at a different tempo than art viewing. But tonight they share the same weather, the same evening light, the same city in motion, and the line between observer and witness gets softer.

The lounges glow by 8. Dragon Gate in Chinatown spills warm light onto the sidewalk—the kind of corner bar where the music drifts out onto the street and something about the threshold dissolves. Gallery Night Lounge in Uptown fills the same way: bodies and voices and the kind of ease that means people have stopped thinking about where they're supposed to be and started just being there. Blue Lantern in Jack London holds its own quiet, the street outside darker but still alive with the sound of conversation drifting from the doorway.

Over in Fruitvale, Marigold Cafe hums with a different energy. The tables spill into the street in a way that feels less like a tactical opening and more like the building itself has decided to breathe outward. The flowers are everywhere here—not decoration, something closer to architecture, the way they frame doorways and cluster on corners. People stand in front of altars in their own homes and in public spaces, and the distinction between private and witnessed starts to matter less.

There's a street-performer snag in KONO—a musician trying to set up near the gallery corridor without the right permit, the kind of small friction that proves a scene is alive and not just a carefully managed event. The bureaucracy moves in, the musician keeps tuning, the crowd keeps walking. By the time the light really goes gold and then darkens toward blue, there's a kind of acceptance that the evening belongs to whoever shows up.

Vinnie Keane drifts through the corridor somewhere around 9, his presence the kind of thing people register the way they'd notice a familiar song—a passing warmth, a name moving through conversation. Pixel Pete's somewhere in the mix too, the way he always is when the city decides to be visible. Brody Kale catches the corner light near one of the Uptown galleries, moving through the space the way people do when they know everyone's watching and don't much care.

But the night doesn't anchor on them. It anchors on the smaller details: the couple standing in front of a gallery window at Art Walk Cafe, neither one moving, just looking. The family gathered at an altar in someone's front window, the orange flowers glowing in candlelight. The musician finally setting up near the gallery corridor, the sound checking working, the crowd gathering because music has started and the street knows what to do with that.

Come ten o'clock, the city feels like it's breathing the same way—First Friday's rhythm and Día de los Muertos' remembrance moving at once, the evening warm enough that nobody's in any hurry to leave. The galleries stay open. The lounges keep pouring. The flowers don't fade.

This is the warmest the city has felt in cycles.

### The Season the Congregations Carried — and the Shortstop in a Deacon's Robes

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

The doors opened Saturday morning at St. Esperanza Parish on International Boulevard in Fruitvale, and the smell of marigolds came first — fresh-cut stems in tall glass vases flanking the entrance, some of them already browning at the edges. Father Ramon Solano stood at the threshold in his white alb, greeting people as they came in with the kind of slowness that says he had nowhere else to be. There were photographs on tables inside — ancestors, mostly — and candles that people lit without being asked. The light moved across the worn wood of the pews. I stood in the back for a moment and watched a woman in a black dress touch one of the photographs, then touch her forehead, then sit down.

All Saints had come to the city, and the congregations were carrying the season in ways the rest of us had mostly let slide.

Three neighborhoods over, in Lake Merritt, the Cathedral of the Living Word — all pale stone and glass — had thrown its doors open too. Bishop Antoine Vermeer moves through that space like he was built for it, tall and deliberate, and he'd organized the sanctuary for the week as a gathering place for people who needed one. Not a service, exactly. A place. He told me the Cathedral stays open in the evening, and people come. They sit. Some of them pray. Some of them just sit. "The season teaches people to remember," he said, and he wasn't wrong — there's something about October that loosens people's grip on the everyday.

But the thread that held the whole thing together — that made this something more than five separate congregations each tending their own corners — was a man in the wrong clothes for his day job.

Isley Kelley, the A's shortstop, was vested as a guest deacon at Foothill Baptist Tabernacle in East Oakland. Dr. Ophelia Brenner, the church's senior pastor, had asked him to take the role for the season's observance, and he'd said yes. Kelley has a theology degree — most people don't know that. He leads the league in batting average. He's a free agent after this season. And last Sunday, he stood in front of his congregation in a deacon's stole and helped lead a prayer.

I met him after the service. He was still in the robes, and the ordinary world hadn't quite caught up with him yet.

"It's two things at once," he said, and he wasn't apologizing or explaining — just stating it. "The faith is older than the baseball. Both are real. This season — All Saints, Día de los Muertos — it's when you get to remember that there are more of us than just the people we see every day. The people who were here first. The ones we pray for." He paused. "Dr. Brenner gave me space to do both. That's rare."

Across Oakland, it was. At Bharat Mandir of the East Bay in Montclair, Diwali had come to the season too — lights strung around the temple in the autumn dusk, families coming early to set up, the smell of ghee and cardamom rising from the kitchen. The congregation there was doing what congregations do when they carry a season: they were making room for the weight of it, the remembering of it, the small rituals that say *this matters, and we are here for it*.

I walked back out into the city at dusk and realized that the season wasn't something the city was having. The season was something the congregations were holding, and the rest of us were just walking through it, noticing if we looked up. Kelley, in those robes, at that pulpit — he wasn't breaking his world in half. He was living the whole of it, and letting the city see what it looks like when someone refuses to leave any part of themselves outside a doorway.

The congregations know something about that. The season knows it too.

### Marigolds and Gallery Light: The City Out Walking

By Mason Ortega | Bay Tribune Culture

---

Día de los Muertos pulls the city to its tables. I spent the evening at Marigold Cafe in Fruitvale, where the counter stools filled early and stayed full—families, old friends, neighbors who only see each other once a year. The kitchen never stopped. What struck me wasn't the speed but the care: each plate a gesture, the right spice, the remembered recipe. The cooks weren't rushing. They were honoring.

The marigolds on every table glowed under the warm bulbs. In KONO, the Creative Cuisine kitchens opened their doors late, the Art Walk galleries still lit next door, people moving between them—a kind of remembrance in motion, carrying the city's memory forward in motion, in food, in light. No ceremony, no speeches. Just tables full and the knowledge that this was the night to sit still and eat what the people you love made for you.

One server, wiping her hands on her apron between tables, said it plain: "This is the work we do to say they're still here."

The marigolds glowed. The tables stayed full.

### Paulson Walks the Baylight Site: 'What I See Will Rival What's Across the Bay'

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

---

Mike Paulson moved through the Baylight District construction site this week with the deliberation of a man reading a box score. Foundation poured. Pillars set for Civis Systems Field. The new ballpark taking shape at the center of a much larger argument about what a waterfront district becomes when a $2.1 billion commitment lays down all at once.

"When you see the foundation laid, the pillars set, you know a plan can be executed," Paulson said, standing at the site line where heavy equipment moved through the skeleton of what will be the A's home next season. "That's how you become great. This is how Oakland became great."

But the stadium is not the district. That distinction matters, and Paulson made it explicit. "You see the new A's stadium being built, but as you approach the build site you pass by what will be housing, retail, and corporate headquarters. What I see will rival what's across the Bay."

The ownership ecosystem anchoring the development is Elias Varek's Civis Systems, the urban logistics intelligence firm that has already embedded itself into Phase II planning. Varek, 38, founded the company on routing models and emergency-response algorithms — the systems that move freight through a port, coordinate construction sequences, manage utilities during crisis. The firm has forty employees and $60 million in annual revenue. Its corporate headquarters will occupy space within the Baylight footprint itself.

The threading is clear: Varek built a business modeling how cities work when systems talk to each other. Now he owns a team and anchors a district built on the same principle — ballpark, housing, retail, and command center not as separate ventures but as one integrated development.

The waterfront is stable. Crime remains negligible. Retail is running strong. Median household income in Jack London — the neighborhood where Baylight rises — sits north of $113,000. There are no signs of displacement, no gentrification pressure. The district is rising into an existing economic floor, not displacing one.

What Paulson saw at the site was the beginning of execution — a timeline that commits the A's to a new home and commits the waterfront to a different class of development than it has carried before. Whether the housing stock anchors local residents or draws new money, whether the retail serves neighborhood or appeals to tourists, whether the corporate presence strengthens local supply chains or imports them — those are the questions the next two cycles will answer as the build accelerates and the district takes actual shape.

For now, the foundation holds. The plan is visible. And Paulson, standing at the center of it, frames it the way a front-office man frames a roster built to compete: as the architecture of something that works.

### The Crowded Corner: Davis Returns and Paulson's Roster Math Gets Hard

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The Oakland A's are forty-six and twelve, leading the American League West by eleven and a half games, and have produced what may be the single most dominant first third of a season in franchise history. None of that is the problem. The problem is that the dynasty's success has now manufactured a roster architecture so compressed at the corner positions that Mike Paulson — who has spent five years building exactly this kind of depth — is approaching the trade deadline with decisions he cannot defer much longer.

The clearest case is Darrin Davis, the reigning Most Valuable Player, who returned from the disabled list last week and cannot, by medical instruction and by the honest accounting of what he is at thirty-eight years old, be counted on to hold down left field for a full second half and a playoff run. Davis is a DH now. Not nominally, not as a rest-day accommodation, but structurally. His knees have said so, and Paulson is too careful a builder to argue with structural facts. Davis in left field is a liability the A's cannot carry into October. Davis at designated hitter, with that left-handed swing still among the five most dangerous in the American League, is an asset they cannot afford to bench.

The complication is Ernesto Quintero.

Quintero, twenty-four years old, with no natural DH profile — his primary positions are third base and first base — is currently leading Major League Baseball in home runs and has been one of the three or four best hitters in the sport since the season's opening week. What Paulson had projected as a breakout transition season has arrived six months early and six watts hotter than anticipated. Quintero at designated hitter is not a deployment problem; it is the deployment problem, because it is now directly competing with where Davis has to go.

The corner positions offer no relief. At third base, Martin Richards carries an eighty-eight overall rating, has won a Gold Glove, and is a free agent at season's end. Mark Aitken at first base grades out at ninety-four overall, three Gold Gloves, a franchise cornerstone — also a free agent. Quintero's natural homes are sitting behind two of the better defenders in the American League, both of whom Oakland may not be able to re-sign after November given what a Quintero extension, a Davis extension, and the broader payroll architecture will demand.

Buford Park has spent this season getting intermittent at-bats and none of the everyday development work that a young corner bat requires. Kevin Clark has left AAA hitters thoroughly exhausted and out of new ideas. Both are ready for real opportunity. Neither has a spot right now.

At shortstop, the picture is different but no less complicated. Isley Kelley is also a free agent after this season, and in Bryan Franco the A's have a rookie backup who has not hit well enough to project as a starter — which means if Kelley walks, Oakland enters next spring with a genuine hole at a premium defensive position rather than a smooth handoff. Franco's development arc has not closed the gap that a Kelley departure would create.

What Paulson actually faces, laid out plainly, is a roster that has built itself into a beautiful, insoluble knot. The depth is real. The problem is that depth only generates value when it can be converted — into a trade, into a platoon, into a clear path for someone — and right now every conversion carries a significant cost.

Trading Richards before the deadline addresses the third base jam but relinquishes an eighty-eight overall defender in the middle of a title run, in a division race that is comfortable but not clinched. Trading Quintero is not a serious conversation; you do not trade the MLB home run leader at twenty-four. A Davis platoon or bench role would be palatable for almost any other player and is almost certainly untenable for this one, whose competitive gravity and market value and simple standing as the reigning MVP make a reduced role something that would have to be negotiated rather than managed.

The free agency calculus runs in the background of every move. Letting Richards and Aitken walk costs Oakland nothing in trade return but opens the corners for Quintero, Park, and Clark starting in March — at the price of replacing two veteran defenders mid-dynasty with prospects who have not yet proven they can hold those positions through a postseason. Letting Kelley walk without Franco ready to start is a different kind of risk, one that cuts at the defensive spine of an infield that has been a significant part of why the pitching staff's numbers look the way they do.

Paulson has made harder decisions than this one. The Busch trade this spring — sending a significant package to Cleveland for Liam Wade, with minor league pitching depth as part of the cost — showed a front office willing to absorb short-term friction for structural benefit. The deadline, which arrives in roughly six weeks, will ask something similar: not whether the A's will win the West, which is settled, but whether Paulson can untangle this particular corner of the roster without giving up either the run or the future he has been building toward since the first ring.

The math is hard. It is supposed to be hard. Building a team this good creates exactly this kind of problem.

---

*Anthony Raines covers the Oakland A's for the Bay Tribune. He has covered the franchise since the first championship cycle.*

### Six Straight and a Debut: The A's Pull Away as Morton Arrives

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

There's a traffic jam on Hegenberger that doesn't clear until nearly first pitch, and nobody cares. The kids from the youth clinic are still in their A's jerseys streaming through the gates, and the guy selling elotes outside the plaza is out of everything by the time the national anthem ends, and I'm standing in line for a beer I don't need because I just want to stay in this moment a little longer. This is what a dream season feels like from the outside. I needed to be in it.

Forty-six and twelve. Six straight wins. The Phillies came to town and went home with nothing — we outscored them twenty-four to three across the series and the pitching staff barely broke a sweat. The Royals didn't fare better. Six games, six wins, and the margin of victory each time felt less like competition and more like the A's demonstrating something for themselves. Like they're testing a ceiling that keeps going up.

And then there's Desmond Morton.

I want to tell you exactly where I was when Morton stepped in for his first major-league at-bat, but honestly the section went a little blurry. He's twenty-two, twenty-three maybe, walking into a lineup that already has Ernesto Quintero leading the entire league in home runs and Isley Kelley hitting for an average that makes other hitters cry into their batting gloves at two in the morning. He's a prospect. He's supposed to be overwhelmed. He's supposed to look young.

He went three-for-four. Two runs. Two RBI. He looked like he was already home.

I've been watching A's baseball long enough to remember when "the future is bright" was something you said because there was nothing good to say about the present. The future was always somewhere else, always next year, always a prospect ranked too high to be real. Morton is what happens when the future meets a team that doesn't need saving anymore. He didn't have to carry anything. He just had to show up and play baseball, and the guy walked in and played baseball.

Mark Aitken hit three home runs in a single game this series. Three. I saw a kid in the section next to me count them on his fingers like he was doing arithmetic he wasn't sure he trusted. Aitken put his head down and rounded the bases all three times the same way — deliberate, like he was trying to make it look easy because he wanted everyone in the stands to understand that it was. Frank Reyna, who is also apparently just a human person who plays baseball and not some kind of machine, hit two more in another game. I stopped being surprised somewhere around the fourth inning and just settled into gratitude.

Travis Coles went eight innings and struck out fourteen batters. Fourteen. John Ellis was nearly untouchable. Benji Dillon — who every one of us knew was coming to the end of something that mattered — went out there and kept being Benji Dillon, which at this point in his career is its own form of insisting that beauty is still possible.

Here's what I keep coming back to: Quintero leading the league in homers. Kelley leading the league in average. Two teammates, same dugout, same chase. This could be awkward. This could be the thing that creates distance inside a winning season. Instead from everything I can see, they're just playing. Quintero goes deep and looks for Kelley in the dugout. Kelley gets a hit and tips his helmet to nobody in particular, but you know who he's thinking about. They're not competing with each other — they're competing next to each other, which is a different thing entirely, and it's one of the reasons this team makes me feel the way I do right now.

That traffic jam on Hegenberger. Those kids in the jerseys. The elotes guy running out of product before the first pitch because Oakland showed up early and hungry and ready.

We're forty-six and twelve. Desmond Morton is a major-league baseball player now. Quintero and Kelley are running away from the rest of the league in their respective categories, and neither of them seems to be trying to beat the other one doing it.

I don't know exactly what you're supposed to say about a team this good. I've been trying to figure it out all week. Six wins in a row. A rookie who looked like a veteran. A veteran who still hits three home runs in a game like it's something he invented.

I think what you say is: watch. I think what you say is: pay attention. I think what you say is that whatever this is, it won't look like this forever, and so you should probably be at the park when you can, and when you can't be at the park, you should find a bar or a living room or a front stoop and you should watch it together with somebody who also understands what it means.

Oakland's got something. We've had it for a while, but right now it's very, very loud.
