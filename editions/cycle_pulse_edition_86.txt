## $230 Million, Forty Years of Waiting: Council Authorizes Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II

*The vote is done. The community benefits framework is locked in. Now the question is whether Oakland keeps its word this time.*

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

Calvin Turner has driven the stretch of East 12th Street past the Fruitvale BART station for eleven years. He knows the rhythm of the crossing — buses stacking behind the signal at International Boulevard, cyclists threading between parked delivery trucks, pedestrians waiting on a corner that has not been redesigned since the Reagan administration. He fixes machines for a living. He understands what it looks like when something was built for a smaller load than it now carries.

"Phase I was supposed to fix it," Turner, 38, said outside his Fruitvale shop this week. "Phase II better actually do it."

This week, the Oakland City Council authorized Phase II of the Fruitvale Transit Hub project — a $230 million investment in one of the city's most transit-dependent neighborhoods, passed with strong support from the OPP caucus and Council President Ramon Vega. The authorization moves the project from a completed community visioning process into the design and build phase, anchored by a negotiated BART memorandum of understanding and a community benefits framework that backers say fixes the shortfalls of Phase I.

It is the largest infrastructure investment in Fruitvale's history. Whether it transforms the neighborhood or displaces it depends on what happens next.

[Photo: DJ Hartley / Bay Tribune]

**What the Vote Authorizes**

The council did not vote to break ground Tuesday. It voted to authorize the project budget, lock in the visioning framework as binding decision criteria, and direct the city's Planning Bureau to release a design consultant RFP next month. Final design selection comes at a council vote scheduled for several years out, following three community sessions on design scenarios beginning next spring.

Planning Lead Elena Soria Dominguez, who grew up three blocks from the station and has run the Fruitvale planning portfolio for the city since 2038, told the council that the community input phase had produced an unusually clear mandate. Eighty-nine residents participated in two intensive sessions — 73 of them neighborhood residents. None called for canceling the project.

"What they said was: build it, but build it with us, not on top of us," Soria Dominguez said in her council presentation.

The visioning framework locks in six non-negotiable commitments: 20 percent of commercial space reserved for existing businesses at pre-development rents for twenty-five years; a minimum 35 percent affordable residential floor in all three design scenarios, with the project's full scope targeting 67 percent affordability across 420 units; a minimum $15 million business stabilization fund; 30 percent of construction jobs for Fruitvale residents between the ages of 18 and 30; a 15,000-square-foot community plaza managed by the city or a nonprofit; and a binding community benefits agreement that must be signed before any construction permit is issued.

The three design scenarios — ranging from a market-driven approach to a maximum community benefit model — all meet those non-negotiable requirements. Community members will recommend a scenario to the council, which retains final authority.

**Why the Comparison to Phase I Matters**

Turner was a teenager when Phase I of the Fruitvale BART hub was completed. He watched the promises about local hiring and a community stabilization fund arrive in print and then dissolve in practice. The stabilization fund was underfunded. The local hiring outcomes were never publicly accounted for. The affordability units built in the first phase had no long-term lock.

He is not alone in that memory. Rose Delgado, who represents District 3 and attended both community visioning sessions, told colleagues before the vote that Phase I left the neighborhood with infrastructure lessons it intended not to repeat.

"This neighborhood has been patient," Delgado said from the dais. "Phase I was promised. Phase II needs to deliver."

The binding community benefits agreement is the mechanism that separates the authorization language from the Phase I history. It cannot be signed after construction begins. It is a precondition. Soria Dominguez told the Tribune that if a developer cannot meet the community requirements, the project goes back to the design phase.

"The city has leverage," she said. "Zoning, permits, parking, transit connections — all of it requires city approval. We negotiated hard on Phase I. We negotiated harder here."

**The Coalition on the Vote**

Council President Ramon Vega, who scheduled the vote after reviewing the visioning documentation, said from the dais that there was no procedural reason to delay. His statement before the session: "The visioning is done. The BART MOU is negotiated. This goes to a vote." He added that the project represented exactly what the council should be doing — authorizing infrastructure when the work is ready.

Janae Rivers, whose District 5 includes East Oakland transit corridors that would eventually connect to the expanded hub, called the project an overdue commitment. "Fruitvale has been waiting for transit justice for forty years. This vote says that ends now."

James Mobley voted yes. Warren Ashford voted yes — a notable crossover from the CRC bloc. Leonard Tran also voted yes. Nina Chen voted no. Elliott Crane, still in recovery, was absent.

Mayor Avery Santana, who had advocated publicly for the vote this week, framed the BART MOU as the foundation. "Fruitvale doesn't need more promises about transit. It needs a BART connection that works and happens on time."

**The Clock That Starts Now**

Rafael Phillips, 26, has worked restaurant service in Fruitvale for three years and volunteers at a food pantry on International Boulevard on weekend mornings. He watched the visioning session livestream from the back of a café on East 12th Street last month.

"I kept waiting for someone to say what actually happens if the developer doesn't follow through," Phillips said this week. "What's the penalty? What's the enforcement?"

The framework's answer is financial penalties for non-compliance, quarterly public reporting, and a community oversight committee with decision authority — not advisory status. Soria Dominguez acknowledged to the Tribune that those mechanisms are only as strong as the city's willingness to enforce them.

The design consultant RFP goes out next month. Community sessions on design scenarios begin in 2042. Council takes a final vote on design and the completed community benefits agreement sometime in 2043.

Calvin Turner drove past the Fruitvale station on his way to a job site Thursday morning. He has been driving past it his whole adult life.

"Forty years of waiting and now they're saying five more years before they break ground," he said. "I believe it can happen. I want to believe it can happen. I just know what Phase I looked like."

By Mags Corliss | Bay Tribune

This is the edition where Oakland does three things at once and none of them are easy.

Fruitvale gets its $230 million authorization — the biggest infrastructure commitment in the neighborhood's history. The Transit Hub Phase II vote passed with a binding community benefits framework that didn't exist in Phase I, when the promises arrived in print and dissolved in practice. ## Twelve Families. $0 Disbursed. That Changes This Week.

*The Stabilization Fund's first money finally moves — but 277 families are still in a pipeline that takes twenty months to clear.*

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

The letter had been ready for weeks.

Marcus Webb, the program director for the West Oakland Stabilization Fund, drafted Beverly Hayes's determination letter the same week his office completed its first review of 18 applications. Her case was straightforward: a 58-year-old home health aide earning $38,000 a year, verified West Oakland residency, documented displacement risk. She qualified under every Phase 1 criterion. Her award: $18,500 in relocation assistance.

The letter sat in Webb's files. The fund had no disbursement authorization.

That changed this week. Mayor Avery Santana and Chief of Staff Laila Cortez signed the Phase 1 disbursement authorization, releasing Phase 1 disbursement funds to the 12 West Oakland families whose applications cleared Webb's review — the first of 47 total approved households to receive money from the fund's $4.2 million Phase 1 allocation. It is the first money to move through the fund since the council authorized $28 million last year.

"Beverly Hayes is waiting," Santana said in a public statement. "The families approved for assistance deserve the money now, not promises next month."

**The Gap Between Approval and Action**

The authorization this week closes a gap that Webb had been documenting for months. The fund's original $28 million was authorized in early 2041. Phase 1's $4.2 million allocation was approved by executive authorization shortly after. Applications began arriving almost immediately. Eighteen were reviewed in the most recent reporting period — twelve approved, three denied, three returned for additional documentation.

The bottleneck was not Webb's office. It was the step after his office: the executive authorization to actually release the funds. Webb filed a formal escalation memo to the Mayor's office last month. In it, he named the problem directly. "I am not asking for policy guidance," he wrote. "I am asking for the executive authorization that was already established when Phase 1 was approved."

Denise Carter, whose District 1 includes West Oakland, said at a council session this week that the authorization should have come sooner. "Beverly Hayes waited. Twelve families waited. Today they get their money. That's what West Oakland families deserve."

Council President Ramon Vega, who voted yes on the original Stabilization Fund authorization, backed the disbursement action. "Marcus Webb's team did their job. Now the city needs to do its job. Get the money to the people."

**The Longer Math**

The twelve families authorized for Phase 1 disbursement represent a fraction of what the fund was built to address.

Webb's status report, filed this week, shows 295 applications received. Of those, 18 have been reviewed. Two hundred seventy-seven remain in queue. At his office's current processing rate — 15 to 18 applications per month with three staff reviewers — those 277 applications will take between 18 and 20 months to clear. That timeline assumes no additional applications, no staffing disruptions, and no further authorization delays.

Warren Ashford, who voted against the Stabilization Fund when it passed and has voted against every major initiative since, nonetheless called on the city to answer the processing-rate question before celebrating Phase 1. "Fast authorization is good," Ashford said in a written statement. "Sustainable processing of 295 applications is the real test." His office requested a quarterly performance audit on processing speed and timeline projections.

That request is notable coming from a consistent fund opponent. It is also not wrong. The fund passed on the promise that displaced West Oakland families would get help. Twelve have cleared the review threshold. Two hundred seventy-seven are still waiting.

Webb's office said it has capacity to maintain the current review pace. The question the mayor's authorization does not answer is whether that pace is fast enough for the people it was meant to serve.

## Fourteen Days to Answer One Question

*OARI's 45-day implementation clock hits Day 31. Dispatch integration is the single blocker. Chief Montez's public statement is encouraging — but it's not a signature.*

**OARI Implementation Clock: Day 31 of 45.** Dispatch integration specification returned twice by OPD ECC. Written approval not yet received.

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

At Day 31 of the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative's 45-day implementation clock, OARI Program Director Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz sent Police Chief Rafael Montez a memo with a deadline attached.

The dispatch integration specification — the technical document that would allow Oakland's 911 system to route behavioral health crisis calls to OARI's civilian responders instead of OPD — had been returned twice from the Police Department's Emergency Communications Center with clarifying questions. The first time: call triage criteria. The second time: backup protocols and real-time police data visibility. Dr. Tran-Muñoz's office had answered both rounds.

The memo asked for written approval within 48 hours. The reason was arithmetic. Without dispatch integration, OARI cannot deploy. Without deployment by Day 45, the program that passed the council 5-4 in February does not meet its operational commitment. The hiring pipeline has eight people finalized or under offer. The training curriculum is ready. The community advisory boards in D1, D3, and D5 are scheduled.

Dispatch integration is the single blocker. Fourteen days remain.

Chief Montez responded publicly this week. "OPD is ready to coordinate with behavioral health responders," he said in a statement. "When OARI teams are operational, our dispatch system will route appropriate calls to them. The community deserves a seamless transition, not a turf war."

The statement is encouraging. It does not constitute written approval of the integration specification. The distinction matters.

**What the Program Needs by Day 40**

Dr. Tran-Muñoz's escalation memo was blunt about the mathematical reality. Dispatch integration approval must arrive no later than Day 40 — five days before the hard close — to allow for testing and certification. If approval stalls past that date, OARI cannot launch with full operational capacity on Day 45.

The program's contingency plan, outlined in its internal milestone report, allows for a District 1-only launch with six to eight responders covering core hours in West Oakland if the full three-district deployment isn't achievable. But that contingency requires the same dispatch approval. A civilian response team that can't receive routed 911 calls is not an operational response team.

Nina Chen, who voted against OARI in February, put the timeline arithmetic plainly in a written statement this week. "Day 30 of 45. Zero operational teams. The math doesn't work." Her office is skeptical the timeline was realistic from the start.

Leonard Tran, who voted for OARI and whose D2 sits outside the first-round pilot districts, said he is watching the Day 45 deadline closely. "I voted yes on OARI because the model is sound. But Day 30 with zero teams deployed is not the pace I expected." He said he would be "the first to say we need to recalibrate — not cancel, but recalibrate" if Day 45 passes without operational teams.

**The Question Downtown Keeps Asking**

Gloria Meeks has worked night shifts in a Downtown parking garage on Broadway for four years. She wrote to the Tribune in June after the OARI vote, asking when Downtown would be in the program. The answer then was: not the first round. The three pilot districts — D1 covering West Oakland, D3 covering Fruitvale, D5 in East Oakland — are all OPP neighborhoods. Meeks's district is not among them.

The answer this week is the same. Before Downtown can even be considered for expansion, the pilot districts have to prove the model works. Mayor Santana acknowledged the question in a public address this week: "Day 30 is not halfway. It's the moment to show the work is real." He said no extensions would be granted.

Tran formally requested an expansion review pathway for D2 last month, citing a response time gap in his district. Vega has said pilot data could open a D4 conversation. Both requests depend on a successful Day 45 launch.

**Forty-Five Days Was the Promise**

The 45-day clock was built into OARI's authorization because the council majority that passed it needed to demonstrate the program could move quickly. The CRC bloc voted no in part because they argued the timeline was too aggressive for a new civilian response infrastructure. The OPP and IND supporters who passed it argued the timeline was achievable.

Both arguments are still on the table. They resolve in fourteen days.

Chief Montez's public statement says OPD will not be the bottleneck. That statement needs to be followed by a signed dispatch integration approval. Dr. Tran-Muñoz's office asked for it in writing. So far, what has arrived is a public expression of goodwill.

Fourteen days is time enough to sign a document. It is not time enough to build something that should have been signed earlier.

BUSINESS TICKER

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

The first Stabilization Fund money is finally moving. Mayor Avery Santana signed the Phase 1 disbursement authorization this week, clearing the way for the 12 approved families to receive funds from the $4.2 million pool that has been sitting approved-but-undisbursed since the $28M fund launched. One of those names, Santana said publicly, is Beverly Hayes — 58, a home health aide from West Oakland, who was approved for $18,500 in stability assistance and has been waiting. That the mayor named her specifically matters: it means someone in this government understands that "approved" and "received" are not the same thing, and that the gap between them has consequences. What the authorization does not address is the other 295 applications still pending at the Office of Economic and Workforce Development. Marcus Webb's team processes 15 to 18 applications per month. At that rate, the remaining 277 get through in something close to twenty months — nearly two years after the fund launched. The CRC's Warren Ashford filed a request this week for quarterly performance audits on processing velocity, which is the right question dressed in bureaucratic language: can this city get money to people before they've already had to make the hard decisions? I've been following Darius Clark, a bakery worker in West Oakland who was handed an application form months ago and told to wait. I don't know yet whether he's among the 47 or still among the 295. That uncertainty is its own answer.

Across the waterfront, the Baylight Authority has filed all five documents required for the September 15 mobilization deadline: mobilization timeline, anchor tenant disclosure, TIF language, remediation bond, and workforce agreement. Mayor Santana called it proof that a $2.1 billion stadium happens when the paperwork is signed. The CRC is calling it a thirty-year tax lock — a TIF zone that claims three decades of rising property value from 65 acres of premium waterfront land to service the bond debt. Both descriptions are accurate. For contractors and construction trades, the September 15 deadline represents the first concrete date in a process that has so far produced commitments but no jobs. The Baylight construction project has a 40 percent local hire target baked into its workforce agreement — 1,400 construction positions projected, 560 of them supposed to go to Oakland residents. The remediation bond being filed is the instrument that actually clears the soil for that mobilization to begin. Whether the local hire pipeline infrastructure exists to deliver on that target when the shovels go in is the question the labor desks should be watching.

The one Oakland economy running in real time right now is Jack London after dark. Art & Soul Festival has pushed foot traffic along the waterfront corridor to the highest levels of the summer — the strip from Harborline Grill down toward Dockhouse BBQ has been moving steadily since the festival opened, and the Blue Lantern Bar and Green & Gold Tavern have both been running late covers on weekends. I spoke with Jalen Hill this week — he's 35, works the line at a Jack London kitchen, has been putting in doubles since late June. He said the festival crowd is different from the usual summer surge: older, more mixed, people who came for the music and stayed for dinner. "It's not just the regulars," he told me. "It's the whole city showing up." Whether that translates into permanent economic lift for the corridor or fades when the festival ends is the question every restaurant owner along the water is quietly running the math on.

## What August Holds

*St. Columba marks the Assumption as Fruitvale navigates a hard summer*

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

The candles at St. Columba Catholic Church on International Boulevard went up Tuesday evening, the way they always do for the Assumption of Mary — not with ceremony or announcement, but with the quiet efficiency of a congregation that has done this for decades. Someone set fresh flowers at the base of the side altar. Someone else propped open the heavy side door to let the August air move through the nave. By the time the seven o'clock Mass started, the pews were full.

I'd been in Fruitvale most of the day. The neighborhood felt tighter than usual — not angry, not broken, just drawn in, the way people look when they're managing something they don't talk about yet. A few shopkeepers on International had their gates half-down before sunset. A woman I passed outside the BART plaza was on her phone, her voice low and firm, working something out. Fruitvale in summer is always loud. This week it was listening.

[Photo: DJ Hartley / Bay Tribune]

Inside St. Columba, Calvin Turner, 38, a mechanic from Fruitvale who volunteers at the parish's Saturday food pantry, was helping set up extra folding chairs along the side aisle before Mass. He stacked them with the practiced ease of someone who's done this a hundred times. I asked him why he keeps showing up — to the food pantry, to the feast days, to the church in general.

He thought about it for a second. "Because this is the thing that's here," he said. "Not a fund, not a program. This is here."

He wasn't wrong. The Stabilization Fund — $4.2 million approved by the council nearly two years ago — has not reached this neighborhood in any material way that anyone on International Boulevard can point to. The faith institutions fill the space: the weekly food pantry, the seniors lunch, the informal networks that form around parish hall tables and remain long after the last plate is cleared. These aren't stopgap measures. At St. Columba, they predate the Stabilization Fund by years.

The Assumption is a high holy day in the Catholic calendar — the taking up of Mary into heaven, body and soul. For a lot of parishes, it's quiet. At St. Columba, in Fruitvale, it has always drawn a full house. Families who don't come for ordinary Sundays come for this. The church knows it's one of those nights when the pews hold grief and gratitude in equal measure, and doesn't try to sort them out.

Father Manuel Avila, who has led St. Columba for eleven years, gave a short homily. He said something that stayed with me: that feast days aren't escapes from the world outside. They're ways of insisting that the world outside isn't everything. I wrote it down. I have no idea if he's right, but I understand what he's reaching for.

After Mass, people gathered on the sidewalk outside the way they do after night services in August — slowly, still warm from being indoors, not quite ready to leave. Calvin Turner stood near the door, saying goodbye to people he knew. An older woman stopped to squeeze his arm without saying anything. He nodded. I don't know what it meant. He didn't explain it and I didn't ask.

I walked back to my car past the shuttered storefronts and the open taquerias and the kids still out at nine on a Tuesday night. Fruitvale is not a neighborhood that breaks easily. It has been asked to absorb a lot in the last two years — construction noise from corridors over the hill, the long wait for money that was announced as certain, the summer heat that makes everything harder.

What it has is St. Columba. The candles. The folding chairs. The mechanic who stacks them.

That's not a small thing. Oakland knows that.

## The Work That Stays

*Andre Lee has been showing up for two years. He's not done.*

By Elliot Graye | Bay Tribune Culture [OPINION]

Andre Lee, 55, an electrician from Temescal, drove to East Oakland on Saturday morning and got in the volunteer line at Allen Temple Baptist Church before six-thirty. He does this most weekends — not always Allen Temple, not always the same neighborhood, but somewhere in the network of faith institutions that keep this city fed when the larger infrastructure hasn't caught up.

He's been doing it for two years.

I first noted him at St. Columba in Fruitvale, where he volunteered at the seniors lunch program. Then he turned up at Acts Full Gospel in West Oakland at the community health fair. Now he's unloading produce at Allen Temple, on 85th Avenue, while the Saturday morning food pantry forms its line outside.

Allen Temple Baptist Church has been a pillar of East Oakland for more than sixty years. The food pantry runs every Saturday without fail. It does not advertise. It does not issue press releases. The line forms because word traveled, the way word has always traveled in neighborhoods like this — from neighbor to neighbor, not from flyer to bulletin board.

Lee carried a crate of oranges through the side entrance and set it on the sorting table. He went back for another.

"You ever get tired of this?" I asked him.

"Of what?" he said. He wasn't being difficult. He genuinely seemed to be considering whether "this" — the lifting, the driving, the showing up before sunrise — was a thing one got tired of.

"I get tired of other things," he said eventually. "The commute. The paperwork. Not this."

He is not an organizer. He holds no title at Allen Temple or anywhere else. He's an electrician who volunteers, and the distinction matters to him. I've learned not to call him a coordinator or a leader because he'll correct you, calmly, and keep moving.

Across town that same morning, Masjid Al-Islam, also in East Oakland, opened registration for its new job training session — the kind of workforce development program that fills a gap the Stabilization Fund was meant to help close. The mosque has run these workshops for years, before the fund existed, before anyone announced that it would. The participants who signed up Saturday morning came because they heard it worked.

Andre Lee doesn't attend Masjid Al-Islam. He's not Muslim. He's not Catholic, either, as far as I can tell, though he's spent a lot of Saturday mornings at Catholic food pantries. I asked him once what he believed. He said: "I believe in the line forming."

That's not theology. But it's something.

The Allen Temple pantry ran until nearly noon. Lee stayed until the end, then drove back across town. He had a job Monday morning — a rewiring project in Temescal. He'd be up by five.

The faith institutions of Oakland — Allen Temple, St. Columba, Masjid Al-Islam, the Cathedral, the others — are running at elevated capacity this summer. They were doing this work before the policy arrived. They are doing it while the policy waits. They will, almost certainly, be doing it after whatever comes next. Andre Lee shows up because the line forms. The line forms because the institutions hold. The institutions hold because someone keeps showing up.

The city is lucky it's not choosing between them.

## The City at Full Volume

*Art & Soul Festival returns to Downtown Oakland*

By Kai Marston | Bay Tribune Arts & Entertainment

By six o'clock Saturday evening, the blocks around Frank Ogawa Plaza smelled like grilled corn and sound system resin — that particular August smell you only get when Oakland has been outside all day and doesn't plan on going in.

Art & Soul Festival is back. It was always going to come back. It comes back every year like an agreement Oakland renews with itself: that whatever else is happening, whatever is unresolved, whatever is pending approval or waiting on a check — summer gets this. Downtown gets this.

The stages were going when I arrived. The crowd moved the way festival crowds move in August heat — not rushing, not lingering, just drifting between whatever pulls them. A woman near the food vendors was dancing to something playing from three stalls away, eyes closed, not bothering to pretend she wasn't. A kid in a Warriors jersey had set up a folding table selling zines, and he was doing fine. Two men argued amiably about which stage to hit next, then split up and went to neither.

Art & Soul is not an intimate event. It fills the streets. It fills the air. The Uptown corridor, which has had its own creative energy building all summer, fed into it Saturday the way a tributary feeds a river — people walking down Telegraph toward the festival, stopping in at galleries on their way, then moving on. The arts scene in Oakland doesn't wait for a festival to activate. But the festival gives it a shared focal point, a night when the dispersed energy concentrates.

This city is carrying a lot right now. The Stabilization Fund checks that haven't arrived. The neighborhoods adjusting to construction noise and displacement pressures. The faith institutions running at capacity because the gap between policy and delivery is real and the churches fill what they can. All of that is true.

It's also true that Oakland knows how to be two things at once. Worried and celebratory. Wary and grateful. Tired and here.

Art & Soul has always known this. The festival doesn't pretend the city isn't complicated. It's built from it — from the muralists and the musicians and the vendors and the dancers and the kids who brought their zine tables and didn't bother asking permission. Oakland shows up complicated and calls it a weekend.

By nine o'clock, the lights were still going. The line for the food vendors had barely moved. Somewhere across town, the candles at St. Columba had burned down. Somewhere in East Oakland, Allen Temple's parking lot was dark again.

The city was all of it at once.

## The House Keane Built

*On the academy, the farewell, and what Oakland does with the things it loves before they go*

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

I drove past the old firehouse on Mandela Parkway last Tuesday, the one that sat empty for seven years until someone finally figured out what to do with it. I didn't know yet what it had become. I just noticed that the windows had light in them. In West Oakland, that matters.

Then I got the release about the Vinnie Keane West Oakland Baseball Academy. And I had to sit in my car for a while.

Let me be straight about what Vinnie Keane did. He took a building this city couldn't figure out how to use, spent his own money rebuilding it from the studs out, and opened a free baseball program for every kid in West Oakland who wants to play — all levels, no cost, no waitlist. He did this in the last year of his career. He did this while he is still playing. He will not be around to run this thing when the season starts next month, because he will be in a dugout wearing the green and gold, playing out the final chapters of a career that will almost certainly end in Cooperstown.

This is not a press release. This is not a charity photo op. This is a man who looked at the neighborhood where he became a legend and decided that his last act before the door closes would be to build something that stays when he leaves.

I know what the cynics will say. They'll say it's a tax move. They'll say a rich man giving away what costs him nothing in real terms is not generosity, it's optics. They'll say Oakland has seen players "invest in the community" before, and what usually happens is a ribbon cutting and a disappearing act.

They're wrong. And here's why.

Vinnie Keane has been in Oakland for eleven years. He's played in six championship series in this city. When the dynasty was built, he wasn't the architect — he was the face. He was the one who made it feel like the whole thing was possible, who played with that particular kind of belief that teams either catch or they don't. I've watched him in this city long enough to know that the firehouse wasn't an impulse. That project took years of planning. The foundation had been working with the city on the building transfer for three years before this announcement. He didn't do this for coverage.

Dante Nelson, a security guard who has lived in West Oakland for twenty years and brought his nephew to the opening last Saturday, told me: "I grew up near this firehouse. We used to ride our bikes past it. I never thought I'd see the lights come on." He paused for a second. "My nephew is eight years old. He's going to learn to play baseball in there."

That is the thing the cynics can't account for. An eight-year-old in West Oakland is going to learn to play baseball in a building that was given to him by a man who plays first-ballot Hall of Fame numbers every October. That's not optics. That's what it looks like when someone decides that the neighborhood where you became great deserves something back.

I'll be honest — the farewell season hurts. Hal Richmond will write something beautiful about what it means to lose Keane and Kelley and Dillon in the same year, and he'll be right. That is a specific kind of grief and I'm not ready for it. The A's go 10-3 in the preseason and I should be celebrating. Instead I'm sitting in front of a firehouse on Mandela Parkway thinking about what October is going to feel like when the last game is over.

But Keane gave us something to look at after October. The academy is going to be there in November. It's going to be there next spring. Some kid is going to throw a curveball in that building in twenty years and not know who paid for the lights.

That's the kind of goodbye Oakland deserves. Not a ceremony. Not a monument. A building with the windows lit up.

I drove past the old firehouse. I saw the light. That's Vinnie Keane, in the end — the guy who made sure something was left on when he walked out.

## What Comes Next: Seymour's A's Emerge From Spring With a Record and a Question

*Preseason dispatch: 10-3 heading into opening week. The rookies are pushing. The veterans are finishing. The manager is threading a needle.*

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

The Oakland A's have gone 10-3 through preseason play, their best spring record in four years, and Deacon Seymour has managed it all — the wins, the lineup shuffles, the farewell-season personalities, and a group of rookies who have made every roster decision harder than anyone expected — without a single visible wobble. The season opens in three weeks. The machine is running.

But here is the honest question the record doesn't answer: what does this club actually look like in October, when Isley Kelley is running on whatever the final reserves of fifteen years have left him, when Benji Dillon is working his last rotation turns on a left arm that has given Oakland five Cy Young Awards and three thousand two hundred nineteen strikeouts, when Vinnie Keane is pulling on a jersey for the last time in the uniform he made famous? The 10-3 says the parts fit together. The autumn will say whether the seams hold.

That tension is real, and it lives in Seymour's lineup cards every morning.

On one side: the core that built this dynasty. Kelley, who has hit four hundred forty-seven home runs and won three MVP awards and whose value to this clubhouse is measured in a currency that no analytics dashboard tracks. Keane, the six-time champion whose presence in right field is still a daily referendum on belief. Davis, fully cleared from ACL rehab and operating in left field — a two-time World Series MVP who accepted the outfield move when Ernesto Quintero took over at designated hitter without a word of public complaint.

On the other side: four prospects who have spent the last month making the front office uncomfortable in the best possible way.

Sidney Tumolo, the second baseman, is hitting .261 with two home runs and six hits through his preseason appearances, solid numbers that don't fully convey the crispness of his footwork at second base or the way he's handled left-handed pitching. Bryan Franco, competing for shortstop time, is hitting .400 with two home runs and five RBI in limited action — the kind of spring line that gets organizational people to stop hedging when they talk about him. Buford Park at third base has a .389 mark with seven hits. And Clark Saryan, the closer acquired via trade, has not allowed an earned run across six and one-third innings with seven strikeouts.

The mathematics of this situation are not subtle. Kelley's contract expires after this season. Martin Richards is entering the final year of his deal at third base. The organization did not sign Tumolo and didn't trade for Saryan to let them age in Triple-A. Seymour knows this. He is navigating it without making it a story — which is, by itself, a kind of management accomplishment.

"We've got a lot of options," Seymour said during a media availability session this week at the Coliseum. "That's a good problem. Our job right now is to make sure everybody is ready when the regular season starts. After that, we figure it out."

The Coliseum stood largely empty behind him as he spoke, a few reporters and a couple of maintenance workers visible in the lower deck. Preseason in late August has its own particular quality — the light is right, the urgency is not quite there yet, and the thing that makes it feel like baseball rather than work is the sound of the game itself, unhurried and necessary.

Travis Coles, who signed a two-year, forty-six million dollar deal after arriving in the earlier trade, is entrenched in the starting rotation going into the season, which shapes what Tumolo's situation actually looks like. The organization has pieces. How they arrange those pieces — whether Tumolo stays up, whether Franco pushes Kelley into a reduced role, whether Saryan's spring carries into a legitimate closer role — is the architectural question that will define what the A's dynasty's final chapter looks like.

Seymour's first year of managing was complicated before a pitch was thrown, between the lineup leak controversy of spring and the strength coach dismissal that he addressed directly and cleanly. His 10-3 preseason record is an early answer to the question of whether he could hold this together. It is not the last answer.

The season starts in three weeks. Oakland is paying attention.

## The Last Morning: On Kelley, Keane, and Dillon in the Final Year

*A Richmond Archive Essay*

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

I have been covering Oakland baseball for forty-four years, and I have learned that endings tend to arrive slowly, then all at once — a great hitter fading into a platoon role, a rotation ace's velocity chart declining in increments too small to mourn individually, until the morning you realize you are watching a last season without quite having understood when it began. Oakland has watched stars leave before. Reggie went to New York and came back only in history. Rickey returned and returned and returned, until even the returning became its own kind of story. Catfish and Blue and Fingers in the days when the franchise wore its chaos like a second uniform.

But I have not seen a September quite like the one that is approaching.

This year, in the same season, the Oakland A's are playing the final chapters of Isley Kelley, Vinnie Keane, and Benji Dillon. Three men who arrived at different times and by different paths, who collectively built this dynasty as surely as any front office decision or any analytics model, who will all put on the green and gold for the last time this October. Three careers concluding simultaneously, like three rivers meeting at once, and the city is going to have to find a way to say goodbye to all of them before the calendar turns.

I do not know if that is possible. I am not certain it should be.

Kelley first. The Machine from Mississippi has played fourteen years in Oakland, has hit four hundred forty-seven home runs and collected two thousand three hundred ninety-eight career hits, has won the MVP award three times, has played shortstop with the kind of mechanical precision that makes routine things look like art. What the statistics do not convey is the presence — the way opposing dugouts treated him as a variable they could not neutralize, the way Oakland dugouts treated him as a fixed point around which everything else could orbit. You build a lineup around Isley Kelley because he is the player who makes your other players better without asking credit for doing it. That is a rarer quality than the home runs, and it does not appear in any box score.

Keane is different in almost every dimension. Six championships. The two thousand thirty-one MVP. Four hundred thirty-six career home runs for a man who arrived in Oakland without anyone expecting greatness and proceeded to become the face of a franchise in a city that needed a face to believe in. Where Kelley gave you precision, Keane gave you something that functions more like permission — he played like he expected everything to work out, and sometimes the whole team seemed to catch that feeling from him like a current. In twenty-five years of watching outfielders in Oakland, I have not seen anyone who understood the theater of the position the way Keane understands it.

Dillon is the one I find hardest to write about. Five Cy Young Awards. Three thousand two hundred nineteen strikeouts. Two hundred wins. Left-handed and precise in a way that seems almost personal, as if he is not simply trying to get hitters out but making an argument about what pitching should be. I was in the press box the night he struck out fifteen Astros in a playoff start, and the sound of the stadium afterward — not the noise during, but the sound after, when it was over and people were trying to process what they had watched — is one of those things I keep somewhere behind my professional memory, in the room where I keep the things I actually care about.

What strikes me now, sitting in the press box with three weeks remaining before the regular season opens, is that this is not three endings happening simultaneously by coincidence. These three men chose to finish together. They are all healthy enough to have played another year somewhere else. They are finishing here, in Oakland, in the same September. That is a decision.

I have written retirement pieces for players who played out the string, who held on past the point where grace required letting go. This is not that. Kelley and Keane and Dillon are still playing. The preseason record is ten and three. Deacon Seymour has built something real around them and alongside them and, when the season ends, after them. The dynasty is not concluding because it failed. It is concluding because three of the men who made it possible are choosing to close the chapter on their own terms, in the city that made them famous.

Oakland has always understood that kind of loyalty. The city has not always been able to reward it the way it deserved. This October, at least, we will have the chance to try.

I will be in the press box for every game. My mechanical keyboard will be there too — you can probably hear it from the parking lot. That is something I picked up in the Bash Brothers era and never put down. Some habits are more persistent than championships. Some stories deserve to be written by someone who has been waiting long enough to understand what he is watching.

This is going to be a season worth watching.

## The Iron Corner, Present as Always

*Aitken at the Fruitvale vote. Little League in its sixth year. The player who keeps showing up.*

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

Mark Aitken was not in a batting cage last Thursday. He was not in a weight room or watching film or doing any of the things a major league first baseman does in the final weeks before a regular season opens. He was in a gallery seat at the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II developer selection hearing, sitting quietly and listening, the way he has been doing for the better part of three years in civic rooms around Oakland.

The A's first baseman has attended more than four city meetings, council sessions, and planning hearings in that time. He brought Benji Dillon to the DEIR session last year. He has not testified, has not run for anything, has not attached his name to advocacy organizations in ways that generate press releases. He attends. He listens. He leaves. The meetings continue. The city moves.

This week, while the Fruitvale vote was being covered by the Tribune's civic desk, Aitken's presence was the sports story — not because anything dramatic happened, but because of what it has come to mean for an Oakland A's player to occupy those seats consistently, without a camera crew and without a speech prepared. Oakland is a city that has watched athletes make appearances for twenty years, and the city is fairly good at distinguishing between an appearance and a commitment. Aitken has made the distinction easy.

He is also, separately, running a Little League program that enters its sixth year this August. The Aitken Little Leaguers offer all levels of baseball free to the community — not as a foundation event, not as a promotional tie-in, but as a six-year-old operating commitment that has continued through contract negotiations, through playoff runs, through an offseason in which the A's made significant roster moves and Aitken navigated them without public disruption.

Raymond Polk, a contractor who has been a regular in the conversations around Oakland's civic future, was in the gallery at the Fruitvale hearing as well. Polk has been patient about a lot of things in this city — franchise futures, neighborhood development, the usual slow grinding of civic machinery. When asked about seeing Aitken in the room, he said: "At this point I just expect him to be there. That's not nothing. That's actually the whole thing."

Aitken's numbers going into his final season of his current contract are what they have always been: three Gold Gloves, a .273 career average, one hundred ninety-five home runs, a Playoff MVP in two thousand thirty-seven. He is the architectural player on a dynasty club — the one Deacon Seymour can build a lineup around precisely because Aitken will do what is required without requiring management of the fact that he is doing it.

The civic attendance and the Little League program and the quiet presence in planning rooms are the same instinct applied to a different field. Aitken plays first base the way he attends meetings: reliably, without theater, with full attention to the work.

The regular season opens in three weeks. Aitken will be at first base. Before that, apparently, he will be in whatever room matters.

## What "Long Term Commitments Were Not Made" Actually Means

*Bulls ownership told the whole league that Paulson isn't locked up. At 56-22, that silence is its own statement.*

By Selena Grant | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

Let's be precise about what the Bulls ownership group said this week: "Long term commitments were not made."

Not "Paulson is our guy." Not "we're working to extend him." Not anything that would make a front office executive feel secure in their position at the end of a 56-win season, with a playoff run still ahead of them and the Golden State Warriors — 39-28, openly interested — watching from across the country.

The ownership group was speaking on the record about the structure of Paulson's original deal. They acknowledged, apparently without much calculation about how it would read, that when Paulson came to Chicago, both sides understood this was not a locked-in arrangement. They even described it as something they regret. The word "regret" appearing in a business article about your sitting general manager, while your team holds the best record in the Eastern Conference, is not nothing.

To be clear about what 56-22 means: this is the best regular-season record Chicago has had since the dynasty era. Paulson built this roster in two seasons — assembling an interior anchor in Walker Kessler (who posted 22 points and 10 rebounds against San Antonio last week and followed it with 18 and 12 against New York), a rookie point forward in Hank Trepagnier who is the front-runner for Rookie of the Year, and a point guard in Josh Giddey who has been the most consistent distributor in the Eastern Conference since the All-Star break. That roster was not an accident. It was a specific philosophy — physical, multi-positional, built for a long playoff run — executed against market constraints that most GMs would have used as an excuse for mediocrity.

The Knicks game Thursday illustrated both the strength and the persistent complication of what Paulson built. Ayo Dosunmu posted 24 points and controlled the game from start to finish. Adash Stanley — 20 points, 9 assists, 2 steals — was, for most of the night, the best player on the floor. In the third quarter he was whistled for his sixth technical foul of the season after a tie-up in the paint. It was settled quickly. The Bulls won 107-99 and retained the top seed in the East.

Six technicals in a season is a pattern. A person familiar with how the front office monitors this put it simply: in the regular season, Stanley's physicality is a weapon. In a playoff series, where referees apply tighter standards and one possession can shift the momentum of a quarter, a poorly timed technical becomes a liability you cannot scheme around. The question is not whether Stanley's edge is real — it is. The question is whether it is being managed with the discipline a title run requires, or simply tolerated because the wins have made the conversation easy to defer.

Against San Antonio, the Bulls lost 115-111 in a game where Trepagnier finished with 9 points and 8 rebounds but couldn't impose himself offensively. The Spurs held their ground in a rematch and showed that this roster's best version is not automatic — it has to be earned each night. That loss did not crack anything. The W3 streak that followed closed the regular season narrative on terms that favor Chicago.

But the franchise narrative is something else entirely. When a front office speaks publicly about the absence of long-term commitments, they hand every team in the league a piece of information they didn't have before. They also tell the players who came to Chicago — in part because of who is running the organization — that the architect of this roster does not have the security a 56-22 team might otherwise assume.

Paulson has not commented on the ownership statement. He has not commented on Golden State. Those two silences are starting to feel like the same silence.

The Bulls have the record to win a championship this spring. They may or may not have the front office continuity to build on it. That's an unusual pair of sentences to be writing about the best team in the East, but it is the pair that fits.

## The Phone Is Face-Up Now

*Paulette Okafor sat at Romano's with the phone face-up for the first time in two months. That's how Bridgeport processes an ownership statement.*

By Talia Finch | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

The coffee at Romano's on 35th was the same as always on Friday morning — dark roast, ceramic mugs that have been washed approximately ten thousand times without losing their weight. What was different was Paulette Okafor's phone.

For two months I've been watching that phone. Face-down on the table at Romano's, face-down at the counter at the pharmacy, face-down in whatever conversation Paulette was in the middle of, because if a notification came through about Mike Paulson and Golden State she didn't want to see it by accident. "At some point the silence is the statement," she told me last month. She was talking about Paulson's silence. She had arranged her entire morning routine around not having to know.

Friday the phone was face-up.

"I read it before I came in," she said. She works two blocks off Halsted in an elementary school office, twenty years in, knows everyone in this part of Bridgeport by their kid's name before she knows theirs. She was on her second coffee. "The ownership. That quote."

Long term commitments were not made.

She said it back to me almost word for word, the way you repeat something that doesn't quite make sense yet. "I'm trying to figure out if that's supposed to be reassuring. Like, 'it's fine, nobody made any promises.' But if nobody made any promises, then he can just leave."

Raymond Polk came in around eight-fifteen. He does that — works a job, circles back to Romano's between sites when the scheduling allows. He's a contractor. He's been patient about Paulson in every conversation I've had with him, the kind of patient that comes from watching this city cycle through coaches and executives and promising seasons that dissolved before they got anywhere. Bridgeport patience. The specific kind that has survived a lot.

He sat down and Paulette said it again. The quote. He nodded slowly, the way you nod when you already saw it.

"They're saying they didn't lock him in," he said.

"That's what they're saying."

He turned his coffee mug around in his hands once. "So why didn't they?"

Nobody had an answer for that. The thing about Paulson — and I've written this before, and people in this neighborhood say it before I can — is that he's from Bridgeport. That's not a ceremonial fact. It's not the kind of detail that appears in a press release and then evaporates. He grew up here. He went to games here when the Bulls were bad. He came back and made them good. Raymond told me months ago: *Bridgeport doesn't produce GMs who win.* He said it like a man who had spent his whole life watching the neighborhood produce everything else and not that. Plumbers, electricians, firemen, teachers, and then Mike Paulson.

"You think he leaves?" Paulette asked him.

Raymond was quiet for a moment. Above the counter the television was on, sound off, showing highlights from the Knicks game. Adash Stanley mid-celebration after a shot, the United Center going bright. The Bulls were 56-22. Best record in the East. Paulson built that.

"I think," Raymond said carefully, "he'd be a fool not to at least listen."

He paused.

"And I think we'd be fools to hold it against him if he did."

Paulette stared at her coffee. The phone stayed face-up on the table. She wasn't hiding from it anymore. I don't know if that's peace or resignation. Maybe in late August in Bridgeport, with a playoff run still ahead and an ownership group saying things that sound casual about a man who is not casual to this neighborhood, there isn't a clean line between those two things.

I left them there. The mugs still on the table. The TV still on, sound still off.
