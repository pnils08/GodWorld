### Seven in the Game: The Dynasty's Thanksgiving Showcase

**Seven Oakland Athletics made the American League All-Star roster — nearly a third of it. That number is not luck. It is a decade of construction made visible in a single midsummer night, and Darrin Davis's return is its emotional peak.**

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

When the American League All-Star roster was announced at the break, seven Oakland Athletics were on it. Seven. Out of a twenty-five-man squad representing the best players in the league, nearly a third wore green and gold. That number does not arrive through luck or a favorable ballot campaign. It arrives through a decade of construction — drafting well, spending precisely, keeping the core together long past the point when most franchises find reasons to move on.

The A's sit at seventy-one wins and twenty-five losses, winners of their last four. But the All-Star haul is a different kind of accounting than the standings line. The standings tell you how a team is doing this season. Seven men in a midsummer classic tell you what a franchise has been doing for ten years.

Travis Coles drew the starting assignment on the mound. He is twenty-one years old and pitching like a man who has already served his time in the league — which, in the only way that matters, he has. The Oakland system shaped him, and the returns on that investment arrive in his delivery, his command, his postseason-ready composure at the break. A starting nod at twenty-one is not a compliment; it is a clinical observation by the people who set rosters for a living.

Danny Horn started in center field. Horn's arc across this dynasty has been the quiet one — the player who does not demand attention and therefore goes underestimated until someone tallies the home runs and realizes he has been among the league's most dangerous outfielders without ever announcing it. He has earned the center-field designation on merit, which is the only way any of this means anything.

Isley Kelley is at shortstop, as he has been for the better part of a generation. Three MVPs. Four hundred and forty-seven home runs. A Gold Glove presence at a position that does not permit passengers. Kelley's contract runs out after this season, and what comes next is genuinely open — he is a free agent, not a retiree, and nothing about his performance this year suggests the market will have doubts about him. What the All-Star nod signals is simpler than any contract calculus: the baseball world still considers Isley Kelley one of the best players in it. The years in Oakland have not diminished him. They have defined him.

Eric Taveras won the Home Run Derby. He did it against teammates Horn and Ernesto Quintero, which tells you something about the depth of the Oakland lineup that three men from the same dugout were among the finalists in a competition built around the league's most spectacular hitters. Taveras's emergence this cycle has been one of the season's real stories — a player who arrived with questions about his contact rate and has answered them in ways that put him in Derby conversations. The second-base position now carries legitimate offensive weight.

And then there is Darrin Davis.

Davis did not go to the All-Star Game as a sentimental selection. He went because he is Darrin Davis, two-time MVP, left fielder, the man this franchise has built emotional architecture around since before the current winning run began. The better story is what he did in the first week back after his injury — three home runs, five runs batted in, the Drifter intensity back in full force. That production is not a reclamation narrative. It is a statement. When Davis is in the lineup and hitting the baseball hard, the Athletics are a different team — not in the standings sense, where they are already operating at a remarkable clip, but in the atmospheric sense. The energy in that dugout when Davis is locked in is something that does not appear in any box score.

Seven players. One franchise. A decade of work made visible in a single night.

The window, as it always is, is not infinite. Free agency takes players. Time takes others. The pieces that assembled this thing — the draft capital, the development work, the front office patience — do not come together this completely very often, and the people who study roster construction for a living know what they're looking at when they see it. Seven All-Stars from a single club is not an accident and it is not a hot streak. It is the output of a system that has been getting the significant decisions right for long enough that the results compound.

Oakland's dynasty does not announce itself with speeches or ceremonies. It announces itself with selection notices going to seven different lockers in the same clubhouse, and the quiet understanding among everyone in that room that they built something worth selecting.

### Thanksgiving, and the Quiet Machinery

By Margaret Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

The light comes late and leaves early now, and the lake takes it without complaint. This is the week Oakland sets a table. After a heavy cycle, the city is breathing easier — you can feel it in the storefronts, in the altars lit across five traditions in the same seven days, in a dynasty that put seven of its own in the All-Star Game and made it look like arithmetic. We are grateful. We have earned the right to say so plainly.

But gratitude is not the same as looking away, and a paper that only counts blessings is not doing its job. So we will hold two things in one sentence. This cycle the quiet machinery of accountability posted real numbers: the Stabilization Fund's repair plan held on its first true test, and Baylight's director ended three cycles of silence to name a conflict she could have kept in the dark. That is the city working — not loudly, not for credit, but on the record.

And still, one in ten of us is unwell. The illness rate has not climbed and it has not broken; it sits where it sat, persistent, the thing the season would rather not mention. We mention it. Not as alarm — as attention. That is the watch a city keeps for its own, even at a full table. Especially then.

Pass the plate. Keep the lights on. Both.

— M.C.

### The Slot and the Street: Fruitvale's Transit Bargain Comes Due

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

---

Reggie Soto has been watching the corner of International and 35th since before the fencing went up.

The auto-body shop owner is 47, runs the kind of place where you know the customers by name and the customers know yours. When the Fruitvale Transit Hub broke ground two cycles ago, he wrote this paper a letter. He wasn't hostile. He wasn't hopeful. He was exact: the work has to stay here.

That letter landed in the Tribune's inbox before a single cubic yard of concrete had been poured. It wasn't cynicism. It was a question with a deadline attached.

The deadline arrived.

---

The Community Benefits Agreement governing the Fruitvale Transit Hub Phase II is executed — signed, tripartite, in force. Thirty-four prevailing-wage apprenticeship slots. Anti-displacement protections written as contract conditions, not aspirational language. A $180,000 workforce set-aside. Pre-apprenticeship prep sessions already running at the Fruitvale Rec Center, two blocks from where Soto parks his first car of the morning.

Eloise Soria-Dominguez, the project's planning lead, put it plainly when the document cleared last cycle: "Not pending, not days away — signed."

That is a different sentence than most city planning leads get to say at this stage of a $230 million project. It is worth noting. It is also not the end of the question Reggie Soto asked.

---

The question was always downstream of the signature.

Who fills the 34 slots? Local hires from the corridor, or workers recruited from three neighborhoods over because the paperwork moved faster?

The CBA's answer to that question is structural. Rosario Medina is the designated compliance monitor — the person whose job it is to track whether local-hire commitments survive contact with a general contractor's staffing preferences. Delia Vargas-Ruiz holds the D3 intake liaison role; Jerome Pittman covers D5. Their function is intake — connecting residents to pre-apprenticeship prep before the slots open to outside competition.

The council linked this framework to the just-activated Oakland Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline when INIT-007 passed unanimously last cycle. That 9-0 vote was not routine proceduralism. It was the council saying, in the clearest available language, that the apprenticeship slots and the local pipeline are the same commitment, not parallel ones.

Soria's office calls Medina's monitor role "the teeth." Whether that's accurate depends on what Medina's reports show when MTC reviews the project's progress and the board votes in the cycles ahead.

---

Walk the Fruitvale corridor on a weekday morning and the neighborhood does not read like a place waiting to be saved.

Retail is up. Sentiment is up. The storefronts between International and the BART overpass have not been vacant for cycles. New businesses opened last cycle. Longtime businesses are still there. The displacement pressure the critics predicted when Phase II was announced has not materialized in the data or in the conversations happening outside Soto's shop.

What Fruitvale looks like right now is a neighborhood that built something and is watching to see whether it gets to share in what it built.

That is a different problem than distress. It requires a different kind of accountability.

---

Soto has not written another letter.

The Tribune reached him this week outside the shop, between a brake job and a windshield. He said he'd heard about the CBA being signed. He said he knew about the Rec Center sessions — one of his son's friends had gone to the intake meeting.

"I'm watching," he said. "That's all I said I'd do."

He went back inside.

The compliance monitor has her first reporting period. The intake liaisons have their lists. The slots are real. The question is whether the mechanism is.

Soto's watching. So are we.

### CRC Concedes Its OARI Math Was Wrong

By Bay Tribune Civic

---

The Civic Reform Coalition built much of its case against the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative on a single number: $420 a call. This cycle the coalition's own District 7 member retired it on the record. "The number we used was wrong. That is on us, and I am saying so on the record," Warren Ashford told the council. The corrected figure, from OARI director Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, is $2,807 a call — and her district-by-district cost-avoidance breakdown puts the program's return at roughly 3.9 to 1. The complication sits in District 8, where Nina Chen, who voted no, now watches her own district post the program's strongest numbers. "I voted no on a $420 figure," Chen said. "The figure was $2,807. Those are not the same vote."

### A Week of Many Altars: Oakland's Thanksgiving Convergence

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

---

I walked into the Gurdwara Singh Sabha on Fruitvale Avenue on Wednesday morning and the langar kitchen was already loud with purpose. Bhai Manjit Singh stood at the edge of a prep station where three volunteers were cutting onions and ginger, their hands moving with the rhythm of people who've done this a hundred times before. Outside, the late-fall light was thin and cool. Inside, the air smelled like ghee and cilantro and the particular warmth of a kitchen that doesn't slow down.

"Guru Nanak Gurpurab," Bhai Manjit said, gesturing toward the work. "The birth of our Guru. We feed everyone."

The langar kitchen is not new to the city — the Gurdwara has been on Fruitvale for five years now — but this was the first time the Tribune has walked through those doors in time to witness it. The week that brought Thanksgiving to Foothill Baptist Tabernacle in East Oakland, All Saints observances to the Cathedral of the Living Word on Lake Merritt and to St. Esperanza in Fruitvale, and Diwali celebrations still glowing at Bharat Mandir in Montclair, also brought Guru Nanak Gurpurab to this kitchen. Five faiths, five traditions, all converging on the same days of the week. One city, many altars, all lit.

The question, when I first heard about the coverage gap, was how to hold all of them at once. The answer came through an unexpected messenger.

Isley Kelley was the bridge.

I found him on Tuesday evening at Foothill Baptist Tabernacle in East Oakland, where he served as honorary deacon during the Thanksgiving service. Kelley is the A's shortstop, a man who studies theology in the off-season and carries a worn Bible his mother gave him. He doesn't announce his faith to reporters. He lives it.

"I'm here because this is where I pray," he told me, in the quiet way he has of saying the thing that matters most. "When you show up in a place, you serve."

Dr. Ophelia Brenner, who has led Foothill Baptist's 5,000-member congregation for decades, called him to the pulpit without fanfare. Kelley stood in the deacon's place and read from Scripture, and the room — two hundred people who'd come to give thanks in the middle of a hard year — held still. He wasn't a guest. He was a member of the faith, doing the work.

At St. Esperanza in Fruitvale the next morning, Fr. Ramon Solano was preparing for All Saints Day. The parish smells like candle wax and the particular quiet of a place built for intention. Families were bringing photographs of their dead to place on the altar. The mood was grief and remembrance both — the kind that holds without rushing.

"All Saints is a day we remember those who've shaped us," Fr. Solano said. "The ones we've lost. The ones we're still learning from."

I thought about what Kelley had said about showing up.

At the Cathedral of the Living Word on Lake Merritt, Bishop Antoine Vermeer was preparing for his own All Saints service, and the cathedral was bright with the particular quality of light that comes through tall windows. The community was gathering. The mood was contemplative and hopeful both — the kind that says: we are here, and we remember, and we continue.

That same week, at Bharat Mandir in Montclair, Diwali was still burning. Oil lamps on the altar, the smell of incense, families moving through the temple in colors — deep reds and golds and purples. The festival of lights, still glowing at the tail end of its week-long observance.

And at the Gurdwara on Fruitvale, the langar kitchen had been preparing meals all morning.

I asked Bhai Manjit what the langar means to the community.

"It means no one eats alone," he said. "That's the whole thing. You come as you are. You sit as you are. You eat. You are part of this."

By Wednesday evening, the kitchen had prepared food for three hundred people. Kelley, who had served as deacon at Foothill Baptist the night before, showed up with other volunteers. He was not there as a celebrity or a guest. He was there as a person doing the work.

The langar spread itself across long tables — dal, vegetables, bread, rice, yogurt. People came steadily all evening. A woman from the neighborhood. A man who'd heard about the meal and decided to come. A family with three children. Kelley stood behind one of the serving tables and ladled dal into bowls, moving with the same quiet intention I'd seen him bring to the deacon's pulpit.

"This is what Guru Nanak believed in," Bhai Manjit said, watching the work unfold. "Equality. Everyone eats the same food. Everyone sits together. That's the beginning of everything else."

I left the langar around eight o'clock and drove across the city — from Fruitvale to Lake Merritt to East Oakland — thinking about convergence. Not as collision. As coexistence. A city where five different altars could be lit in the same week, where five different traditions could make their claims on gratitude, remembrance, light, and service, and the city would hold them all.

The mood across Oakland that week had shifted. You could feel it — a rebound from the heaviness of the cycle before, a holiday season opening. People were gathering again. Not in spite of each other's difference, but alongside it.

On the last evening, I went back to the Gurdwara to sit in the langar one more time. Kelley was there again, this time not serving but sitting, eating with everyone else. No camera, no announcement. Just a man in a community, doing what communities do.

"What brought you back?" I asked.

"The food," he said, with a small smile. "And the people. And the work." He paused. "That's the whole thing, I think. Showing up. Staying."

It was a simple thing, the way he said it. But it held the whole week — all five altars, all five traditions, all of Oakland gathering in its many houses, proving over and over again that belief takes many forms, that service looks the same everywhere, and that a city is strongest not when everyone thinks the same way, but when everyone shows up and does the work.

---

The langar continues every Wednesday at Gurdwara Singh Sabha, Fruitvale Avenue. All are welcome.

### The Foundation and the Pillars: Baylight Phase II Steps Into the Light

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

---

Keisha Ramos waited three cycles too long to name the conflict.

The Baylight Authority Director finally put it on the record this week — a four-part recusal protocol that firewalls the development's second phase from a tangle of overlapping interests so visible that its absence had become its own story. Equity minority. Non-contracting. Naming rights firewalled. Tenancy LOI non-binding. The specificity matters because it exists to contain one man's holdings: Elias Varek owns Civis Systems, owns the Oaks, holds equity in Baylight, and his firm's name is on the new A's stadium rising inside the district.

That triple overlap is not a scandal. It is a test of governance — and for three cycles, Ramos let it sit in silence.

"The process is clean — and now it's documented," Ramos told the Authority this week, according to her filed statement. She did not concede the delay. But the three cycles of silence speak for themselves, and the firewall is finally on the record. That is the story.

---

Civis Systems is headquartered in West Oakland. Forty employees. Sixty million in revenue. Varek, 38, built the firm on urban systems intelligence — modeling transit, construction, emergency response, utilities. The company embedded itself in Baylight's planning architecture early; Varek's equity stake in the district followed. Then the A's stadium naming deal landed: Civis Systems Field, on the site where the A's will play, inside Jack London, where the Phase II development adds housing, retail, and what Varek's firm has been modeling all along.

The recusal protocol Ramos outlined is not a barrier. It is an acknowledgment. Civis loses planning work. Varek holds equity and naming rights — both disclosed, both firewalled from the approval process. The board still votes on Phase II on its merits. Ramos still oversees it.

"When you see the foundation laid, the pillars set, you know a plan can be executed," Mike Paulson, the Oaks general manager, said this cycle. "That's how you become great, this is how Oakland became great. You see the new A's stadium being built but as you approach the build site you pass by what will be housing, retail, and corporate headquarters. What I see will rival what's across the Bay."

That vision — housing, retail, and Varek's naming rights — shares the same square blocks.

---

The protocol exists because Phase I proved what's at stake. The first phase of Baylight delivered 1,400 construction and operations jobs. Forty-three percent went to local workers — three percentage points above the contractual requirement. Twenty-one percent of trades apprenticeships went to women. The numbers held. The mechanism worked.

Phase II is $2.1 billion. The gate schedule is now public: a bidder shortlist narrowing the pool of four to three, then a public award recommendation, then council authorization in regular session — majority vote, not consent calendar. Ramos will oversee each gate without Civis Systems as a planner. Varek keeps his equity and his stadium name.

The conflict is visible. The firewall is documented. The official who let it sit too long finally named it.

That is not corruption. That is governance working, three cycles late.

### The Cost of Coming Back: Davis Returns, the Roster Shifts

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports

---

There's a moment — you know the one — when the good news lands and your first feeling isn't joy. It's the math.

Darrin Davis is back. The whole city exhaled when it was confirmed. I exhaled. This is a man we watched carry this franchise before some of the kids in the left-field bleachers knew who Isley Kelley was. He belongs in green and gold. He belongs at the Coliseum. Nobody is arguing that.

But rosters are not feelings. Rosters are arithmetic. And when you welcome a legend home, somebody pays the tab.

---

Buford Park is a Yankee now.

I want you to sit with that for a second before we move on, because we tend not to sit with these things long enough. Park was twenty-two years old. He was working himself into the conversation at third, doing the kind of unglamorous daily work that scouts notice and columnists undervalue. He didn't ask to be on the wrong side of the equation. He was just the chip the front office needed to make the numbers work.

That is what a winning franchise looks like from the inside. It is not just trophies and curtain calls. Sometimes it is a twenty-two-year-old kid packing a bag for New York because your center fielder is needed at third and your DH is needed at third and your veteran third baseman is needed in a different conversation entirely. That is the cold arithmetic of a roster at 71-25. Every piece has a function. When a new piece arrives, something shifts.

Park was the piece that moved.

---

Ernesto Quintero is twenty-five years old and last cycle he was the number-one prospect on every list that mattered. He earned that. He arrived in this lineup and produced immediately, the way the scouts said he would, the way the ones who'd watched him in Double-A told you he would if you asked the right questions. He was not supposed to be playing third base this week.

He is playing third base this week.

I've gone back and forth on how to feel about that. On one reading it's a demotion story — the breakout kid shoved to a new position to clear the path for the returning star. But I don't think that's actually what this is. Quintero is twenty-five. He is going to play fifteen more years in this league if his body holds. He can learn a new position. The A's are asking him to absorb something, not diminish him — there's a difference, even if it doesn't feel like one in the middle of a season at 71-25 with a pennant in your peripheral vision. He'll be fine. I believe that.

I'm just noting that "fine" required accommodation. And accommodation always costs somebody something.

---

Martin Richards is the part of this story I keep coming back to.

Eight years. Three All-Star selections. The kind of career that, in most cities with most rosters, means you are untouchable at your position until you decide you're not. Richards has been that player. He showed up every spring, he played through the dog days of summer at a level that warranted the respect he'd built, and the city knew what it had in him.

Now he platoons.

I reached him yesterday and his answer was clean, the way only certain veterans can make an answer clean when the situation isn't: "Not ideal but understand, just focused on winning right now."

That's it. That's the whole quote. No bitterness, no performance of graciousness, just the actual thing itself — a man who has been around long enough to know that winning is the only frame that makes any of this bearable. You don't accumulate eight years in a championship window without learning how to absorb the things the championship costs you.

I've been thinking about that quote since he said it. There's a whole speech compressed inside it. Not ideal. I've been here long enough that I know what "not ideal" means coming from a man who spent eight seasons as a starter. It means it hurts. It means he went home after the conversation and probably didn't sleep great. It means he had to find the part of himself that could make peace with it.

But then: understand. He gets why. He sees the roster. He sees the record. He sees Davis in that lineup and he understands what that adds, even if what it subtracts is him.

And then the only part that matters to him right now: just focused on winning.

That is professionalism. That is what eight years of building something does to a man. The championship window is closing — everybody in the clubhouse can feel it, everyone who's been watching this team for a decade can feel it — and Richards has decided he's going to be useful inside that window even if "useful" looks different than it did last spring.

I don't know if I could do that. I genuinely don't.

---

Here's what I know: the A's are 71-25. The dynasty is not a metaphor, it is a fact that keeps arriving in the box score every morning. Davis is back and healthy and that lineup gets better.

But the cost is real. A young prospect boards a flight to New York. A breakout kid adjusts his footwork around the hot corner. A decorated veteran checks his starting slot and swallows it and says the only thing worth saying.

We don't get the return without the bill. We never do. The least we can do is name what the bill was.

"Not ideal but understand, just focused on winning right now."

Yeah. Me too, Marty. Me too.

### Paulson Bets the Future on Now: Oaks Trade Up to No. 1

By Anthony Raines | Bay Tribune Sports

---

The Oakland Oaks have not played a game. They do not have a practice facility that has hosted a single regular-season minute, no playoff lore, no retiring numbers, no banner-raising ceremony that the fanbase has ever waited through. What they have, as of this cycle, is the first overall pick in the NBA draft — and the future first-round selection and the player they surrendered to get there will matter considerably more than the one night of celebrating it.

Mike Paulson made that trade. He moved from fifth to first, sending an unprotected first-round pick three years out — labeled Y5C1 in league filings — along with Herbert Jones, a rotation-caliber piece, to climb five spots. That is the arithmetic of the move: one established player now, one premium selection later, in exchange for the guarantee of selecting the best prospect in a class rather than the fifth-best. Whether the arithmetic resolves in the Oaks' favor depends entirely on who sits at the top of that board, and how long Paulson's timeline turns out to be.

The context worth understanding is that Paulson has made this kind of calculation before. His one season running basketball operations for Chicago — the cycle the Bulls reached the Finals — was built around accelerating a window that looked, from the outside, like it needed another year of patience. He pushed it forward. They lost the Finals. The analysis of that cycle is complicated: the loss was real, but so was the reach, and the organization's trajectory shifted permanently because of the aggression. That is the proof-of-concept Paulson is leaning on in Oakland. He is not, by disposition, a man who builds slowly when he believes the foundation is already there to build on.

The Oaks franchise is a first-year operation. There is no foundation yet — not in the sense of a culture, a championship standard, a player-development pipeline producing known commodities at the top of the rotation. What exists is Elias Varek, who at thirty-eight years old founded Civis Systems and now owns this franchise, and Paulson, who runs basketball operations day to day. What exists, now, is the first pick.

Paulson introduced Elliot Abraham at the announcement, describing him as the operational extension of himself — "the young Jedi," in Paulson's framing — someone who functions as Paulson's presence in day-to-day basketball decisions. That is a meaningful structural admission for a first-year franchise: the GM is already building a second layer of organizational voice, someone who can translate the front office's thinking to the floor without Paulson himself needing to be the sole channel. It is a systems-builder's instinct. It also raises the natural question, for a franchise this new, of whether the infrastructure around the number-one pick is ready to support what a number-one pick demands.

The unprotected future first is the honest cost of this trade. An unprotected pick three years out from a West-conference expansion franchise is a meaningful asset — if the Oaks develop into a playoff team on schedule, that pick slides into the mid-to-late lottery range and the trade costs them relatively little in retrospect. If the development is slower, if Year 3 finds the Oaks still searching for the pieces that fit around their top selection, that pick could be a lottery ticket handed to the team that took their number five. Those two futures are not equidistant in probability. Most expansion franchises, built from scratch, are still building in Year 3. Paulson is betting this one will not follow that pattern.

He may be right. The first pick is real. The player who comes with it has not yet been named in any official capacity, and that name will tell the rest of the story — whether this was a franchise-altering move or an expensive lurch toward a player who needed a different situation. What can be said now is that Paulson treated Year 1 the way experienced operators treat Year 4: with urgency, with a willingness to spend future capital, and with the quiet confidence of someone who has already watched what happens when you press a window before it's ready, and decided he would rather press it than wait.

The Oaks are open. The thread begins here.

### Re: A City That Held Me

I came to Oakland in 1983 with two suitcases and a job laying carpet. Forty years is a long time to watch a neighborhood change. I got up this morning, looked at the Laurel hills from my porch, and thought about how much quieter things are now that I've stepped back. I'm not tired the way I used to be. I'm grateful. Grateful for the work, grateful for the people I installed carpet for, grateful that I'm still here to see it. The city held me. This Thanksgiving I'm thinking about all of that. Not in a sad way. Just — clear.

— Jiu Wong, Laurel

### Re: Fruitvale Rising

Four years ago I was working two jobs. This cycle I got promoted to senior PT at the Fruitvale health center, and I'm training two younger therapists on the way up. That's not luck. That's the neighborhood rising. I see it in the waiting room — families showing up, people booking appointments months ahead because they trust what's here now. Fruitvale didn't get left behind. We built something real. For Thanksgiving I'm grateful for my neighbors who believed in this place when it would've been easier to leave.

— Lucy Ferreira, Fruitvale
