Multiple sources indicate Mike Paulson, Oakland Athletics General Manager, has been approached by the Chicago Bulls organization regarding a potential front office role. The nature of the position remains unclear, as does whether Paulson would maintain his baseball operations responsibilities simultaneously.
Paulson has overseen Oakland’s baseball operations during a period of sustained success, including multiple playoff appearances and the 2040 campaign’s 105-57 regular season finish. No official comment has been issued by either organization.
League sources suggest dual-role arrangements are rare but not unprecedented in cross-sport executive structures. The timing—during Oakland’s playoff run—has raised questions about focus and organizational stability.
Further updates pending official statements.

MAGS CORLISS — Civic Culture
“The Ernesto Moment: When a Kid from Nowhere Became the October Son”
September 26th, 2040. Bottom of the sixth. Astros up by one. Ernesto Quintero—who had been in Las Vegas 48 hours earlier—stood in the box with two runners on.
The crowd knew his ranking (#3 prospect in baseball) before they knew his face. That’s the strange math of modern fandom: we love potential before we love people.
Then he hit a home run.
Not a narrative home run. Not a Hollywood home run. Just a baseball that left the yard and plated two insurance runs in a game Oakland needed to win to stay alive in the division race. Benji Dillon got the win. Quintero got the headline.
I talked to three fans outside the Coliseum afterward. Maria Echeverria, longtime season ticket holder, Section 215: “I didn’t know if I should cheer for the moment or worry we’re burning through prospects.”
Carlos Ming, who drove from Hayward: “That’s our future right there. That’s what we’ve been waiting for.”
And Jesse Kwan, who just turned 18 and wore a jersey with no name on the back yet: “I’ll remember that forever. I was here for his first homer.”
Quintero went 1-for-3. One hit, two RBIs, one moment that mattered. Baseball reduces to box scores, but neighborhoods remember the night a 23-year-old became real.
