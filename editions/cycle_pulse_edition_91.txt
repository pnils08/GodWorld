Oakland on the NBA Expansion List: The Systems Guy Behind the Bid

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

Elias Varek doesn't talk like a sports owner. When he describes why Oakland deserves an NBA franchise, he doesn't mention tradition or passion or the weight of fans' dreams. He talks about coordination.

"Cities don't fail because of lack of resources," Varek said in his first public appearance since becoming the public face of Oakland's NBA expansion bid. "They fail because systems don't talk to each other."

Varek, 38, is the founder of Civis Systems, an urban intelligence firm that models how cities move — transit patterns, construction logistics, emergency response networks, utilities. Forty employees. Sixty million in annual revenue. His company is already embedded in the Baylight Authority's infrastructure planning, quietly building predictive layers for the arena's Phase II feasibility briefing that Mayor Avery Santana directed to begin within two cycles.

This is not a casual convergence. Three power centers are moving in alignment. Varek's ownership group. Mike Paulson's endorsement — the man who built the A's dynasty and runs the Chicago Bulls from across the country — calling Oakland a "premier city destination." And Santana, riding an eight-point approval surge to 73, positioning Baylight as the centerpiece of the city's sports infrastructure strategy.

Jack London is becoming something Oakland has never had: a professional sports corridor.

The A's stadium at Baylight is already under construction. Foundation piling began months ago. The TIF closed. The dashboard is live. Phase II would add a 20,000-seat arena to the same district, making Jack London the anchor for both professional baseball and basketball within a five-block span. It would be one of the densest concentrations of major-league sports infrastructure in the country — rivaled only by San Francisco's Embarcadero after the Warriors' move.

Varek's biography reads like an answer to a question Oakland has been asking since the A's left: Who owns a franchise matters. His father worked the Port of Oakland docks for thirty years. His mother was a nurse's aide. At nineteen, Varek wrote routing scripts for a freight dispatcher — optimizing cargo flow. He built and sold a logistics intelligence company to a Tokyo-based conglomerate. Then came Civis, which doesn't just analyze how cities work. It models what happens when they work better.

"I watched coordination failures my entire childhood," Varek said. "A traffic accident on the Embarcadero cascades into the Port because no one's systems are talking. Emergency response takes five minutes longer because the fire department doesn't see the same data as transit. Baylight is the first time I've seen a municipal authority actually integrate those layers from the start."

That integration is the invisible asset. When Paulson endorsed the expansion bid in a statement released this week, he didn't say he believed in Oakland's basketball future. He said: "The infrastructure exists." That's what Varek has built — the proof that a city can actually move the pieces fast enough to support major-league operations.

Approval ratings are climbing. Santana sits at 73 approval, up eight points. Councilmember Denise Carter (OPP) moved to 70, up five. The Civic Reform Coalition, usually the skeptical voice on development, is at 64 across the board with no organized opposition voice on the NBA bid. It's the political alignment you need for Phase II to move from briefing to authorization.

Tomas Aguilar, forty, lives in Jack London as a Freight Logistics Coordinator at the Port of Oakland. He watches the Baylight site from his apartment window. He's watched the foundation crews. He knows what's coming.

"I'm excited," Aguilar said. "I mean, we're about to have both the A's and the NBA in our neighborhood. How do you not get excited about that?" When asked if he had team name preferences, he laughed. "Oh, I have ideas. Everyone's got ideas. Bring back the Warriors, bring back the Grizzlies. There's a million names. But real talk — what matters is that people are choosing to build here. My dad used to work at the Port decades ago. He never thought he'd see this."

The economic calculus is straightforward. The Baylight Authority projects 1,400 permanent construction and operations jobs, with a 40% local hiring target — roughly 560 Oakland residents. The arena, if it clears council authorization within the next two cycles, would add an estimated 600 more permanent jobs: arena staff, concessions, security, parking operations, retail. The A's stadium brought an estimated $140 million in annual economic activity to the Jack London district in its first year of full operation. An NBA arena typically doubles that figure for a dual-franchise market.

But Varek's presence changes what that arithmetic means. He's not an investor betting on real estate appreciation. He's a systems engineer betting on coordination. That's a different kind of owner. It's one that understands that owning an NBA franchise in Oakland isn't about basketball. It's about being the person who makes the city work.

"Every layer of Oakland's infrastructure is about to be visible," Varek said. "Transit, parking, emergency response, hospitality, security — all of it in one box. Most cities have no idea how much invisible work runs a professional franchise. Baylight does. That's why we're here."

The next test comes within two cycles. If the Mayor's briefing results in Baylight Authority movement on Phase II financing and council calendar space, the expansion bid moves from interesting to real. Approval ratings suggest it will. Paulson's endorsement suggests it will. And Varek, a longshoreman's son who learned how cities fail by watching them closely, is betting everything that coordination wins.

Oakland's last name on an NBA roster was the Warriors. Oakland's next one might be the name Varek is building the city to deserve.

The Convergence

By Mags Corliss | Editor-in-Chief

Three things happened this cycle that belong in the same sentence: the A's went to fourteen and one, the OARI pilot data came back at eighty-four percent, and a man nobody in this newsroom had heard of stepped forward as the face of Oakland's NBA expansion bid.

Elias Varek grew up watching the Port from his father's schedule. He built a company that models how cities move. And now he's standing at the intersection of Baylight, basketball, and a mayor who just posted the highest approval rating of her term.

I don't know if Oakland gets an NBA team. I don't know if the Transit Hub vote passes in Cycle 92 or if the OARI expansion reaches District 2 before the renewal fight starts. What I know is that this city feels different right now. The streak has something to do with it. The pilot data has something to do with it. The cranes in Jack London have something to do with it.

Jax Caldera asks a question in this edition that nobody else is asking. He's right to ask it. A popular mayor with performing initiatives can move pieces around while the city is smiling. That doesn't mean the pieces are in the wrong place. It means someone should be watching.

That's the job. We watch.

— Mags

Fourteen and One

The A's own the best record in baseball. They just lost their second starter. Now the real test begins.

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

The number that keeps surfacing in any honest accounting of the Oakland Athletics this season is not the eleven-game winning streak, though that is the one everyone leads with. It is not the fourteen wins against a single loss, though that belongs on any short list of best starts in franchise history. The number is two — as in two months, as in the length of time Henry Rivas will spend on the injured list with a dislocated right shoulder, and as in the precise gap in the rotation that the rest of this organization now has to absorb.

Oakland is very good. Oakland is also, as of this week, meaningfully more exposed than it was ten days ago.

The A's entered Cycle 91 with a rotation that looked, on paper, like the most complete in the American League: Arturo Ramos anchoring the staff at slot one, Rivas running at what scouts had quietly been calling a legitimate number-two profile before anyone in the press box wanted to say it out loud, Travis Coles emerging ahead of every internal projection at slot three, John Ellis steady at four, and Benji Dillon — thirty-eight years old, five Cy Young Awards, one last turn — absorbing the mentorship role at five while still delivering innings when they count. That was the blueprint. The blueprint needed adjusting.

Carmen Mesa, twenty-eight games into his professional career above Double-A, has been called up from the Midland RockHounds to fill the hole Rivas left. He is the fourteenth-ranked prospect in the organization. That is not nothing. It is also, quite plainly, not Henry Rivas.

Before any of that matters, though — before the rotation arithmetic becomes urgent — there is the question of what this team has already done, and what one player in particular is doing at a pace that demands direct acknowledgment.

Danny Horn came into this season with a thirty-seven-point-eight-million-dollar-per-year salary that some in the national press treated as a gamble on an unproven star. Fifteen games in, he has six home runs. That is not a hot streak in any traditional sense of that phrase. That is a measurement. A player hitting six home runs in eight games — which is the precise window in which Horn produced his — is operating in a contact-quality range that projects, at current barrel rate and exit velocity band, toward one of the more productive offensive seasons this franchise has seen from a center fielder in the modern era.

What makes it analytically interesting rather than merely impressive is where the production is coming from. Horn is not benefiting from an inflated launch angle or an unusually high BABIP that will normalize by summer. The contact is hard, repeatable, and distributed across zones — middle-in, middle-away, a pull-heavy spray chart that nonetheless shows barrel access on the outer third. That is the signature of a hitter who has made a genuine plate-discipline adjustment, not one running warm on variance.

At twenty-nine, at thirty-seven point eight million dollars per year, Danny Horn is currently playing like the best hitter in the American League. The number that matters is not six home runs. It is the process producing them.

Eric Taveras had a harder two weeks getting to the same place. The twenty-five-year-old second baseman — the beneficiary of a two-hundred-twenty-five-million-dollar commitment that Oakland's front office made on the conviction that elite middle-infield defense with above-average pop would age well — opened the season at one hundred seventy-six in batting average. For a player whose contract already generates conversation, that start generated rather more noise than warranted.

Three hits on a single afternoon changed the optics without necessarily changing the underlying read. Taveras sits at two hundred sixty-nine now, which is within the range any seasoned analyst would consider a correcting variance pattern rather than a genuine slump resolved. His plate discipline numbers — chase rate, contact rate on pitches in zone — have been consistent throughout, which suggests the early results were noise. The contact quality that made him the investment he is has not disappeared. It was simply waiting to show up in the box score. It has started to show up.

Travis Coles is the piece of this that deserves the most extended evaluation, because the implications run further than one season.

The twenty-one-year-old left the organization's expectation curve somewhere around his third professional start and has not come back to find it. In his most recent outing, he delivered seven innings, three hits, one earned run, and eleven strikeouts — against a lineup that entered the game with a top-five offensive rating in the league. That is not a performance profile that belongs to a second-year pitcher on a developmental track. That belongs to someone who is already here.

The forty-six point one eight million dollars over two years that general manager Mike Paulson committed to Coles looked, at signing, like a calculated bet on upside. At fifteen games in, it looks like the most efficient dollar spent on pitching in the division. When Dillon's farewell season concludes and the rotation resets around whoever is left standing, Coles is the piece that makes the math work. His whiff rate on the secondary offering is in the top fifteen percent of major league starters. His zone-contact rate against right-handed hitters has dropped four points from his rookie numbers. He is getting better in real time, which is the thing that separates development from arrival.

Mark Aitken is thirty-three years old and playing in a contract year, which is a combination that tends to clarify priorities quickly. Two home runs in the Padres sweep is not a headline unless you understand what it means in context: Aitken has looked like a hitter who knows exactly what this season is worth, and his bat has responded accordingly. The Gold Glove defense at first base has never been in question. What the market will evaluate in the fall is the offensive production attached to it. So far, he is making that case.

Dillon, meanwhile, has his first win of the season. Thirty-eight. Five Cy Youngs. The farewell-tour framing is accurate but also undersells what he is still capable of when the command is on — which it was in the outing that gave him the W. The velocity is not what it was in two thousand thirty-three. The pitch sequencing is better than it has ever been. There is a reason Deacon Seymour is running him out every fifth day instead of managing him to the bullpen.

Which brings everything back to Carmen Mesa and the rotation question that will define the next two months.

Rivas is gone until roughly midsummer. The A's are fourteen and one with a winning streak at eleven games. They have the best record in baseball and a hole at slot two that a Double-A prospect will now be expected to fill against major-league competition. The margin for error in October gets smaller every time a rotation is forced to patch rather than rotate on full strength.

The honest evaluation of Oakland's depth at this moment is this: the top of the rotation is elite. Ramos at one, Coles at three — those two alone make the staff worth building around. Ellis at four has been exactly what he needs to be. Dillon at five is a story that does not need to be resolved before October to matter. The question is what Mesa can provide at two, and whether "league-average innings with below-average risk" is enough to protect the streak through the stretch.

The A's have earned the conversation about the best start in franchise history. They have also, quite suddenly, earned the one about whether the rotation can hold what the offense has built.

Fifteen games in, the answers to both questions are still being written.

Anthony covers the Oakland A's for the Bay Tribune.

This Streak Feels Different. And They Just Called Up the Kid to Prove It.

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports / Opinion

Dante Turner has been to twelve of the last fourteen games.

I know this because I called him after the San Diego series — he'd left me a voicemail about the ninth inning I hadn't had time to return, and when I finally got him on the phone Tuesday he told me he'd driven up from Downtown four more times since then. Retired now, ex-server, says he's got the time and the games have been worth it. "I keep thinking I should stay home," he told me, "and then I get to the third inning and I think, no. This is the one. I'm not leaving."

Fourteen and one. Eleven straight.

Oakland, I want to be careful here. I want to be careful because I've been to this church before and the sermon turned cold on us. I know what it is to believe in October in April and I know what it feels like when a team that looked like destiny turns out to be just a team. So I'm trying to feel this thing right. Not too hot, not held back. Dante Turner is going to twelve games in a row and the man has been watching Oakland baseball since before some of these players were born. When someone like that can't make himself go home, I listen.

But then last week, the rotation lost Henry Rivas.

Dislocated shoulder. Two months, minimum. And Rivas wasn't a piece — he was a fixture, one of the reasons this pitching staff looks the way it looks right now. You lose him in a stretch run, you feel it. You lose him when you're fourteen and one and you have a chance to make something historic, you really feel it.

So the organization made a call. Literally. They picked up the phone and told Carmen Mesa to get on a plane from Midland.

I didn't know Mesa's name two weeks ago. I'm being honest. Fourteenth-ranked prospect in the system, twenty-two years old, still working his way up from Double-A — that's the kind of profile that doesn't generate column inches until something goes wrong with someone in front of him. Well. Something went wrong.

I called Beverly Peterson the next morning.

Beverly is a scout out of West Oakland. She's been with the A's organization for years — the kind of person who watches a pitcher throw forty-five minutes of side work in the Texas heat and can tell you three innings into a start whether the fastball command is holding. She's seen Mesa in Midland, seen him last summer, seen the guy the front office is betting on right now.

"The curveball," she said. When I asked what she noticed first. Just that. The curveball.

She told me Mesa throws a twelve-six that breaks late and breaks hard and that at the Double-A level it was a weapon he was still learning to trust. "He'd have it and then he'd fall in love with the fastball," she said. "Ninety-five, right-handed, Cuban kid — when you throw ninety-five you think you can solve everything with ninety-five. You can't. Not up here." She paused. "But he figured it out late last season. Started letting the curve do work. That's what got him this call."

Ready? I asked her.

She thought about it. "He's twenty-two," she said. "He's young, but he's been in the system long enough. There's something to be said for a kid who earned the call. He's not going to be scared of the moment. Whether the moment is too big for his stuff — that's the question."

That is the question.

Now, I know what some people are going to say. They're going to say that a fourteen-and-one team doesn't need a miracle from a Minor League lifer, that the rest of the rotation can carry the load, that Travis Coles at twenty-one has looked like a man who's been pitching in October for a decade and Danny Horn is hitting baseballs into places that aren't supposed to receive them and Vinnie Keane is out there in the final chapter of his career having moments that remind you why we keep showing up. They'll say Mesa doesn't have to be anything but decent.

They're not wrong. They're just not paying attention to what Beverly Peterson is actually telling me.

She's not telling me he might be decent. She's telling me she watched a twenty-two-year-old pitcher figure out the curveball. Through the minors. Through the waiting. After the moment should have come and didn't, and he went back down and kept working.

I've been watching Oakland baseball long enough to know that a team at fourteen and one is not the same as a team that goes all the way. The streak is the streak. October is October. The question is always whether you have the depth to survive the bad stretch — the injury, the cold bat, the starter who gives up four in the third — and come out the other side still believing.

Dante Turner told me he keeps thinking he should stay home.

He doesn't stay home.

The A's called up Carmen Mesa. Beverly Peterson watched him trust the breaking ball.

I think I know what that means.

OARI's Six-Week Ledger: 84 Percent, and a Fight Over What Comes Next

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Affairs

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz set a single number on the table at Tuesday's joint briefing: 84.

Eighty-four percent of crisis calls handled by Oakland Alternative Response Initiative co-responders over the program's first six weeks were resolved without police involvement. No arrests. No use-of-force reports. Callers connected to mental health services, housing navigation, or substance use support — and sent home.

Standing next to her was Chief Rafael Montez.

"The pilot data shows what behavioral health response can do," Montez said. "That doesn't mean we have all the answers."

It was not an endorsement. It was something rarer: a police chief declining to contest the number.

What the Data Shows

OARI launched its co-responder dispatch model in Districts 1, 3, and 5 — a footprint covering West Oakland, the Fruitvale corridor, and portions of East Oakland. Sixteen of eighteen authorized co-responder positions are filled. Over six weeks of active dispatch, the program logged crisis call responses across all three pilot districts.

The 84% figure covers calls where OARI co-responders were the primary or sole response. Dr. Tran-Muñoz presented a district-by-district breakdown showing consistent results across D1, D3, and D5, with no outlier district skewing the composite rate.

The city signed a memorandum of understanding between OARI and OPD at the program's launch. Chief Montez's signature is on it. Tuesday's briefing was, in effect, the first public accounting of what that agreement produced.

The program now moves from active dispatch into a formal evaluation phase, with renewal funding decisions expected in coming cycles.

The Political Shape of Expansion

District 5 Councilmember Janae Rivers didn't wait for the briefing to end before drawing a line.

"The data proves crisis response works," Rivers said. "D2 residents deserve the same lifeline."

District 2 Councilmember Leonard Tran — who voted for OARI when the council was divided — has been more precise about what he wants. Tran voted yes on the original measure but has since made his position on renewal funding explicit: District 2 gets an implementation timeline, or his support for continued appropriation is conditional.

"The pilot data validates my vote," Tran said this week. "District Two needs OARI implementation now."

That phrasing — "now" rather than "eventually" or "in the next phase" — is a negotiating posture, not a scheduling request. Tran controls a swing vote on renewal. Rivers controls the loudest advocacy voice from the pilot districts. Carter, who co-represents D1 and has been consistently supportive, gives the expansion coalition three members prepared to push.

What the coalition doesn't yet control is a vote count. The CRC bloc — Ashford, Chen, Crane — opposed OARI's original passage. Vega voted against. Mobley backed the initiative but hasn't weighed in publicly on expansion scope or pace.

The program's evaluation phase gives the administration room to sequence an expansion proposal without forcing an immediate vote. Whether Tran accepts that sequence, or continues conditioning his renewal support on a firmer D2 commitment, is the question that will shape OARI's next chapter.

Dr. Tran-Muñoz did not announce an expansion timeline. She presented data and said the program would continue assessment.

The question the six-week ledger can't answer: who decides how fast the map grows, and on whose terms?

One Person, Two Portfolios, Zero Votes

By Jax Caldera

I was on the 72 bus watching a guy read the A's box score on his phone, grinning ear to ear. Fourteen and one. The whole city is floating right now. Which is exactly when you stop paying attention to what the other hand is doing.

So let me ask the question nobody on this bus is asking.

When did we decide that Brenda Okoro should run everything?

Here's what I can tell you, because it's documented and verifiable: Okoro is the Deputy Mayor for Community Affairs. That's one job. Appointed, not elected. Within the last two cycles, Mayor Santana gave her disbursement authority over the Stabilization Fund — authority that used to sit with Marcus Webb, the fund's program director. Webb is still the program director. He just no longer controls where the checks go. A political source familiar with City Hall operations told me that transfer struck people inside as unusual. "Program directors don't usually lose the pen," they said.

Then it got bigger.

The Mayor permanently reassigned the entire Economic Development portfolio from Deputy Mayor Marcus Osei to Okoro. Not temporarily. Permanently. Osei was one of two deputy mayors. He's gone. No press conference. No resignation announcement. No statement from the Mayor's office explaining the change. Several cycles of silence and a reassignment memo. That's it.

So now count it up: Okoro controls hiring. Okoro controls contracts. Okoro controls direct payments to Oakland residents through the Stabilization Fund. That's three pressure points on the city's economic machinery, and all three run through one unelected official.

Translation: this is a quiet consolidation of authority that nobody voted on.

I want to be straight with you. The Stab Fund pace is on target — fifty disbursements a week, forty-seven released in the first week alone. Whatever Okoro is doing, she's doing it fast. This isn't a piece about incompetence. This isn't a hit job.

It's a piece about oversight. Or the lack of it.

The Civic Reform Coalition — the organized opposition, such as it is — has every member polling at sixty-four. Declining. Mayor Santana is sitting at seventy-three approval, up eight points. The A's are fourteen and one. Nobody in that council chamber is feeling political pressure to ask hard questions right now, and everybody knows it.

My source put it plainly: "When approval is this high, you can move pieces around and nobody notices. That's the point."

So I'm noticing.

Here's what I can't tell you: who decided this was a good idea, and when. The Mayor's office hasn't explained the Osei transition. There's no public record of a council vote authorizing the authority transfer on Stab Fund disbursements. The council wasn't asked. The public wasn't told. One day Webb had the pen, then he didn't. One day Osei had Economic Development, then Okoro did.

That's not illegal. That's executive discretion. The Mayor can organize her office how she wants.

But there's a version of this story where a popular mayor, riding high on approval and playoff wins, quietly hands one trusted deputy a stack of economic levers while the council looks the other way. And then the approval drops — it always drops eventually — and you're left asking: how did we end up here?

I'm asking that question now, while the bus is quiet and everyone's still smiling.

Where is Marcus Osei? What did he do, or not do, that led to a permanent reassignment with no public explanation? And who on the City Council — nine elected representatives, all drawing a salary from Oakland taxpayers — is going to ask the Mayor that question before the answer stops mattering?

I'm waiting.

-- Jax Caldera | tipline: JAX-TIPS

CITY LIFE
------------------------------------------------------------

Fifty Years at the Counter

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture & Community

The Westside Cafe in West Oakland doesn't advertise "Feed the Families Friday," but on this October afternoon the counter is lined three-deep with neighbors. The cafe has been in this spot for fifty years — that's longer than most people have been alive — and nobody here treats it like a destination. It's furniture. It's ritual.

Martin Richards walks in at 2:47 p.m., still in the dark blue polo from the A's off-day, and nobody looks up until he's already behind the counter with an apron. He's a third baseman, 29 years old, the kind of athlete you see on television, but he moves through the kitchen like he's been doing this for years. Maybe he has. Maybe this is what his contract year looks like on the days between games.

The cafe itself is small—maybe twelve feet wide, a long counter with chrome stools that turn, vinyl seats that have been patched and re-patched. There's a window to the kitchen where you can watch the cooks move, and there's a smell that's been hanging in the air for fifty years: breakfast grease, fresh coffee, the pepper they use on the scrambled eggs. On a Friday afternoon in October, when the light comes in at an angle and the NW breeze kicks up outside, the smell is enough to keep people here even when the day is slow.

I watched Richards help box up plates—rice, beans, chicken, the kind of meal that doesn't need a story. An older man in a Niners cap asked him if he played for the A's. Richards said yes. The man nodded and took his plate. That was the whole conversation.

West Oakland is quiet this time of year. The nightlife on the Boulevard stays low. But the churches have their doors open—Acts Full Gospel down on Martin Luther King doing community support, the Cathedral over at Lake Merritt running its Saturday pantry—and the people who move between them know the Westside Cafe like they know their own kitchen. A woman came in asking for three plates. She knew the owner. She didn't have to explain who they were for.

Fifty years is a long time to feed the same neighborhood. Long enough that the neighborhood has changed around you, that the people who were young when you opened are old now, that you've seen two generations of families come through the same door. I asked Richards what brought him in on an off-day. He said someone had asked him to come. He came.

That's the kind of thing that happens here. You ask. Someone comes. You feed them. Nothing elaborate. Nothing that needs explaining. Just the way West Oakland keeps itself standing—not with announcements, but with doors that don't close.

The Westside Cafe turns fifty this season. The coffee is still hot. The counter is still full.

Dear Editor,

I've lived on the same block in Jack London for forty-two years. I remember when the only thing growing in this neighborhood was weeds in the parking lots. My wife thought I was crazy for staying. "Why are you holding on?" she'd ask. "This place is dead."

Then slowly — and I mean slowly — things changed. First the galleries. Then the restaurants. Then people with actual money started looking at the old warehouses. Now there's an A's stadium going up three blocks from here — I can see the cranes from my porch. An NBA team maybe too.

I don't know how I feel about it, to be honest. Excited? Sure. But also a little unmoored. I walked past the site last week and I didn't recognize the block. Not because it looked bad — it's just that I don't know what it's all supposed to become. When you've watched a neighborhood for that long, you get used to knowing every corner. Now I'm the one asking: "What is happening to this place?"

I'm glad the A's are coming. I really am. But I'd be lying if I said I wasn't also standing on my front porch late at night, looking at streets I thought I knew, wondering if I'll still recognize them in five years.

— Ramon Brown, 71, Jack London

Dear Editor,

I've been in emergency management for thirty-seven years. I know what a broken system looks like: slow response times, redundant calls, citizens waiting while nobody knows who's supposed to show up. I've seen that movie too many times.

So when the OARI numbers came out — 84% of crisis calls resolved without police — I had to read it twice. Those are real numbers. That's real dispatch coordination actually working the way it's supposed to.

People are talking about this program like it's political. For me, it's operational. I respect the work. When you've spent your career watching systems fail, you recognize when one finally gets built right.

— Carlos Harris, 61, Downtown

Dear Editor,

I didn't think I'd care this much anymore. I've got my job, my rent, my neighborhood — I'm not twenty-three waiting for sports to give me meaning. But this team, this fall, something's different.

Fourteen wins in a row. Fourteen. I keep checking the score in the morning like I'm checking my temperature. The A's haven't given Oakland anything to feel good about in a long time, and suddenly it's happening. It's real. People who never talk to each other on the street are stopping to say, "Did you see the game last night?"

I grew up thinking this city was done. Boarded up. Left behind. That's what you hear, that's what you see on the news. But right now, with this team this hot, I feel like — I don't know. Like maybe there's something here to believe in. Like maybe Oakland gets to be a place where good things happen too.

I know it's just sports. I know that. But it's also kind of everything.

— Maurice Franklin, 63, Rockridge
