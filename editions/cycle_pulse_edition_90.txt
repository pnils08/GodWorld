Crisis Responders Finally Deploy as Police Chief Signs OARI Agreement

The program that stalled for three months went live the same morning its loudest critic held a press conference naming the man who just signed it

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

On the same morning that Councilmember Janae Rivers stepped to a podium at the West Oakland Neighbors Association office on Peralta Street and named Police Chief Rafael Montez as the obstacle blocking Oakland's alternative crisis response program, Montez was signing the document she said he'd refused to sign.

Neither of them knew what the other was doing until it was done.

The Oakland Police Department's dispatch integration MOU for the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative — the agreement that had been stalled for more than three months after the council authorized the program on a 5-4 vote — was signed by Chief Montez this week, effective immediately. Co-responder teams will begin taking crisis calls in Districts 1, 3, and 5 as the primary response to behavioral health and substance use emergencies.

"Behavioral health calls are not police calls," Montez said in a written statement. "Today, they'll get a behavioral health response."

Mayor Avery Santana had issued a direct order to Montez the previous Friday, telling him the signature was not optional and setting an end-of-business deadline. "We hired 18 responders to deploy a program the council voted for," Santana said. "The Police Chief's signature is not optional."

What followed was a compressed, parallel sequence: Santana's ultimatum went out Friday. Rivers scheduled her press conference for Monday morning. Montez signed before lunch.

Rivers did not back down at the podium even after learning the MOU had been executed. "Chief Montez has been asked to sign the OARI dispatch integration agreement for three months," she told reporters. "The OPP caucus represents 120,000 Oakland residents. Forty-seven thousand of those live in West Oakland, Fruitvale, and East Oakland. They voted for a different kind of public safety response."

Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz, who leads the OARI program, had 16 of 18 responders hired and trained before the dispatch agreement was finalized. Her team has been operationally ready for weeks, waiting on a signature to become real. Under the new protocol, OPD dispatch routes crisis calls in D1, D3, and D5 to OARI co-responder teams first. Officers remain available as backup per established protocol.

In his statement, Montez described the decision as operational rather than political. "Crisis calls handled by behavioral health professionals have lower risk profiles and better outcomes," he wrote. "The dispatch protocol is sound. Delay serves no public safety purpose."

That framing — a technical decision by a professional agency — pointedly omitted any reference to the months of pressure from the Mayor, the council, and, as of Monday morning, public naming by the district councilmember. Montez did not hold a press conference. He submitted a written statement.

The program's passage in the council chambers was itself close. OARI was approved 5-4, with Carter, Rivers, Delgado, Mobley, and Tran voting in favor. Vega, Crane, Ashford, and Chen voted no. District 2 Councilmember Leonard Tran, who cast the deciding vote, was not among those celebrating the deployment this week. His attention is already on the next phase — what D2 gets, and when.

For now, in Districts 1, 3, and 5, the program is live.

After Eight Months, Stabilization Fund Checks Finally in the Mail

Webb loses the checkbook; Okoro takes over; Beverly Hayes gets $18,500

By Carmen Delaine | Bay Tribune Civic Ledger

Beverly Hayes, 58, a home health aide from West Oakland, has known her check was approved since the spring. The amount — $18,500 — would cover the rent gap between her current apartment near 34th and Adeline and the cheaper unit two neighborhoods over that she's been trying not to need. The money was authorized. Her application was stamped. Weeks passed.

This week, it ships.

Mayor Avery Santana announced an enforcement action Monday that transfers disbursement authority for the West Oakland Emergency Stabilization Fund from OEWD Director Marcus Webb to Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro, effective immediately. The 47 families whose applications have been approved — Hayes among them — will receive their checks by October 7.

"Forty-seven families approved for stability money don't get to wait indefinitely," Santana said. "Okoro has the checkbook now."

The action was pointed. The fund, passed by the council 6-3 two years ago with $28 million authorized, had approved 47 applications and mailed zero checks. Webb retains program oversight and responsibility for processing the remaining 295 applications currently in queue, but the authority to release funds is gone from his desk.

Webb's office did not respond to a request for comment.

Council President Ramon Vega, whose oversight committee reviewed the approved applications, announced his own mandate alongside the Mayor's action: a minimum of 50 applications processed per week, with weekly reporting to the full council. The 295 still in review will work through that pace or not at all.

"Forty-seven families have been approved," Vega said. "They don't need more review — they need their checks."

For families waiting in the longer queue, the target math is meaningful. At 50 applications per week — and assuming all result in approvals — the full pending pool would clear in roughly six weeks. But approvals are not guaranteed, and the review process itself has drawn criticism for its pace and opacity. The weekly reporting requirement is new.

Darius Clark, a bakery worker in West Oakland who submitted his application earlier this year, is among those in the pending pool. Whether his application falls in the first round of the weekly targets or later depends on the sequencing Okoro's team establishes — that process is not yet public.

Brenda Okoro has been managing the Stabilization Fund's community-facing operations for several weeks, a role that expanded when Deputy Mayor Marcus Osei began his medical recovery. Her office declined to discuss specifics of the disbursement schedule beyond confirming the October 7 deadline for the 47 approved checks.

Hayes, for her part, has not changed her plans based on the check's expected arrival. She'd been told it was coming before.

EDITOR'S DESK
------------------------------------------------------------

By Mags Corliss | Bay Tribune

October came in with a breeze off the Bay and the sound of things actually happening.

I have sat in this chair for eighty-nine editions watching initiatives pass council votes and then stall in implementation hallways. I have written headlines about programs approved, funding authorized, deadlines set — and then written the follow-ups about deadlines missed, funding frozen, and programs that existed on paper and nowhere else.

This month is different. Not because the city suddenly became competent — it took an executive order, a public shaming, and a press conference that arrived twenty minutes too late to change the thing it was protesting. But the results are real. Chief Montez signed the OARI dispatch agreement. Crisis responders will take their first calls in Districts 1, 3, and 5. Beverly Hayes will receive $18,500 she was promised eight months ago. Foundation piles will go into the ground at the Baylight waterfront.

Things moved. Not gracefully. Not on time. But they moved.

The paper you are reading reflects a city in October that is delivering on its promises — late, pressured, and under watch, but delivering. The A's are 7-1. Danny Horn has six home runs. The Bulls are in the Finals. The weather is 62 degrees and perfect. If you are looking for a month where Oakland feels like Oakland is supposed to feel, this is it.

We are watching.

The Vote That Made OARI Possible Has a Price Tag Attached

Leonard Tran cast the deciding vote — and he wants the city to know what he expects in return

By Luis Navarro | Bay Tribune Investigations

Leonard Tran cast the vote that made OARI happen. He wants the city to remember that.

District 2 Councilmember Tran was the fifth and deciding vote on the Oakland Alternative Response Initiative — the 5-4 margin that passed the program in council. Without him, the behavioral health co-responder pilot now launching in Districts 1, 3, and 5 does not exist. His constituents in Downtown and Chinatown supported the concept. He voted accordingly.

His district is not in the pilot.

That gap — the deciding vote for a program that won't serve his neighborhood first — was always the arrangement. Tran went along with it. This week, he clarified what going along was worth.

"I cast the deciding vote on OARI for my district," Tran said in a statement Monday. "If D2 expansion stalls, so does my support for renewal funding."

The statement arrives as the pilot is just now beginning. Responders in D1, D3, and D5 have not fielded their first call yet. The program has no track record, no data, and no timeline for expansion. Tran is not waiting to raise the issue.

His position, stated plainly: when OARI comes back to the council for renewal funding, the renewal package must include a specific D2 expansion timeline — months, staffing numbers, budget allocation. Vague language about future consideration is not acceptable. If the expansion plan is absent from the renewal package, Tran will recommend the council reassess whether to fund the next phase.

"The program works," Tran said. "Prove it in the pilot. Then bring me a D2 plan, and I'll fund the next phase. Stall on expansion while other neighborhoods build a track record, and I'll recommend the council reassess that renewal vote."

The statement has an audience of one, primarily: Mayor Avery Santana, who has spent the past month leveraging her authority to get Chief Montez to sign the dispatch agreement. Santana now has the program she fought for. Tran is registering what he expects in return.

Whether that leverage holds depends on what the pilot looks like in six months. If Dr. Vanessa Tran-Muñoz's teams generate the outcomes-data behavioral health advocates have been projecting — lower officer involvement, faster crisis resolution, stronger follow-up care — the case for D2 expansion will make itself and Tran's vote becomes a formality. If the pilot sputters, his leverage evaporates alongside the program's credibility.

Neither Rivers nor the Mayor's office responded specifically to Tran's statement. They don't have to respond yet. The renewal vote is months away. For now, OARI launches in three districts. District 2 waits.

What Tran did not say, but his statement implies clearly: the coalition that passed OARI is not the same coalition that renews it. The deciding vote is watching.

Baylight Piling Begins October 10: Transparency Fight Reshapes Megaproject

Ramos executes financial close while Ashford demands the dashboard come first

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

The Baylight Authority will execute its $2.1 billion financial close and begin foundation piling on October 10, moving the megaproject from negotiation and environmental remediation into visible construction on the waterfront. But the project's entry into the physical build phase has surfaced a structural conflict: Council Member Warren Ashford introduced a motion last month demanding a transparency dashboard before the TIF close, while Keisha Ramos, the Authority Director, has committed to delivering the accountability framework simultaneously with the financial close — making it impossible for the council to condition the fund release on dashboard completion.

"We are delivering the transparency layer," Ramos said in a statement to the Tribune. "It will be operational on October 10. The idea that we should stop a $2.1 billion project because a dashboard isn't ready first — that's not how capital mobilization works."

Ashford's position reflects deeper skepticism about TIF fund governance. The Baylight project secured 30 acres under a TIF authority last year that will recirculate property tax revenue back into the project for three decades. Ashford, who established the council's quarterly TIF oversight framework, argues that the dashboard must precede the financial close to ensure council visibility into how the Authority intends to deploy those funds and against what benchmarks.

"I'm not blocking this project," Ashford said in an interview. "I'm asking for accountability to exist before we hand over $2.1 billion in captive tax revenue. The dashboard lets us see hiring targets, construction schedules, contract awards. If Ramos can deliver it simultaneously, fine — but simultaneously isn't the same as precedent."

The dispute hinges on what "transparency" means operationally. The Authority's dashboard, according to Ramos's statement, will track construction progress, local hiring figures against the 40 percent target (roughly 1,400 jobs citywide, 560 in Oakland), and TIF fund allocation by category. The council's oversight framework — introduced in the TIF vote itself — calls for quarterly reporting on these metrics.

Ashford's motion asks for the dashboard to exist before the TIF close creates the obligation to report. Ramos is moving forward with or without that sequencing.

What remains unresolved is the relationship between the Youth Apprenticeship Pipeline — a $12.5 million labor development program established three years ago to feed skilled workers into local construction — and Baylight's actual staffing needs. The program was designed to ramp up skilled labor ahead of the project's construction start. That window has passed — piling begins this month — and the Tribune has not found updated enrollment or placement figures for the pipeline this year.

Foundation piling will be visible from the waterfront corridor and from Jack London Square, marking the project's shift from planning into execution. Construction traffic and staging areas will alter the logistics of the port district's immediate surrounds. The Authority has committed to weekly construction alerts posted on the project website. This week, a crew will begin site preparation.

Stabilization Fund Checks Begin Rolling: First 47 Households This Week

The money that took eight months to move finally hits West Oakland mailboxes

By Jordan Velez | Bay Tribune Business

Forty-seven Oakland households are scheduled to receive Stabilization Fund disbursements this week after Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro accepted transfer of authority from the previous administrator, OEWD Director Marcus Webb, and approved a processing acceleration that targets fifty households per week for the remainder of the quarter. The move clears a seven-month backlog that has left 295 additional households approved or provisionally qualified but waiting for checks since the fund's initial authorization in January.

Beverly Hayes, a fifty-eight-year-old home health aide in West Oakland, is among the forty-seven. She applied for the fund in March, was approved in August, and is scheduled to receive $18,500 this week — the amount the fund calculates for a single-income household living in the highest-displacement corridor of the city. Hayes has lived in her current apartment for fourteen years. Rents in her building have remained stable, but property tax assessments have risen 60 percent in that span, creating pressure on the owners to raise market rates when leases renew.

"I do not know if $18,500 holds me here for two years or three," Hayes said in an interview. "But it means I can stay while I figure out the rest."

Darius Clark, a bakery worker in West Oakland who applied for the fund in February, remains in administrative review. A Tribune attempt to learn whether Clark is among this week's forty-seven approvals was unsuccessful. The Stabilization Fund administration does not release partial candidate lists — only confirmation once approvals are finalized. Clark's case has circulated through five prior editions without resolution, making his status this week journalistically pending.

The acceleration was triggered by two operational changes. First, the Mayor transferred disbursement authority from Webb to Deputy Mayor Brenda Okoro in late September, signaling a shift in the portfolio's political priority. Second, Council President Ramon Vega, who administers the fund's application and review process, committed the fund to processing fifty households per week — a target that would exhaust the current queue of 295 approvals within six weeks, pending no new applications or appeals.

The Stabilization Fund authorized $28 million in March. Of that, $4.2 million was allocated to Phase 1 disbursements — the mechanism through which the forty-seven households and the remaining queue are being paid. The fund's structure divides anti-displacement resources by neighborhood and by income level. West Oakland, where Hayes and Clark both live, carries the highest per-capita displacement pressure; the fund prioritizes applications from that corridor.

The economic implication is one that low-income households across Oakland are watching: whether anti-displacement reserves — limited and finite — can actually interrupt the displacement timeline. Hayes's $18,500 is a substantial sum for her economic position (home health aides in Oakland median earnings sit around $43,000 annually), but it is also a one-time check that absorbs rent increases for a defined window, not a permanent income adjustment or lease protection.

"This is a band-aid," said Elena Lewis, a longshoreman at the port who lives in Jack London, in an interview. "It's a good band-aid. But the math underneath — that's where the displacement pressure actually is. If rents rise 5, 6 percent a year, the fund can slow it but not stop it."

The Tribune will track the processing curve over the next six weeks and report when the 295-household queue reaches exhaustion.

October Breathes Different Here

The light changes, the breeze arrives, and the city remembers what seasons feel like

By Maria Keen | Bay Tribune Culture

The light hits the streets slant at 4 p.m. now, and the whole city reshapes itself. You notice it first on the concrete — shadows from the oaks on Telegraph fall differently, the late-afternoon gold lasts longer, catches on the glass storefronts and makes everything look like it's been polished. The air breaks at 62 degrees. There's a breeze from the Bay that doesn't feel cruel anymore.

I walk down Telegraph on a Tuesday and the first thing I notice is the crowd. Not a crowd, exactly — just more people standing still. Eating at the cafe tables. Turning their faces up like plants.

At the corner of Telegraph and 18th, I meet Philly Rodriguez tying the apron on a vendor stand that wasn't there last week. He moved to Temescal in August, he tells me, after a reorganization at work downtown. "I wanted to notice seasons again," he says, adjusting the umbrella angled against the October sun. "You don't do that in an office. You just turn the thermostat up or down."

What he's doing here is temporary — helping a friend run produce from a community garden for exactly four weeks while the harvest peaks. "October's the sweet spot," he says. "Tomatoes still coming. Greens starting. You get both." His hands are stained green from the morning. He doesn't seem to mind.

This is what October does here. It doesn't announce itself. It just changes the rhythm. The sports bars fill earlier — there's something about the light that makes people want to be around other people, sitting at long tables, drinking beer that tastes the way October tastes. The music venues on Telegraph go from half-full to sold out. The galleries in Jack London district stay open later.

In Rockridge, the farmers market on Broadway has doubled in size. I watch vendors arranging squash and late stone fruit, sweeping the excess leaves from their tables. The air smells like leaf-loam and fresh basil. Someone is playing a guitar from the corner. October brought them back, or maybe they never left — just the season makes people notice.

The construction on Grand Avenue doesn't feel as loud this month. The breeze carries sound differently. The sunlight makes the site look less wounded somehow, more temporary.

By six o'clock, the temperature drops and people start moving with intention again. The cafe tables empty. The vendors start closing their stands. But for those four hours between afternoon and evening, the whole city does what Philly described — turns its face up and remembers that seasons happen to us, not just around us.

"I'll do this for October," he says, counting the cash from the afternoon sales. "After that, I don't know. But I'll know what October looks like in Temescal now. That's something."

When the City Gets Crowded Again

Four neighborhoods feel the school-year surge — and the rhythm changes overnight

By Angela Reyes | Bay Tribune Education

School starts, and the whole city inhales. The buses on Broadway run fuller. The cafes stay open an extra hour because there are three more people sitting in the corner booth than there were in September. The bike racks overflow. Something shifts.

Four neighborhoods saw 8% jumps in residential population between August and September. West Oakland. Rockridge. Piedmont Avenue. Chinatown. The desks at Home Depot sold faster. The bike shops started restocking. The bus drivers began the small negotiations of knowing their regulars again — who gets off at 16th, who always carries a heavy backpack, whose mom picks them up on Tuesdays.

I spend a morning at a cafe in Rockridge. The barista knows the names of six students who come in on the way to school. She doesn't ask if they want their usual anymore — she just makes it. "September hits different," she tells me. "You can see it coming in August. Families start checking listings. Things move faster. By Labor Day weekend, you just know. The neighborhood changes."

What does it look like? It looks like a girl with a cello case waiting for the 12 on Telegraph. It looks like someone asking if the 51 bus stops on 40th Street anymore. It looks like parents walking their kids the same three blocks to school, same time every morning, and by the second week of October, those parents are waving to each other. Recognition takes ten days.

On Piedmont Avenue, the sidewalk cafes get louder. Not from adults — from the acoustic hum of someone studying at a corner table. Papers. Textbooks. The person next to them is also studying. They don't talk much, but they're both there. The quiet has a different shape.

In West Oakland, the laundromats stay open longer. The community center runs the same programs but fuller — someone told a friend, and the friend brought two more people. The rhythm doesn't change. The volume does.

Chinatown moves faster in October. The alleys between Grant and the 880 fill with people moving in and out of buildings. The grocery stores mark their prices differently — they know they've got four more weeks of students buying instant noodles before those students move out again in May. Some neighborhoods are just passing through for someone. For others, this is the anchor. October is when you feel the difference.

By the end of the month, the thing that felt new in September just feels normal. That's how you know the neighborhood has actually changed — not from posters or announcements, but from the moment when you stop noticing that the buses are fuller and you just assume they will be. That's October in those four neighborhoods. That's when the city remembers how to be crowded.

The Man in Center Is Doing Something Real

Danny Horn has six home runs in eight games — and the bar stools at Heinold's are paying attention

By P Slayer | Bay Tribune Sports [OPINION]

I was at the bar on Thursday night when Horn went deep in the seventh. Not the Coliseum — I couldn't get out there. The game was on the screen at Heinold's, the old place right on the waterfront in Jack London Square, and DeShawn Hartley — the photographer who shoots every home game for this paper when he's not behind a wedding lens — was the first one off his stool.

"Watch the box score tomorrow," he said to no one in particular. "He's doing it again."

Danny Horn has six home runs.

Oakland, I need you to stop and feel that number. Because six home runs in eight games from your center fielder is not a hot week. It is not a blip. It is a man who has found something in the mechanics of his swing and refuses to put it back.

I'll be honest with you. I have watched Danny Horn play center field in this city for seven seasons. I have seen him hit thirty home runs in a year and have the conversation be about someone else. He is the best player on this roster — the highest-paid, the highest-rated, the one the front office built around — and somehow, in a year with a farewell tour and a trade acquisition and a rookie at short, the man in center was still the part of the lineup you took for granted. Not because he was quiet. Because he was constant.

Then he started hitting baseballs into the bleachers like he was angry at October.

Now, I know what some of you are saying. You're saying eight games is eight games. You're saying every player on a hot team gets hot in October, and this is the A's at 7-1, which means the whole rotation is dealing and the lineup is clicking and everyone looks good in front of the right pitching. You'll say wait for the correction. Wait for the pitcher who figures him out in the second time through the league.

You're not wrong to be cautious. But you're missing what I'm watching.

There is a difference between a hot player and a changed player. A hot player is riding variance — the balls that die in the gap are finding gaps, the grounders that should be outs are finding holes. A changed player has altered something fundamental about the way he approaches an at-bat, and the results aren't luck, they're evidence. When Horn went deep in the Guardians blowout and then came back two games later and did it again in San Diego, those weren't the same swing getting lucky twice. Those were two different pitchers making two different mistakes because Horn forced them into uncomfortable counts and punished them both.

DeShawn turned back to the bar after the second one went out on Thursday and said, "He's not chasing anymore."

He's not chasing anymore. I don't know if you've tracked how that plays when a hitter stops expanding his zone, but I'll tell you what it looks like from a bar stool in Jack London: it looks like a man who has decided what he wants from a pitcher and is willing to wait for exactly that, and nothing else. That's not a hot week. That's a decision.

Six home runs. The A's at 7-1. Vinnie Keane watching from the dugout with six championships on his resume and another October potentially ahead of him.

Somewhere in all of that, in the middle of the farewell tour and the rotation suddenly becoming elite and the rookie finding his footing at shortstop, Danny Horn became the reason people are watching the game.

Go watch the man in center field.

Coles, Rivas, and the Rotation Nobody Saw Coming

Two starts in and the pitching staff is outrunning the offensive story

By Anthony | Bay Tribune Sports

When Travis Coles signed his deal with Oakland last winter — a two-year agreement worth forty-six million, one hundred eighty thousand dollars — the front office framed the acquisition as rotation depth. A dependable arm for the middle of the order. Someone who could eat innings on nights when the ace needed rest and the young arms needed time to develop.

Nobody called him the centerpiece of an elite rotation. That framing felt premature in February. After eight games, it no longer does.

Coles has made two starts since the season opened, and in both he was difficult to solve. His second outing, the nine-to-one win over San Diego, was the one that carried an argument. San Diego is not a last-place club. They have contact-first hitters who work deep counts and make starters earn every out. Coles gave them very little to work with — a performance that fit neatly into what his minor-league coaching film suggested he was capable of and what the Oakland front office clearly believed it was buying.

But the broader story is not Coles alone. Henry Rivas, working the three-to-eleven win over Cleveland, gave the A's the kind of start that changes how a pitching staff carries itself. When a rotation has one brilliant arm, the other starters pitch to keep the game close. When the two-starter throws like an ace, the rest of the unit breathes differently — looser counts, cleaner sequences, better rhythm. The A's scored eleven runs against Cleveland, but runs do not arrive in the eleventh through bad pitching alone. Rivas set the table by keeping hitters uncomfortable and leaving manager Deacon Seymour with clean innings to work with.

The result is a rotation that is, through eight games, functioning at a level that makes the run differential comprehensible. Oakland has outscored opponents by a margin that reflects genuine pitching depth, not offensive spike. The lineup has contributed — Danny Horn's six home runs are the most discussed number on the team at the moment, and rightly so — but runs are only half the equation, and the pitching half has held up its end.

There are questions worth asking before this calculates into projection. Arturo Ramos, the staff ace and Opening Day starter, has not been tested by a club of genuine offensive weight yet. Benji Dillon, the left-handed veteran in the five slot who is pitching his final season, will face lineups later in the month that will tell more about what he has left than any October matchup can. And Bryan Franco, the young shortstop who showed in his first major-league action that he could be a factor at the plate, is part of a lineup that will eventually encounter a pitcher who has scouted him carefully.

None of that diminishes what is happening right now.

Seymour, in his first year managing this club, is working with a rotation that has, through eight games, not given him a reason to reach for the bullpen before the sixth inning. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, exactly what a first-year manager wants in October — pitching long enough to trust, clean enough to project, with enough variation in the arsenal to keep opponents from settling into a comfortable approach.

The rotation wasn't supposed to be the headline. The headline was the farewell season, the trade paying off, the kid at short. The pitching staff was supposed to be the supporting evidence.

Through eight games, it is the argument.

What Vinnie Keane Sees From the Dugout Steps

436 home runs, six championships, and a team going 7-1 in October — the farewell season might have one more chapter

By Hal Richmond | Bay Tribune Sports

There is a way that men who have won many championships watch baseball games. I have spent forty years looking for it, and I am not certain I can describe it precisely, but I know it when I see it. It is not arrogance. It is not boredom. It is something closer to fluency — the expression of a person for whom the language of October has become so familiar that he no longer needs to translate it. He simply understands.

Vinnie Keane stands on the dugout steps at the Oakland Coliseum and watches a game already going seven-and-one, and I wonder what he reads in it.

He is thirty-seven years old, in the final months of a career that has produced four hundred thirty-six home runs, six championships, and a particular brand of Oakland mythology that does not belong to statistics alone. He is in the designated hitter's role now — that quiet acknowledgment that the legs and the range have done their work and the bat is what remains. He will not play every inning. He will watch, and think, and when the moment arrives, he will step to the plate with decades of accumulated knowing in his hands.

I covered the first of his six titles. I remember standing in the Coliseum tunnel after the final out and watching Keane — young then, loud, celebratory in the way of a man who cannot yet believe that the thing he has spent his whole life wanting has actually arrived. He was not fluent yet. He was still translating.

Six championships later, the language is his native tongue.

What I cannot know, but what I suspect, is that a team running seven-and-one in October looks different to a man who has won championships than it does to the rest of us. We see a hot start and feel the small vertigo of hoping. He has felt that vertigo and then survived the subsequent months and then stood in the October infield holding a trophy, enough times now to know exactly how long the road is between a good start and what that start might become.

Danny Horn has six home runs. The rotation is dealing — Henry Rivas brilliant in Cleveland, Travis Coles patient and sharp against San Diego. Benji Dillon, who is also making his final pass through the league, is anchoring the back of the rotation with the kind of craft that does not announce itself. Isley Kelley, in his own farewell season, is still at short, still the axis around which this defense rotates.

And at the center of it, the question: does Vinnie Keane get one more October?

I do not mean the question in terms of playing time or roster construction. Those details belong to other columns. I mean it the way the long view means it — does this team, in this year, carry a man who has given six championships to this city all the way to the place where the season becomes something more than a season? Does the city get to celebrate Keane the way it celebrated him before, confetti and asphalt and the sound of the waterfront at midnight?

The answer is seven-and-one. It is also only October.

I have been doing this long enough to know that I should not write the answer before the question has finished being asked. I have started columns in October thinking I knew where the season was going and been wrong in November. I have stood at the typewriter — and later the keyboard — in what felt like the middle of a dynasty and not known I was standing at its end.

What I know is this: Vinnie Keane is watching from the dugout steps, reading the game in a language I have spent forty years trying to learn, and the team he is watching is going seven-and-one.

For now, that is enough.

Bulls in the Finals

Giddey, Stanley, and the defense that nobody believed in until it won them a conference title

By Selena Grant | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

Let me say this plainly: no one in their right mind predicted this team would make it here without trading a single piece of the core.

The Chicago Bulls are in the NBA Finals. That sentence required eighteen months of proof before I was willing to type it without a qualifier attached. Now I'll type it again: the Bulls are in the NBA Finals, they earned the right the hard way — through the Eastern Conference bracket, against teams that had every incentive to slow them down — and what's happened since Mike Paulson rebuilt this roster is not an accident.

Start with Josh Giddey, because you have to start with Giddey. He finished the Eastern Conference Finals averaging 27 points, 8.4 rebounds, and 7.1 assists. Those are not supporting-cast numbers. Those are the numbers of a player who arrived in Chicago two seasons ago as a project and turned himself into the engine of a Finals team. The triple-double threat on any given night is real now, not theoretical. Whatever the front office gave up to get here — and Paulson gave up real assets — the math works.

Adash Stanley had the defining sequence of the conference run. Down eight in Game 4 against Boston, Stanley scored eleven points in four minutes on nothing but pull-up threes and one drive that drew a flagrant. He's been chippy all season — the technical fouls are documented, the league has noticed — but chippy and effective are not mutually exclusive, and Stanley in crunch time is effective. He finished at 14.2 points and 5.8 assists for the series. His three-point percentage held above forty. Boston's perimeter defense is good. Forty percent against them is a statement.

Ayo Dosunmu does something for this team that doesn't show up in the highlights: he makes the defense coherent. The Bulls are minus-3.1 points per 100 possessions better with Dosunmu on the floor than off it. He posted 18 points and 7 assists in the Game 6 close, the game where the Bulls actually closed, and he did it without looking like he was trying. That's the tell. Dosunmu never looks like he's trying. He just produces.

Matas Buzelis and Patrick Williams deserve credit for what they've absorbed without complaint. Williams at the four has been asked to guard everyone from stretch fours to backup centers this postseason, and he's done it. Buzelis is the x-factor the way he's always been the x-factor — you don't know when he's going to go for 20, but when he does the team wins. In five playoff games this run, Buzelis's 20-plus-point outings have all resulted in Chicago wins.

The Finals opponent will test something this team hasn't had tested yet: sustained pressure over a full seven-game series from a team with equal or superior talent. The East bracket did not offer that. The questions about depth, about whether the second unit can hold leads, about whether Stanley's technical foul habit becomes a liability when the referees are watching — those questions are still open.

But here's what's also true: I watched this team play fifty-four games before I was willing to call it real. I've watched them in the postseason. The defense is real. Giddey is real. The culture Paulson built — accountable, no stars on the sideline during crunch time, no excuses about fatigue — that's real.

The city believes it. The question now is whether the roster can finish it.

35th and Halsted, October

The tamale truck, the corner booth, and the morning a neighborhood decided to believe

By Talia Finch | Bay Tribune Chicago Bureau

The tamales at Darius Reeves's truck smell like pork and cumin and something I can't name, and on a Tuesday morning in October with the Bulls in the NBA Finals, there's a line that starts before he's finished lifting the steam tray lid.

Darius is forty-four, born in Bronzeville, running this truck at 35th and Halsted for three years. He told me once that he picked this corner because it's between the Red Line stop and Romano's — foot traffic in both directions, morning and lunch. This morning he's not thinking about foot traffic. He's thinking about the same thing everyone on this block is thinking about.

"I've been doing this corner since 2038," he said, wrapping two pork tamales in foil without looking down at his hands. "I've seen this street after bad losses. I've seen it after the trade rumors. I've never seen it like this." He gestured at the line, which had grown by four people in the time we'd been talking. "These people don't normally talk to each other."

He's right. I watched a man in a FedEx uniform compare defensive rotations with a woman in hospital scrubs for seven minutes. Neither of them looked like they wanted to be anywhere else.

Inside Romano's, the ceiling fans were off again — October finally arrived in Bridgeport, and the room held a chill that felt earned after the summer's heat. Paulette Okafor was at her usual table by nine, phone face-up, coffee in front of her. She works at the elementary school two blocks off Halsted and she's been coming in early most mornings of the season. Today felt different.

"I want to be careful," she said, wrapping both hands around her mug. "I've wanted things before." She's sixty-two and she's seen enough Chicago sports to know the shape of a dream that doesn't land. But she was smiling when she said it. "I want to get to a Finals game and not think about anything else. Just a game. Just basketball."

Raymond Polk arrived twenty minutes later, hard hat on the table beside his coffee. He does contractor work across the South Side and he was supposed to be at a job in Canaryville by ten. He didn't seem to be in a hurry.

"The thing about this," he said, meaning the Finals, meaning all of it, "is that it's real now. You can't take it back. They made it. Whatever happens after — that already happened."

He said it the way a man says something he's been rehearsing for weeks without knowing it. Paulette nodded. Darius Reeves, visible through Romano's window, handed a tamale to a woman in a Bulls jacket and pointed toward the restaurant, saying something that made her laugh.

Out on 35th, a kid on a bike had a pennant zip-tied to the handlebars. It caught the October wind and snapped tight as he passed the corner, and for a second the whole block turned to watch him ride south, toward the park, toward the lake, toward wherever a city goes when it finally starts to believe.

LETTERS TO THE EDITOR
------------------------------------------------------------

Dear Editor,

Eight months. That is how long I waited between the day the city told me I was approved and the day the check arrived. Eight months of checking the mailbox. Eight months of my daughter asking if we were moving yet. Eight months of a city that stamped my paperwork and then forgot about it.

The check came this week. $18,500. I held it in my hands and cried — not because I was grateful, though I am. Because I was relieved. There is a difference. Gratitude is when someone gives you something. Relief is when someone finally stops making you wait for what was already yours.

I am staying in my apartment. I am staying in West Oakland. That is what this money means. For now, that is enough.

— Beverly Hayes, 58, West Oakland, home health aide

---

Dear Editor,

I live on 73rd Avenue in District 5. Last year my neighbor's son had an episode — wouldn't come inside, standing in the middle of the street, talking to someone none of us could see. We called 911. Two squad cars showed up. The officers were decent about it. But they had handcuffs and radios and training for confrontation. What they did not have was training for a twenty-two-year-old man who was scared and hearing voices.

This week I learned that next time, a crisis counselor comes instead. Someone trained for exactly that moment. I don't know if it will work. I do know that what we had wasn't working. My neighbor's son spent three days in custody before anyone evaluated him. A counselor might have had him home that night.

I am willing to find out.

— Denise Okafor, 44, East Oakland, school bus driver

---

Dear Editor,

I want to understand the math on Baylight. $2.1 billion committed to a Tax Increment Financing district that locks public revenue for thirty years. Councilman Ashford asked for a transparency dashboard before the TIF closes. Director Ramos says she will deliver one simultaneously. That is not the same thing. "Before" means you see the terms and then you agree. "Simultaneously" means you agree and then you see the terms.

I am not against development on the waterfront. I am against closing a thirty-year financial commitment without the oversight mechanism in place first. Once that door shuts, it does not reopen. Ask anyone who has signed a contract they could not review in advance how that usually works out.

— Lorraine Castillo, 67, Jack London, retired city planner

---

Dear Editor,

October in Oakland is the secret the rest of the country never learns. Sixty-two degrees. A breeze that comes off the lake like it has somewhere to be. The light hits the hills around four in the afternoon and turns everything the color of honey.

I walked down to Lake Merritt yesterday with no agenda, no errand, no complaint to file. I sat on a bench near the pergola and watched joggers and strollers and a man teaching his daughter to skip rocks. The geese were unimpressed by all of us. The water was flat and gold.

There is no policy in this letter. No demand. Just a man who has lived near this lake for thirty-one years telling you that some days the city does not need to be fixed. It just needs to be noticed.

— Robert Tran, 71, Lake Merritt, retired maritime engineer
