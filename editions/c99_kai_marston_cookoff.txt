**The Great Mandela Parkway Deep-Fry Disaster**
**By: Kai Marston**
**Cycle 99 | Thanksgiving Day**

The overcast sky above West Oakland was locked at 57 degrees, and the wind cutting down Mandela Parkway carried the distinct, perilous scent of boiling peanut oil. 

It was the third annual Thanksgiving Cook-Off at the Vinnie Keane West Oakland Baseball Academy. The rehabbed firehouse is usually a sanctuary for youth baseball, but today it was ground zero for neighborhood bragging rights. 

Vinnie Keane, the A's designated hitter who currently has six World Series rings and 436 career home runs to his name, was manning a commercial-grade smoker. He’s in his farewell season, the living heartbeat of the Oakland dynasty, but on Thanksgiving, he is just a guy trying to keep his brisket from drying out. 

His wife, Amara Keane, a senior veterinarian who runs a clinic up in Rockridge, was working a much more stressful zone defense. While Vinnie signed autographs, Amara was intercepting well-meaning uncles trying to slip turkey legs to neighborhood stray dogs. 

"Oakland generosity is a beautiful thing," Amara told me, gently prying a rib bone from a golden retriever mix. "But it is an absolute nightmare for canine gastrointestinal tracts."

The mood in the city was electric. The A's are 71-25. They just swept the Red Sox. The streets are vibrating. That baseball fever is exactly what brought the disruption.

At 1:15 PM, a crew of hardcore A's tailgaters—already pre-gaming the rest of the season—arrived outside the academy with a 30-quart turkey fryer and too much ambition. They wanted to feed the neighborhood. Instead, they fed the fire department. 

When a frozen twenty-pound bird hit the boiling oil, the resulting fireball illuminated the overcast sky. It was the exact "tailgate grill fire" that Engine Company 4 had to respond to today. 

But before the sirens even crested the hill, Vinnie Keane—the man who didn't flinch when he hit the home run that won the 2031 championship—calmly walked out of the academy holding a heavy-duty chemical extinguisher. 

"Back up, guys," Vinnie said, pulling the pin. 

He extinguished the apocalyptic fryer with the same smooth, effortless swing that has defined his 18-year career. The crowd of kids and tailgaters erupted in a standing ovation. Nine minutes of cheers. 

Amara Keane watched from the academy doors, holding two rescued dogs by their collars. She just shook her head and smiled. "He really can't help himself," she said. "Always has to be the closer."

The fire was out, the brisket was safe, and the A's magic number keeps shrinking. Happy Thanksgiving, Oakland.
